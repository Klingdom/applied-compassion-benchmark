# Market Review — Benchmark Publisher Presentation Practices
## For: Compassion Benchmark Daily Briefing (compassionbenchmark.com/updates)
## Date: 2026-05-19

---

## Per-Institution Findings

### 1. Freedom House — Freedom in the World

- **Changes vs. levels:** Leads with level (Free / Partly Free / Not Free) and numeric aggregate score; changes highlighted only when a status category shifts. Year-over-year deltas published in supplemental tables, not in the headline summary.
- **Evidence citation:** Each country narrative cites specific events inline with year and brief source attribution (e.g., "following the January 2024 amendment to…"). Full methodology document separate but linked. No inline hyperlinks in the published report — evidence lives in prose, not a linked source registry.
- **Interim updates:** Annual only. No public interim alerts between report cycles. Researchers publish op-eds and blog posts through FH channels but these are not labeled as score events.
- **Copyable pattern:** The "Status Change" summary table at the start of the annual report — a scannable list of which countries moved band and why, in one sentence each. High-signal, low-noise entry point before narrative detail.

---

### 2. Transparency International — Corruption Perceptions Index

- **Changes vs. levels:** Leads with score (0–100) and global rank. Changes framed by rank movement and score delta vs. prior year; significant rank drops highlighted in press release headers. Band/tier language used loosely ("high corruption," "low corruption") without formal band definitions.
- **Evidence citation:** CPI explicitly states it is a composite of 13 third-party data sources; each source is listed with methodology link. Individual country findings cite the source data inputs (e.g., "World Bank CPIA, V-Dem"). No event-level sourcing — the unit of evidence is a data source, not a specific incident.
- **Interim updates:** Annual only for the composite score. TI publishes interim "Corruption Barometer" surveys between cycles, but these are separate products with distinct methodology, not labeled as score updates.
- **Copyable pattern:** The explicit source-layer model — separating "what the score is built from" (data sources) from "what changed" (event narrative). Compassion Benchmark's dimensional math is a structural analogue and should be made equally visible.

---

### 3. EIU — Democracy Index / Worldwide Cost of Living

- **Changes vs. levels:** Strongly level-first. Rankings by score are the core deliverable. Regime-type category changes (Full Democracy → Flawed Democracy) get headline treatment; numeric movements do not unless large. The Cost of Living index publishes interim city-level rankings twice yearly, but changes are framed as new rankings rather than delta-from-prior.
- **Evidence citation:** Methodology white paper published separately; survey questions disclosed; expert assessor panel described but not individually named. Country narratives cite conditions but rarely cite specific events with dates and sources.
- **Interim updates:** Democracy Index is annual. Interim publications are framed as separate products (Global Outlook, Regional Roundtables), not as score events. Paywalled for institutional subscribers.
- **Copyable pattern:** Regime-category language as the primary public signal, with numeric score as secondary. Users remember "downgraded from Full Democracy" more than a 0.4-point movement. The band-crossing emphasis Compassion Benchmark already uses for China (Developing → Critical) is the right structural parallel.

---

### 4. Reporters Without Borders — World Press Freedom Index

- **Changes vs. levels:** Rank-first, score-second. Year-over-year rank change (e.g., "fell 12 places") is the primary movement signal. The 2024 redesign introduced five indicators as sub-scores alongside the composite, making dimensional attribution visible for the first time.
- **Evidence citation:** Indicator methodology published openly. Country narratives include specific journalist arrest events with dates. RSF also maintains a separate "Barometer" tracker (journalists killed, detained) with near-real-time updates — distinct from but linked to the annual index.
- **Interim updates:** Annual composite. The Barometer operates as a live secondary feed. The two products are clearly labeled as different instruments — the Barometer does not claim to update the index score, it updates an underlying fact record.
- **Copyable pattern:** The Barometer / Index separation — a live event feed that accumulates evidence without claiming to change the formal score until the release cycle. Compassion Benchmark's confirmation records and sub-threshold logs function analogously and should be presented with similar framing: evidence accumulating, score pending.

---

### 5. Edelman Trust Barometer

- **Changes vs. levels:** Annual reveal is a major media event anchored on the "trust gap" framing (not a numeric score movement). Interim "Trust Pulse" surveys are released quarterly and framed as sentiment shifts, not index updates. Changes are expressed as percentage-point moves in survey responses, not composite scores.
- **Evidence citation:** Survey methodology (sample sizes, fielding dates, markets) disclosed in technical appendix. No event-level sourcing — the evidence unit is survey response data.
- **Interim updates:** Quarterly Trust Pulse releases between annual reports. These are explicitly branded as supplemental, not as revisions to the annual Trust Barometer score.
- **Copyable pattern:** The "interim pulse" concept — a formally labeled interim product that feeds the annual score without claiming to replace it. Compassion Benchmark's daily briefing is closer to this model than to a score-release model. Labeling the briefing explicitly as "evidence accumulation" rather than "score update" resolves the tension between daily cadence and annual-style authority.

---

### 6. RepRisk / Sustainalytics / MSCI ESG

- **Changes vs. levels:** All three are change-first for institutional subscribers. RepRisk's RPR (Risk Rating) is updated weekly; alerts fire when a company's rating changes. Sustainalytics publishes "controversy alerts" as discrete events. MSCI ESG Ratings update continuously with a published "rating history" per company. Movement is the product for institutional users; levels are the reference.
- **Evidence citation:** Each controversy alert cites specific incidents with source (news article, regulatory filing, NGO report) and date. RepRisk tags incidents by ESG issue category and affected stakeholder. Source transparency is highest in this group of eleven — incident-level sourcing is the baseline expectation.
- **Interim updates:** Continuous or weekly for all three. Updates are labeled as rating changes, not new reports. The institutional subscriber model means rigor is table stakes — any unexplained movement generates client questions.
- **Copyable pattern:** Incident-level source citation as the default, not the exception. Each score movement is accompanied by the triggering event, its source, and its dimensional attribution. Compassion Benchmark's digest already does this (e.g., "EU Council; Morgan Lewis confirmation — Tier-3 external accountability determination") and should surface this pattern in the public briefing.

---

### 7. CDP — Carbon Disclosure Project

- **Changes vs. levels:** Score (A through D-) is the primary signal; the A-List is a major annual event. Change is presented as "moved from B to A" — categorical movement, not numeric delta. Companies that decline are noted but not headlined.
- **Evidence citation:** Scores are self-reported via CDP questionnaire; scoring methodology published; third-party verification required at higher score levels. The evidence unit is the company's own disclosure, not external event sourcing.
- **Interim updates:** Annual only for formal scores. CDP publishes sector and thematic reports between cycles.
- **Copyable pattern:** The A-List reveal as a positive-signal flagship event. Compassion Benchmark lacks a positive-signal flagship (the floor confirmations for Russia/Israel receive proportionate but not celebratory coverage). A periodic "high-score movers" publication could create a positive-signal complement.

---

### 8. World Justice Project — Rule of Law Index

- **Changes vs. levels:** Both, but with discipline. The report leads with global and regional averages, then highlights "notable decliners" and "notable improvers" as named tables. Numeric change (e.g., "-0.04 on Factor 2") is reported but narrative emphasis is on direction and pattern, not arithmetic precision.
- **Evidence citation:** Eight factors, 44 sub-factors, 140,000+ household and expert survey responses. Methodology published in full. Country narratives describe conditions but cite survey data aggregates rather than specific events.
- **Interim updates:** Annual only. No interim products.
- **Copyable pattern:** The "notable decliners / notable improvers" table format — a named, scannable list of who moved significantly and in which direction, separate from the full ranking. Directly applicable to the daily briefing's summary table.

---

### 9. SIPRI Arms Transfers Database / Yearbook

- **Changes vs. levels:** Data-first. SIPRI publishes trend figures (five-year moving averages) precisely to suppress year-to-year noise. Single-year changes are deliberately de-emphasized. The Yearbook is an annual narrative synthesis over a continuous data release.
- **Evidence citation:** Transfer data is documented by deal — supplier, recipient, weapon system, quantity, delivery year, source documentation. The database is the evidence trail; the yearbook narrative cites from it. Primary sources are government procurement records, defense industry reports, and verified news. The citation model is archival: every claim traceable to a specific documented transfer.
- **Interim updates:** The database is updated continuously; the Yearbook synthesizes annually. SIPRI press releases accompany the trend data publication in March each year.
- **Copyable pattern:** The archival citation model — every scoring event should be traceable to a specific documented incident with source. Compassion Benchmark's methodology category system (naming and dating each anchor instance) is a structural equivalent and should be surfaced publicly.

---

### 10. ACLED — Armed Conflict Location and Event Data

- **Changes vs. levels:** Event-first. ACLED does not publish index scores or rankings. It publishes event counts, fatalities, and actor attribution with location and date. Change is shown as week-over-week event frequency, not as a composite score movement. The "ACLED Snapshot" weekly brief leads with the most significant new event clusters.
- **Evidence citation:** Each event in the database has a source (news article, NGO report, government statement) with URL and date. Source credibility is rated. The database is the citation — users can drill to the underlying event record. No narrative claim is made without a database anchor.
- **Interim updates:** Database updated twice weekly; weekly Snapshot published; regional monitors publish longer analyses. The cadence question is answered by clear product tiers: real-time database, weekly brief, monthly regional analysis. Each tier makes its own authority claims without overstating.
- **Copyable pattern for cadence:** The explicit tier model. ACLED's weekly Snapshot does not claim to be the database; it summarizes significant patterns from it. Compassion Benchmark's daily briefing should similarly be framed as a pattern summary drawn from the accumulating evidence record, not as a score publication event. This removes the pressure to justify every daily observation as a formal scoring action.

---

### 11. Our World in Data

- **Changes vs. levels:** Both, with interactive context. OWID's default chart view shows time-series for each indicator; change is always visible relative to a baseline. But the editorial framing of Data Insights (their daily/weekly feature) leads with the observation ("child mortality has halved since 1990"), not the numeric delta.
- **Evidence citation:** Every chart links to the underlying dataset and the original source (UN, WHO, World Bank, academic study). The citation chain is: observation → OWID dataset → original source → methodology note. Full transparency at every layer. "Learn more" links are standard, not exceptional.
- **Interim updates:** Charts update when underlying data updates (varies by indicator — some daily, some annual). OWID does not claim to have updated a metric; it reflects upstream source updates. Data Insights are published at irregular intervals (roughly daily) and are clearly editorial, not data events.
- **Copyable pattern for cadence:** The distinction between data events and editorial observations. OWID publishes editorial insights continuously without claiming that the underlying data changed. Compassion Benchmark's confirmation records and sub-threshold findings are editorial observations — they should be labeled as such, not forced into the score-change framing.

## Synthesis

### Three Most Applicable Patterns

**1. The incident-level evidence anchor (RepRisk / ACLED / SIPRI)**

The highest-authority benchmark publishers in this set treat every claim as traceable to a specific dated incident with a named source. Compassion Benchmark's digest already does this in the internal record ("EU Council, April 23, 2026; Morgan Lewis confirmation"). The gap is that this citation chain is not yet visible in the public briefing. The /updates page should surface the evidence anchor for each score event — not just the finding, but the source, the date, and which dimension it affects. This is the single most credible-looking practice in the field and also the most under-used in public benchmark communication.

**2. The tier model for cadence (ACLED / OWID / RSF Barometer)**

ACLED and Our World in Data both solve the daily-cadence-without-losing-rigor problem the same way: by distinguishing clearly between the evidence record (which accumulates continuously) and the formal analytical product (which is published on a defined schedule). RSF does this with the Barometer vs. the Index. Compassion Benchmark should explicitly label the daily briefing as an evidence-accumulation record, with formal score changes as discrete events within it. This removes the implicit pressure to treat every daily observation as a score event, and it accurately describes what the digest already is.

**3. Band-crossing as the primary public signal (Freedom House / EIU / CDP)**

All three institutions lead with categorical movement — status changes, regime-type downgrades, A-List additions — rather than numeric deltas. Numeric scores are supporting detail. Compassion Benchmark already does this internally (China's Developing → Critical is the lead item in the May 18 digest). The public briefing should reflect this hierarchy: band crossings and status changes headline; numeric deltas are the supporting row in the evidence table. Sub-threshold movements and confirmations are secondary content.

---

### Where the Field Is Weak (Gaps Compassion Benchmark Can Own)

**Differentiated signal types are almost entirely absent.** No institution in this set publicly distinguishes between: a score that changed, a score that was confirmed under new evidence, and a score under boundary pressure. Freedom House, TI, and WJP publish only annual changes. ACLED and OWID publish continuous events but no composite scores. The ESG providers distinguish alerts from ratings but not confirmation from movement. Compassion Benchmark's existing internal taxonomy — formal change / sub-threshold / confirmation / boundary watch — is more granular than anything in this field and should be made visible. A public-facing signal type label (e.g., "CONFIRMED," "BOUNDARY WATCH," "BAND CHANGE") on each daily briefing item would be novel and immediately useful to a research audience.

**Evidence trails are opaque in annual publishers and nonexistent in survey-based publishers.** TI, EIU, and Edelman cite data sources but not events. SIPRI and ACLED cite events but produce no composite scores. The combination — composite score + dimensional attribution + event-level source citation + named methodology category — is not done by any institution in this set. Compassion Benchmark's internal digest already contains all four layers. The work is making it legible publicly.

**Interim signals between annual cycles are uniformly under-exploited.** Only RSF (Barometer), RepRisk (weekly alerts), and ACLED (twice-weekly database) operate in near-real-time. Annual institutions treat the period between releases as a dead zone. For entities with fast-moving situations — active conflict states, entities under legal proceedings — this is a significant authority gap that Compassion Benchmark's daily cadence fills by default.

---

### On Citing Primary Sources, Signal Differentiation, and Evidence Authority

The authoritative citation pattern from this field: **named source + date + institutional type + dimensional attribution**. Example from the May 18 digest that already meets this standard: "EU Council; Morgan Lewis confirmation — Tier-3 external accountability determination, BND dimension." The public briefing should preserve this chain at the same resolution. Do not summarize to "EU sanctions" without the date, issuing body, and dimension. The specificity is the credibility signal.

Signal differentiation should be explicit in the briefing structure. The May 18 digest already tracks: band crossings (China), sub-threshold movements (OpenAI), confirmations (Russia, Israel, Hungary), and boundary watches (China, Oracle, Hungary, Marshall Islands). These are four categorically different types of finding. The public page should label them as such. "CONFIRMED AT FLOOR" is a different claim than "BOUNDARY WATCH — 0.3pt above Critical" — both are meaningful, but only if readers can see the difference.

Evidence authority follows from the distinction between assessment-triggered changes and evidence-accumulation records. The ESG and ACLED models show that continuous monitoring gains authority from consistency and traceability, not from annual release cadence. Compassion Benchmark's daily briefing should not try to mimic the authority of an annual release — it should claim the authority of a continuous, traceable monitoring record, which is a different and more defensible form.
