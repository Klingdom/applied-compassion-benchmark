## Impacts and Insights

- **The adjudicated-vs-unadjudicated boundary is the cycle's defining methodological lesson.** Three major corporate entities (3M, UnitedHealth, CVS) and one major country entity (United States) were assessed on related themes — institutional harm through systemic policy and operational conduct. The US proposal was generated; the corporate entities were not. The distinguishing variable is not the severity of the alleged harm but whether a judicial authority has found the conduct unlawful. This is a structurally important discipline: the methodology's value lies partly in its resistance to generating proposals on the basis of accusations alone.
- **Seven floor entities in one cycle is notable.** The floor cohort (Russia, Sudan, South Sudan, Afghanistan, Myanmar, xAI/Grok, Yemen) spans every major harm category. This suggests the floor designation is not a methodological failure mode but a reflection of genuine concentration of the worst institutional conduct across a small number of entities. The floor is not a rounding down — it is an evidence-grounded designation that requires active-perpetration or equivalent across multiple dimensions.
- **The Pacific rotation cluster (Kiribati, Marshall Islands, Nauru, Tuvalu, Sao Tome) confirms stable.** These entities face structural pressures (climate displacement, nuclear legacy, small-state governance) that are priced as continuation. The rotation system is correctly distinguishing between structural-priced conditions and new acute signals.
- **Sovereign-enforcement density in healthcare and insurance is accelerating.** The AZ AG cartel suit extends the enforcement cluster to eight named insurers. The FILED-BUT-UNADJUDICATED ruling is holding, but the density is approaching a level where a single adjudication is likely within the annual assessment window.
