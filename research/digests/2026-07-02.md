## Impacts and Insights

**El Salvador's first-baseline assessment raises the most analytically precise application of the methodology in the July series: when is a seed score vs. an evidence-based score?** The 20.3 seed was a population-weighted estimate assigned at index build; tonight's 15.0 is derived from documented, dated, in-force conduct. The -5.3 delta is not a reassessment of El Salvador's prior conduct — it is a replacement of an estimate with evidence. The methodology handles this as a "first baseline" rather than a "downgrade," but the operational effect is a band crossing. This is why the proposal is at medium confidence: the framework is internally consistent, but the absence of a prior baseline means there is no prior assessment to compare against for drift detection.

**The ACT 2.4 retention for homicide reduction is the most contested finding in tonight's assessment.** El Salvador's homicide rate declined from one of the world's highest to among its lowest under Bukele's administration — a genuine civic benefit. The ACT dimension scores responsiveness, efficacy, and resource mobilization in reducing harm to people. The detention mechanism that produced the homicide reduction is itself the primary civil liberties harm scored elsewhere. Retaining ACT credit while docking seven other dimensions for the same state of exception is a deliberate trade-off, not an oversight. The composite effect of holding ACT at 2.4 (vs. dropping it proportionally) is that the proposed score (15.0) sits above the region's lowest scorers (Bahrain 9.4, Mali 12.5), reflecting a genuine partial-positive.

**Five first baselines in one cycle suggests the scanner's priority routing logic is bringing in genuinely unassessed entities with relevant news exposure** — not recycling previously assessed entities. Pakistan, Taiwan, Georgia, and Princeton had all been priority-flagged on events attributable to external actors (Chinese pressure on Taiwan, federal action on Princeton, monsoon floods in Pakistan, chronic crackdown in Georgia), and all four confirmed at their seed/prior scores. El Salvador was the exception: genuinely new in-force conduct cleared the threshold.

**The subdim-sidecar milestone is structurally significant.** Prior cycles logged assessment findings at the composite and dimensional level only. Tonight's 5 sidecars add per-subdimension evidence — specific evidence items linked to specific subdimension codes (e.g., A3 Blind Spot Mitigation, B4 Refusal Ethics). This creates the data layer needed for dimension-trend analysis, cross-entity subdimension comparison, and eventually automated detection of recurring sub-threshold movements that compound into threshold events.

## Methodology Notes

### First-Baseline Codified-Conduct Assessment (Applied: El Salvador)

El Salvador's assessment establishes the first-baseline codified-conduct procedure:
1. **Seed score vs. evidence-based score:** The 20.3 seed was assigned at bulk-import without per-entity evidence. The formal assessment applies the canonical formula to dated, documented conduct events.
2. **Chronic vs. new-window evidence:** The state of exception (since 2022) is chronic. The indefinite re-election amendment (July 2025) and foreign-agents law (May 2025) are in-force own-conduct within the scoring window.
3. **ACT retention rationale:** The homicide reduction is a documented outcome of the state of exception. ACT scores what the government does to protect people from harm. The detention mechanism is scored separately under BND, ACC, and EQU. Retaining ACT credit for outcome while docking other dimensions for mechanism is a deliberate methodological trade-off — not double-counting.
4. **Confidence level (medium):** First baselines start at medium confidence absent a prior cycle's assessment to validate against. Drift detection is not available. The medium rating will upgrade to high after the first reassessment confirms the score.
5. **Conservative anchoring vs. peers:** The 15.0 proposed score is conservative relative to peers: Bahrain (9.4) and Mali (12.5) have longer documented state-perpetration records; El Salvador is above both, reflecting that the ACT-dimension homicide-reduction credit genuinely distinguishes the cases.
