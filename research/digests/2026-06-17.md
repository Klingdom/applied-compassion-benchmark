## Impacts and Insights

The June 17 cycle is analytically defined by the K.G.M. verdict's methodological significance, not its composite effect. This is an unusual configuration: the cycle's most important analytical event produces no composite delta. The insight is about the evidence tier, not the score. For entities already at or near their dimensional floors, new adjudicated evidence reinforces the existing score rather than creating new downward space. The benchmark's proportionality framework prevents a single bellwether verdict from moving a composite when the relevant dimension is floored and the broader litigation is overwhelmingly pre-adjudication.

The two-cycle evidence-tier illustration (June 16-17) is also a product of the assessment calendar's density: five major tech-sector cases across two nights, all resolved through the same framework, producing consistent outcomes. This kind of density is rare and analytically useful -- it demonstrates the framework's coherence under simultaneous multi-case pressure.

The zero-proposal result on a night with genuinely significant new adverse evidence (Meta verdict, India HRW documentation, Iran execution data) is the methodological story. The benchmark's floor architecture means that the evidence-to-score relationship is non-linear at the bottom of the scale: additional adverse evidence at or near a dimension floor produces reinforcement, not movement. This is correct methodology, but it requires explicit communication to avoid the misread that 'no score change = no significant findings.'

Bolivia's third confirmation cycle at the applied 6.3 (June 14 applied, June 16 confirmed, June 17 confirmed) closes the immediate post-application window. The score is stable; the forward risk is defined by the June 15 law's activation potential, not by further baseline assessment.
