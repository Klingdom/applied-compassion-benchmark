## CYCLE HEADLINE

This back-fill cycle is defined by two first-baseline anchors — Elevance Health and Bolivia — both replacing unanchored placeholder scores with evidence-grounded positions. Neither is a marginal delta: Elevance Health's -7.5 reflects two concurrent active DOJ False Claims Act enforcement actions; Bolivia's -5.0 reflects an active 14-day humanitarian blockade with three documented deaths. Together, they illustrate a core benchmark discipline: placeholder scores are not neutral — they systematically overstate an entity's compassion standing until real evidence anchors them.

The cycle's methodological contribution is the formal introduction of the HUMANITARIAN-BLOCKADE-FIRST-BASELINE-PROTOCOL, which governs how live-crisis first-baseline assessments are issued: downward from the placeholder anchored to documented harm, with a 30-day post-resolution reassessment trigger, and without consuming the full Critical-band room prematurely. This protocol will recur as the benchmark works through the large body of never-assessed countries.

The Turkey documented hold is the cycle's most analytically detailed confirmation. Three discrete May 26 crackdown events — Izmir tear gas dispersal, Güzelbahçe mayor detained, 93 social media accounts blocked — were each evaluated against the categorical-novelty test established in the May 25 ruling and each failed it. This is not absence of evidence but disciplined application of a published standard. The hold at 15.1 is well-reasoned and provides the substantive basis for subsequent carry-forward holds in the May 27 cycle.

## Impacts and Insights

**On the first-baseline backlog:** The May 26 cycle provides two examples of the benchmark's most structurally important ongoing task: replacing unanchored placeholder scores with evidence-grounded baselines. The Fortune 500 index carries approximately 400+ entities with unanchored or near-placeholder scores. The health insurance sub-sector pattern — where DOJ enforcement actions produce anchored Developing-band scores of 25-31 — suggests the uniform 37.5 placeholder systematically overstates institutional compassion quality. This is not a scoring error in any single cycle; it is a structural artifact of the benchmark's genesis, where index construction preceded systematic assessment.

**On the humanitarian-blockade methodology:** Bolivia's provisional first-baseline introduces a precedent for live-crisis assessment that differs structurally from the benchmark's primary approach of assessing chronic governance patterns. Bolivia's score is provisional not because the evidence is uncertain but because the crisis is still evolving. The 30-day reassessment trigger is methodologically sound, but it creates a category of "provisional published scores" that the benchmark has not previously formalized. If provisional scores become common (as first-baseline assessments expand into the developing world, where crises are more frequent), the methodology will need a published convention for how provisional status is displayed.

**On the Turkey categorical-novelty framework:** The five-test framework used in the May 26 Turkey assessment (lethal force, mass new arrests, formal CHP closure, emergency declaration, floor-proximity discipline) is the most rigorous single-entity hold justification in the current cycle series. It demonstrates that holding a score is not a passive failure to assess but an active analytical decision requiring the same evidentiary discipline as an apply. This framing — holds are scored decisions, not non-decisions — is worth formalizing as a methodology principle.

**On the scale-floor problem:** Russia (0.0) and Iran (2.5) are the benchmark's most urgent unsolved methodological question. Both entities continue generating credible Tier-1 evidence of severe harm, and both sit at or near the scale floor. The current convention — document and annotate, but do not score below 0.0 — preserves scale coherence but creates an asymmetry: entities improving from near-floor positions get scored; entities at or near the floor with additional evidence cannot. This asymmetry will eventually produce visible inconsistency if two floor-adjacent entities have meaningfully different evidence records but identical scores.
