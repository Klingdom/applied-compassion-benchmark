## Impacts and Insights

**Three false positives screened in one cycle is unusual and diagnostic.** In prior cycles, the screening discipline has caught one false positive per cycle at most. Catching three in a single cycle — using three distinct §3e-bis rules — demonstrates both the quality of the evidence environment (multiple plausible-sounding signals) and the robustness of the screening methodology. The Rwanda stale-baseline false positive is now appearing in two consecutive cycles (Jun 23 and Jun 24), which suggests the scanner's baseline-provenance cache for Rwanda should be corrected to prevent recurrence.

**The Mali flag-for-review illustrates a real methodological tension in the scoring architecture.** When a peer-consistent reweight has been applied to one Sahel country (Burkina Faso, Jun 1) but not yet to its neighbor (Mali), the scoring implicitly treats similar documented conduct differently. The UN Special Procedures documentation for Mali is substantively comparable to the evidence that triggered Burkina Faso's reweight. The flag-for-review is the correct instrument — but the coordinator decision should be treated as urgent given the accumulating evidence base.

**Lebanon and Ukraine together are the cleanest test of the attribution rule under simultaneous active conflict.** Both entities confirm at their existing scores because harm caused by external perpetrators is attributed to those perpetrators, not to the victim state. Lebanon (17.2) is scored on its own governance conduct, not on Israeli military actions. Ukraine (50.0) is scored on its own conduct, not on Russian strikes. The symmetry across both cases in a single cycle is methodologically important — it demonstrates the rule operates consistently across geopolitically charged contexts.

**The Houthi detention of 69+ UN staff is the most significant new own-conduct evidence in this cycle.** Prior Yemen floor assessments rested on the compound of famine, airstrikes, and aid obstruction. Arbitrarily detaining UN personnel — and having one die in custody — is a qualitatively distinct category of state-adjacent conduct: deliberate targeting of the international humanitarian response infrastructure itself. It is own-conduct evidence, not externally attributed harm, and it deepens the scorable evidence base for Yemen at the floor.

**The Anthropic Congressional deadline (Jun 26) is a rare instance of a known near-term state event that could affect an AI lab's scored status.** If Congress extracts an explanation that reveals the BIS order was disproportionate, Anthropic's already-documented cost-of-conscience standing could be reinforced. If the government defends the order without modification, the regulatory-risk precedent for all frontier AI labs becomes more entrenched. Either outcome is evidence for the next cycle.
