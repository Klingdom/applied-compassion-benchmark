## Key Highlights

- **This was an override-heavy night, not a routine confirmation cycle.** 6 of 8 applied changes were filed by the assessor as `flag-for-review` (calibration concern), not as recommended score changes, and the founder explicitly overrode that routing in all 6 cases. This is a material departure from recent nights' pattern of long confirmation-dominant stretches (7 consecutive zero-proposal nights through 2026-07-18) and should be read as a deliberate one-time correction pass, not a new steady-state override rate.
- **Egypt and Freeport-McMoRan are honest edge cases that this digest cannot dress up as good news.** Both rose a band on negative within-window evidence (refugee deportations; a mine-worker fatality) specifically screened by the assessor as false positives under screening rule 3 (upgrades must not be produced by negative triggers). Both were applied anyway. The correct, honest description is: a first full review found existing positive infrastructure a flat placeholder score had never counted, applied at the same moment as continuing negative conduct — not an improvement in institutional behavior.
- **State Farm's −47.1 is likely the largest single-entity delta ever applied in this pipeline**, and it was applied at the assessor's own suggested-softer-landing recommendation overridden. This is the first time in the visible history of `score-update-log.md` that a founder override has gone in the harsher direction on a magnitude-warned proposal (Egypt/Freeport-McMoRan overrides went the lenient direction, applying upgrades the assessor tried to block; State Farm went the strict direction, applying a harsher score than the assessor suggested).
- **The scan needed three attempts to pass a brand-new integrity gate** (`validate-scan.mjs`, created today). This is a genuine process-quality win — 100% sourcing, 100% in-window dating — but it produced materially fewer evidenced entities (55) than the previous failed attempt claimed (179), and that reduction is entirely attributable to correctly excluding unsourced and out-of-window material, not to reduced world activity.
- **The severity-inversion finding from the scan's own `scoring_note` is a standing methodology problem, not fixed tonight.** Eleven countries with confirmed active-conflict, mass-casualty, or famine evidence (Ukraine, Israel, DRC, Iran, Russia, Yemen, Sudan, South Sudan, Somalia, Afghanistan, Haiti) all tied at priority 57, ranked below six routine corporate settlements, purely because they were assessed 2026-07-18/19 (staleness_score=0) while the corporate entities had never been assessed (staleness_score=25, higher importance weight). Watch for future proposals citing this precedent to argue for similar overrides; the screening rule's deterrent value depends on it remaining the exception, not becoming routine.

## Impacts and Insights

- **Tonight is a clean case study of what a flat-placeholder-to-evidence-based transition looks like at scale.** Eight entities moved between −47.1 and +13.7 points in a single cycle purely from replacing bulk-import scores with individual 40-subdimension reviews (plus, for State Farm, genuine new regulatory evidence). This is useful evidence for the broader claim that most Fortune 500 entities' published scores materially understate uncertainty until individually assessed.
- **The founder's override authority was exercised in both directions in the same batch** — leniently for Egypt/Freeport-McMoRan (against the screening rule's intent) and strictly for State Farm (against the assessor's peer-calibration caution). This is worth flagging as a traceability question for future audits: is there a consistent principle governing when override discretion moves toward leniency versus strictness, or is it being decided case-by-case? This digest takes no position on whether tonight's decisions were correct — only that the pattern is not self-evidently consistent and should be watched.
- **The allegation-versus-finding distinction was applied correctly and should be modeled going forward.** State Farm's downgrade rests on a completed California regulatory examination (a finding), with the Oklahoma RICO suit weighted only as corroborating context (an allegation). This is the right discipline and stands in useful contrast to State Street's situation, where the only new within-window evidence was itself an unproven allegation (the Texas-led antitrust suit) layered onto an already-priced-in prior assessment.
- **Evidence gap:** the Oklahoma RICO suit and the Texas-led antitrust suit are both unresolved in court; State Farm's final penalty amount from California has not yet been announced ("millions of dollars" is the only figure available); the MSHA Morenci fatality report is explicitly preliminary. Several of tonight's most consequential findings rest on evidence that is still developing.

## Egypt / Freeport-McMoRan Public Framing (editorial judgment call)

Both Egypt and Freeport-McMoRan rose a band this cycle on evidence that included a negative event (refugee deportations; a worker fatality) with no accompanying new positive conduct — only newly-counted *existing* infrastructure. There is no way to describe these as "improvements" or "good news" without misleading a reader who does not already understand the placeholder-correction mechanism. The public briefing (`site/src/data/updates/daily/2026-07-20.json` and `research/digests/2026-07-20.json`) handles this by:
1. Stating plainly in the headline/summary/topSignals that "the increase is not a compliment."
2. Attributing the negative evidence to its named source (Human Rights Watch; MSHA) without softening it.
3. Explaining the actual mechanism (a previously-uncounted existing program) rather than implying new positive conduct occurred.
4. Never using the phrase "improved" or "positive development" in connection with either entity's score change.

This is judged to meet the bar of "honest and not misleading to a public reader." If in a future cycle this framing proves insufficient, the correct escalation is to omit the entity from the public topSignals list entirely and describe the change only in the boundaryWatch/recentAssessments tables with minimal narrative — that was not judged necessary here because the placeholder-correction mechanism is explainable in plain language.
