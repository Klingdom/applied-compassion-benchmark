## Impacts and Insights

**AI safety governance is under commercial pressure in a way the benchmark has not previously documented at this scale.** The Anthropic case shows that even the most safety-committed lab in the AI index will move structural safety commitments under documented government contracting pressure — while maintaining narrower red lines. This produces a new scoring challenge: how does the benchmark weight partial-commitment retention (maintained red lines) against structural-commitment abandonment (RSP pre-deployment pledge dropped)? The SYS -0.8 weight and the ACC +0.2 and INT +0.1 offsets represent the assessor's best current judgment, but the methodology community should explicitly deliberate on this framework.

**Published scores are systematically more optimistic than assessed scores for mid-range Countries entities that have recently undergone rapid downward movement.** India (published 34.4, working baseline 28.1 after two cycles), Senegal (published 37.5, working baseline 33.6), Nigeria (published 23.4, proposed 21.9 pending from May 14) all reflect a gap between the canonical published score in the index file and the current working baseline. This gap creates public-facing accuracy concerns. The pipeline should establish a more regular index-file update cadence for entities undergoing rapid change.

**The 19-category two-night methodology harvest (May 14–15) is evidence that the benchmark's evidentiary surface area is expanding faster than its taxonomy.** The methodology is encountering novel conduct structures — safety-pledge-reversals-under-government-pressure, maritime-deportation-without-due-process, perverse-procurement-incentive, drone-as-primary-weapon — that do not fit cleanly into existing dimensional sub-anchors. This is a healthy sign of the benchmark's coverage breadth but creates a taxonomy-maintenance burden that will require dedicated attention in coming weeks.
