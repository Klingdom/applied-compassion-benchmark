## Impacts and Insights

**The July 3 cycle is the benchmark's most evidence-rich confirmation cycle to date.** Fifteen false positives screened represents not a pipeline failure but methodological success: the screening framework absorbed a simultaneous convergence of external-state harm (Ukraine, Lebanon), non-state harm (Sudan, Mali, Burkina Faso, Yemen), exogenous disasters (Venezuela earthquake, DRC Ebola), pre-adjudication matters (UnitedHealth), and duplicate-control cases (El Salvador, China, Nigeria) — and correctly disposed of all fifteen without a false score movement.

**The Russia/Ukraine attribution decision is now in its 12th consecutive cycle of identical application.** This is analytically significant: it means the pipeline has applied the §3e-bis(3) directionality rule consistently across 12 different evidence packages from the same conflict, including cases where the harm events varied significantly in magnitude (from single-night strikes to record-monthly casualty tolls). Consistency at this scale validates the rule as operationally stable, not just theoretically coherent.

**The Sudan and Yemen floor reinforcements raise a structural observation: what does it mean for a 0.0-floor entity to receive new atrocity documentation?** The El Fasher crimes-against-humanity report and the Yemen UN-staff detentions are among the most severe evidence items logged this cycle. But they cannot move scores that are already at 0.0. The benchmark's floor design means the worst actors are effectively removed from the volatility model. The practical implication is that forward-trigger design for floor entities matters more than the current-cycle assessment — the humanitarian community needs the upgrade-trigger specification (ceasefire, aid access, accountability) to be precise and observable.

**The DRC Ebola international spread is the first multi-continent transmission from this outbreak.** Uganda and European cases shift the geopolitical salience of the outbreak, which may increase international funding and response pressure. This does not automatically move DRC's score (response capacity, not spread geography, is the scored element), but it may increase the probability that the WHO corridor obstruction trigger — if it occurs — triggers international accountability action that generates additional scorable conduct evidence.

**Published score vs. proposed score discipline is critical this cycle.** El Salvador's rotation-state composite had drifted to 15.x (the proposed value), which the July 3 cycle correctly identified and corrected to 20.3 (the published value). Mali's composite had drifted to 7.5 (an incorrect value), corrected to 12.5 (published). Both corrections confirm that rotation-state must track the published index, not the proposed score or a manual entry error. The anti-misread protocol is working.
