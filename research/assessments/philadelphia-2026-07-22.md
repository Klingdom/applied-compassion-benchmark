## Key Findings

- Why it matters: Philadelphia is on track to record fewer than 200 homicides in 2026 for the first time since the 1960s — a major public-safety turnaround the city links to sustained community investment.
- The city is backing that trend with structure: $24 million to 147 community groups in 2025 and a first round of a $25 million community-led violence-prevention program to 150+ organizations in 2026, explicitly aimed at root causes.
- But Philadelphia is still the poorest big US city, and officials candidly say they cannot fully explain the homicide decline. One strong year is genuine positive conduct, but not yet a proven, durable structural shift.

## Strongest Dimensions

- Systemic Thinking and Action — large community-led violence-prevention funding and a historic homicide decline.

## Weakest Dimensions

- Equity — the highest poverty rate among large US cities and persistent coverage gaps for the most vulnerable.

## Evidence Gaps

- The $25M program is in its first 2026 funding round with no outcome data yet, and officials cannot attribute the homicide drop to specific interventions — so efficacy is scored conservatively.
