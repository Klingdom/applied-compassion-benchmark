## The Corporate-Domicile Question: Where It Belongs

Delaware's role as the incorporation venue for most large US public companies is a genuine and unusual factor. This assessment places it in **three specific subdimensions** and deliberately excludes it elsewhere.

**Where it counts against Delaware:**

- **S4 Structural Critique (scored 1/5).** Corporate franchise and entity fees supply roughly one third of Delaware's operating budget. The Delaware Department of State projected about $2.1 billion flowing to the general fund from corporate franchise sources by June 30, 2026, and franchise tax revenue rose 1% from $1,324,400,000 in FY2025 to $1,338,000,000 in FY2026 ([Spotlight Delaware, 2026-02-09](https://spotlightdelaware.org/2026/02/09/civics-101-corporate-franchise-taxes/); [Delaware Public Media, 2026-05-19](https://www.delawarepublic.org/2026-05-19/may-defac-corporate-franchise-revenue-up-1-from-2025)). Delaware does not publicly question this structure. Delaware is also repeatedly identified as the leading single source of anonymous shell entities worldwide ([Transparency International](https://www.transparency.org/en/news/delaware-the-us-corporate-secrecy-haven); [Tax Justice Network](https://taxjustice.net/2015/11/27/how-the-u-s-a-became-a-secrecy-jurisdiction-2/)). The anchor for 1 is "does not question structures that sustain need for its services." Delaware exceeds that anchor in the wrong direction: in 2025 it legislated to entrench the structure.

- **I1 Consistency Under Pressure (scored 2/5).** In December 2024 the Delaware Court of Chancery voided Elon Musk's Tesla pay package. Musk moved Tesla and SpaceX out of Delaware and campaigned publicly for others to follow — the "DExit" campaign. Delaware responded within roughly three months. Senate Bill 21 passed the Delaware Senate 20-0 on 2025-03-13, passed the House 32-7 on 2025-03-25, and was signed the same month ([Best Lawyers](https://www.bestlawyers.com/article/delaware-overhauls-corporate-law-stem-dexit/6710); [Temple 10-Q](https://law.temple.edu/10q/dexit-vs-the-billionaires-bill-how-s-b-21-will-reshape-delawares-courts/)). SB21 narrowed shareholder protections, created a safe harbor for self-dealing transactions, and strengthened the presumption of director independence. The Delaware Supreme Court upheld it as constitutional on 2026-03-02 ([Spotlight Delaware, 2026-03-02](https://spotlightdelaware.org/2026/03/02/delaware-supreme-court-upholds-law-designed-to-counter-a-dexit-wave/)). Whatever the merits as corporate policy, the sequence is a documented case of a commitment yielding to financial pressure within months.

- **AB3 Transparency (scored 3/5, capped).** Delaware's public-records regime is separately weak and interacts with the corporate secrecy point. See AB3 below.

**Where it does NOT count:**

The assessment does **not** credit Delaware for the Court of Chancery's reputation for judicial quality, and does **not** penalise Delaware for the conduct of companies merely incorporated there. Compassion Benchmark scores an entity's own conduct. A Delaware-incorporated company's labour practices in Ohio are Ohio conduct and that company's conduct, not Delaware's. Delaware's own conduct is the *choice of legal architecture and the defence of it* — which is scored, once, at S4 and I1.

### What drove the movement

Three forces explain most of the five-year change.

1. **Litigation, not self-initiation, drove the equity gains.** The single largest compassion improvement of the period — Opportunity Funding, now a permanent weighted funding stream exceeding $60 million a year — came from a 2018 lawsuit brought by the NAACP Delaware State Conference and Delawareans for Educational Opportunity, settled in 2020. Delaware delivered on the settlement, which counts for something. It did not start the reform.

2. **A governor change flipped one major outcome.** Governor John Carney vetoed medical aid in dying on 2024-09-20. Governor Matt Meyer signed the identical policy on 2025-05-20. The same bill, the same legislature, the opposite result. This is direct evidence that at least one significant compassion commitment was leadership-dependent rather than structurally embedded.

3. **Revenue dependence set a hard ceiling.** Every improvement above sits inside a budget that draws roughly a third of its revenue from being the most management-friendly incorporation venue in the country. When that revenue was threatened in 2025, Delaware moved faster on SB21 — under three months from introduction to signature — than on any social policy in the five-year window.

**Net trajectory: improving on what Delaware delivers, flat-to-declining on what Delaware is.** A point-in-time score from 2021 would have been roughly 47–50. The 2026 score of 53.8 reflects genuine gains, held back by structural facts that have hardened rather than loosened.

## Key Findings

- **Delaware is a genuinely middle-performing state, and that is the point.** It scores 53.8 out of 100 — Functional. It has no exemplary dimension and no critical one. The spread between its best dimension (Equity, 65) and its worst (Boundaries, 40) is just 25 points. Most benchmarked entities are lopsided; Delaware is evenly mid-range.

- **Delaware's biggest compassion win came from a lawsuit, not from Delaware.** The 2020 settlement with the NAACP Delaware State Conference created a permanent weighted school funding stream that reached $63 million in FY2025. Delaware delivered on it. But the reform began with litigation filed against the state in 2018, and an independent 2023 review still found education funding short by upward of $1 billion.

- **One third of Delaware's budget depends on being the easiest place in America to incorporate — and Delaware defended that in 2025.** Corporate franchise revenue supplies about a third of the state's operating budget, roughly $2.1 billion to the general fund by mid-2026. After a court ruling threatened that position, Senate Bill 21 passed the Senate 20-0 and the House 32-7 within three months and narrowed shareholder protections. Delaware moved faster on corporate revenue than on any social policy in five years. This drives the two lowest subdimension scores in the assessment.

- **Delaware saved lives fastest where it acted upstream.** Confirmed drug deaths fell 36% in one year — from 527 in 2023 to 338 in 2024 — after the state targeted naloxone distribution to the highest-risk zip codes. That decline outpaced the national trend and is the single clearest case of Delaware's own action reducing suffering.

- **The state cannot house the people it counts.** In January 2025 Delaware counted 1,585 people without homes, a 16% rise in one year, with children under 18 making up 27.3% of them. Statewide there are about 1,200 emergency shelter beds and fewer than 400 transitional beds. Delaware measures this problem well and cannot yet meet it, which is why Boundaries scores lowest at 40 out of 100.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Equity (65/100)** — the only dimension in the Established band. Driven by the weighted Opportunity Funding formula, automatic expungement reaching about 290,000 people, and formal acknowledgment of historical school funding harm co-remedied with the NAACP.
- **Action (60/100)** — the 36% drop in drug deaths and sustained multi-year delivery of paid family leave and expungement.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Boundaries (40/100)** — the shelter system cannot refer people onward, DOC runs roughly 18% vacancies on mandatory overtime it admits harms staff, and the 2023 federal child welfare review tied caseworker shortage to missed visits.
- **Integrity (45/100)** — the SB21 episode is a documented case of a commitment yielding to financial pressure, and the medical-aid-in-dying reversal between governors shows compassion policy that was leadership-dependent rather than structurally embedded.

## Evidence Gaps

Scored conservatively and flagged rather than estimated:

- **A5 Anticipatory Awareness (low confidence).** HB466, the Healthy Communities and Environmental Justice Act, was introduced in the 151st General Assembly (2021–2022) and would have required cumulative-impact review before permitting in overburdened communities. I could not verify enactment and found no evidence of an operative statewide requirement. Scored 2 on the assumption it did not become law. **If HB466 or a successor was in fact enacted, A5 rises to 4 and AWR rises from 3.2 to 3.6.**
- **E1 Affective Resonance (low confidence).** No independent testimony study of how Delawareans experience state service interactions was located. Scored at the middle anchor by default.
- **B3 and B4 (medium confidence).** No evidence found of a statewide coordinated-entry or refusal protocol for homeless services. If Delaware operates a functioning coordinated-entry system with tracked referrals, both scores rise and BND moves from 2.6 to about 3.0.
- **B1 and I3.** DOC vacancy data is from 2023; no 2025 or 2026 staffing figures were located. Statewide employee turnover data was not found.
- **Web search budget was exhausted before I could research Delaware's 2026 budget response to federal Medicaid cuts, and Delaware's state-level immigration enforcement posture for 2025–2026.** Both would inform EQ1, AC4 and I1. Neither is reflected in the current scores.

## Confidence Level

**Overall confidence: Medium-High.**

- High confidence: EQU, ACT, SYS, ACC. These rest on dated primary sources — federal CFSR results, published state mortality data, legislative records with roll-call votes, and settlement documents.
- Medium confidence: AWR, EMP, INT.
- Lower confidence: BND, which relies more heavily on journalism than on institutional data, and contains two subdimensions where absence of evidence drove the score down.

The composite is unlikely to move more than about 4 points in either direction on further evidence. The single largest swing factor is the HB466 verification question.
