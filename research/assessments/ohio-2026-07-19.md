## Calibration Note (read before comparing to the published index)

The published `us-states.json` file contains only 21 of 51 entries: the top 8 and the bottom 13 of the original ranking. Ranks 9 through 38 — the entire middle of the distribution — are missing. A score of 38.1 would place Ohio 9th among the 22 entities in that file. **That placement is an artifact of the missing middle, not a claim that Ohio is the 9th most compassionate US state.** In a complete 51-state ranking, Ohio would sit in the missing middle band, above the all-2.0 Midwestern cluster (Indiana, Missouri, North and South Dakota, each 25.0) and far below Connecticut (83.0), Minnesota (84.4) and Massachusetts (94.4).

Peer sanity checks applied during scoring:
- **Against Indiana / Missouri (25.0, all dimensions 2.0):** Ohio scores higher because it operates real, dated, published detection and outcome machinery those files do not evidence — annual Pregnancy-Associated Mortality Review with preventability findings, an annual infant mortality report with cause-specific racial disparity ratios, and a Medicaid behavioral health program with published utilization outcomes.
- **Against Connecticut (83.0, mostly 4.0):** Ohio scores far lower because it lacks independent verification of its own performance, has removed an oversight body during the assessment window, and repeatedly declined to fund needs it had itself costed.
- **Against Louisiana / Alabama / Mississippi (21.9, 12.5):** Ohio scores higher on Accountability and Systemic Thinking because of the $250.7M FirstEnergy restitution order and the H2Ohio upstream program, both of which have measured results.

## Key Findings

- **Ohio saves more lives than it used to, and answers for itself less.** Predicted overdose deaths fell from 4,847 in December 2023 to 3,136 in December 2024 — a drop of more than 35%, among the largest in the country. In the same period the state abolished its independent prison oversight body and walked away from its own school funding plan.

- **Ohio measures its failures honestly and then does not fund the fix.** The state's own reviews publish damning numbers: 66% of pregnancy-related deaths were preventable, and Black infants die at 2.5 times the rate of white infants. The Fair School Funding Plan costed the gap at $3.04 billion; the 2025 budget provided $281.9 million, leaving $2.75 billion unfunded and cutting the highest-poverty districts by $34.6 million.

- **The state ran an election on maps its own Supreme Court had ruled illegal.** Between January and May 2022 the Ohio Supreme Court struck down five successive sets of legislative maps as unconstitutional partisan gerrymanders. The Ohio Redistricting Commission never produced a lawful map, and the November 2022 midterms went ahead anyway. Ohio has never acknowledged or repaired this. It is the single heaviest drag on the Accountability and Integrity scores.

- **Ohio's compassion is concentrated in one term-limited person.** Governor Mike DeWine defended Springfield's Haitian residents against his own party's presidential ticket, vetoed the ban on gender-affirming care for minors, quietly commuted a death sentence, and on 16 June 2026 called for abolishing the death penalty. The Legislature overrode him on HB 68 and cut his flagship H2Ohio program by 39%. DeWine leaves office in January 2027, and little of this is embedded in policy.

- **One real repayment landed.** After the $60 million HB 6 bribery scheme — Ohio's largest corruption case, which sent former House Speaker Larry Householder to prison for 20 years — Ohio regulators ordered FirstEnergy utilities to pay $250.7 million on 19 November 2025, including $186.6 million refunded to customers. It is Ohio's clearest act of concrete repair in the period, and it took a federal prosecution and five years to arrive.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Systemic Thinking (3.0 raw / 50.0 scaled)** — the only dimension reaching Functional. H2Ohio pays farmers to stop nutrient runoff at the source across 2.5 million acres, with U.S. EPA measuring an 8–10% phosphorus reduction into Lake Erie. The 2023 creation of the Department of Children and Youth consolidated child services that had been split across four agencies. DeWine's death penalty and Springfield positions are genuine structural critique at personal political cost.
- **Awareness, Action and Accountability (all 2.6 raw / 40.0 scaled)** — carried by real, dated, published machinery: the Pregnancy-Associated Mortality Review, the annual Infant Mortality Report, OhioRISE's published outcome reductions, the Ohio Checkbook's first-in-nation U.S. PIRG transparency rating, and the FirstEnergy restitution order.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Boundaries (2.2 raw / 30.0 scaled)** — the weakest area, and partly an evidence problem. Ohio publishes almost nothing about staff sustainability, refusal ethics or consent quality. Three of five subdimensions were scored at 2 with low confidence because evidence was absent rather than because practice is known to be poor. The Medicaid expansion trigger is the clearest concrete failure: roughly 770,000 people hold coverage that can end automatically without their being told.
- **Empathy, Equity and Integrity (all 2.4 raw / 35.0 scaled)** — pulled down by decisions that treat identity groups differently by statute (HB 68, SB 104, SB 1), by resources moving away from highest need under constraint, and by a pattern of major decisions contradicting the state constitution as its own Supreme Court reads it.

## Evidence Gaps

- **Boundaries is the thinnest dimension.** B1, B4 and B5 rest on Tier 2 evidence and are marked low confidence. Ohio publishes no workforce sustainability data for county caseworkers or corrections staff, no warm-referral outcome tracking, and no independent review of consent comprehension. These scores should be treated as provisional.
- **No independent audit of Ohio's own detection systems was located.** PAMR and the infant mortality reviews are state-run. The strongest independent Ohio-specific assessment found — the Health Policy Institute of Ohio 2026 Health Value Dashboard, ranking Ohio 43rd of 51 — is produced by a nonprofit, not commissioned by the state.
- **The CIIC abolition is recent and its effects are not yet measurable.** The June 2025 transfer of prison oversight to the Attorney General may or may not preserve function. A1, A4 and AB3 should be re-examined once a full cycle has passed.
- **No community testimony was located** for E1, E4, AB5 or B2. Every Tier 5 anchor in this methodology requires community confirmation, which a desk-based assessment cannot supply. This structurally caps several subdimensions at 3.
- **Executive/legislative split.** Ohio is unusually hard to score as one entity because its governor and legislature have acted in opposite directions on multiple assessed items. Where this occurred, the assessment scored the state's net enacted conduct and noted the split explicitly.

## Confidence Level

**Overall: medium-high.** Six of eight dimensions rest on Tier 4 or Tier 5 evidence with specific dates and figures. Boundaries (medium-low) and Empathy (medium) are weaker. The composite is unlikely to move more than ±3 points under additional desk research; a Certified Assessment with institutional access would most likely move Boundaries, which is currently scored on absence of evidence.

## Recommended Next Steps

Ohio's Developing band and its profile — improving outcomes, eroding accountability infrastructure — indicate a structured improvement roadmap rather than strategic interpretation.

- **Developing**: Consider a [Certified Assessment](/certified-assessments) for a structured improvement roadmap. Priority areas: Boundaries (scope clarity on Medicaid expansion conditionality; workforce sustainability reporting), Awareness (restoring independent oversight for incarcerated Ohioans), and Accountability (formal acknowledgment of the 2022 redistricting harm and the 25-year DeRolph non-compliance).
