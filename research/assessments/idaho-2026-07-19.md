## Cross-State Consistency Flag: the AB3 Transparency rule

The adopted rule caps AB3 at 3 where a broad public-records exemption sits alongside an oversight body still publishing damaging findings. **I declined to apply it to Idaho.**

The rule targets jurisdictions where an entire branch removed itself from the records regime, as North Carolina and Virginia did. Idaho has no such carve-out. Its Public Records Act instead lists 92 narrow, enumerated exemptions — more auditable than a branch-wide exclusion. Both halves of Idaho's transparency machinery are also live: the watchdog published damaging uncompelled findings in June and December 2025, and the Controller's portal publishes nightly transaction-level spending down to school districts. The Massachusetts output contingency is met.

**This is the single largest judgement call in the assessment, and it moves Idaho across a band line.** AB3 at 4 gives 40.6 and Functional. Capping AB3 at 3 gives 40.0 and Developing. A reviewer who reads 92 exemptions as functionally broad would land Idaho in Developing.

I recommend the rule be clarified: an enumerated-exemption regime, however long the list, should not trigger the cap on its own. The trigger should require a branch-level or office-level exemption that removes a whole class of record-holder.

### Score Difference Analysis

**The published number was never a measurement.** Idaho, Indiana, Missouri, North Dakota and South Dakota all carried composite 25.0 with identical dimension vectors and `last_assessed: null`. Running those dimensions through the scoring function returns exactly 25.0, which confirms the placeholder was generated from invented inputs. No dimension-by-dimension reconciliation is possible, because there is nothing on the published side to reconcile against.

**Accountability is the biggest correction, at +30 points.** The placeholder implied Idaho barely acknowledges failure. Idaho in fact operates two durable transparency institutions. The Office of Performance Evaluations is a statutory legislative body that published, unprompted, that the state has no formal process for investigating abuse of children in care facilities. That finding led to new law, 63 new staff and $23.2 million. Transparent Idaho publishes transaction-level spending updated nightly.

**Empathy is genuinely low, and the placeholder happened to land near it.** House Bill 135 makes immunizations, disease treatment, prenatal care, children's food aid and food pantry access conditional on immigration status. That is differential treatment written into statute, which is the bottom anchor for E3. The 988 consent barrier points the same way.

**The correction is not uniform.** Idaho scores near the observed floor on Empathy and Equity while scoring in the middle band on Accountability, Systemic Thinking and Integrity. It is a state with unusually good self-inspection machinery pointed at a service base with severe access gaps.

## Key Findings

- **Idaho spent a year as the only US state that did not review why mothers die.** The Legislature let the Maternal Mortality Review Committee expire on 2023-07-01, then reinstated it in 2024. The rebuilt committee approved three catch-up reports on 2026-01-27 and reported that every pregnancy-associated death in 2022 was somewhat or likely preventable.

- **Idaho's watchdog is a real institutional strength, and it is the main reason the score is not lower.** The Office of Performance Evaluations found in June 2025 that Idaho has no formal process for investigating abuse of children in care facilities. The state responded with 63 new child welfare staff and a $23.2 million budget increase, a 20.5 percent rise.

- **Rural Idahoans have lost practical access to childbirth care.** Fourteen rural hospitals had no labor and delivery unit in 2025. Only 23 OB-GYNs serve the more than half a million people living outside the seven largest counties. Some women drive two to three hours for prenatal care.

- **Idaho wrote identity-based exclusions into statute.** House Bill 135, signed April 2025, requires immigration-status checks before immunizations, communicable disease treatment, prenatal care, children's food aid, short-term shelter, and food from pantries taking state money. This produces the assessment's only score of 1 out of 5.

- **The state honoured its voters on Medicaid, then narrowed the promise.** Voters passed expansion with 61 percent in 2018. A Republican Senate chair halted repeal in 2025 at real political cost. The Legislature then added work requirements, and House Bill 913 in April 2026 required three months of documented compliance before a person may enrol at all.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Systemic Thinking (3.0)** and **Integrity (3.0)** — Upstream workforce and housing strategy, genuine multi-agency coalitions, and a demonstrated willingness to publish numbers that make the state look bad.

- **Equity (2.2)** — Severe and measured access gaps in rural obstetric care, layered enrolment barriers, and no located disparity-monitoring programme outside maternal mortality.

## Evidence Gaps

- No disaggregated outcome data by race or ethnicity outside maternal mortality was located, holding EQ3 at 2 with medium confidence.
- No formal acknowledgment of, or repair for, a specific historical harm was located. EQ5 rests only on the existence of the Council on Indian Affairs. Low confidence.
- No independent review of informed-consent quality in Idaho services was found. B5 is low confidence.
- Idaho has had one governor for the whole 2021–2026 window, so I5 could not be tested against an actual transition.
- Community testimony (Tier 5) was sparse throughout. Almost all evidence is Tier 3 and Tier 4. No subdimension reaches 5, because no dimension has independent audit and community confirmation together.

## Recommended Next Steps

Idaho sits at the Developing/Functional boundary. Consider [Advisory Support](/advisory) to translate these findings into action, with priority on the two lowest dimensions: the statutory exclusions driving Empathy to 2.0, and the rural access gaps driving Equity to 2.2. The existing Office of Performance Evaluations is the natural vehicle — Idaho already has the inspection machinery, and the gap is between what it finds and what changes.
