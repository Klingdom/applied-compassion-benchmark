## Cross-State Consistency: AB3 Transparency Rule (v3)

**Disposition: APPLIED — cap at 3.**

**Limb (a), structural transparency failure: MET.** On 2025-01-02, in *O'Neill v. Gianforte*, the Montana Supreme Court recognised a gubernatorial privilege letting the governor withhold otherwise public records containing candid advice. That is an exception to a right to know written into Article II, Section 9 of the Montana Constitution. On 2025-05-16 the governor vetoed House Bill 271, which would have narrowed that privilege by statute, and also vetoed a companion bill limiting judicial privilege. Separately, the health department denied a blanket records request covering every state investigation into deaths, injuries and assaults at Montana State Hospital. This is a branch escaping scrutiny, not a long list of subject-matter carve-outs, so the Idaho scope limit does not apply. It is structural rather than procedural, so the North Dakota scope limit does not apply.

**Limb (b), surviving output: MET.** The Legislative Audit Division is independently funded and still publishing damaging, uncompelled findings, including reports to the Legislative Audit Committee in September 2025, January 2026 and April 2026, and a Medicaid eligibility review whose findings the state budget director publicly disputed in 2026. The Massachusetts output contingency is satisfied — the watchdog is producing work, not merely existing on paper.

**Connecticut counter-case tested and rejected.** Montana starts from a stronger formal position than Connecticut: its right to know is constitutional rather than statutory, and its Supreme Court strengthened enforcement on 2025-05-30 by holding that people who win a right-to-know case should presumptively have their legal fees paid. But Connecticut scored above the cap because its legislature *defeated* the erosion, rejecting Senate Bill 1226 in 2025. Montana did the reverse: its legislature passed the fix and the governor vetoed it. Montana also has a live, documented blackout on deaths at a facility that lost federal certification over deaths. Montana belongs with the applied group.

**Scoring effect.** The cap is not binding on the outcome. AB3 scores 3 on the merits regardless, because the constitutional right and the fee-shifting decision lift Montana above the floor of 2. Composite with AB3 at 3 (the scored position): **41.9, Functional**. Composite if AB3 were floored at 2, giving ACC 2.4: **41.3, Functional**. A 0.6-point difference; no band change.

## Key Findings

**1. Montana made two of its most important promises permanent — quietly.** In March 2025 the state deleted the repeating expiry date on Medicaid expansion, which covers nearly 80,000 adults and had lived under a sunset clause since 2015. In May 2025 it did the same for the Montana Indian Child Welfare Act. A Republican governor signed the Medicaid bill with no press release. Doing the right thing without claiming credit is rare, and it is the single strongest integrity signal in this assessment.

**2. Montana's constitution promises more than the state delivers, and the state keeps legislating around the difference.** Montana guarantees a clean and healthful environment, a right to know, and equal protection. Within 18 months its own supreme court ruled against the state on all three: the climate review ban in December 2024, the transgender care ban in May 2025, and the identity documents policy in April 2026. The state then rewrote two of those laws to restore the form while removing the effect. That pattern drives I4 Values Alignment to 1 of 5, the lowest score in this assessment.

**3. When federal oversight ended at the state psychiatric hospital, so did public visibility.** Montana State Hospital lost federal certification in April 2022 after four patient deaths. The state then refused a records request covering every subsequent investigation into deaths, injuries and assaults there, because state-level investigations are not public record. As of December 2025 the hospital had still not regained certification and still had no electronic system for tracking medical records — nearly four years and $15.9 million later.

**4. Montana dropped 134,000 people from Medicaid, and most of them lost coverage over paperwork.** Between April 2023 and January 2024, 63 percent of those removed were dropped for failing to return forms, not because they were found ineligible. Childhood enrolment fell 27 percent, the second-largest drop of any US state. Montana chose to run this over 10 months when 43 states took 12 to 14, and chose not to pause child disenrollments as North Carolina and Kentucky did.

**5. Montana's housing reform is genuinely upstream work, and it held.** The 2023 package legalised duplexes and accessory dwellings by right, opened commercial zones to housing, and required 20-year growth plans. The state defended it through four years of litigation it could have settled, and on 2026-03-17 the Montana Supreme Court upheld all of it unanimously. This is the clearest case in the assessment of the state attacking a cause rather than a symptom.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Systemic Thinking (3.2 raw / 55 scaled)** — the highest-scoring dimension. Driven by the housing supply reform, a roughly $930 million permanent trust, and a documented near-50 percent fall in the foster care population.
- **Awareness (3.0) and Equity (3.0)** — Montana publishes real, race-disaggregated data on its worst disparities, including missing Indigenous persons and Native child removal, and acts on some of it legislatively.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Empathy (2.2 raw / 30 scaled)** and **Boundaries (2.2 / 30)** — the joint lowest. Empathy is dragged down by legislated discrimination (E3 = 1) and by records denials to bereaved families (E4 = 2). Boundaries reflects depleted frontline institutions and automated coverage closures with no evidenced alternatives.
- **Integrity (2.4 / 35)** — carried almost entirely by the single score of 1 on Values Alignment.
- **Accountability (2.6 / 40)** — harm established externally rather than disclosed, and no evidence of concrete repair to those harmed.

## Evidence Gaps

- **B4 Refusal Ethics and B5 Consent Orientation are scored at low confidence.** No direct evidence was found on how Montana declines service or designs consent. Both scores rest on indirect inference from procedural disenrollment rates. A certified assessment would test these directly.
- **AB5 Reparative Action is scored at low confidence.** Absence of evidence of repair is not the same as evidence of no repair, though the national reporting on unanswered families is suggestive.
- **Corrections oversight was not established.** No independent monitoring body for Montana's prison system was found in public sources for 2025–2026; the most recent binding oversight found was the 2022 Disability Rights Montana settlement.
- **The Montana State Hospital 2026 CMS survey outcome is unresolved.** The state filed on 2025-12-23 and a survey was expected in spring or early summer 2026. No published result was found as of 2026-07-19. A recertification would be material to AC1, AC3 and AB2.
- **Small-state caveat.** Montana's population is roughly 1.1 million and press coverage is thinner than in large states. Thin coverage was treated as an evidence gap, not as evidence of good conduct. All population comparisons here are share-based or per-capita.

## Published Index Comparison

Montana does not currently appear in any published Compassion Benchmark index. `site/src/data/indexes/us-states.json` contains 21 of 51 jurisdictions and does not include Montana. This assessment is a **first baseline**: there is no prior composite, rank or band to compare against.

**Recommendation:** add Montana to the US States index at composite **41.9 (Functional)**. Rank must remain null. The published index lost ranks 9–38 in the original extraction and renumbered the survivors contiguously, so its rank column is not a national ranking. Ranks should be assigned only when the index is rebuilt with all 51 jurisdictions.

## Recommended Next Steps

Montana lands in the **Functional** band: systems exist but have significant gaps. Consider [Advisory Support](/advisory) to translate these findings into strategic action, with priority on the three lowest-scoring subdimensions — E3 Non-Judgment (1/5), I4 Values Alignment (1/5) and E4 Validation (2/5) — which together account for most of the distance between Montana's current score and the Established band.
