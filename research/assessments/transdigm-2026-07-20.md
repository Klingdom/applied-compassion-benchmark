## Assessment Context

TransDigm was surfaced by the 2026-07-20 scan on its abandonment of a $960 million acquisition of Stellant Systems on 2026-07-13, after the Department of Justice said it would sue to block the deal. The DOJ's stated reason goes to the heart of TransDigm's compassion profile: the merger would have left the US military with a single source for radar components used in the Navy's Aegis Combat System and the Air Force's F-16. That is the same pattern the Department of Defense Inspector General documented twice — acquire a sole-source supplier, then raise prices.

## Key Findings

- A federal watchdog found TransDigm charging profit margins as high as 4,451 percent. The Department of Defense Inspector General reviewed 47 parts and found excess profit on 46 of them, totalling $16.1 million.
- It happened again two years later. A second Inspector General report found nearly $21 million in additional excess profits on 105 of 106 spare part types, which is why Systemic Learning scores 1 out of 5.
- The Inspector General described the harm as the business model itself. TransDigm "acquires companies with sole-source contacts, and then hikes up prices on the spare parts those companies manufacture."
- The Justice Department stopped the latest attempt. TransDigm abandoned a $960 million purchase of Stellant Systems after DOJ said the deal would leave the military with a single source for Aegis and F-16 radar components.
- TransDigm did repay the money once. After a congressional hearing in 2019 it refunded the full $16.1 million, which is the single clearest piece of reparative evidence in its record and keeps Reparative Action at 2 rather than 1.

## Strongest Dimensions

Action (raw 1.6) at 1.6 is nominally the highest dimension, which itself describes the profile. Reparative Action (AB5) and Correction Willingness (AB2) at 2 of 5 are the most defensible individual scores: TransDigm repaid $16.1 million in full after congressional pressure in 2019, and it walked away from the Stellant deal rather than litigate with the DOJ.

## Weakest Dimensions

Awareness (raw 1.2), Boundaries (raw 1.2) and Integrity (raw 1.2). Autonomy Preservation scores 1 of 5 because sole-source lock-in is not an accidental by-product of the business — it is the acquisition thesis. Consistency Under Pressure, Non-Performance, Values Alignment and Resilience of Care all score 1: the same conduct recurred across two Inspector General reviews six years apart.

## Evidence Gaps

The Inspector General findings date from 2019 and 2021 and are the backbone of this assessment; no equivalent examination covering 2022 to 2026 was located, so it is possible TransDigm's pricing conduct has changed without that change being visible. TransDigm publishes minimal corporate responsibility information, so employee treatment, safety performance and community investment are essentially unevidenced — Self-Sustainability, Internal Consistency and several Empathy subdimensions are scored 2 on absence of evidence rather than on findings. No employee-review aggregate was verified. Product quality and airworthiness performance, which for an aerospace parts maker is a genuine safety-of-life issue, could not be assessed at all.
