## The short version

New Mexico has built some of the strongest anti-suffering funding machinery in the United States. Voters wrote early childhood funding into the state constitution. The state offers free child care to every working family, free college tuition, and has a one-billion-dollar behavioral health trust fund. It plans water use 50 years out.

It also has one of the weakest delivery records in the country. Its child welfare agency was sued by its own Department of Justice. It has been out of compliance with a court order on at-risk students for eight years. It spent 843.5 million dollars on behavioral health and watched appointment access fall.

The gap between what New Mexico funds and what New Mexico delivers is the single defining fact of this assessment. That gap is why a state with the highest Systemic Thinking score in the assessed peer group lands in the Functional band.

## Cross-state consistency: AB3 Transparency rule v3

**Disposition: declined — precondition not met. AB3 scored on the merits at 4.**

Limb (a) of the rule requires either a branch of government exempting itself from the records law by a blanket or near-blanket claim, or the state attacking the oversight body itself. Neither happened in New Mexico.

The New Mexico Legislature is a public body under NMSA 1978 Section 14-2-6 and is subject to the Inspection of Public Records Act. A 2013 amendment exempts legislators' emails. That is one enumerated carve-out, and under the Idaho refinement an enumerated exemption alone does not trigger the cap.

No evidence was found that New Mexico narrowed, defunded or removed independence protections from any oversight body between 2021 and 2026. The opposite is documented. The Legislative Finance Committee Program Evaluation Unit, the Office of the State Auditor and the independently elected Department of Justice are all funded and all publishing damaging, uncompelled findings against executive agencies.

A serious transparency failure does exist, but at agency level: CYFD withheld records from the state's own investigators, police, auditor, court monitors and courts. That is scored under E4 Validation at 1 and reflected in AB1 — it is an agency practice, not a structural exemption in the records law or an attack on a watchdog. The Foundation for Open Government's suit against the Department of Public Safety is a procedural failure of the North Dakota type, scored on the merits. It is why AB3 is 4 rather than 5.

**Scoring effect:** on the merits, ACC is 3.4 and the composite 57.5. Had the cap applied, AB3 would be 3, ACC 3.2, and the composite 56.2. The band does not change. New Mexico joins Connecticut as a counter-case where a functioning oversight apparatus scores above the conditional cap.

## Key Findings

- **New Mexico funds compassion better than almost any state and delivers it worse than most.** It has a one-billion-dollar behavioral health trust fund, constitutionally protected early childhood money, free child care and free college. Yet its own Legislative Finance Committee reported on 2026-06-18 that the share of people who could actually book a behavioral health appointment fell from 10 percent to 6 percent. Money is not the constraint. Delivery is.

- **The state's own lawyers sued its own child welfare agency, and the reason matters.** On 2026-04-08 the New Mexico Department of Justice published findings after reviewing 20,000 pages and interviewing 150 witnesses. It found CYFD used confidentiality law as a shield against its own state's investigators, auditors, court monitors and courts, and retaliated against foster parents who spoke up. That single finding drives the Empathy score to 40 out of 100.

- **Overdose deaths went the wrong way, alone in the country.** Between 2024 and 2025 drug deaths fell about 14 percent nationally. In New Mexico they rose about 22 percent, to roughly 900 people. This happened during the largest behavioral health investment in state history.

- **New Mexico gave real power away, which is rare.** Of a 90-million-dollar Indian Education Fund covering fiscal years 2026 to 2028, 80 percent goes straight to pueblos, tribes and nations, each writing its own plan and budget. Most states consult. New Mexico transferred control.

- **The state's compassion is paid for by oil and gas, and it does not discuss this.** New Mexico manages the volatility carefully — Senate Bill 26 builds a permanent fund toward 30 billion dollars by 2035 against a forecast revenue decline. But it does not question a model where child welfare depends on fossil fuel extraction. Structural Critique scores 2 out of 5, the lowest subdimension in an otherwise exceptional dimension.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Systemic Thinking (80/100)** — the highest of any dimension assessed here and near the top of the 17-state peer group. New Mexico plans in decades, funds in perpetuity, and cedes control to those better placed to act. Three subdimensions score 5.
- **Awareness (70/100)** — the detection apparatus is genuinely good: independent court monitors, a program evaluation unit that publishes damaging findings, a 50-year water forecast, and mandatory tribal consultation across all agencies.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Boundaries (35/100)** — the state promises more than it can deliver and has no visible plan for the people it turns away. Scope Clarity and Refusal Ethics both score 2, and staff sustainability scores 2.
- **Empathy (40/100)** — driven almost entirely by Validation at 1 out of 5. When New Mexico causes harm, its instinct at agency level has been to invoke confidentiality rather than acknowledge.

## Evidence Gaps

- **B5 Consent Orientation** rests on thin evidence and is marked low confidence. No public review of consent quality in New Mexico's health, child welfare or civil commitment systems was found.
- **E3 Non-Judgment** has good disaggregated data but no independent disparity audit and no confirmation of mandatory bias training, so it is scored conservatively at 2.
- **Corrections vacancy figures** are reported in secondary sources without a dated primary state document; the corrections evidence used here is limited to what could be verified.
- **Community testimony** (Tier 5) is largely absent outside court-appointed monitors. No independent survey confirming whether New Mexicans feel served was located. Several subdimensions are therefore capped at 4.
- This is a desk-based assessment. A Certified Assessment would test the delivery gap directly with the agencies and communities involved.

## Recommended Next Steps

New Mexico is in the **Functional** band at 57.5, near the top of it. Its problem is not vision or money; it is conversion.

- **Functional/Established**: Consider [Advisory Support](/advisory) to translate benchmark insights into strategic action. The specific priority is the funding-to-delivery gap: New Mexico scores 5 out of 5 on Resource Mobilization and 3 out of 5 on Efficacy, 2 out of 5 on Scope Clarity and 2 out of 5 on Refusal Ethics. Raising Action alone to 4.0 would put the composite at 59.4 — still Functional. Reaching the Established band requires simultaneous improvement in at least two dimensions, most plausibly Boundaries and Empathy.
