## The central question for Utah: institutions or civil society?

Utah looks good on social statistics. It has the fourth-lowest poverty rate in the United States, high measured social mobility, and dense charitable networks. None of that is what this benchmark scores.

This benchmark scores what an institution *does*, not the condition of the population it governs. A state can inherit low poverty from demography, industry mix, and a large private welfare system it does not run. Utah has all three.

The evidence here is unusually direct. ProPublica obtained a memorandum of understanding under which Utah counts volunteer hours at Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints welfare facilities toward the state's own federal anti-poverty spending obligation. Over roughly a decade, that arrangement let the Utah Legislature avoid at least $75 million it would otherwise have been legally required to spend. The state also counts a share of church disaster aid shipped out of state — aid that may never reach a Utahn.

That is the clearest possible statement of the distinction. A private institution does the caring. The state books the credit. Under this methodology, the state gets scored on the booking, not the caring.

So this assessment deliberately declines to credit Utah for outcomes it did not produce, and it scores the state on its own conduct: what it detects, what it funds, what it discloses, and what it does when its own evidence tells it something it does not want to hear.

## Key Findings

- **Utah's good social statistics are largely not the state's work, and this assessment does not credit them.** ProPublica obtained a memorandum of understanding letting Utah count Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints volunteer hours toward its own federal anti-poverty spending obligation. Over about a decade this let the Legislature avoid at least $75 million it would otherwise have been required to spend. Low poverty in Utah is real; the state buying its way out of the obligation to address it is what gets scored.

- **Utah commissioned its own medical evidence and then legislated against it.** The state paid for a systematic review of gender-affirming care for minors. The review found the care largely improves outcomes and reduces suicide risk. In February 2026 the Legislature set the finding aside and voted to make its ban permanent. This single act drives four separate subdimension scores down — validation, efficacy, correction willingness and values alignment.

- **Utah is unusually good at long-horizon environmental work and unusually bad at honouring its voters.** The state has appropriated more than $300 million for the Great Salt Lake since 2022 and delivered nearly 400,000 acre-feet of water to it between 2021 and 2025 — a measured outcome. In the same period it overrode a voter-approved Medicaid expansion, rewrote a voter-approved redistricting law, and had a judge impose a congressional map in November 2025 because its own did not comply.

- **The state dismantled its records watchdog while its audit watchdogs kept working.** SB 277, effective 7 May 2025, replaced a 33-year-old independent seven-member records appeals committee with one governor-appointed director. Meanwhile the Legislative Auditor General published findings that Utah's child welfare agency missed more than 3,200 deadlines to lay eyes on an endangered child in fiscal 2025.

- **When Utah's electorate pushed back hard enough, the state reversed.** Protect Utah Workers gathered more than 320,000 signatures — a state record and more than twice the legal threshold — against the 2025 collective bargaining ban. The Legislature repealed the law in December 2025 rather than face the referendum. It is the strongest single piece of evidence that Utah's institutions can still be moved.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Awareness (60/100) and Systemic Thinking (60/100)** are Utah's best areas, and for the same underlying reason: the state builds durable measurement systems and keeps them running. Fourteen consecutive statutory Intergenerational Poverty reports and four consecutive sessions of Great Salt Lake legislation are both genuine long-horizon institutional behaviour, not announcements. Utah measures better than most states in this peer set.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Integrity (30/100)** and **Boundaries (30/100)** are the floor. Integrity fails on a documented multi-instance pattern of overriding ballot initiatives and on commissioning evidence the state then ignored. Boundaries fails on consent — more than 300 involuntary commitment beds in the planned homeless campus — and on refusal ethics, where the alternative offered to people denied state aid may be a church that conditions help on religious participation.

## Evidence Gaps

- No independent audit of racial or ethnic disparity in Utah service outcomes was found, which held EQ3 at 2 rather than allowing a higher score on demonstrated absence of disparity.
- Deaths in custody data for the Utah Department of Corrections across 2024–2026 was not available in aggregate published form; scoring relied on the November 2025 mental health audit instead.
- Post-exit outcome data for Family Employment Program participants was not located, which limits confidence in B2.
- Current-year TANF denial rates were not available; the ProPublica figure dates from 2021, so B4 is scored with medium confidence on a five-year-old primary source corroborated by no newer contradicting evidence.
- The Utah Supreme Court had not ruled on the Utah Fits All voucher programme as of this assessment date, so the education-funding constitutional question is noted but not scored.
