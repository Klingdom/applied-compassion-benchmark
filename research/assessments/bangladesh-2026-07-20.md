## Assessment Context

Bangladesh was surfaced by the 2026-07-20 scan on Human Rights Watch reporting of violence in Gopalganj that killed five people, followed by hundreds of arbitrary detentions and 10 murder cases filed against more than 8,400 mostly unnamed people. A date check is essential here and changes the reading: the Gopalganj clashes occurred on 2025-07-16, not in the 2026 scan window. What is in-window is the publication of HRW's World Report 2026 documenting them. Bangladesh was last assessed on 2026-06-16, when it was upgraded 5.4 points on the strength of the November 2025 labour-law reforms and its exit from the ITUC ten-worst-countries list.

The assessed composite of 36.3 is 3.5 points below the published 39.8 — inside the confirmation threshold, so no change proposal is filed. Boundaries remains the strongest dimension at raw 2.8, holding the gains credited in June: ratification of ILO Conventions 155, 187 and 190, the right to refuse unsafe work, and extension of labour protections to domestic, agricultural and shipbreaking workers. Empathy (raw 2.2), Equity (raw 2.2) and Integrity (raw 2.2) sit below the published baseline, reflecting mass political detention under the interim government and the routine denial of bail.

## Key Findings

- The scan trigger is a 2025 event reported in 2026. The Gopalganj killings happened on 2025-07-16; what falls inside the scan window is HRW's World Report 2026 describing them, not new violence.
- Mass political detention is the central ongoing harm. HRW reports the interim government "has arbitrarily detained thousands of people it considers political opponents," with bail routinely denied.
- Charging 8,400 mostly unnamed people is the clearest accountability failure. Ten murder cases naming an overwhelmingly unidentified mass of defendants is the opposite of individualised justice.
- Bangladesh did investigate itself, which is worth crediting. An inquiry committee found both the Awami League and the National Citizen Party responsible for the Gopalganj clash, rather than blaming only the banned opposition.
- The June 2026 upgrade still holds. Labour reforms ratifying three ILO conventions and extending protection to domestic and shipbreaking workers keep Boundaries the strongest dimension, and nothing in this window reverses them.

## Strongest Dimensions

Boundaries (raw 2.8). Autonomy Preservation, Scope Clarity, Refusal Ethics and Consent Orientation all score 3 of 5, supported by the November 2025 labour-law package that lowered the union formation threshold, added 120-day maternity leave and created a right to refuse unsafe work. This is the dimension where Bangladesh has made real, documented structural progress.

## Weakest Dimensions

Empathy (raw 2.2), Equity (raw 2.2) and Integrity (raw 2.2), all at 2.2. Values Alignment scores 2 of 5: an interim government that came to power through a protest movement against arbitrary detention is now itself detaining thousands arbitrarily. Signal Amplification is also 2 of 5, since political opponents have no safe channel to raise harm.

## Evidence Gaps

No autopsies were performed on those killed at Gopalganj, which permanently limits any assessment of state responsibility for those deaths and directly caps Validation (E4) and Reparative Action (AB5). Independent verification of detention numbers is unavailable; HRW's figures are the best available but are not corroborated by a treaty body or judicial finding. Ready-made garment sector working conditions, which dominate Bangladesh's labour compassion profile, were not re-examined in this cycle and are carried from the June assessment. Rohingya refugee conditions in Cox's Bazar are not covered here and are a significant unexamined component of the Equity score.

## Calibration Note

IMPORTANT PROVENANCE NOTE. Bangladesh's published 39.8 was set on 2026-06-16 by a documented UPGRADE of 5.4 points, applied on labour-reform evidence. The scan trigger for tonight is negative but describes events from July 2025 that predate that upgrade. Proposing a downgrade on evidence the June assessment already had access to would contradict the documented history and is exactly the false positive that screening rule 4 is designed to catch. The correct outcome is confirmation. A genuine downward reassessment would require new within-window evidence of detention or violence in mid-2026, which this cycle did not find.
