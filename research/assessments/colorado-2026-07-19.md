## The headline finding in plain language

Colorado writes unusually good law and then does not reliably deliver it.

The state has built more compassion infrastructure into statute than most US states: a legal duty to collect health data broken out by race, disability, sexual orientation and gender identity; an environmental justice act; paid family leave; automatic criminal record sealing; a school funding formula rewritten to follow student need; and a constitutional right to abortion. That is a genuine, durable foundation.

But in the twelve months to July 2026 the delivery record is poor and, in two areas, alarming. The US Department of Justice opened a civil rights investigation into Colorado's prisons and youth facilities on December 8, 2025. A Denver judge ruled in February 2026 that the state prison system had been coercing inmate labor in violation of the state constitution — a provision Colorado voters added in 2018. Colorado's drug overdose deaths rose more than 10% in 2025 while the national total fell almost 14%. And the state officially missed its first legally binding climate target at the end of 2025.

Colorado scores 52.5 out of 100 — Functional. Strong design, unreliable execution, and weak self-correction.

## Key Findings

- **Colorado writes better law than it delivers.** The state has more compassion commitments in statute than most US states — a legal duty to collect health data by race, disability and gender identity; an environmental justice act; paid family leave used by 135,000 people in year one; automatic record sealing for 140,000+ people. Its Equity score of 65 out of 100 is its only Established-band dimension. But its Action score is 45 out of 100, because the outcomes those laws promised are not arriving.

- **The federal government is investigating how Colorado treats people in its custody.** On December 8, 2025 the US Department of Justice opened a civil rights investigation into whether Colorado prisons fail to provide adequate medical care and sanitary conditions, and whether the state's youth facilities use excessive force and fail to feed detained children adequately. Two months later a Denver judge ruled the Department of Corrections had coerced prison labour under threat of solitary confinement, violating a state constitutional amendment Colorado voters passed in 2018. The state fought that case for four years.

- **Colorado's overdose deaths went up in 2025 while the rest of the country's went down.** US overdose deaths fell almost 14% in 2025, to an estimated 69,973. Colorado was one of the few states to move the other way, rising 10% or more — despite $550 million appropriated in 2021 to transform behavioural health. This is the single clearest efficacy failure in the assessment.

- **Children reporting abuse inside state-licensed facilities were not believed.** Of 1,154 calls to Colorado's child abuse hotline from youth treatment centres over five years, about 92% were screened out. One facility, El Pueblo Boys & Girls Ranch, generated 243 reports in a single year and only 12% were assigned for review. This drove the Validation subdimension to 2 out of 5 and is the strongest single piece of evidence against Colorado in this assessment.

- **Transparency is the accountability bright spot.** Colorado's independent State Auditor named 48 findings in the FY2025 statewide audit, including 501 late accounting adjustments totalling $24.1 billion and material weaknesses at named agencies. Governor Polis vetoed a bill in April 2025 that would have slowed public records responses, and the legislature rejected a similar bill again in 2026. Colorado discloses its own failures — it just does not reliably fix them.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Equity (65/100)** — the only Established-band dimension. Driven by a 5.9% uninsured rate that held through the Medicaid unwinding, Medicaid-equivalent coverage extended to undocumented children and pregnant women, automatic record sealing that requires no application, and an Attorney General-imposed consent decree over the Aurora Police Department with independent public monitoring.
- **Awareness (60/100)** — statutory data-collection mandates covering race, ethnicity, disability, sexual orientation and gender identity, plus three separate structural advocates with standing authority (environmental justice ombudsperson, Child Protection Ombudsman, Health Equity Commission).
- **Systemic Thinking (55/100)** — Colorado legislates upstream by habit: firearm sale restrictions, for-cause eviction protection, permitting reform in overburdened communities, record sealing, paid leave.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Action (45/100)** — the flagship commitments are missing their targets. Overdose deaths rising against a national decline, the first statutory climate target missed and now two years behind, universal preschool quality standards deferred three times, and Marshall Fire support programmes closed while a quarter of destroyed homes remained unbuilt.
- **Boundaries (45/100)** — Colorado over-promises and under-warns. Universal preschool, behavioural health transformation and disaster recovery all left people committed before limits surfaced. The developmental disability waiver admits only 10-20 people a month from its waitlist, and roughly 87,000 hotline calls were screened out in 2025 without an evidenced alternative pathway.
- **Empathy (50/100)** and **Accountability (50/100)** — both dragged down by the same underlying pattern: harms established by outsiders rather than admitted by the state, and the accounts of people in state care treated as not credible.

## Evidence Gaps

- **Affective Resonance (E1) is scored at low confidence.** No systematic community testimony on whether Coloradans feel cared for by state services was found in public sources. The score of 3 rests on programme design quality against two documented episodes of stakeholder anger.
- **Consent Orientation (B5)** rests on statutory protections rather than any evidence about whether consent processes are understood by low-literacy or limited-English populations. No independent review was located.
- **Resilience of Care (I5) is structurally untestable in this window.** Jared Polis has been governor for the full five-year trajectory (2019-2026). No leadership transition has occurred, so the durability of Colorado's practices is asserted from their statutory form, not demonstrated. Polis is term-limited in January 2027; the January 2027 transition will be the first genuine test and should trigger reassessment.
- **Child Protection Ombudsman outcome data.** The office publishes performance plans and case counts, but the uptake rate of its recommendations by CDHS and counties was not locatable, which caps A4 at 4 rather than allowing a judgement on whether signals actually influence decisions.
- **Colorado 2025 overdose data is preliminary.** CDPHE marks 2025 figures as subject to change. The AC3 score of 2 rests substantially on this finding; if final data revises the increase downward materially, AC3 warrants review.

## Five-Year Trajectory (2021-2026)

**2021-2022 — Build phase.** The strongest legislative period. HB21-1266 environmental justice act, SB21-181 health equity strategic plans, the Greenhouse Gas Roadmap, the Office of Gun Violence Prevention, the Behavioral Health Administration with $550 million, HB22-1157 disaggregated data collection, Cover All Coloradans, SB22-099 Clean Slate. Governor Polis rescinded the Sand Creek proclamations in August 2021.

**2023-2024 — Delivery phase, mixed.** FAMLI benefits began January 2024 and performed well. Clean Slate phase one began July 2024. HB24-1448 rewrote school finance. HB24-1098 gave tenants for-cause protection. Voters passed Amendment 79 with 62%. But universal preschool launched without quality standards, the Medicaid unwinding disenrolled 48% of those reviewed against a 31% national average, and the EPA reclassified the Front Range to "serious" ozone nonattainment.

**2025 — Strain becomes visible.** SB25-003 firearms, SB25-276 immigrant protections and SB25-183 abortion coverage all enacted, all carrying real political cost. Polis vetoed the CORA restriction. Against that: $250 million in federal health funding terminated in March, a hiring freeze in August, the FY2025 audit's 48 findings, the DOJ prison investigation opened December 8, and the climate target formally missed on December 31.

**2026 to date — Contraction.** A ~$1.5 billion shortfall closed largely on Medicaid and on Cover All Coloradans. The February prison labour ruling. The BHA hiring scandal in April. The Mesa County lawsuit in May. The child abuse hotline investigation in June. The legislature continued to legislate protectively — HB26-1276 and SB26-5 on immigration detention — while the Governor stayed hands-off.

The trajectory is a state that built well, then lost delivery capacity to fiscal constraint and institutional drift, while retaining unusually good disclosure of its own failures.

## Recommended Next Steps

Colorado scores in the **Functional** band (41-60): systems exist but have significant gaps.

- Consider [Advisory Support](/advisory) to translate these benchmark findings into strategic action — specifically on the gap between statutory design and delivered outcome, which is the defining pattern in this assessment.
- The three findings warranting immediate institutional attention are the child abuse hotline screening practice, the corrections conditions now under federal investigation, and the divergence of Colorado's overdose trend from the national trend.
- Reassessment is recommended after the January 2027 gubernatorial transition, which will provide the first real test of Resilience of Care (I5), and after the DOJ CRIPA investigation reports.

## Methodology and Limitations

This is a desk-based assessment using publicly available information, covering all 8 dimensions and all 40 subdimensions, with a five-year trajectory window (2021-2026) weighted most heavily toward the twelve months to July 2026. Composite computed via `computeCompositeFromDimensions` in `site/scripts/lib/scoring.mjs`, methodology v1.2.

Scoring covers Colorado's own conduct as a state jurisdiction. Federal policy imposed on Colorado is not scored against the state; federal interaction is noted only where Colorado exercised genuine discretion — for example, enacting SB25-276 and defending it against a federal lawsuit (scored), or the termination of $250 million in federal health funding in March 2025 (noted as context, not scored against the state).

Approximately 30 web searches were used, within the assigned budget. No search cap or systematic tool failure was encountered. One primary source, the DOJ press release on the CRIPA investigation, returned HTTP 403 to direct fetch; its date (December 8, 2025) and contents were verified against three independent contemporaneous news reports rather than the publisher's own page, and this is flagged as a minor verification degradation.

This assessment is based on publicly available information and does not constitute a formal Compassion Benchmark Certified Assessment. For a comprehensive, assessor-led evaluation, visit compassionbenchmark.com/certified-assessments.
