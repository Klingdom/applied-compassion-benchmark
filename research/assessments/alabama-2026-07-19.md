### Score Difference Analysis

The published 12.5 is not a disputed research judgment. It is a placeholder. Alabama, Arkansas and Mississippi carry byte-identical dimension arrays and the identical composite, with `last_assessed: null`. No research process produces that result for three independent jurisdictions. The comparison table above is therefore a comparison against an artefact, not against a prior finding.

The two largest gaps, ACT and SYS at +32.5 each, both come from the same missed evidence. Alabama's Literacy Act and Numeracy Act produced the largest 4th-grade math gain of any state on the 2024 National Assessment of Educational Progress, moving Alabama from 52nd to 32nd nationally, and Alabama was one of only two states scoring above its pre-pandemic level in both reading and math. That is a measured, independently administered, upstream result. A benchmark that scores Alabama at 12.5 has not seen it.

The dimensions where research confirms a very low score are EMP at 1.8 and INT at 2.0. Those are earned, and they rest on judicially established facts rather than on reputation. A federal panel found the state's 2023 congressional map was drawn with racially discriminatory intent. The 11th Circuit found the nitrogen execution protocol inflicts unconstitutional suffering. At least 202 people died in Alabama prisons in 2025 at nearly three times the national state-prison mortality rate, and the state neither publishes comprehensive death data nor accepted the federal findings against it.

Alabama does not become the new floor. Georgia at 26.9 remains the lowest state assessed at full depth. Alabama at 31.9 sits between Iowa at 30.6 and Arizona at 36.2. The prison and voting-rights findings are among the most severe in the assessed set, and the divergence between them and the education results is exactly what a working benchmark should show.

### Recommendation

The published score is severely understated and should be replaced. Recommended action: `update-entity`, setting composite 31.9, band Developing, and all eight dimension values, with `last_assessed` set to 2026-07-19. Rank must stay null until the index is rebuilt with all 51 jurisdictions. Arkansas and Mississippi carry the same placeholder and should be flagged for their own first baselines rather than inheriting Alabama's numbers.

## Key Findings

- **Alabama's prisons are the single most severe finding in this assessment, and they pull five separate dimensions down at once.** At least 202 people died in Alabama prisons in 2025. The mortality rate was 957 deaths per 100,000 people, against a national state-prison average of 330. The US Department of Justice sued the state in December 2020 over unconstitutional conditions and the case was still unresolved in July 2026. The Department of Corrections rejected the findings publicly rather than acknowledging them.

- **Alabama's schools are the strongest positive finding, and they are measured by someone else.** On the 2024 National Assessment of Educational Progress, Alabama's 4th-grade math score rose 6 points, the largest gain of any state, moving Alabama from 52nd to 32nd nationally. Alabama and Louisiana were the only two states scoring above their pre-pandemic level in both reading and math. This is the work of the Literacy Act and the Numeracy Act, funded and coached for years before the results arrived.

- **The state moved from crisis to signed law in about three weeks on IVF, which cuts against what most readers expect.** After the Alabama Supreme Court's February 2024 LePage ruling shut down fertility clinics, patients and providers reached lawmakers directly and the governor signed an immunity statute on 2024-03-06. The fix was incomplete, because it left the underlying embryo-personhood question open, but the responsiveness was real and it is scored as such.

- **A federal court found Alabama drew its 2023 congressional map with racially discriminatory intent, after the Legislature passed a map the state itself admitted fell short of a Supreme Court order.** This is the clearest available evidence on three separate subdimensions: whether the state suspends judgment across identity lines, whether its decisions match its stated values, and whether commitments hold under pressure.

- **Alabama denies parole against its own rulebook.** In May 2025 the Bureau of Pardons and Paroles guidelines recommended release in 79 percent of cases and the board followed them in 22 percent of decisions. In November 2025 it denied 189 of 224 cases. Refusing people against your own published criteria, without reasons tied to those criteria, is the behaviour the framework scores at the floor.

- **Alabama has not expanded Medicaid, and the cost is measurable.** Roughly 154,800 low-income adults have no coverage pathway and the state forgoes about $181.6 million in 2026. Meanwhile more than a third of Alabama counties are maternity care deserts, and Alabama has the 3rd highest infant mortality and 9th highest maternal mortality rate in the country.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Systemic Thinking (2.8/5)** and **Action (2.8/5)** are the highest, and both rest on the same two things: early-grade education reform with independently measured results, and large sustained infrastructure investment. More than $2.5 billion in broadband since 2018, $460 million in BEAD awards covering 92,000 unserved locations, and $203 million for rural health transformation. Root Cause Orientation at 4/5 is the single highest subdimension score in the assessment.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Empathy (1.8/5)** is the lowest. Three of its five subdimensions score at or near the floor. Non-Judgment and Validation both score 1/5, driven by the judicial finding of discriminatory intent, the ban on required diversity training that removes the corrective mechanism, and the state's practice of contesting harm findings rather than acknowledging them.
- **Equity, Boundaries and Integrity all sit at 2.0/5.** The common thread is that Alabama's own published standards, whether parole guidelines, a Supreme Court order, or an execution protocol, do not bind its conduct.

## Evidence Gaps

- **B5 Consent Orientation is scored low-confidence.** Evidence outside the prison labour litigation was thin. The score of 2 reflects absence of evidence rather than established failure.
- **Prison death data is structurally unavailable.** Alabama has no agency publishing a comprehensive record, despite a 2021 statute requiring quarterly reporting. The 202 figure comes from an advocacy group's records request. This limits confidence on E1 and AB5 and is itself scored as a transparency failure under AB3.
- **The outcome of *United States v. Alabama* was not available.** Trial was set for April 2026 and no verdict was confirmed as of this assessment. A finding for the United States would likely lower AB1 and I4 further; it cannot raise them.
- **I5 Resilience of Care cannot be properly tested.** One governor has held office for the entire assessment window, so no leadership transition is available to observe.
- **Maternal Mortality Review Committee findings were not retrievable at document level.** The committee's existence and mandate are confirmed; the content of recent recommendations is not, which caps confidence on A1 and E5.

## Recommended Next Steps

Alabama scores in the **Developing** band. Key structures are emerging but remain inconsistent or reactive.

- Consider a [Certified Assessment](/certified-assessments) for a structured improvement roadmap. The highest-leverage single change indicated by this profile is not a new programme. It is bringing the state's own published standards, starting with the parole guidelines it follows in 22 percent of cases, into force.
