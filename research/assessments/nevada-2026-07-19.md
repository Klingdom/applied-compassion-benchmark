## Correcting three premises in the research brief

Three items in the tasking questions rest on premises that the evidence does not support. Recording them because a false premise carried into a score is a scoring error.

1. **The 2024 Equal Rights Amendment.** Nevada's constitutional equal rights amendment was approved by voters once, on 2022-11-08, with 57 percent support. It was not a two-cycle measure and did not need a second vote in 2024. Nevada's ranked-choice voting question was the two-cycle measure, and it failed in 2024.
2. **The 2025 federal land bill.** The Southern Nevada Economic Development and Conservation Act is a bill in the United States Congress, not a Nevada state law. It had not been enacted as of the most recent evidence found. It is therefore scored only where Nevada exercised its own discretion, and it cannot be credited to the state as delivered housing supply.
3. **A prisons ombuds or inspector general.** Nevada does have an Office of the Inspector General, but it sits inside the Department of Corrections and reports to it. It is an internal review unit, not independent oversight. There is no independent corrections ombuds in Nevada.

## Key Findings

- **Nevada has been last in the country for mental health care two years running, and the structures it built have not moved the number.** Mental Health America ranked Nevada 51st of 51 for overall and youth mental health for 2025, the second consecutive year at the bottom. The state created an Office of Mental Health, made its behavioural health clinic programme permanent in Medicaid, and put $10 million into 988 crisis lines. There is one mental health professional for every 400 residents. Building infrastructure and changing outcomes are not the same thing, and only the first has happened.

- **The biennial legislature is not itself the problem — what the state does with the gap is.** Nevada is one of four states that legislate only every other year, meeting for at most 120 days in odd years. That constraint is survivable: Nevada can call special sessions and did, in November 2025. But the special session's headline item was a $120 million-a-year film tax credit, which failed by one vote, rather than the service failures. Nevada enters 2026 facing a roughly $191 million revenue shortfall with no regular session scheduled. The arrangement is not scored; the choice of what to bring back for is.

- **Water is where Nevada does long-term thinking better than almost any state, and it shows the state is capable of it.** Per-person water use in the Las Vegas Valley fell about 55 percent since 2002 while the population grew by roughly 750,000. Nearly all indoor water returns to Lake Mead and is credited against Nevada's Colorado River draw. The 2021 law banning Colorado River water for decorative grass was written with a 2027 compliance date and defended in court. This is a 25-year policy held across four governors and two parties.

- **Housing is where Nevada's choices cut against its own stated goals.** Nevada has 17 affordable rental homes for every 100 extremely low-income renter households and is short about 78,000 homes. It is the only state where an eviction proceeds unless the tenant files first. In June 2025 the governor signed more than $180 million for attainable housing and, in the same period, vetoed Assembly Bill 283 on eviction filing, Assembly Bill 280 on senior rent increases, and Assembly Bill 201 on sealing eviction records.

- **Nevada tells the truth about its own failures more readily than most states, but only outside corrections.** The state published an after-action report on the 2025 ransomware attack naming an employee download as the entry point and admitting the intruder went undetected from May to August. It refused to pay the ransom. Its legislative auditors publish unflattering findings nobody requested. In prisons, by contrast, 166 in-custody deaths since 2024 came to light through legislative questioning, and a legislator called the state's own mortality report "a lot of unexplained deaths."

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Awareness (3.4/5) and Systemic Thinking (3.4/5)** are Nevada's joint highest. Awareness rests on genuinely differentiated processes — a codified tribal consultation statute binding every state agency, a Tribal Health Authority Council seating tribal health directors, rural Medicaid differentials — plus a legislative audit function that keeps finding things. Systemic Thinking rests almost entirely on water: a 25-year conservation record, cross-system return-flow accounting tied to a seven-state compact, and a willingness to defend an unpopular restriction in court.
- **Integrity (3.2/5)** is carried by two subdimensions scoring 4: the state treats its own workforce consistently with its stated values, and its core commitments survived a change of governing party in 2023.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Empathy, Equity and Boundaries all sit at 2.6/5 — the Developing band.** The common thread is that Nevada's structural choices place the burden on the person in difficulty. Summary eviction requires the tenant to act first. Medicaid rates are acknowledged as too low to buy access. Repair for the patient-dumping scandal came from a jury rather than the state.
- **Values Alignment (I4) at 2/5 is the single lowest subdimension.** Nevada repeatedly funds a stated priority while simultaneously blocking the measures that would protect the same population.

## Evidence Gaps

- **Consent (B5).** No evidence was found of any review of state consent materials for readability or genuine informed understanding. Scored 2 for absence, confidence low. This is a gap in available evidence, not a confirmed failure.
- **Autonomy outcomes (B2).** Nevada publishes activity and enrolment data but no measurement of whether help reduces dependence. Confidence low.
- **Corrections oversight.** Nevada's Inspector General sits inside the corrections department. No independent body publishes verified in-custody death analysis, so scoring on prison conditions relies on legislative hearings and news reporting rather than audited data.
- **Maternal health.** The 2025 Minority Health Report added maternal mortality and severe maternal morbidity as indicators, but no state-level outcome trend was recoverable within this assessment. No maternal-specific subdimension score rests on it.
- **Wage theft outcomes.** The $122 million annual estimate comes from an independent academic study; the state recovered about $4.29 million in fiscal 2024. The recovery figure is state data; the gap figure is not, and the two are not directly comparable.

## Cross-State Consistency

The AB3 transparency rule v3 was applied and **triggered**. Disposition: **applied (cap at 3)**, joining Virginia, Massachusetts, Washington, Missouri and Indiana.

- Limb (a), structural transparency failure: met. The Nevada Public Records Act has been interpreted not to reach the Legislature at all, on separation-of-powers and legislative-privilege grounds, and NRS 218F.150 makes Legislative Counsel Bureau work confidential and immune from discovery or subpoena. This is a branch exempting itself, not a list of subject-matter carve-outs, so the Idaho scope limit does not save it. It is also not a merely procedural weakness, so the North Dakota limit does not apply.
- Limb (b), surviving output: met. The LCB Audit Division continues to publish damaging, uncompelled findings, including the 2025 transportation audit and the audit of the state employee health plan. The Massachusetts output contingency is therefore satisfied and the cap of 3 holds rather than the floor of 2.
- Scoring effect: AB3 = 3 gives ACC 2.8 and a composite of **48.1**. Had the floor of 2 applied instead, ACC would be 2.6 and the composite **47.5**. Both land in the Functional band, so the cap changes the number but not the band.

Nevada scores in the **Functional** band: systems exist but have significant gaps. ## Methodology and Limitations

Assessed against all 8 dimensions and all 40 subdimensions, covering the trajectory from 2021 to 2026 with the most recent twelve months weighted most heavily. Scores reflect Nevada's own conduct. Federal policy imposed on the state is excluded; where the state exercised discretion within a federal framework — taking the Medicaid expansion, administering opioid settlement funds, choosing not to pay a ransom — that discretion is scored.
