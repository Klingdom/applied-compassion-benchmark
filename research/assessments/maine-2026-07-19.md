## Cross-State Consistency: AB3 Transparency Rule

**Disposition: declined — precondition not met.**

Maine does not trigger either limb of the AB3 cap. Its Legislature exempts working papers and drafts until they are signed and distributed, which is a carve-out on works in progress rather than a branch exempting itself. Executive records, including the Governor's, are covered by the Freedom of Access Act. Maine's roughly 300 statutory exemptions are enumerated subject-matter carve-outs, which the Idaho refinement says do not trigger the cap. The public access ombudsman's lack of appeal authority is procedural weakness, which the North Dakota refinement says should be scored on the merits. And Maine has not curtailed its watchdog — OPEGA keeps its remit and funding.

Following the Connecticut counter-case, AB3 is scored on the merits at 4. **Scoring effect:** AB3 at 4 gives ACC 3.6 and a composite of 55.6. Applying the cap at 3 would give ACC 3.4 and a composite of 55.0. The band is Functional either way.

## Key Findings

- **Maine's drug policy is working, and the state can prove it.** Maine recorded 390 fatal overdoses in 2025, down 20 percent from 490 in 2024. That is the third straight annual decline and the lowest total since 2019. The state credits naloxone distribution and OPTIONS outreach workers placed in all 16 counties. This is rare: a state that made an upstream bet, measured it, and cut downstream suffering.

- **Maine investigated itself over Lewiston and published what it found.** After the October 2023 mass shooting that killed 18 people, the state created an independent commission. In August 2024 that commission found a Maine sheriff's office had legal authority under the yellow flag law to seize the gunman's firearms a month earlier and did not use it. The state then amended the law within eight months. Commissioning an inquiry that blames your own agencies carries no reputational upside, and it is the single strongest integrity signal in Maine's record.

- **About 1,000 people faced criminal charges in Maine with no lawyer, and the state fought the court order to fix it.** A judge ruled in March 2025 that Maine was violating the Sixth Amendment. Rather than comply, the state appealed and won a pause on the reform order in December 2025. This is the clearest case in the record of Maine treating a proven harm as a legal problem first.

- **Maine says the Wabanaki Nations were wronged, then blocks the fix.** The 1980 Settlement Act leaves the five Wabanaki Nations with less authority over land, taxation and justice than 570 other federally recognised tribes. Maine has made real partial repairs, including handing tribal courts exclusive jurisdiction over lesser crimes in 2024. But in June 2025 the Governor vetoed a bill barring the state from seizing tribal land, even though it passed with bipartisan support. Tribal leaders called the veto "protecting an old guard."

- **The oldest population in the country is losing its care infrastructure.** Maine had 132 nursing homes in 1995 and has 79 now, with 27 closing since 2014. Hancock County has none left. About 200 hospital patients wait for a long-term care bed on any given day. This is Maine's most predictable need and its least adequate response.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Accountability (3.6)** and **Awareness (3.6)** — driven by unusually dense independent oversight. OPEGA, the Government Oversight Committee, the Child Welfare Ombudsman and the Lewiston commission all publish damaging findings that nobody compelled them to publish.
- **Systemic Thinking (3.6)** — a statutory four-year climate planning cycle with measured progress, plus upstream investment in paid leave, harm reduction and eviction prevention that produced measurable downstream reductions.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Boundaries (2.6)** — the weakest area by a clear margin. Maine promises things it does not deliver and does not tell people in advance: counsel it does not provide, MaineCare payments withheld mid-year in two consecutive years, and shelter capacity roughly a third of measured need.
- **Empathy (2.8)** — held down almost entirely by E4 Validation at 2. When shown it was causing constitutional harm, Maine's institutional reflex was appeal rather than acknowledgement.

## Evidence Gaps

- **B5 Consent Orientation** is scored at 3 with **low confidence**. No independent review of consent quality in Maine's public programmes was found. This is scored as an evidence gap, not as good conduct.
- **OPEGA report publication dates** could not be confirmed to day precision from the publisher's own URL paths; those evidence items carry year precision.
- Maine's population is under 1.4 million and press coverage is correspondingly thin. Absence of reported failure was not treated as evidence of good conduct anywhere in this assessment.
- The Lewiston victims fund controversy of 2026 involves the Maine Community Foundation, a private charity, not a state agency. It is excluded from scoring. Only the legislators' June 2026 call for an investigation is counted, as state conduct.
- Maine's Governor leaves office in January 2027. I5 Resilience of Care is scored on one tested transition (2019); durability across the coming transition is unknown.
