## Key Findings

**1. New Hampshire fought hardest against the people it had already admitted harming.** The state ran the Youth Development Center for decades. It has now agreed to pay $239 million to about 1,700 survivors. But when a jury awarded David Meehan $38 million on 2024-05-03 — the largest contested personal-injury verdict in state history — the state's own lawyers argued the award should be $475,000. To get there, the state argued in a brief reported 2025-09-08 that roughly 200 child rapes were legally a single incident, comparing them to one oil spill. That single argument drives the Empathy score to 25 of 100.

**2. The state changed the rules one day after survivors could no longer opt out.** Survivors dropped their lawsuits because they were told an independent administrator would decide their awards. Changes stripping that independence and handing the Attorney General a veto took effect one day after the claim-filing deadline closed. The Attorney General then rejected about 20 percent of settlements the previous administrator had already approved. Boundaries scores 20 of 100 — the lowest dimension — because consent was obtained on terms the state then altered.

**3. On health crises, New Hampshire is genuinely good, and the numbers prove it.** Overdose deaths fell from 486 in 2022 to 287 in 2024, a 40 percent drop. Children boarding in emergency rooms waiting for a psychiatric bed fell from nearly 50 in February 2021 to 2 in March 2026. Child protection assessments per worker fell from 90 in 2016 to 16. These are measured outcomes, not activity counts, and they carry Action and Systemic Thinking to 45 of 100 each.

**4. New Hampshire defunded its own transparency watchdog and left the job empty for a year.** The Legislature cut the Right-to-Know Ombudsman budget from $105,000 to $30,000. The ombudsman quit in July 2025. On 2026-07-02, a year later, nobody had been nominated. In the same budget cycle the House voted to abolish the Office of the Child Advocate outright, and half its staff were restored only in negotiation.

**5. The revenue structure is the untouched root cause, and courts have said so twice.** New Hampshire has no income or sales tax and draws 61 percent of local public revenue from property tax — more than any other state. Its poorest families pay 5.9 percent of income in property tax; its richest pay 2 percent. Courts ruled the resulting school funding unconstitutionally low twice in 2025, in ConVal in July and Rand on 2025-08-20. The state has not remedied it. Structural Critique scores 1 of 5.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Awareness (50/100)** — the best-instrumented dimension. Daily psychiatric bed counts, monthly drug monitoring, a homelessness data hub and an annual child protection data book give the state real visibility into who is suffering.
- **Action, Accountability and Systemic Thinking (45/100 each)** — carried by measured, published improvements in overdose mortality, emergency-room boarding and child protection caseloads, and by a standing fatality review commission that produces systemic change.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Boundaries (20/100)** — the lowest score, driven almost entirely by the settlement fund. Scope Clarity and Consent Orientation both score 1 of 5 because roughly 1,700 people surrendered legal rights on a description of a process that the state then changed.
- **Empathy (25/100)** — the oil spill argument, the diversity and inclusion ban, and the sanctuary policy ban together mean the state neither validates the experience of those it harmed nor maintains the infrastructure for suspending judgment across difference.
- **Integrity (30/100)** — Values Alignment scores 1 of 5. A trauma-informed process rewritten after the deadline, an attempted $21.4 million diversion of restricted opioid money, and a defunded transparency ombudsman form a consistent pattern.

## Evidence Gaps

- **Deaths in custody.** Only one case is well documented publicly: Jason Rothe died on 2023-04-29 after being restrained in the secure psychiatric unit, ruled a homicide, with a wrongful death suit filed in April 2026. New Hampshire has no published in-custody death dashboard, so B1 and I3 rest on staffing data rather than outcome data. Confidence marked high on staffing, but the mortality picture is incomplete.
- **The Meehan appeal is undecided.** The New Hampshire Supreme Court heard argument in November 2025 and had not ruled as of 2026-07-19. AB5 and E4 score the state's litigation conduct, which is established, not the eventual outcome, which is not yet decided in court.
- **Racial and ethnic disparity data.** New Hampshire is demographically homogeneous and, since the equity office was renamed and agency equity work banned, publishes little disaggregated outcome data. EQ3 and E3 are scored on the dismantling of the measuring apparatus rather than on measured disparities. Confidence medium.
- **State employee wellbeing outside corrections.** No published statewide turnover or burnout series was located, so B1 generalises from corrections, the worst-affected agency.
- **2026 homelessness count.** The January 2026 point-in-time figures were not published in time for this assessment.

This is a desk-based assessment using publicly available information. A formal Certified Assessment would involve direct institutional engagement, including access to internal turnover data, survivor testimony gathered under the benchmark's own protocol, and interviews with the Office of the Child Advocate.

## Recommended Next Steps

New Hampshire lands in the **Developing** band (21–40): key structures are emerging but inconsistent or reactive. The state has proven it can deliver measured improvement — the overdose and boarding records are among the strongest in this campaign — while simultaneously conducting itself as an adversary toward the people it has admitted harming. That contradiction, not a lack of capability, is what holds the score down.

- **Developing band:** Consider a [Certified Assessment](/certified-assessments) for a structured improvement roadmap. The highest-leverage single change is the settlement fund: restoring administrator independence would lift Boundaries, Empathy and Integrity together.
