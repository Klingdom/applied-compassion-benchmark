## Key Findings

- **Maryland builds good machinery and then underfunds the people inside it.** The state created strong permanent structures — an independent Correctional Ombudsman, statewide eviction right-to-counsel, the first Tenants' Bill of Rights in the country — while spending $350 million on 6.7 million overtime hours in fiscal 2025 because its own agencies were short-staffed.

- **The state reduced what it owes abuse survivors after the harm was established.** In April 2025 Maryland cut the damages cap for government child sex abuse claims from $890,000 to $400,000 per claimant, for previously time-barred claims not filed before 2025-06-01. There are 12,305 claims against the state. No money is budgeted to pay any of them. This single decision drives the low scores in Validation (E4), Reparative Action (AB5) and Consistency Under Pressure (I1).

- **Disability services were cut in two consecutive budgets, and the real loss is larger than the headline.** Maryland cut $164 million from the Developmental Disabilities Administration in fiscal 2026 and $126 million in fiscal 2027. Because these are Medicaid dollars that draw a federal match, advocates put the combined fiscal 2027 service loss above $250 million.

- **Where Maryland measures outcomes, it performs well.** Opioid deaths hit a 10-year low in 2025, down 57% from the pandemic peak. Eviction right-to-counsel was estimated to avoid $46 million in downstream costs in 2023–2024. Systemic Thinking is Maryland's strongest area at 4.0 of 5.

- **Maryland's compassion is more legislative than executive.** The legislature overrode the Governor's veto to create a Reparations Commission on 2025-12-16 by 93-35 and 31-14. The Governor's veto letter had argued there had been "enough study." Integrity is the weakest area at 2.4 of 5.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Systemic Thinking (75/100).** Maryland works upstream and publishes long-horizon data. The ENOUGH Initiative attacks concentrated child poverty through 27 community-led organizations. Deficit projections run to fiscal 2031. The state took costly structural positions, including banning 287(g) agreements and taxing capital gains.
- **Awareness (70/100).** Maryland detects suffering through structures that are hard to switch off: an independent Correctional Ombudsman covering 11 juvenile facilities, Racial Equity Impact Notes attached to bills, and published health disparity data.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Integrity (35/100).** Commitments bent under budget pressure. The abuse damages cap fell, disability services were cut twice, the largest state union rejected a 2% offer, and the Governor vetoed a reparations study his own legislature then restored.
- **Boundaries (40/100).** Helping is not coming from a sustainable base. Overtime substitutes for staffing, correctional officers warn about violence, and disability applicants face months-long delays with no documented alternative-referral path.

- Current disaggregated health coverage data for Maryland was not available at the granularity needed; EQ1 was scored conservatively at 3 with low confidence.
- No independent third-party audit of state workforce sustainability was found, so B1 rests on union reporting and the state's own overtime figure.
- Community testimony confirming whether Juvenile Services actually fixed the 2025 Ombudsman findings was not available; A3 and AB2 were held at 4 and 3 rather than 5.
- Evidence of cultural co-design outside the immigrant-rights context was thin, holding E5 at 3.

## Attribution Note

Maryland has the highest federal-workforce concentration of any state, and lost roughly 25,000 federal jobs during 2025. Federal job cuts are not scored against Maryland. What is scored is Maryland's own discretion in response: the resource portal, the Protect Our Federal Workers Act signed April 2025, fast-track state hiring and zero-interest emergency loans. Likewise, the 287(g) ban and the sensitive-locations law are scored because Maryland chose them; federal immigration policy itself is not scored. The state's budget deficit is partly federally driven, but how Maryland allocated the remaining money — cutting disability services while doubling eviction prevention — is entirely a state choice and is fully scored.

## Recommended Next Steps

Maryland is in the **Functional** band. Systems exist and several are national firsts, but there are significant gaps between what the state builds and what it sustains. Recommended: [Advisory Support](/advisory) to translate these benchmark findings into strategic action, with priority on Reparative Action (AB5), Consistency Under Pressure (I1) and workforce sustainability (B1).
