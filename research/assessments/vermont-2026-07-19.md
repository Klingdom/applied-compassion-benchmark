### Score Difference Analysis

**Empathy, -47.5 points.** The placeholder at 4.7 raw implies Vermont's community members sit as decision-makers, that harm reports are acknowledged before investigation, and that community accounts are treated as primary evidence. The 2024-2026 record shows the opposite on the state's largest social programme. In early June 2025 the Department for Children and Families told motel residents that nights sheltered under the governor's executive order would count against their 80-night limit, contradicting the understanding residents and advocates had planned around. When roughly 906 households left motels, the state published no destination data. End Homelessness Vermont, a nonprofit, tracked 124 of them. Community accounts were the only evidence available, and they were produced outside the state.

**Boundaries, -45.0 points.** The placeholder at 4.4 raw implies limitations are communicated before commitment and that nobody is turned away without a concrete alternative. The motel wind-down is the direct counterexample. Roughly 1,500 people lost shelter under a rule that changed mid-stay. Outreach workers found 15 to 20 formerly sheltered households living outside in Brattleboro alone. This is the single largest driver of the composite gap and it is not ambiguous.

**Accountability, -40.0 points.** Vermont's transparency genuinely earns a 4. Its systemic learning does not. The Corrections Monitoring Commission was created in 2022 in response to sexual misconduct allegations at the state's women's prison, was required to produce annual reports, produced none, and expired on 2025-07-01. Separately, the State Auditor found that management decisions nullified roughly $517,000 of improper payment findings. Failures were detected and then not institutionalised into change.

**Integrity, -40.0 points.** Vermont's reputation is strongest here and the evidence is most divided. It scores 4 on non-performance because it funds and publishes research that indicts its own police. It scores 2 on values alignment because it holds about 150 residents in a for-profit prison 1,500 miles away, and 2 on consistency because it missed a climate target written into its own binding law and then repealed the main mechanism for meeting it.

**Where the placeholder was closest.** Equity at -17.5 and Awareness and Systemic Thinking at -20.0 are the three dimensions where Vermont's reputation is most nearly earned. 97 percent health coverage with the racial gap published, a statutory Truth and Reconciliation Commission, a self-imposed payroll tax for child care, and a regulator willing to cut the state's largest hospital by $88.5 million are all real. They are why Vermont scores 52.5 rather than in the thirties.

## Key Findings

- **Vermont's biggest compassion failure is how it ended help, not whether it gave help.** The state sheltered thousands of homeless Vermonters in motels, then wound the programme down. About 906 households, roughly 1,500 people, left after restrictions took effect on 2024-09-15. Over 800 more, including nearly 300 children, were scheduled to exit on 2025-07-01. The state did not publish where any of them went. A nonprofit tracked 124 households and found 16 housed.

- **The rules changed while people were relying on them.** Residents and advocates understood that a governor's emergency order paused the 80-night limit on motel stays. In early June 2025 the Department for Children and Families told them it had not. That single decision is why Vermont scores 2 out of 5 on both scope clarity and validation.

- **Vermont holds about 150 of its own residents in a for-profit prison in Mississippi, 1,500 miles from home.** The state contracts with CoreCivic for up to 300 beds at the Tallahatchie County Correctional Facility. This is the sharpest gap between Vermont's reputation and its conduct, and it drives the Integrity score down.

- **The oversight body created after a prison sexual misconduct scandal never did its job.** Vermont set up the Corrections Monitoring Commission in 2022 and required it to publish annual reports. It published none, stopped meeting by June 2023, and expired on 2025-07-01.

- **Vermont is genuinely strong at seeing problems and at spending money upstream.** 97 percent of Vermonters had health coverage in 2025, among the best rates in the country, and the state published the racial gap in that figure. Act 76 created a payroll tax in 2024 to fund child care, and the subsidy programme now reaches close to 12,000 children. Overdose deaths fell 37 percent from the 2022 peak. These are why the score is 52.5 and not lower.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Awareness (65.0) and Systemic Thinking (65.0)** are tied at the top. Vermont detects suffering through standing, disaggregated, published data systems and an independently elected auditor who finds real problems. It also invests upstream: Act 76 on child care, Act 73 on education finance, and a health regulator willing to cut the state's largest hospital by $88.5 million.
- **Equity (60.0)** follows, carried by near-universal health coverage and a funded statutory reckoning with the state's eugenics history.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Boundaries (40.0)** is the weakest by a clear margin, and the only dimension in the Developing band. Scope clarity, refusal ethics and consent orientation all score 2 out of 5, all traceable to the motel programme wind-down.
- **Empathy, Accountability and Integrity all sit at 45.0.** Each contains at least one genuine 4 alongside two scores of 2. Vermont knows things and says things it does not always act on.

## Evidence Gaps

- **B5 Consent Orientation is scored at 2 with low confidence.** No public evidence was found on how Vermont obtains or verifies informed consent in its benefit and service programmes. The score reflects two negative signals rather than a documented process.
- **No independent community testimony was located** on whether Vermonters feel genuinely cared for by state services. This capped E1 at 3 and EQ1 at 4, both of which require community confirmation to reach the top anchors.
- **The Truth and Reconciliation Commission's final report is not due until mid-2027,** so EQ5 and AB5 are scored on process rather than on delivered repair.
- **No systematic post-exit outcome data exists for the motel programme** because the state did not collect it. Scores for AC5 and B4 rest on nonprofit and journalistic tracking, which is Tier 3 and 4 evidence rather than Tier 5.
- This is a desk-based assessment using publicly available information. It did not include direct institutional engagement with Vermont agencies.

## Recommended Next Steps

Vermont scores in the **Functional** band (41-60). Systems exist and several are genuinely good, but they have significant gaps concentrated in how the state ends and limits help.

- Consider [Advisory Support](/advisory) to translate these benchmark findings into strategic action, particularly on Boundaries, where a single programme drives three subdimension scores of 2 out of 5.
