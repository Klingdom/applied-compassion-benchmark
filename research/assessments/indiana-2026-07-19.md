## Cross-State Consistency: AB3 Transparency Rule

**Rule (adopted 2026-07-19):** Where a broad public-records exemption coexists with an independently funded oversight body still publishing damaging, uncompelled findings, cap AB3 at 3 rather than flooring at 2. The Massachusetts rider adds that the cap holds only while the oversight body still produces findings.

**Applied: yes.** Indiana fits the rule, with one variation worth recording for future assessments.

The obstruction here is not a records exemption carved out around an oversight body. It is a 2024 statute, House Enrolled Act 1338, that curtailed the oversight body itself. The law restricted the Public Access Counselor to the plain text of public access law and valid Indiana court opinions, and converted the post from a fixed four-year term to service at the governor's pleasure. That is arguably a more direct transparency harm than the Virginia or Massachusetts fact patterns.

The output contingency is satisfied on two independent grounds. First, the Public Access Counselor still publishes: a 2026 advisory opinions page is live and opinions issued from October 2025 onward. Second, and more decisively, the Indiana State Board of Accounts is a separately constituted audit body still publishing damaging, uncompelled findings against executive agencies — including the attendant care provider audits that produced a $198,031,230 extrapolated improper-payment finding in April 2026.

**Scoring effect:** applying the cap gives AB3 3, ACC 2.8, composite **31.9**. Declining the cap and flooring AB3 at 2 gives ACC 2.6, composite **31.3**. Both figures were produced by `computeCompositeFromDimensions`. The band is Developing either way.

**Proposed revision to the rule:** extend the wording to cover curtailment of the oversight body itself, not only exemptions carved out around it. Indiana's outcome is unchanged because the State Board of Accounts independently satisfies the output contingency. But a state that curtailed its *only* oversight body would need the floor of 2, and the current wording would not clearly deliver that.

## Key Findings

- **Indiana told the truth about its own biggest failure, then made the people least able to absorb it pay for the fix.** The state disclosed a roughly $984 million Medicaid forecasting error in December 2023 before anyone else found it. It then identified about 1,600 medically complex children as a cost driver and removed paid family caregiving from them.

- **A federal appeals court found Indiana's response likely broke federal disability law.** On 2025-08-11 the Seventh Circuit upheld an injunction against the attendant care change, holding it likely violates the Americans with Disabilities Act. Indiana had already lost once at the district court and chose to appeal. This drives Validation to 1 out of 5 — the lowest anchor.

- **Indiana built something genuinely good and dismantled it in two years.** The 2023 legislature put $225 million into local public health across all 92 counties and $100 million into behavioural health. By May 2025 the public health programme was cut 64%, from $100 million to $40 million. Health leaders said the cut came years too early to show results.

- **The stated value and the budget decision were three weeks apart.** Governor Mike Braun signed nine "Make Indiana Healthy Again" executive orders on 2025-04-15. The legislature cut the state's main public health programme in early May 2025. That gap is why Values Alignment scores 1 out of 5.

- **Indiana's oversight bodies still work, which is why the score is not lower.** The State Board of Accounts publishes damaging audits against executive agencies. The Public Access Counselor still issues opinions despite a 2024 law narrowing its remit and removing its term protection. The Maternal Mortality Review Committee publishes that 71% of pregnancy-associated deaths were preventable. These are uncompelled disclosures the state gains nothing from.
