## Scope and attribution note

This assessment scores the conduct of the State of Alaska — the governor's office, state departments, the Legislature, and state-run oversight bodies. It does not score federal policy imposed on Alaska.

Alaska requires unusual care on this point. Roughly 60 percent of Alaska land is federally owned. The Alaska Native Claims Settlement Act (ANCSA, 1971) and the Alaska National Interest Lands Conservation Act (ANILCA, 1980) are federal statutes. Tribal jurisdiction in Alaska is largely a federal-law question. Indian Health Service and tribal health organizations deliver much Alaska Native health care outside state control.

Where the state exercised real discretion, it is scored. Three examples used throughout:

- **The food stamp backlog is a state failure.** The Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP) is federally funded but state-administered. Alaska's Division of Public Assistance controlled processing.
- **Village relocation credit is limited.** Newtok's move was paid for mostly with federal money — $25 million from the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act and $6.7 million from the Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA). The state's role was technical and planning assistance.
- **Tribal recognition was a genuine state choice.** House Bill 123 (2022) was a state statute the state was under no federal obligation to pass.

## Key Findings

- **Alaska is the only US state that shares statewide health planning authority with tribes — and that is the single biggest reason it scores as high as it does.** Healthy Alaskans 2030 is co-led by the Alaska Department of Health and the Alaska Native Tribal Health Consortium, and is the only statewide health improvement plan in the nation run as a state-tribal partnership. Combined with House Bill 123 (2022), which gave statutory recognition to 229 tribes, and the Tribal Child Welfare Compact, this produced Alaska's strongest scores: Systemic Thinking 65.0 out of 100 and Equity 55.0.

- **Alaska made thousands of hungry people wait months for food aid, then argued in court that they had no right to complain.** Federal law gives the state 7 to 30 days to act on a food stamp application. A federal judge ordered the state on 2024-12-31 to process applications on time and file monthly reports. The backlog still persisted in December 2025. On 2026-05-21 the state won summary judgment by arguing federal food-stamp law creates no privately enforceable right — a legal win that scored as a compassion failure across four subdimensions.

- **Alaska took away its own prison oversight in 2018, and 84 people have died in state custody since 2020.** The state created an internal investigative unit inside the Department of Corrections in 2016 after a report tied widespread failures to at least six deaths, then dissolved it in 2018 during budget cuts. The department reported a record-tying 18 in-custody deaths in 2025. No independent oversight body has been restored. This is the clearest case in the assessment of a state unlearning a lesson it had already paid for.

- **The state vetoes the things it says it cares about.** The administration cut Village Public Safety Officer recruitment funding to $15,775 of the $55,775 requested while citing officer vacancies as a problem. In June 2026 it vetoed $11.25 million in Medicaid provider payments, $6.4 million for child care workforce, plus early intervention, infant learning, and Head Start grants. The Legislature overrode education vetoes twice in 2025, by 46-14 and 45-14. Integrity scored 35.0 out of 100 — Alaska's weakest area.

- **Alaska finishes some jobs completely and abandons others entirely.** It cleared 3,059 backlogged sexual assault kits from a 2017 inventory down to 15 untested kits in 2025, with a 145-day average turnaround and a survivor-facing tracking portal. Over the same period, a 2025 legislative audit found that $20.7 million and 110 new positions "did not effectively increase services for Alaskan children," with 70% of experienced caseworkers still over the 2018 statutory caseload cap.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Systemic Thinking (65.0/100)** — the only dimension in the Established band. Alaska plans on a ten-year horizon with published targets, maps effects across adjacent systems, and has genuinely ceded authority to tribal organizations better positioned to deliver. Its governor also proposed a statewide sales tax and higher oil taxes in January 2026, a politically costly structural position in a state that abolished its income tax in 1980.
- **Equity (55.0/100)** — carried almost entirely by Historical Harm Acknowledgment (4/5). Statutory recognition of 229 tribes and a codified child welfare compact are concrete reparative actions co-developed with the harmed community, not statements.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Integrity (35.0/100)** — the gap between stated priorities and budget decisions is the defining pattern. Commitments to schools, child care, and rural public safety were cut under fiscal pressure and, in the education case, restored only by legislative override.
- **Action (40.0/100)** and **Boundaries (40.0/100)** — both Developing. Response standards exist in law and go unmet for years. People in acute need are turned away without alternatives: Alaska's only short-term adolescent residential mental health program closed in May 2025 with no state replacement.

## Evidence Gaps

- **Anticipatory Awareness (A5) scored 2/5 at low confidence.** No published pre-decision harm assessment process was located. This may reflect absence of public documentation rather than absence of practice. A Certified Assessment with institutional access could move this score.
- **Affective Resonance (E1) has no community testimony.** The 2/5 score rests on documented service failures — SNAP waits, emergency room boarding — not on direct accounts from Alaskans about whether they felt cared for. Tier 5 evidence is missing here.
- **Consent Orientation (B5)** rests on 2026 legislation that had not taken effect at the time of assessment. Its real-world effect is untested.
- **No independent audit of racial or regional disparity correction was found.** Alaska disaggregates data well but no third-party verification of corrective action was located, capping EQ3 and E3 at 3/5.
- **Municipal versus state attribution on homelessness is imperfect.** The most granular homelessness data covers Anchorage municipal programs. State-level responsibility could not be cleanly isolated, so this evidence was weighted lightly.

## Recommended Next Steps

Alaska sits in the **Functional** band. Systems exist but have significant gaps.

- Consider [Advisory Support](/advisory) to translate these benchmark findings into strategic action — particularly on the Integrity dimension, where the gap between stated priority and budget decision is the single largest lever available.
- The highest-value structural change identified by this assessment is restoring independent oversight of in-custody deaths. The state has already demonstrated it knows how; it dissolved the mechanism in 2018.
