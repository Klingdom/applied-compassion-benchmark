## Five-Year Trajectory, 2021–2026

Michigan improved sharply between 2021 and 2024, then stopped. The state gained roughly 14 points
in four years and has moved less than one point in the two years since.

**Direction: strong improvement 2021–2024, then flat with a slight decline 2025–2026.**

**2021 — the low point.** Michigan entered the period carrying unresolved Flint liability, a
collapsed unemployment system, and a child-welfare system under federal supervision since 2008. The
$626 million Flint settlement received court approval, and the COVID racial-disparities task force
was producing measurable gains, but structural change had not yet landed.

**2022 — early consolidation.** The task force published its report in March 2022 documenting
narrowing case and death disparities. MDHHS filed formal corrective action plans in April 2022 under
the child-welfare settlement. Detection improved faster than action.

**2023 — the largest single-year gain (+6.9 points).** Unified Democratic control produced the
densest compassion-relevant legislative year in modern Michigan history: Elliott-Larsen expanded to
sexual orientation and gender identity (March), extreme risk protection orders signed (22 May),
right-to-work repealed, the state EITC raised to 30%, and a law requiring 100% clean energy by 2040
(November). Equity and systemic thinking both rose. In the same year, however, the Michigan Supreme
Court's rejection of one-person grand jury indictments ended all Flint criminal prosecutions —
capping accountability gains.

**2024 — peak (52.5).** Universal free school meals, the Achievement Scholarship and the Community
College Guarantee took hold. In July 2024 *Mothering Justice v. Attorney General* found the
legislature's earlier adopt-and-amend manoeuvre on minimum wage and paid sick leave
unconstitutional, restoring worker protections the state had itself stripped.

**2025 — first decline (−0.6).** Three things reversed direction. FOIA expansion passed the Senate
33–2 on 29 January and was declared dead by the House Speaker the next day. The February 2025
minimum-wage and sick-leave compromise softened the reinstated ballot measures. And in September
2025 UIA resumed collections against roughly 350,000 pandemic-era claimants, months after settling
the very lawsuit about improper collections.

**2026 — flat, with new harm signals.** Positive: the FY27 budget preserved free meals and the
scholarship, and the Attorney General escalated litigation against federal immigration detention
expansion. Negative and serious: at least eight prisoner deaths between mid-March and mid-May 2026,
four investigated as homicides, including three deaths at the Women's Huron Valley Correctional
Facility. Auditor General findings on the 211 hotline and Medicaid contract oversight landed in the
same window. These offset one another; the composite holds at 51.9, but the risk profile worsened.

## Key Findings

- **Michigan finds problems well and fixes them slowly.** The state has strong detection
  infrastructure — a poverty task force, an environmental justice mapping tool, race-disaggregated
  health data, and an independent Auditor General that publishes damaging findings. But Awareness
  (65/100) outscores Action (50/100) by 15 points. The gap between knowing and doing is the single
  clearest pattern in this assessment.

- **Michigan effectively closed the racial gap in COVID-19 deaths, and third parties verified it.**
  Average new cases among Black residents fell from 176 per million per day in March 2020 to 59 per
  million per day by October 2020. Black death rates fell to 8.6 per 10,000 in 2022. Duke University
  and the National Governors Association both published case studies of the method. This is the
  strongest single piece of evidence in Michigan's file.

- **The state's own front door for help was giving people wrong information.** A June 2026 Auditor
  General audit found Michigan 211 — the hotline residents call for food, housing, health care and
  crisis help — failed database accuracy standards in 6 of 7 quarters reviewed and missed its
  90-second answer target in every quarter, taking 3 to 6 minutes instead.

- **Michigan's child welfare system has been under federal court supervision since 2008 and met only
  5 of 26 performance requirements as of January 2025.** In the first quarter of state fiscal year
  2025 it licensed 131 non-relative foster homes — 18.2% of its own goal — while 195 homes closed, a
  net loss of 64 homes.

- **Residents cannot file a public-records request with the Michigan governor or legislature.**
  Michigan is one of only two states with this exemption. Reform bills passed the Senate 33–2 on 29
  January 2025 and were declared dead by House Speaker Matt Hall the next day; the effort was still
  stalled in January 2026.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Awareness (65/100)** — Standing detection channels with regular review, a structural signal-
  amplification role in the Environmental Justice Public Advocate, and disaggregated data practice
  that has been externally documented as a national model.
- **Equity (60/100)** and **Systemic Thinking (60/100)** — Medicaid expansion covering more than 1
  in 4 residents, universal free school meals, tuition-free community college, and documented
  willingness to take legally risky public positions against federal immigration detention
  expansion.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Boundaries (40/100)** — Michigan's lowest score. A governor and legislature exempt from public
  records law, a referral system that was inaccurate most quarters, and thin evidence on consent
  practice.
- **Accountability (45/100)** and **Integrity (45/100)** — Harm is acknowledged after external
  establishment rather than before it, repair is litigated and slow, and internal conditions in
  state-run facilities fall well short of the state's external message.

## Evidence Gaps

- **No NAEP or state education outcome data was retrievable** within this session's research budget.
  Education efficacy (AC3) is therefore scored on health and anti-poverty evidence alone.
- **No maternal or infant mortality disparity data was retrievable.** EQ3 rests on COVID-era
  evidence, which is now three to six years old. A current-decade disparity measure could move EQ3
  in either direction.
- **B5 Consent Orientation is scored at low confidence.** No independent review of Michigan state
  consent processes was located; the score of 2 reflects absence of evidence plus one negative
  signal, per the conservative default rule.
- **A5 Anticipatory Awareness is scored at low confidence.** MiEJScreen's actual use in pre-decision
  screening is documented as a capability, not as a requirement with recorded outcomes.
- **Prison death reporting relies partly on advocacy-press sources.** The Bridge Michigan reporting
  is independent and reliable; the World Socialist Web Site accounts are partisan and were used only
  for details corroborated elsewhere (the class action, the mold findings in Judge Murphy's June
  2025 order, MDOC vacancy counts from legislative reports).

## Federal Interaction Note

Per assessment scope, Michigan is scored on its own conduct. Two federal interactions bear on the
reading but were not scored as state conduct:

- **H.R. 1, signed 4 July 2025,** imposes twice-yearly Medicaid renewals effective 1 January 2027,
  with a first Michigan cohort facing a 31 March deadline. Coverage losses that follow are federally
  driven. Michigan's *handling* of those redeterminations will be scoreable in the next cycle.
- **ICE arrests in Michigan rose from 952 in 2024 to 3,298 in 2025,** with 1,075 more in the first
  half of March 2026 alone. This is federal action. Michigan's own conduct — the Attorney General's
  reporting form, the Romulus lawsuit, and the fact that only about 7 of roughly 575 Michigan law
  enforcement agencies hold 287(g) agreements — is scored positively under A4 and S4.

## Confidence Statement

**Overall confidence: medium-high.**

Thirty-two of forty subdimensions rest on Tier 3 or better evidence, including nine on Tier 5
sources (federal court monitor reports, Office of the Auditor General performance audits, the Duke
and NGA independent case studies, and the University of Michigan Medicaid evaluation). Eight
subdimensions are marked medium-low or low confidence and are flagged in the Evidence Gaps section.
Michigan is unusually well-documented for a US state because it operates under an active federal
consent decree and maintains a genuinely independent Auditor General — both of which produce
adversarial, verifiable evidence that most states do not generate.

The five-year trajectory carries **medium** confidence. Year-by-year dimension scores are
reconstructed from dated events rather than from contemporaneous assessments, so individual annual
figures should be read as directionally reliable rather than precise.
