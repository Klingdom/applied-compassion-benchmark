## This run supersedes a degraded assessment

This assessment replaces an earlier run of the same date that returned a composite of **51.9**. That
run hit its search budget before it finished and degraded in three specific ways:

1. **EQ3 (Bias Awareness) rested on COVID-era data that was three to six years old.** The prior run
   scored Michigan's bias awareness entirely on the 2020–2022 Coronavirus Task Force on Racial
   Disparities. No current-decade disparity measure was retrieved.
2. **B5 (Consent Orientation) was scored 2 at low confidence on the absence of evidence.** No search
   had located Michigan's actual consent policy framework.
3. **A5 (Anticipatory Awareness) was scored 3 at low confidence.** MiEJScreen was described as a
   capability with no documented use in real decisions.

All three were re-researched independently in this run against primary sources. Two other
corrections follow from the re-run:

- **A factual error was removed.** The prior run stated that four Michigan prisoner deaths were
  "investigated as homicides." Primary reporting does not support this. Of the four deaths at the
  Women's Huron Valley Correctional Facility between 13 May and 2 July 2026, the released causes are
  a pulmonary blood clot, a suicide, and a cardiac arrest. That claim traced to a partisan source and
  has been struck.
- **A large program was missed entirely.** The prior run did not find **Rx Kids**, Michigan's
  $270 million prenatal and infant cash program. It is the single most consequential piece of
  evidence in Michigan's file and it moved four subdimension scores.

**Net effect: composite rises from 51.9 to 53.8.** Three dimensions changed: EQU 3.4 → 3.6,
BND 2.6 → 2.8, SYS 3.4 → 3.6. The band is unchanged at Functional.

**On the Boundaries penalty specifically:** the task asked whether Michigan's BND 2.6 was correctly
sized. It was **slightly oversized**, for two reasons, and is corrected to 2.8. See
[The Boundaries penalty, re-examined](#the-boundaries-penalty-re-examined) below.

#### The Boundaries penalty, re-examined

The task asked whether Michigan's BND penalty was correctly sized and correctly evidenced. It was
**slightly oversized**. Two problems, both now corrected.

**Problem 1: one fact was penalised twice.** The prior run made the FOIA exemption the primary
evidence for B3 (Scope Clarity) *and* a primary driver of AB3 (Transparency). Public-records law is a
transparency fact. B3 asks a different question: does the entity tell the people it serves what it
can and cannot do? Michigan fails B3 on its own service evidence — the 211 material condition — and
that is now the primary citation. FOIA remains as context, not as the load-bearing fact.

**Problem 2: B5 was scored on absence of evidence.** The prior run gave B5 a 2 at low confidence
because no consent policy was found. One targeted search found MDHHS Administrative Policy API 181
and the statutory Mental Health Code recipient-rights framework. The evidence existed; the prior run
ran out of budget before reaching it. That is a search failure, not a Michigan failure, and scoring a
state down for the assessor's incomplete search is a methodology violation. B5 corrects to 3.

**The FOIA finding itself is verified, and is more severe for Michigan than the framing suggests.**
Michigan and Massachusetts are indeed the only two states exempting both governor and legislature.
But the two are not equivalent. **Michigan is the only state in the country with a blanket
*statutory* exemption for the governor and executive office staff.** In Massachusetts the exemption
rests on a state high-court ruling that the public records law does not explicitly list the governor
— a judicial interpretation, reversible by legislation without repealing an exemption. Michigan's is
written into the statute.

The reform history is also worse than a simple exemption. Senate Bills 1 and 2 were the first two
bills introduced in the 2025-26 Senate and passed **33–2**. House Speaker Matt Hall declared them
dead the next day, calling them "a very low priority." As of March 2026 they had sat in the House
Government Operations Committee for over a year, and a senator was proposing a ballot measure to
bypass the Legislature entirely. Michigan is not a state that has failed to get to this; it is a
state where one chamber's leadership actively stopped it after the other chamber voted 33–2 for it.

**Cross-state consistency flag for the reviewer.** If Massachusetts is published at 94.4 with a high
BND score while carrying the *weaker* version of the same exemption, then either the Massachusetts
BND score is wrong or Michigan's is. On the methodology, Michigan's statutory blanket exemption is
the more severe fact. This should be resolved when Massachusetts is re-assessed at full depth. It is
flagged here rather than resolved because the published Massachusetts figure comes from the corrupt
legacy index and is not a valid anchor.

Michigan improved sharply between 2021 and 2024, then largely plateaued, with a modest 2026 uptick

**Direction: strong improvement 2021–2024, then flat.** Michigan gained about 15 points in four
years and has moved less than one point in the two since.

**2021 — the low point.** Michigan entered the period carrying unresolved Flint liability, a
collapsed unemployment system, and a child-welfare system under federal supervision since 2008. The
$626 million Flint settlement received court approval and MDHHS launched Healthy Moms, Healthy
Babies, but structural change had not yet landed.

**2022 — early consolidation.** MDHHS filed formal corrective action plans in April 2022 under the
child-welfare settlement. Detection improved faster than action.

**2023 — the largest single-year gain (+6.9 points).** Unified Democratic control produced the
densest compassion-relevant legislative year in modern Michigan history: Elliott-Larsen expanded to
sexual orientation and gender identity (March), extreme risk protection orders signed (22 May),
right-to-work repealed, the state EITC raised to 30%, and a law requiring 100% clean energy by 2040.
In the same year the Michigan Supreme Court's rejection of one-person grand jury indictments ended
all Flint criminal prosecutions, capping accountability gains.

**2024 — peak (54.4).** Universal free school meals, the Achievement Scholarship and the Community
College Guarantee took hold. Rx Kids launched in Flint. In September 2024 the state settled a civil
rights complaint over a Detroit hazardous waste facility, committing EGLE to environmental justice
and cumulative impact analysis in hazardous waste licensing.

**2025 — first decline (−0.6).** FOIA expansion passed the Senate 33–2 on 29 January and was
declared dead by the House Speaker the next day. In September 2025 UIA resumed collections against
roughly 350,000 pandemic-era claimants, months after settling the lawsuit about improper collections.
In January 2025 NAEP results placed Michigan 44th in fourth-grade reading.

**2026 — flat, with sharply divergent signals.** Positive: Rx Kids expanded to the entire Upper
Peninsula and Detroit and held $270 million through a hostile June hearing; the FY2027 budget
preserved free meals; MDHHS publicly disclosed a projected 500,000-person Medicaid coverage risk.
Negative and serious: four deaths at the Women's Huron Valley Correctional Facility between 13 May
and 2 July; Auditor General findings on the 211 hotline, Medicaid contract oversight and the school
services program. These offset; the composite holds at 53.8 but the risk profile worsened.

## Key Findings

- **Michigan finds problems well and fixes them slowly.** The state has strong detection
  infrastructure — a poverty task force, an environmental justice mapping tool, race-disaggregated
  health data, and an Auditor General that publishes damaging findings about the state itself. But
  Awareness scores 65 of 100 while Action scores 50. That 15-point gap between knowing and doing is
  the clearest single pattern in this file.

- **Michigan pays families cash during pregnancy, and it is working.** Rx Kids gives $1,500 during
  pregnancy and $500 a month for a baby's first year, with no income test. It has reached more than
  13,000 families in over 35 communities. Independent research found fewer premature births, fewer
  low-birthweight babies and fewer NICU admissions. Infant maltreatment allegations in Flint dropped
  after launch. The state put $270 million behind it and kept that money in the July 2026 budget one
  month after legislators attacked the program in a public hearing.

- **The state's own front door for help was giving people wrong information.** Michigan 211 is the
  number residents call for food, housing, health care and crisis help — about 576,000 contacts a
  year. The Auditor General found the database met its 90% accuracy standard in only one of seven
  quarters reviewed, and classified the failure a "material condition," its most serious category.
  People were sent to services that were not there.

- **Four women died in a Michigan prison in seven weeks.** Between 13 May and 2 July 2026, four
  people died at the Women's Huron Valley Correctional Facility. One was days from release. One death
  was ruled a suicide. The facility faces two federal lawsuits, one alleging toxic mould. Two US
  senators asked the federal Department of Justice to investigate. This is the largest single reason
  Michigan's Integrity score sits at 45 of 100.

- **Michigan is the only state with a blanket statutory exemption shielding its governor from public
  records law.** Residents cannot file a Freedom of Information Act request with the governor or the
  Legislature. Reform bills passed the Senate 33–2 in January 2025. The House Speaker declared them
  dead the next day, calling them "a very low priority." They were still stuck in committee in March
  2026.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Awareness, Equity and Systemic Thinking (all 65/100).** Michigan detects need through standing,
  reviewed channels; it covers about 2.5 million people on Medicaid and 1.4 million students with
  free meals; and it funds upstream work that has measurably reduced downstream harm.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Boundaries, Accountability and Integrity (all 45/100).** Michigan acknowledges harm after outside
  parties establish it rather than before, repairs slowly and through litigation, keeps its own
  executive beyond public-records reach, and runs custodial facilities whose conditions contradict
  its public message.

## Evidence Gaps

- **No community testimony was located for I1 or I2.** Both are capped at 3 because anchors 4 and 5
  require the affected community to describe trust or care directly. Desk research cannot supply it.
- **B2 lacks longitudinal self-sufficiency outcomes.** Michigan Reconnect's first formal completion
  calculation is due Spring 2026 and was not published at assessment time. The 12.5% credential rate
  is a program-reported figure, not an independent evaluation.
- **AC3 has no documented program discontinuation.** Anchor 4 requires a program stopped because data
  showed it did not work. None was found, which caps AC3 at 3 despite strong evaluation practice.
- **Rx Kids evaluation independence is partial.** The child-welfare-involvement study is a preprint
  (arXiv) and the program is evaluated by the universities that run it. Outcomes are corroborated by
  multiple sources but this is not a fully independent audit, so it does not carry Tier 5 weight.
- **The prior run's prison-homicide claim was struck as unsupported.** See the supersession note.

## Federal Interaction Note

Per assessment scope, Michigan is scored on its own conduct.

- **Federal H.R. 1** brings six-month Medicaid renewals and work requirements from 1 October 2026,
  with new work requirements for Healthy Michigan Plan enrollees aged 19–64 from 1 January 2027.
  MDHHS projects more than 500,000 Michiganders could lose coverage. Those losses are federally
  driven. Michigan's *handling* of the redeterminations is scoreable next cycle; its early public
  disclosure of the projected harm is scored positively here under AB3 and I2.

## Confidence Statement

**Overall confidence: medium-high**, and higher than the superseded run.

The three subdimensions flagged as degraded were re-researched against primary sources and all three
improved in confidence: EQ3 from medium to high (now resting on MDHHS data published June 2025 rather
than 2020–2022 COVID data), B5 from low to medium (a formal consent policy effective 1 October 2025
was located), and A5 from low to medium (a settlement-mandated pre-decision analysis requirement and
a published federal-impact projection were located).

Michigan is unusually well documented for a US state because it operates under an active federal
consent decree and maintains a genuinely independent Auditor General. Both produce adversarial,
verifiable evidence that most states never generate. That cuts both ways: Michigan looks worse than
comparably performing states partly because it publishes more of its own failures. This assessment
does not adjust for that, and reviewers should be alert to it as a systematic bias against
transparent jurisdictions.

The five-year trajectory carries **medium** confidence. Year-by-year dimension scores are
reconstructed from dated events rather than contemporaneous assessments, so annual figures are
directionally reliable rather than precise.

**Search budget:** 20 web searches and 4 direct fetches. The run completed within budget. No
degradation occurred and no gaps were filled by inference.
