## Assessment Context

HCA Healthcare was surfaced by the 2026-07-20 scan on a securities-fraud investigation announcement following a sharp cut to full-year 2026 guidance on July 14. That trigger is a weak compassion signal on its own — a law-firm investigation notice is not a finding. The material evidence sits underneath it. HCA is the largest for-profit hospital operator in the United States, and the assessment draws primarily on the sustained, independently documented record at Mission Health in Asheville, North Carolina, where the North Carolina Attorney General is suing HCA over degraded services after its 2019 acquisition.

The assessed composite of 25 is 12.5 points below the published 37.5. Every dimension moved down. The largest gaps are in Accountability (published 2.5 raw, assessed 1.4) and Integrity (published 2.5, assessed 1.6). The published placeholder implied an institution with functioning harm-acknowledgment and internally consistent values. The evidence does not support that: HCA's Mission subsidiary publicly dismissed independent academic research as "neither serious nor impartial," and its own 2026 Impact Report discloses no unmet goals or shortcomings of any kind. Action (published 3.0, assessed 2.8) moved least, because HCA's $4.5 billion in uncompensated care and $40 million in tuition assistance are real, large, and verifiable.

## Key Findings

- HCA cut staff at a hospital it had just bought, and independent researchers say patients paid for it. Court filings show HCA arrived with plans to cut 800 positions at Mission Health, and two-thirds of Mission's 750 physicians left after the acquisition.
- The company dismissed the independent research rather than engaging it. A Mission spokesperson called the Wake Forest University study "neither serious nor impartial," which is why Accountability scores near the floor.
- HCA delivers large amounts of real help. It provided about $4.5 billion in charity care and other uncompensated care in 2025, plus $40 million in tuition assistance for staff. This is why Action remains the strongest dimension.
- HCA's own impact report admits nothing. The 2026 Impact Report contains no mention of unmet goals or shortcomings, which is the behavioral marker of reputation-driven rather than genuine transparency.
- The July 2026 guidance cut is a coverage-loss story, not a compassion failure. HCA lost $400 million in one quarter because patients lost health-exchange coverage and became uninsured. HCA detected and disclosed this quickly, which supports its Awareness score.

## Strongest Dimensions

Action (raw 2.8) and Systemic Thinking (raw 2.6). HCA mobilizes genuinely large resources — roughly $4.5 billion in uncompensated care in 2025 — and invests upstream in the clinical workforce pipeline through more than $40 million a year in tuition assistance and student loan support. These are not gestures; they are structural and sustained.

## Weakest Dimensions

Accountability (raw 1.4) and Integrity (raw 1.6). HCA acknowledges harm only under legal compulsion, dismissed independent research into patient-care outcomes, and publishes an impact report with no disclosed shortfalls. Internal Consistency (I3) scores 1 of 5: nurses report being silenced and two-thirds of physicians at one flagship hospital left.

## Evidence Gaps

The strongest evidence is concentrated on one facility group (Mission Health, North Carolina). HCA operates roughly 190 hospitals; I could not verify whether the Mission pattern is representative or an outlier, and this is the single largest limitation on confidence. HCA's system-wide CMS quality-star distribution and disaggregated outcome data by patient race, insurance status, or income were not obtainable within this assessment. No employee-review aggregate (Glassdoor/Blind) was verified. Equity Bias Awareness (EQ3) is scored 1 on absence of evidence rather than on positive evidence of failure.

## Calibration Note

HCA Healthcare has never been individually assessed; the published 37.5 is a bulk-import placeholder assigned without a 40-subdimension review. The documented scrutiny bias applies directly: adversarial review finds flaws that batch assignment never looked for, so a downward move on first assessment is expected and does NOT by itself represent a decline in HCA's conduct. What makes this proposal defensible is not the size of the gap but the independence and weight of the sources: a North Carolina Attorney General lawsuit, a Wake Forest University working paper, court filings in an antitrust case, and a federal court ruling upholding nurses' right to strike over chronic understaffing. The proposal should be read as "the placeholder was too generous," not "HCA got worse in July 2026." Reviewers should calibrate against other for-profit hospital operators before applying.
