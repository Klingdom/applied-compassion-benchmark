## Key Findings

- **Georgia responds to crises it is forced to confront, and not to ones it can avoid.** After the US Justice Department found Eighth Amendment violations across 17 prisons in October 2024, Georgia committed over $600 million to corrections in about 90 days. It has not made a comparable move on health coverage, where it ranks 49th of 50 states on adult uninsurance at 12.0%. The gap between those two responses is the single clearest pattern in this assessment.

- **Georgia punished the people who documented preventable deaths.** In November 2024, after ProPublica published the state committee's own conclusion that Amber Thurman's death was likely preventable, Georgia dismissed all 32 members of its Maternal Mortality Review Committee. As of March 2025 the state would not say who replaced them. This is why Empathy scores 20 of 100 — near the bottom of the scale.

- **The state stopped counting how people die in its prisons.** Beginning with the March 2025 report, the Georgia Department of Corrections no longer publishes manner of death. In 2025 it reported 301 deaths but identified only 295. Six people died in state custody with no name, facility or cause ever disclosed.

- **Georgia's one strong reform is a mental health law, and it is real.** HB 1013, signed 2022-04-04, requires insurers to cover mental health care on par with physical health, and it created a public complaint repository with annual reporting. It has continued through administrative channels with no publicity benefit — the best available evidence that some Georgia compassion practice is genuine rather than performed.

- **Money went to tax relief while child welfare ran a shortfall.** Georgia signed a $2 billion income and property tax relief package on 2026-03-03. In the same budget cycle, its child welfare agency faced a projected $85.7 million shortfall and a $15.3 million pilot supporting students in poverty was cut for FY2027.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Awareness (35/100) and Action (35/100)** are Georgia's highest scores, and both are modest. Awareness is carried by two genuine structures: the Office of the Child Advocate, an independent statutory investigator, and the Department of Audits and Accounts, which publishes findings unflattering to the state. Action is carried by fast disaster response — $100 million redirected to Helene farm relief by 2024-11-01 — and by the corrections funding package.
- **Accountability, Boundaries and Systemic Thinking all sit at 30/100**, each earning partial credit from a single strong item: the Guidehouse-driven systemic prison changes, the corrections staffing and pay intervention, and the HB 1013 parity regime respectively.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Equity (15/100)** is Georgia's floor. The state has chosen for over a decade not to close a coverage gap it can measure, ranks 45th of 51 on overall health system performance, and faces federal-court evidence that its voting laws produce a 25-percentage-point racial gap in mail ballot application rejections.
- **Empathy (20/100) and Integrity (20/100)** are dragged down by the same behavior seen from two angles: when Georgia's own review bodies produced inconvenient findings, the state removed the reviewers and restricted the data rather than acting on the conclusions.

## Evidence Gaps

- **B5 Consent Orientation** and **S5 Coalitional Compassion** rest on thin evidence. Both are marked low confidence. Georgia publishes little on informed-consent practice in state services, and no systematic record was found of the state ceding resources or leadership to partner organizations.
- **No community testimony (Tier 5) was available** for most subdimensions. Scoring relies mainly on Tier 4 evidence — federal investigative findings, state budget documents, published audits — and Tier 3 policy documents. Where only Tier 2 evidence existed, the lower anchor was applied.
- **Post-$600M outcome data does not yet exist.** As of December 2025 legislators were still asking whether the investment made prisons safer. AC5 and AB2 will need re-scoring once outcome data is published.
- **The DOJ investigation's status is unresolved.** No consent decree has been reached as of mid-2026 and federal enforcement priorities have shifted. If the investigation closes without a decree, the external pressure driving Georgia's corrections improvements disappears — a material downside risk to ACT and ACC.

## Confidence Level

**Overall confidence: MEDIUM-HIGH.** Thirty-two of forty subdimensions rest on Tier 3 or Tier 4 dated public evidence, including a 93-page federal investigative report, published state budget documents, court filings and independent audits. Eight subdimensions (A2, B2, B4, B5, S2, S5, I1, I5) carry medium or low confidence and are flagged individually in the sidecar file.

## Recommended Next Steps

Georgia scores in the **Developing** band. Foundational structures exist in places — statutory parity, an independent child advocate, a functioning audit office — but they are inconsistent, reactive, and in two documented cases were dismantled when they produced inconvenient findings.

- Consider a [Certified Assessment](/certified-assessments) for a structured improvement roadmap, prioritising: (1) restoring published mortality reporting in state custody; (2) reinstating transparent, named membership on the Maternal Mortality Review Committee; (3) publishing outcome data against the $600M corrections investment.
