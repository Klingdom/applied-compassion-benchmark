## Attribution note: state conduct vs. federal policy

Arizona sits on an international border, and border policy is mostly federal. This assessment scores only what Arizona itself chose to do. Proposition 314, the Secure the Border Act, is scored because Arizona voters wrote it into state law and Arizona agencies are implementing it. Federal law H.R. 1 and its Medicaid eligibility cuts are **not** scored against Arizona. What is scored is Arizona's own budget response to H.R. 1, which was to fund extra eligibility workers rather than let paperwork strip coverage.

## Key Findings

- **Arizona's biggest failure is that it fought accountability rather than accepting it.** A federal judge took control of medical care in all nine Arizona state prisons on 19 February 2026, after a lawsuit filed in March 2012, two contempt findings, and more than $2.5 million in fines. Arizona appealed that takeover in March 2026. The judge finalised it on 17 July 2026 and installed a receiver paid $500,000 a year with power to hire, fire, and set the health budget.

- **The state ignored fraud warnings for four years, and roughly $2.8 billion was billed for treatment that often did not happen.** Arizona's Medicaid agency received whistleblower reports in July 2019 and went public in May 2023. A class action represents about 7,000 Native American victims, up to 2,000 of them dead or missing. Arizona has asked the court to dismiss the case.

- **Arizona repaired one historic harm well and is still refusing to repair another.** It signed the Northeastern Arizona Indian Water Rights Settlement on 19 November 2024 for the Navajo Nation, Hopi Tribe, and San Juan Southern Paiute Tribe, authorising $5.1 billion in water infrastructure where about one in three homes lacks running water. Against that, its compensation fund for fraud victims is $6 million — about 0.2% of the amount fraudulently billed.

- **Money goes where politics points, not where need is.** Arizona spent about $1 billion on school vouchers in 2025-26 that state auditors called "haphazard" to oversee, sent $115 million of opioid settlement money to prison operations over the Attorney General's objection, and closed a $122 million disability shortfall by taking $38 million from the Housing Trust Fund.

- **Divided government produced Arizona's only genuinely strong area: working with others.** Arizona offered to cut its own Colorado River water allocation by 27% — the largest offer of any state — and splits its $1.194 billion opioid settlement across 15 counties and 91 cities. Systemic Thinking at 50.0 is the state's only dimension above the Developing band.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Systemic Thinking (50.0)** is the only dimension in the Functional band. Arizona plans on long horizons for water, works across seven states and 22 tribal nations, and has taken costly public positions.
- **Awareness, Equity, and Accountability (all 40.0)** are held up by genuinely independent institutions: the Arizona Auditor General publishes unflattering findings on the state's own flagship programs, and health agencies publish disaggregated disparity data.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Boundaries (25.0)** is the lowest. When Arizona stops helping, it does so without arranging alternatives — people were displaced from suspended treatment facilities onto the street, and shelter capacity is far below need. Frontline staffing is chronically depleted, with only 15% of child-safety workers reaching five years.
- **Empathy (30.0) and Integrity (30.0)** are dragged down by the state's legal posture. Arizona's default response to people it has harmed is to contest liability first. Its compassionate policies also depend heavily on who holds office rather than on durable rules.

## Evidence Gaps

- The Auditor General's September 2025 report on child-abuse investigations (25-109) could not be read directly; it is a scanned PDF that resisted text extraction. Child-welfare subdimensions were therefore scored on secondary reporting and are marked medium or low confidence.
- No independent statewide assessment of Arizona employee wellbeing or burnout exists. B1 and I3 rely on turnover and vacancy data as a proxy.
- No independent audit of consent practices in Arizona's behavioural health system was found. B5 is low confidence.
- Community testimony was available for tribal and disability populations but not for most others. Affective Resonance (E1) is low confidence.
- The class action over the fraud scheme has not been decided in court. No finding of state legal liability exists; scoring reflects the state's conduct, not a court's verdict.

## Recommended Next Steps

Arizona sits in the **Developing** band. The most direct improvements are structural rather than budgetary:

1. **Stop contesting first.** Arizona's Empathy and Accountability scores would move materially if the state acknowledged harm before litigating it — starting with the fraud victims' case and the prison health receivership.
2. **Build an exit protocol.** The single lowest score in this assessment is Refusal Ethics (1/5). No one displaced by an enforcement action against a provider should be left without an arranged alternative.
3. **Match repair to harm.** A $6 million fund against $2.8 billion in fraudulent billing will not be considered adequate by the affected tribes, and community adequacy is what the anchor requires.
4. **Put practices in statute, not executive order.** Resilience of Care scored 2/5 because Arizona's direction reverses at each change of administration.
