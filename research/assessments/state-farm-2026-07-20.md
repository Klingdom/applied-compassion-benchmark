## Assessment Context

State Farm was surfaced by the 2026-07-20 scan on an Oklahoma judge's 2026-07-07 order compelling it to release internal claims documents in litigation that now spans roughly 900 similar suits statewide. The assessed composite of 13.8 is 47.7 points below the published 60.9, crossing two bands from Established to Critical. Accountability collapses from a published 3.5 raw to 1 — every one of its five subdimensions scores 1 of 5, because State Farm contested disclosure of the relevant internal documents as trade secrets and the same claims-suppression pattern recurs across two states and two different perils. Boundaries falls from 3.5 to 1.4, driven by Scope Clarity, Refusal Ethics and Consent Orientation all scoring 1: the Oklahoma complaint alleges State Farm sold replacement-cost coverage while applying undisclosed internal standards that appear nowhere in the policy. Action falls from 3.5 to 1.8 on the regulator's findings of slow investigations, missed 40-day statutory deadlines and unreasonably low settlement offers.

## Key Findings

- California's insurance regulator did not allege wrongdoing — it found it. Examiners reviewed 220 wildfire claims and found 398 violations of state law in 114 of them, roughly half the sample.
- The regulator's language is unusually direct. Commissioner Ricardo Lara said State Farm "delayed, underpaid, and buried policyholders in red tape at the worst moment of their lives."
- A second state alleges the underpayment was designed, not accidental. Oklahoma's Attorney General says State Farm ran a "Hail Focus Initiative" built to reduce roof replacement approvals, and is suing under the state's racketeering statute.
- The pattern spans two states and two different disasters. Wildfire smoke claims in California and hail claims in Oklahoma produced the same behaviour, which is why Systemic Learning scores 1 out of 5.
- State Farm fought to keep the evidence sealed. An Oklahoma judge rejected its argument that internal claims standards were trade secrets and ordered them released, which is the opposite of voluntary transparency.

## Strongest Dimensions

Action Resource Mobilization (4 of 5 is not reached; AC4 scores 3) and Equity Universality (EQ1, 3 of 5) are the only subdimensions above 2. State Farm remains the largest home insurer in California with roughly a million home customers and continues to pay very large volumes of legitimate claims. Scale and continued market presence are genuine, and they are what keeps the score from falling further.

## Weakest Dimensions

Accountability (raw 1) is at the absolute floor — 1.0, with all five subdimensions at 1 of 5. Boundaries (raw 1.4) and Equity (raw 1.6) follow. The recurring theme is a gap between what was sold and what was paid, maintained across states, and defended rather than acknowledged.

## Evidence Gaps

The Oklahoma allegations are unproven; no court has ruled on the merits, and this assessment weights them as corroborating rather than determinative. State Farm's own response to the California Accusation was not obtained, which is a real limitation — the company is entitled to contest the findings and may prevail on some. No evidence was found either way about State Farm's treatment of its own employees and agents, so Self-Sustainability and Internal Consistency are scored 2 on absence rather than on findings. Auto and life insurance lines, which represent a large share of the business, are essentially unexamined here; the entire assessment rests on property claims.

## Calibration Note

MAGNITUDE WARNING — REVIEWER MUST CALIBRATE BEFORE APPLYING. This is a -47.7 point move and a two-band drop (Established to Critical). Two things inflate it and a reviewer should separate them. First, State Farm has never been individually assessed; its published 60.9 is a bulk-import placeholder assigning a near-flat 3.5. Part of the gap is the documented scrutiny bias, not conduct. Second, and unlike the other Fortune 500 entities assessed tonight, the core evidence here is NOT an unproven allegation: the California Department of Insurance completed a Market Conduct Examination and FOUND 398 violations of state law in 114 of 220 sampled claims. That is a tier-5 regulatory determination about the company's core function. The Oklahoma Attorney General's RICO and consumer-protection suit remains unproven and is weighted as an allegation, though it is corroborated by roughly 900 private suits and by a judge rejecting State Farm's trade-secret argument for sealing internal claims documents. Even so, a computed 13.1 places State Farm alongside private-prison and defence-component operators, which is very likely too harsh in peer terms. The recommendation is a genuine downgrade, but reviewers should consider a peer-calibrated landing zone in the Developing band rather than applying the raw computed figure.
