**BND (+5.0) essentially confirms the placeholder.** This is the one dimension where a floor-level guess happened to land near the evidence. Retroactive contract terminations, a seven-year state employee pay freeze, and corrections staff absorbing 48% of all state overtime all point the same way.

### Recommendation

The published 23.4 is **understated and evidentially void**. It should be replaced with 41.9 (Functional). The correction must be published as a **data-defect fix, not as improvement**. Oklahoma did not gain 18.5 points; the placeholder never measured anything. Rank must remain null until the us-states index is rebuilt with all 51 jurisdictions.

## Key Findings

- **Oklahoma's voters, not its Legislature, produced the state's single best equity outcome.** Oklahoma voters wrote Medicaid expansion into the state constitution through State Question 802 on 2020-06-30, after lawmakers refused to act. Coverage began in July 2021. More than 330,000 people have gained it. Lawmakers tried to strip the guarantee out of the constitution in 2026 and failed. This mirrors Missouri's sequence almost exactly.

- **The disability waitlist is the clearest case of a state fixing something it had ignored for 13 years.** In May 2022 the Legislature put $32.5 million toward a backlog of more than 5,000 people waiting for developmental disability services. The wait fell from 13 years to about two, and then to roughly one. Nothing else in this assessment scores as cleanly.

- **How Oklahoma treats a court ruling that limits its own power is its sharpest integrity test, and it is failing.** Five years after McGirt v. Oklahoma restored tribal criminal jurisdiction, the Governor used his final State of the State address on 2026-02-03 to argue state law "should apply to every Oklahoman without exception." He sued his own Legislature over tribal compacts and lost 9-0. The Attorney General took the opposite side and won.

- **Oklahoma acknowledges harm but does not repair it.** The Attorney General told the US Supreme Court that state prosecutors had violated Richard Glossip's rights, which is rare institutional honesty. Oklahoma then re-prosecuted him, and he remained jailed into 2026. On 2024-06-12 the Oklahoma Supreme Court dismissed the Tulsa Race Massacre survivors' suit 8-1, ruling that "relief is not possible under any set of facts." No state repair program followed.

- **The people delivering Oklahoma's care are running on empty.** State employees have had no general pay raise since 2019. A worker earning $50,000 in 2019 had lost 26.39% of buying power by 2025. Corrections staff earned $35.57 million in overtime in 2025 — about 48% of all overtime paid to every state employee. State auditors found mental health agency staff were made to sign nondisclosure agreements and discouraged from talking to investigators.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Action (55/100)** — the disability waitlist reduction and the completed 13-year child welfare turnaround are both measured, published, and independently verified.
- **Awareness (50/100)** — an independently elected State Auditor and a permanent fiscal transparency office both found serious problems in 2025 and both were acted on.
- **Accountability and Systemic Thinking (45/100 each)** — driven almost entirely by an Attorney General willing to take positions that cost him politically.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Boundaries (30/100)** — the state withdrew funding commitments from mental health providers after those providers had already delivered services, and it has underpaid its own workforce for seven years.
- **Empathy (35/100)** and **Integrity (35/100)** — the state jails women at a higher rate than nearly any country on earth, ranks 46th in racial health equity, refused a low-cost federal food benefit for 403,000 children two years running, and maintains an adversarial posture toward its tribal nations.
