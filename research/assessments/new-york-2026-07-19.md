# Compassion Benchmark Assessment: New York

**Entity type:** State
**Sector/Domain:** Government (US State)
**Assessment date:** 2026-07-19
**Composite score:** 56.9/100
**Band:** Functional

## Why it matters (plain language)

New York spends more on helping people than almost any other US state. Its $254 billion budget for the 2026 fiscal year covers universal free school meals for 2.7 million students, health insurance for 1.7 million people through the Essential Plan, and a legal promise to cut child poverty in half by 2031. Those are real commitments, and the state measures whether they work.

But New York fails badly at owning its own harm. State prison officers beat Robert Brooks to death at Marcy Correctional Facility on December 10, 2024. Officers beat Messiah Nantwi to death at Mid-State Correctional Facility on March 1, 2025 — less than three months later. In 2025, the state's prison agency confirmed fewer than 50 of roughly 1,200 excessive-force complaints, about 4.2%. Independent arbitrators gave fired staff their jobs back in every case.

That gap — strong help, weak accountability — is why New York lands at 56.9 out of 100. It scores in the Functional band: the systems exist, but they have serious holes.

### Year-by-year drivers

**2021 — floor year (46.3).** Accountability bottoms out at 1.8. The Attorney General's January 2021 report found nursing home resident deaths may have been undercounted by as much as 50%. In February 2021 a senior aide privately admitted the administration withheld the death toll. Aides had rewritten a health department report to omit 9,250 deaths. Governor Cuomo resigned in August 2021. Offsetting: the $2.1 billion Excluded Workers Fund (April 2021), the Marijuana Regulation and Taxation Act (March 2021), the HALT Solitary Confinement Act, and the Child Poverty Reduction Act (December 2, 2021).

**2022 — stabilization (51.3).** Comptroller DiNapoli's March 2022 audit confirmed a 4,071-death undercount — painful but a genuine transparency act, lifting ACC off the floor. HALT took effect. The Buffalo Tops supermarket shooting (May 2022) prompted red flag law expansion. Systemic and equity structures from 2021 began operating.

**2023 — build-out (57.5).** The Clean Slate Act was signed in November 2023. The $1 billion multi-year mental health plan launched. The Community Commission on Reparations Remedies was established in December 2023. Asylum-seeker response scaled up. Early warning signs appeared in the cannabis equity program.

**2024 — peak (59.4).** The Essential Plan 1332 waiver expansion was federally approved March 1, 2024, adding coverage for roughly 450,000 people. Good Cause Eviction took effect April 20, 2024. Proposition 1, the Equal Rights Amendment, passed in November 2024. Overdose deaths fell 32% year over year. Offsetting and foreshadowing the reversal: the Inspector General's August 2024 HALT report, the Comptroller's July 2024 maternal mortality audit, the shutdown of the Cannabis Social Equity Investment Fund, and the killing of Robert Brooks on December 10, 2024.

**2025 — sharp reversal (53.8, −5.6).** The worst year since 2021. Corrections officers began an illegal strike February 17, 2025; the Governor declared a disaster emergency February 19 and activated the National Guard. Messiah Nantwi was killed March 1, 2025. The state suspended HALT, which a court ruled in July 2025 violated separation of powers. The CDPAP transition to a single contractor disrupted care for roughly 250,000 disabled and older New Yorkers and required a federal injunction. The FY2026 budget broadened involuntary commitment. Partial offsets: universal free school meals, the tripled Child Tax Credit, and the December 19, 2025 prison reform package.

**2026 — partial recovery, not full (56.9, +3.1).** The June 2026 immigration package is the strongest structural-critique action in the five-year record. DOCCS published the WilmerHale external review on June 29, 2026. The 2026 State of the State proposed a $1.7 billion pathway toward universal child care. Offsetting: FY2027 budget amendments delayed DEC climate regulations from 2024 to 2028 and cap-and-invest to 2029; 450,000 people lose Essential Plan coverage July 1, 2026 with no reported state mitigation; DOCCS reported 83 deaths among incarcerated people in the first half of 2026 alone.

**Reading the trajectory:** New York recovered from a governance-integrity collapse in 2021 and built real structural capacity through 2024. Since then it has been unable to convert that capacity into accountability. The 2025 drop was driven almost entirely by the two dimensions that were already weakest — Boundaries and Accountability — which is the signature of a structural rather than incidental problem.

New York's score is not evidence that New York is materially less compassionate than Connecticut. It is evidence that New York has been examined more closely. A comparably deep review of Connecticut, Minnesota or Massachusetts would very likely move those scores down, particularly on Accountability and Boundaries, where adverse evidence only surfaces under adversarial search.

**Recommendation to the index maintainers:** treat this as a known comparability limitation until the published peers receive equivalent-depth reviews. Do not present New York's rank against these peers without this caveat.

## Key Findings

- **New York helps at enormous scale but cannot hold itself accountable when it causes harm.** It scores 65 on Equity and 70 on Systemic Thinking, but only 40 on Accountability. Two men — Robert Brooks and Messiah Nantwi — were beaten to death by state prison staff within three months of each other, in December 2024 and March 2025.

- **The state's own numbers show its discipline system does not work.** Of roughly 1,200 excessive force complaints in New York prisons in 2025, the corrections department substantiated fewer than 50 — about 4.2%. When staff were fired for cause, independent arbitrators returned them to their jobs in every single case.

- **New York breaks its own laws when they become inconvenient.** The state suspended the HALT solitary confinement law during a February 2025 guard strike. A judge ruled in July 2025 that this violated the separation of powers. An Inspector General report had already found in August 2024 that 40% of people were held in solitary longer than the law allowed.

- **The people who deliver New York's compassion are depleted.** State employees worked 22.2 million overtime hours in 2022 at a cost of $1.36 billion. Chronic understaffing contributed to an illegal prison strike that required the National Guard. Boundaries is the state's joint-lowest dimension at 40 out of 100.

- **Real, measurable wins exist and should not be dismissed.** Overdose deaths fell from 6,688 in 2023 to about 4,567 in 2024, a 32% drop. New York served 396 million free school meals to 2.7 million students in 2025-26. The 2021 Excluded Workers Fund paid $2.1 billion to over 130,000 workers whom federal aid excluded — a politically costly act that other states then copied.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Systemic Thinking (70/100)** — New York legislates upstream. The Child Poverty Reduction Act binds the state by law to halve child poverty by 2031, with annual public reporting. The June 2026 immigration package confronts federal enforcement at genuine institutional cost.
- **Empathy (65/100) and Equity (65/100)** — Community members hold real decision seats on the Child Poverty Reduction Advisory Council and the Climate Justice Working Group. Coverage is close to universal: an uninsured rate below 5% and 1.7 million people on the Essential Plan.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Accountability (40/100)** — Harm is acknowledged only after outsiders establish it. The same failures recur. Repair reaches only high-visibility cases. This has been the state's weakest area in every year assessed since 2021.
- **Boundaries (40/100)** — A depleted workforce, a consolidation that stripped choice from 250,000 home care consumers, and a May 2025 law that deliberately broadened involuntary commitment.

## Evidence Gaps

- **Frontline experience testimony is thin.** No systematic independent survey of how New Yorkers experience state services was found. E1 (Affective Resonance) and B3 (Scope Clarity) are scored conservatively as a result — both marked medium confidence.
- **Unmet need is not published.** New York publishes spending well but not the gap between need and provision, capping AC2 and AC4 at 4 rather than 5.
- **Reparations commission outputs are not yet available.** EQ5 is scored on process, not delivered remedy.
- **State versus city attribution is difficult on homelessness.** Much of the sharpest data is New York City-specific. State-level responsibility is real — the right to shelter is a State Constitutional obligation and the state committed $4.3 billion — but the assessment avoided scoring municipal execution as state conduct.
- **Federal interaction was excluded from scoring.** The loss of Essential Plan coverage for 450,000 people on July 1, 2026 was triggered by federal law (H.R. 1, July 2025). New York was scored only on its own response to that shock — specifically, the reported absence of a state mitigation plan — not on the federal action itself.
