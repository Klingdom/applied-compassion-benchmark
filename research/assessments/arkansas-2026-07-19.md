# Compassion Benchmark Assessment: Arkansas

**Entity type:** State
**Sector/Domain:** Government (US state government)
**Assessment date:** 2026-07-19
**Composite score:** 33.1/100
**Band:** Developing

## What this assessment found, in plain language

Arkansas runs some genuinely serious programmes. It put $45.3 million into maternal health. It became the first state in the country to take on pharmacy middlemen. It sent $26.3 million in opioid settlement money to all 75 counties. It raised state worker pay by $120 million a year.

Arkansas also removed health coverage from about 351,000 people in six months. That was the fastest timetable of any state. Roughly 76 percent of those people were cut for paperwork reasons, not because anyone found them ineligible. About 94,000 of them were children.

The gap between those two facts is the assessment. Arkansas can build things. It struggles to tell people what is happening to them, and it does not go back for the people it drops.

## Cross-State Consistency: AB3 Transparency Rule v3

**Disposition: declined — precondition not met.** AB3 scored 3 on the merits, not by cap and not by floor.

The rule needs both limbs. Limb (a) asks whether a branch of government exempted itself from the records law, or whether the state attacked its own watchdog. Neither happened in Arkansas. The 2023 special session opened with four proposed exemptions, including deliberative-process and attorney-client carve-outs that together would have amounted to broad executive self-exemption. Bipartisan public backlash removed them before a vote. What passed was one subject-matter exemption for security-detail records. Under the Idaho refinement, a subject-matter carve-out is structurally different from a branch exempting itself. Arkansas Legislative Audit was not narrowed, not stripped of independence, and not defunded, so the Indiana limb is not engaged either.

Limb (b) is satisfied on its own — Arkansas Legislative Audit still publishes damaging uncompelled findings against DHS, and the Maternal Mortality Review Committee published the 94-percent-preventable figure on 2026-01-15 — but limb (b) alone cannot trigger the rule.

## Key Findings

- **Arkansas's biggest compassion failure is administrative, not budgetary.** The state completed its Medicaid unwinding in six months, faster than any state but Idaho. About 76 percent of roughly 351,000 disenrollments were procedural — the state could not tell whether those people still qualified. It cut them anyway.

- **The people cut were disproportionately children, and no one went back for them.** 94,000 Arkansas children lost coverage. The child uninsured rate hit 6.7 percent in 2023, its highest in ten years, and Arkansas ranks 42nd nationally. When federal officials asked the state to re-enrol eligible children, the governor called the request a political stunt. No reinstatement programme exists.

- **Arkansas had already run this experiment once and did not learn from it in time.** In 2018 it became the first state to condition Medicaid on work reporting; more than 18,000 people, about a quarter of those covered by the rule, lost coverage, and a federal court struck it down. Independent evaluation found no employment gain. The 2023 unwinding repeated the same paperwork-termination pattern at ten times the scale. The design lesson was eventually applied to the 2026 work requirement, five years late.

- **The state is genuinely willing to fight powerful actors on behalf of its residents — in some arenas.** Act 624 of 2025 made Arkansas the first state to ban pharmacy benefit managers from owning retail pharmacies. Three of the largest health companies in America sued, a federal judge blocked the law, and the state is appealing. That is real cost borne for a structural position.

- **One refusal is unusually clean and unusually documented.** Arkansas is the only state that has taken no action to extend postpartum Medicaid coverage from 60 days to 12 months. The governor's own maternal health committee declined to recommend it in September 2024 even though stakeholders raised it in meetings. This is in a state where the maternal mortality ratio is 38.4 per 100,000 live births and the state's own review committee judged 94 percent of those deaths preventable.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Systemic Thinking (45/100)** is the only dimension in the Functional band. Arkansas takes upstream positions that carry cost — the PBM law above all — plans on multi-year horizons, and shares resources with smaller local bodies through the opioid recovery partnership.
- **Integrity (40/100)** is supported by a pattern of publishing its own unflattering numbers without being compelled to, and by a $120 million pay plan that treated staff depletion as a structural problem rather than an individual one.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Boundaries (20/100)** is the only dimension in the Critical band, driven by two subdimensions scored 1. Scope Clarity fails because 76 percent of disenrollments happened without the state knowing whether the person qualified — meaning people did not know what was being asked of them. Refusal Ethics fails because 94,000 children were removed with no alternative pathway, and because Arkansas remains the only state where falling behind on rent and staying put is a crime.
- **Accountability (25/100)** is dragged down by Harm Acknowledgment and Reparative Action, both scored 1. Arkansas contested the existence of the harm and has made no repair.

## Evidence Gaps

- **EQ5 Historical Harm Acknowledgment** is scored with low confidence. Evidence was found on recent programme harms but not on any structured state process for acknowledging older harms, so the score rests on a narrower base than the others.
- **Prison healthcare** specifically could not be evidenced at the level needed to score separately. Overcrowding, staffing and oversight were well documented; medical care inside facilities was not. This may understate a weakness.
- **Community testimony (Tier 5)** is thin throughout. Most scoring rests on Tier 4 published institutional data and news reporting. Several subdimensions that require community confirmation to reach 4 or 5 could not have been scored higher on available evidence, which biases this assessment conservative by design.
- **The 2026 work requirement** is in a penalty-free soft launch. Its real effects will not be visible until 2027, so B5 and AB2 are scored on design intent rather than outcome.

## Recommended Next Steps

Arkansas is in the **Developing** band. Key structures are emerging but remain inconsistent and reactive.

- Consider a [Certified Assessment](/certified-assessments) for a structured improvement roadmap. The highest-leverage targets are the two Boundaries subdimensions scored 1: notice quality and scope communication in eligibility processes, and a defined refusal protocol with concrete alternatives.
