## Methodology-Novel Adjudication: "Reforms-Contested-Not-Abandoned"

The May 21 +6.3 upgrade was conservatively anchored on "reforms-announced-not-enacted." Today's evidence reveals a sub-pattern within that anchoring:

**Question:** When a reform government pushes back on specific reform conditionalities (pension/tax) while maintaining commitment to the broader reform agenda (rule of law), does that warrant a downgrade?

**Ruling:**

1. **Differentiation by reform domain matters.** Rule-of-law reforms were the primary driver of the May 21 upgrade (judicial independence, NGO law repeal, EPPO accession, Sovereignty Protection Office abolition, ICC re-engagement). Pension and tax reform are economic adjustments conditioned by the EC for EU funds unlock, not the rule-of-law restoration that drove the compassion score.
2. **Negotiation is not abandonment.** A government pushing back on EC-imposed economic conditionalities while continuing active rule-of-law reform talks is doing what democratic governments do — bargain over policy. This is materially different from a government withdrawing rule-of-law commitments.
3. **Falsification criteria remain intact.** The May 27 reform-plan submission and May 31 Sulyok deadline are unchanged. If those slip, downgrade is warranted. If they hold, the upgrade is vindicated.

**Precedent established:** "Reforms-contested-not-abandoned" is consistent with the conservative anchoring of the May 21 upgrade. Do NOT downgrade. Watch the May 27 / May 31 milestones. If rule-of-law reforms slip past those deadlines, the upgrade thesis is challenged and a sub-threshold pullback is warranted.
