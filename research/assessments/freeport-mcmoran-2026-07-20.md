## Assessment Context

Freeport-McMoRan was surfaced by the 2026-07-20 scan on the death of a haul-truck operator at its Morenci copper mine in Arizona on 2026-07-06 — the third fatality tied to Morenci since 2021. The assessment window also captures the far larger Grasberg disaster in Central Papua, Indonesia, where a mud rush on 2025-09-08 killed seven contract workers whose bodies were recovered on 2025-10-05. Against that, Freeport-McMoRan maintains an unusually developed third-party assurance apparatus for its sector. Both facts are real and the assessment holds them together rather than choosing one.

The assessed composite of 32.5 is 13.7 points ABOVE the published 18.8. This gap is a placeholder artifact, not evidence of improvement. Awareness (published 1.5 raw, assessed 2.6) and Accountability (published 1.5, assessed 2.6) account for most of it. The placeholder implied a company with essentially no structured detection or harm-acknowledgment capability. That is not accurate: Freeport-McMoRan runs site-level human rights impact assessments conducted by third-party consultants, is validated against ICMM's 38 performance expectations, and publicly confirmed all seven Grasberg deaths and suspended the entire mining district before any legal compulsion. Boundaries (assessed 1.8) and Integrity (assessed 2) remain at or below the placeholder, driven by Self-Sustainability and Internal Consistency both scoring 1 of 5 on the fatality record.

## Key Findings

- Eight workers have died at Freeport-McMoRan operations in ten months. Seven contractors were killed by a mud rush at the Grasberg mine in Indonesia on 2025-09-08, and a haul-truck operator died at Morenci, Arizona on 2026-07-06.
- Worker safety is the company's floor. Self-Sustainability (B1) and Internal Consistency (I3) both score 1 out of 5. IndustriALL reports that unions "have repeatedly reported mine accidents and violations of workers' rights" at Grasberg over the past decade.
- The company does acknowledge harm quickly. Freeport-McMoRan confirmed all seven Grasberg deaths publicly, ran a month-long recovery operation, and suspended the district before restarting only after investigations and remedial plans.
- Freeport-McMoRan has real third-party oversight, which is rare for its published score. It holds Copper Mark assurance, is validated against ICMM's 38 performance expectations, and commissions independent human rights impact assessments.
- The published score of 18.8 out of 100 looks too low, but this is not the night to change it. The company was flagged on a worker death, and raising a score on negative evidence would be a false positive. This is referred to calibration review instead.

## Strongest Dimensions

Action (raw 2.8) and Awareness (raw 2.6). The response to the Grasberg disaster was proportional and expensive: a month-long recovery effort, immediate suspension of the whole minerals district, and a phased restart gated on completed investigations and remedial plans. Detection infrastructure — third-party HRIAs and ICMM validation — is genuinely structured.

## Weakest Dimensions

Boundaries (raw 1.8) and Equity (raw 2). Self-Sustainability scores 1 of 5: the work itself is killing people, repeatedly, across two continents. Historical Harm Acknowledgment also scores 1 of 5, with no documented reparative engagement over the long-running grievances in Central Papua.

## Evidence Gaps

Freeport-McMoRan's compensation and repair to the families of the seven Grasberg workers could not be verified from any public source, which directly constrains AB5 Reparative Action. The MSHA Morenci report is explicitly preliminary and carries no causation finding, so it cannot be scored as a determination of negligence. Community testimony from Amungme and Kamoro communities in Central Papua was not obtainable in English-language sources within this assessment, leaving EQ5 scored on union reporting alone. The Copper Mark quote available on the company's own page dates to 2021 and understates current coverage; the assessment did not credit the broader "all operating sites" claim because it could not be verified verbatim.

## Calibration Note

DO NOT APPLY THIS AS A ONE-OFF UPGRADE. Freeport-McMoRan has never been individually assessed; the published 18.8 is a bulk-import placeholder. The first full 40-subdimension review finds materially more compassion infrastructure than the placeholder assumed — third-party human rights impact assessments, ICMM Mining Principles validation against 38 performance expectations, and Copper Mark assurance. That produces a +13.7 gap. But the anti-false-positive screen (agent definition section 3e-bis) governs here, and this proposal deliberately does NOT clear it. Freeport-McMoRan was surfaced on NEGATIVE within-window evidence: a worker killed at the Morenci mine on 2026-07-06. There is no new within-window evidence of positive, countervailing conduct by the company. Under screening rule 3, negative-trigger evidence must not yield an upgrade, and under rule 5, a belief that a published score is mis-calibrated must be routed to calibration review rather than encoded as a one-off reset. The recommendation is therefore flag-for-review, not upgrade. The correct remedy is a band-wide calibration review of never-assessed Fortune 500 entities sitting in the Critical band, not an isolated move on this one company while it is under active federal fatality investigation.
