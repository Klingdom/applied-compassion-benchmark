## What this score means in plain language

Rhode Island scores 45.6 out of 100. That is the middle of the scale — the benchmark calls it "Functional," meaning the systems exist but have real gaps.

The state is very good at one thing and weak at another, and the gap between them drives the score. Rhode Island covers almost everybody with health insurance. About 98 percent of residents have coverage. But when the state cannot help someone, it often does not say so honestly or offer an alternative. That is why Boundaries, at 2.2 out of 5, is the lowest of the eight areas.

Rhode Island scores below every other New England state assessed so far. New England has not clustered high in this benchmark, and Rhode Island sits at the bottom of the region.

## Cross-State Consistency: AB3 Transparency Rule v3

**Disposition: DECLINED — precondition (a) not met.**

The rule caps AB3 at 3 only when two things are both true. Limb (a) asks whether a branch of government has exempted itself from the public-records law, or whether the state has attacked the watchdog itself. Limb (b) asks whether an independently funded oversight body is still publishing damaging findings nobody forced it to publish.

Limb (b) is clearly met. The Auditor General published 28 uncompelled findings and said state financial reporting is getting worse each year.

Limb (a) is not met. Rhode Island's Access to Public Records Act defines a public body to include any executive, legislative, judicial, regulatory or administrative body of the state. The General Assembly publishes its own records-request guidelines rather than claiming blanket exemption. Judicial case records sit under a separate chapter, which is a subject-matter carve-out, not a branch escaping scrutiny. No evidence was found that Rhode Island narrowed the remit, removed independence protections, or defunded either the Attorney General's records enforcement function or the Office of the Auditor General.

Two scope limits from the rule apply directly. Under the North Dakota refinement, procedural weakness alone does not trigger the cap — the 2025 bill's "vexatious requests" provision was a procedural weakening inside a bill that did not pass. Under the Idaho refinement, enumerated exemptions alone do not trigger it either, and APRA's roughly 29 carve-outs are exactly that.

**Scoring effect:** AB3 was scored 3 on the merits, which equals what the cap would have produced. The composite is 45.6 (Functional) either way.

## Key Findings

- **Rhode Island covers almost everyone, then struggles to deliver.** About 98 percent of residents have health insurance and the state uninsured rate is 2.2 percent, sixth-best in the country. This is the state's clearest achievement. But coverage is not the same as care: 1,900 seniors and disabled residents were set to lose in-network access to Brown Health doctors from 1 July 2026 in a contract fight the state could not resolve.

- **The state promises a door and cannot open it.** This is the single biggest drag on the score. In May 2026 Rhode Island had 1,318 emergency shelter beds for roughly 2,400 people experiencing homelessness. Unsheltered homelessness rose about 470 percent between 2020 and 2025. Boundaries scored 2.2 of 5 — the lowest of the eight areas — because a referral system that routes people to capacity that does not exist is a failure of honesty as much as of resources.

- **Money went in; housing did not come out.** The independent Rhode Island Public Expenditure Council found in 2026 that about $522 million of state investment since 2021 is on track to produce 2,207 affordable units. That is 9.6 percent of a 23,222-unit shortfall. The Boston Globe reported the finding as "no meaningful impact" for most residents.

- **The state punished its vendor, then rehired it.** Attackers used a Deloitte employee's credential to enter the RIBridges benefits system on 2 July 2024 and were not detected until 28 November 2024. Rhode Island notified 735,501 people — most of the state. It recovered $12 million from Deloitte, then awarded Deloitte a new $99 million contract to keep running the same system. That contradiction is the clearest example of why Integrity scored 2.6 of 5.

- **Rhode Island tells on itself, which is rarer than it sounds.** The Office of the Child Advocate sued its own state's child welfare agency. The Auditor General published 28 findings and said things are getting worse. The environment department published its own failure to hit a binding climate target. The education department published seven years of flat reading scores in the district it controlled. This willingness to publish unflattering findings is why Awareness and Systemic Thinking are the strongest areas.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Systemic Thinking (3.4/5)** — highest of the eight. The Act on Climate is a binding statutory target running to 2050 with a citizen right to sue, and the state publishes honest progress against it. The Attorney General sued CVS Caremark, headquartered in Woonsocket, over drug pricing — advocacy that runs against the state's own short-term economic interest.
- **Awareness (3.2/5)** and **Equity (3.2/5)** — near-universal health coverage, near-total statewide traffic-stop data collection with independent academic analysis, and a Child Advocate with genuine authority to act against the state.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Boundaries (2.2/5)** — the shelter capacity gap drives four of five subdimensions. People are refused without alternatives, scope limits are not communicated before reliance, and benefits data collection carries no meaningful option to decline.
- **Empathy (2.4/5)** — the state contests harm claims through the courts before acknowledging them, and does not consult the federally recognised Narragansett Indian Tribe on decisions affecting land.
- **Integrity (2.6/5)** — stated values and actual decisions diverge on public records, vendor accountability and housing.

## Evidence Gaps

- **Staff wellbeing data (B1, I3).** No published state-employee turnover series comparable to what other states publish was found. Scoring relied on DCYF accreditation reporting and the Auditor General's turnover commentary. Confidence: medium.
- **Consent practice (B5).** Scored on the RIBridges breach alone; no evidence found on consent quality in child welfare, corrections or behavioural health. Confidence: **low**, and the score should be treated as provisional.
- **Deaths in custody (E4, AB1).** Rhode Island does not appear to publish a systematic deaths-in-custody dataset. Counts were drawn from news reporting and advocacy sources. This is itself a transparency finding.
- **Small-state coverage effect.** Rhode Island has roughly 1.1 million residents and far less investigative press capacity than larger states. Thin coverage was treated as an evidence gap, not as evidence of good conduct. Where coverage was thin, confidence was marked low or medium rather than a favourable score being inferred.
- **State–municipal attribution.** Rhode Island's small size blurs state and city responsibility more than elsewhere. Encampment sweeps by the Providence Police Department are municipal and are **not** scored as state conduct; they appear only as context for the statewide shelter gap, which is a state responsibility. The ProvPort master plan is run by a nonprofit port corporation and the City of Providence; only the state environment department's air monitoring and grant activity within it is scored.

## Recommended Next Steps

Rhode Island lands in the **Functional** band. On the published guidance for this band, the state would benefit from [Advisory Support](/advisory) to translate benchmark findings into strategic action.

Three specific priorities follow from the score profile:

1. **Close the honesty gap in the shelter system.** Raising Boundaries from 2.2 to 3.0 — communicating capacity limits before people rely on the system, and tracking refusal outcomes — moves the composite to 48.1. Verified with `computeCompositeFromDimensions`.
2. **Publish a deaths-in-custody and solitary confinement dataset.** Rhode Island runs every prison and jail in the state. Recording when and for how long people are held in solitary would address the specific gap holding AB4 at 3.
3. **Finish the public records reform.** The Access to Public Records Act was last substantively updated in 2012. Completing reform, and creating independent enforcement, is the clearest available route from AB3 3 to 4.

No single-dimension correction moves Rhode Island into the Established band. Adding 0.5 to every dimension returns 58.1, still Functional.
