## Assessment Basis — Why This Cycle Is Scorable

The 23.4 published baseline (Developing) priced a federal immigration-enforcement posture that was punitive but operating within contested legal bounds. The 2026-06-04 window introduces **adjudicated** evidence of un-priced harm — distinguishing this from the filed-but-unadjudicated allegations carried elsewhere this cycle (3M, UnitedHealth, CVS):

1. **Adjudicated unlawful-conduct findings (Tier 5).** Federal courts have ruled core fast-track deportation policy *unlawful*: U.S. District Judge Brian Murphy (D. Mass., Feb 25 2026) struck down third-country deportations without notice or opportunity to object as a due-process violation; U.S. District Judge Jia Cobb (D.D.C.) barred expedited removal of parolees admitted at ports of entry. These are judicial findings of unlawful conduct, not mere complaints.
2. **Record-pace deaths in custody with documented expansion despite deaths.** ICE announced the 17th in-custody death of 2026 (a rate of roughly one every six days). The ACLU documents ICE *expanding* detention into warehouses "despite record deaths" — an adjudicated-level AB2 correction-refusal signal.
3. **Two U.S.-citizen fatal shootings by federal immigration officers**, both in Minneapolis (Renee Good, 37, mother of three; Alex Pretti, 37, ICU nurse, shot at a VA hospital during a protest), with officials publicly attributing fault to a victim ("weaponized her car"). This is an E4-validation / AB1-deflection failure at the highest evidentiary tier (deaths of citizens).
4. **National Guard / federal force deployed to five U.S. cities** (Los Angeles, Washington D.C., Portland, Memphis, Chicago) to protect deportation operations — a structural posture of force toward domestic populations.

Per methodology, an adjudicated finding of unlawful conduct against the most-vulnerable (migrants stripped of due process) combined with record-pace deaths and citizen fatalities REVEALS harm beyond what the 23.4 baseline priced, and crosses from confirm to scorable downgrade.
