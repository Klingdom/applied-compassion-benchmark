## Cross-State Consistency: the AB3 Transparency Rule

The adopted rule caps AB3 at 3 where a broad records exemption coexists with a still-publishing oversight body. **Connecticut is the counter-case the rule anticipated, and AB3 is scored at 4, above the cap.**

The rule's cap exists to penalise states that buy credibility from a watchdog while shielding their own branches from disclosure. Connecticut does not do this. Its Freedom of Information Act dates to 1975 and is enforced by an independent Freedom of Information Commission with adjudicatory power. In the 2025 session the legislature **defeated** SB 1226, which would have exempted all public-university teaching and research records from disclosure — the opposite of the self-exemption pattern seen in North Carolina and Virginia. Three separately funded oversight bodies — the Correction Ombuds, the Office of the Child Advocate, and the Office of the Inspector General — were all actively publishing uncompelled, damaging findings in the twelve months to July 2026, so the Massachusetts output rider is satisfied.

**Scoring effect:** AB3 at 4 gives ACC 3.6 and a composite of **52.5**. Applying the cap of 3 would give ACC 3.4 and a composite of **51.9**, both Functional. The rule does not change Connecticut's band, but the reasoning matters for future states.

### Score Difference Analysis

**BND, -57.5 points.** The placeholder's 4.5 would place Connecticut near the top of the sector on sustainable, autonomy-preserving, honestly-scoped help. The evidence does not support this anywhere. The Correction Ombuds found staffing so thin that people were confined to cells; the state ran a five-year erasure programme without building any way to tell beneficiaries it had happened. Two subdimensions (B4, B5) are scored at 2 on thin evidence and are the most likely to move on institutional engagement.

**ACT, -35.0 points.** Connecticut passes good laws and delivers them slowly. Clean Slate was signed in 2021, due to start in 2023, and finished in July 2026. Prison specialty appointments waited more than two years. Special education received 15.7% of its statutory entitlement. This is the clearest "law versus delivery" gap in the assessment.

**INT, -30.0 points.** The DCF trajectory is the single most informative fact about Connecticut in this window. The state did the hard thing — 32 years of consent-decree compliance, every measure met — and then let the gains erode once the judge left. Child-contact ratings fell from 85% to 58% in three years. That is a resilience failure, and the legislature's 2026 response, while genuine, confirms rather than rebuts it.

**EMP, -25.0 points.** A 4 requires community confirmation that most people feel heard. Nine deaths in custody in 2026, mold and sewage in facilities, and 170,000 people not told their records were cleared all cut against it.

## Key Findings

- **Connecticut writes better laws than it delivers.** The state passed Clean Slate in 2021 to erase old low-level convictions automatically. Erasures were due to start in 2023. The backlog was not cleared until 15 July 2026 — three and a half years late, for 170,000 people.

- **Connecticut finished the job and then forgot to tell anyone.** The state has no way to notify people that their convictions were erased. Officials were still "exploring how to notify" in March 2026. A benefit nobody knows they have is a benefit they cannot use.

- **Care did not survive the end of court oversight.** Connecticut spent 32 years under the Juan F. consent decree and exited in March 2022 with every outcome measure met. By early 2025 one DCF child-contact rating had fallen from 85% to 58%. The legislature created new oversight in May 2026.

- **The state pays for findings that embarrass it.** Connecticut's own Correction Ombuds reported in January 2026 that its prisons operate in "sustained institutional failure," with rodents, black mold and sewage backups. Nine people died in state custody in 2026. This is the strongest single signal of genuine, non-performative accountability in the assessment.

- **Connecticut's transparency regime is the best seen in this wave.** Its Freedom of Information Act dates to 1975 and has an independent enforcement commission. In 2025 the legislature defeated a bill that would have hidden public-university research records. Connecticut is scored above the cross-state transparency cap for this reason.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Accountability (3.6/5)** and **Systemic Thinking (3.6/5)** — the Juan F. exit is a rare documented escape from federal oversight through sustained performance, and Clean Slate plus free prison calls are genuine upstream, reparative work.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Boundaries (2.2/5)** — depleted frontline staffing in prisons and child welfare, plus a large-scale failure to tell people what the state had and had not done for them.
- **Action (2.6/5)** — multi-year delays in two flagship programmes and special education funded at 15.7% of assessed need.
- **Integrity (2.8/5)** — driven almost entirely by the post-consent-decree DCF regression.

## Evidence Gaps

- **B4 Refusal Ethics and B5 Consent Orientation** have very little public evidence. Both are scored at 2 under the methodology's rule of defaulting to the lower anchor when evidence is absent. Confidence is low on both. Institutional engagement could plausibly move each by one point, which would raise the composite to about 55.
- **State employee turnover and internal workforce data** were not found in published form. I3 is inferred from oversight findings about prison and DCF conditions rather than from direct workforce data.
- **Manson Youth Institution** monitoring reports are summarised in press coverage; the underlying July 2025 monitor report was not directly retrieved.

## Recommended Next Steps

Connecticut sits in the **Functional** band. Systems exist and several are genuinely strong, but delivery and boundary practice have significant gaps.
