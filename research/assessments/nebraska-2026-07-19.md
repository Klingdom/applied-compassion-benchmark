## Cross-state consistency: AB3 transparency rule v3

**Disposition: APPLIED. AB3 capped at 3.**

Both limbs of the trigger are met.

**Limb (a), structural transparency failure — met on the second branch, curtailing the oversight body.** On 2023-08-16 the Nebraska Attorney General issued an opinion holding that the Legislature's Inspectors General had unconstitutional investigative powers. The opinion was non-binding. The executive branch acted on it anyway: the prison and child welfare agencies cut off the Inspectors General's access to facilities and records, and the Legislature had no meaningful oversight of either agency for six months. LB 298 (2025) later restructured the offices and removed the statutory guarantee of direct real-time computer access to agency records. That is narrowing the remit and removing independence protections, which is exactly the case the Indiana refinement was written to capture.

**Limb (b), surviving output — met.** The oversight bodies are still publishing damaging findings nobody asked for. The Inspector General of Corrections reported on 2025-10-02 that Nebraska ranks first in the nation for prison crowding on operational capacity, and stated that the state's new measurement law would have ended its own crowding emergency on paper. The Inspector General of Child Welfare published rising child death counts on 2025-09-15. The Ombudsman's 2025 report disclosed that a county jail had blocked its phone number. This is not a paper watchdog, so the Massachusetts output contingency does not return the floor to 2.

**Scoring effect.** With the cap, AB3 = 3, ACC = 3.0, composite = 40.0, band Developing. Without the cap, AB3 would score 4 on the merits — Nebraska strengthened its records statute in 2024 and publishes prolific uncompelled oversight — giving ACC = 3.2 and composite = 40.6, band Functional. Both figures were produced by `computeCompositeFromDimensions`, not by hand. The cap is therefore band-determinative for Nebraska and should be reviewed explicitly by any reviewer.

**Note on what did not trigger the rule.** Nebraska's Legislature has not exempted itself from the public records law; the existence of a narrow carve-out for legislative records implies the opposite. Governor Pillen's "executive privilege" denials are ad hoc rather than blanket, and are scored under I4 rather than as limb (a). Under the Idaho refinement, Nebraska's list of subject-matter exemptions alone would not have triggered the cap.

## Questions asked, and what the evidence actually showed

Several research questions carried premises that the evidence only partly supports. Stating these plainly:

**The unicameral, officially nonpartisan legislature.** The premise that Nebraska has the only unicameral, officially nonpartisan state legislature is correct. The premise that this produces different institutional behaviour is only half correct. On defending its own oversight machinery, the structure works: lawmakers voted 39-3 to advance the oversight rewrite, and passed the records fee fix quickly. On contested social legislation, the nonpartisan label does not describe behaviour — LB 574 passed 33-15 on a party-line vote in 2023. And in 2026 the body could not muster 30 votes to override any of five vetoes, including one it had passed 34-15. The structure produces institutional self-defence, not broad deliberative consensus.

**"Nebraska has among the most overcrowded prison systems in the nation."** Confirmed and stronger than the premise. Nebraska ranks first nationally on operational capacity and second behind Alabama on design capacity, per its own Inspector General.

**"Voters approved Medicaid expansion and implementation was delayed."** Confirmed. Approved November 2018, first coverage 2020-10-01. Roughly 23 months. The state cited the need to build a new benefits system. A state senator threatened litigation over the delay.

**Statutory Inspector General for child welfare.** Confirmed and it publishes. Nebraska has two statutory Inspectors General, not one — child welfare and corrections — plus a legislative Ombudsman and a Foster Care Review Office. This is the state's most distinctive institutional asset.

## Key Findings

- **Nebraska scores 40.0 out of 100, sitting exactly on the line between the Developing and Functional bands.** One scoring decision — whether the cross-state transparency cap applies — moves it across. Readers should treat the band as provisional.

- **Nebraska builds unusually good machinery for noticing suffering, then does not fund the response.** It runs two statutory Inspectors General, a legislative Ombudsman that handled 2,550 cases in 2025, and public child welfare dashboards. Awareness scores 55 out of 100, its best dimension. But Boundaries scores 25 and Integrity scores 30. The state sees the problem and then declines to resource it.

- **The state cut off its own watchdogs for six months.** After a non-binding Attorney General opinion in August 2023, prison and child welfare agencies stopped giving the Inspectors General access to facilities and records. The Legislature restored access in February 2024 and rewrote the law in 2025. The watchdogs kept publishing damaging findings throughout.

- **Nebraska approved 350 million dollars for a new prison, then changed how it counts crowding.** A 2024 university study warned the prison would not fix crowding without policy change. LB 150 (2025) switched the emergency trigger from design capacity to the looser operational capacity. The Inspector General noted the state would have exited its 2020 emergency on paper under the new rule.

- **Money moved away from need under pressure.** In the August 2024 special session, 64 percent of the General Fund reduction fell on Health and Human Services operations. In April 2026 the state cut 50 million dollars from a fund that pays for health programmes. In the same year it vetoed a bill requiring 5 percent of subsidised apartments to be wheelchair accessible; the override failed 19-28.

- **A 40 percent underbid became a 110 million dollar bailout.** Nebraska awarded child welfare case management to a bidder 40 percent below the next lowest offer, missed the warning in procurement, then paid 110 million dollars in emergency funds to keep 2,500 state wards served before ending the contract in December 2021.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Awareness (55/100).** Three independent statutory bodies detect and publish. The Ombudsman's investigation into prison good-time credit produced an actual policy change on 2025-12-31.
- **Accountability (50/100).** Systemic learning scores 4 out of 5. Nebraska changed at least three practices after failures: it brought case management in house, closed a records fee loophole within months of losing in court, and created a permanent oversight division.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Boundaries (25/100).** Every one of the five subdimensions scores 2. Prison mental health posts are more than half vacant, 29 counties have no mental health provider, food aid recipients lost purchasing choice, and the state over-promised a service it could not fund.
- **Integrity (30/100).** The 23-month Medicaid delay after a citizen vote, the refusal and later reversal on child food aid, the high-visibility SNAP waiver alongside the low-visibility accessible-housing veto, and a governor without a state-issued phone.

## Evidence Gaps

- **Refusal ethics (B4) and consent (B5) are scored on thin evidence, confidence low.** No published state protocol was found for what happens when someone is turned away. In a state where 29 counties have no mental health provider, this is a material gap.
- **Historical harm acknowledgment (EQ5) is confidence low.** Tribal partnership work is documented; no broader formal acknowledgment or reparative programme was found either way.
- **No state-published deaths-in-custody series was located** for 2024-2026 within this desk research. The Sabata class action was dismissed on class certification grounds in 2020 without settlement, so it produced no verified findings of fact to score.
- **No independent audit of staff wellbeing or turnover across state government** was located, so B1 rests on the corrections-specific Inspector General figures.
- **Small-state caution applied.** Nebraska's roughly 2 million population means fewer national datasets disaggregate it. Where coverage was thin, confidence was marked low rather than scored to the middle.

## Recommended Next Steps

Nebraska falls in the **Developing** band, at its upper boundary. Consider a [Certified Assessment](/certified-assessments) for a structured improvement roadmap. The fastest movement available is in Boundaries, currently 25 out of 100 with all five subdimensions at 2: publishing a refusal and referral protocol, and closing the prison behavioural health vacancy rate, would move the composite materially. Sensitivity testing shows that raising Boundaries alone from 2.0 to 3.0 lifts the composite from 40.0 to 43.1.
