The assessed composite of 19.4 sits 0.9 points below the published 20.3, well inside the confirmation threshold. Dimension-level movement is small and offsetting. Equity and Systemic Thinking score slightly ABOVE the published baseline because the assessment credits verifiable structural progress the bulk import did not capture: the Assurance Maladie Universelle now covers more than 40 percent of the population after a decade of expansion, and near-95 percent primary enrollment for children under 10. The net effect is close to zero. No dimension moved more than 0.5 raw points.

## Key Findings

- A quarter of Djibouti's people face crisis-level hunger. The UN-backed IPC network projects more than 256,000 people at Crisis or worse between July and December 2026, with about 30,000 at Emergency level.
- The hunger is concentrated in refugee camps, and the response is not keeping up. Nearly 70 percent of people in the Ali Addeh and Holl-Holl camps are at crisis level or above, and the IPC found food assistance "generally insufficient."
- Djibouti has made real, measurable health and education gains. Universal health insurance now reaches more than 40 percent of the population, and nearly 95 percent of children under 10 are in school. These lift the Equity and Systemic Thinking scores.
- Political accountability is closed. President Guelleh won a sixth term in April 2026 with 97.81 percent of the vote, after parliament removed the age limit that would have barred him. Two main opposition parties have boycotted since 2016.
- Decades of camp dependency depress the Boundaries score. Refugee households remain "largely dependent on food assistance," with no documented pathway to self-sufficiency.

## Strongest Dimensions

Systemic Thinking (raw 2.2) and Action (raw 2.2) are the strongest, and both are still weak in absolute terms. Djibouti's decade-long expansion of universal health insurance and near-universal primary enrollment are genuine long-horizon investments, and the country's continued hosting of refugees within a regional response framework counts as coalitional behavior.

## Weakest Dimensions

Accountability (raw 1.2) and Integrity (raw 1.2) are the weakest. Harm is denied rather than acknowledged, there is no documented case of course correction under evidence of harm, and constitutional rules were changed to remove an obstacle to the incumbent's re-election. A 97.81 percent result with a boycotted opposition is the defining Integrity fact.

## Evidence Gaps

No independent audit of Djibouti's own social-protection targeting was available. The National Family Solidarity Programme is widely referenced but I could not verify current caseload or outcome data from a primary source, so it is not scored as a credited program. Human rights reporting relies partly on a 2022 US State Department report and a BTI country report; there is no current in-country independent monitoring to draw on. Employee/staff-side evidence for Internal Consistency (I3) is essentially absent and was scored conservatively.

## Calibration Note

This is Djibouti's first individual 40-subdimension assessment; the published 20.3 was a bulk-import placeholder. Per the documented scrutiny bias, adversarial review usually pushes first-time assessed entities downward. Here it did not: the review found offsetting positive evidence (health insurance coverage, school enrollment) that the placeholder had not looked for. The near-zero delta should be read as the baseline surviving scrutiny, not as an absence of findings.
