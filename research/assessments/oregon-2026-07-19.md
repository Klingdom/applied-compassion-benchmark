## The short version

Oregon designs well and delivers badly. The state builds some of the most ambitious social policy in the country, then cannot show that it reached anyone. It is the first state in the nation to pay for housing, food and air conditioners with Medicaid money. It is also the state that spent roughly $800 million on drug treatment and cannot say how many people got treatment. Both facts are true at once, and the gap between them is the whole story of this score.

## Key Findings

- **Oregon spent roughly $800 million and cannot say who it helped.** The Secretary of State reported on 2025-12-17 that the Oregon Health Authority "cannot answer questions about how many people have received services" under Measure 110. This is the single clearest finding in the assessment and it drives Action down to 40 of 100. Money moved. Evidence of help did not follow.

- **Oregon leads the country on upstream thinking, and that is not a slogan.** It is the first state in the nation to use Medicaid to pay for housing, nutrition and climate needs, covering air conditioners, heaters, air filters and back-up generators for five named high-need groups. Systemic Thinking scores 65 of 100, tied for the state's highest dimension.

- **The state cannot reliably supply a lawyer to a person it charges with a crime.** More than 1,000 criminal cases were being dismissed as of 2026-03-06 for lack of counsel. Unrepresented defendants fell to about 1,200 from a May 2025 peak of 3,500, and Marion County cleared its crisis in April 2026 — real improvement from a serious failure.

- **Children in Oregon foster care were harmed at the highest rate ever recorded in 2025.** The rate reached 24.7 incidents per 100,000 care days against a federal target of 9.07, up 21.3% since 2022, while the federal review found Oregon out of conformity on every outcome. Oregon publishes this data itself, which is why the Accountability score is not lower.

- **Oregon keeps its promises to disabled workers and pays fines to break others.** It closed every sheltered workshop and moved 1,138 people into competitive jobs ahead of deadline. It also accrues over $2 million in per-person per-day court fines rather than meet a 7-day competency admission deadline. The difference is whether a court is still watching.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Awareness (65) and Systemic Thinking (65).** Oregon has an independently elected auditor that publishes damaging findings, a statutory Racial Justice Council advising on budgets, a 25-year-old tribal consultation statute, and the country's most ambitious upstream Medicaid design. The state is good at seeing problems and good at naming their causes.
- **Equity (60).** Prioritization of the most vulnerable is documented and structural rather than aspirational, and the Lane v. Brown completion is real reparative action.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Boundaries (35).** Oregon promised a treatment system it had not built, left 8 counties with no deflection option, and dismissed cases rather than provide alternatives. The state overstates what it can do and struggles to decline with dignity.
- **Action (40).** Ambitious design is not matched by outcome measurement, response-time performance, or reliable delivery.
- **Empathy (45) and Integrity (45).** Both are dragged down by the same pattern: harm reports get contested rather than validated, and staff who raise concerns face retaliation.

## Evidence Gaps

- **B5 Consent Orientation** rests on thin evidence and is marked low confidence. No independent review of consent quality in Oregon services was found. Scored at 2 under the conservative-default rule rather than estimated upward.
- **E1 Affective Resonance** lacks direct community testimony. Scoring relied on structural indicators of transactional treatment rather than on people describing their own experience.
- **Wildfire recovery** could not be assessed as state conduct. The available evidence concerns PacifiCorp litigation, not state services to survivors, so it informs only S4.
- **Public records performance** evidence is older than the preferred 2–3 year window and was weighted lightly in AB3.
- Only 13 web searches were available for this baseline. Coverage of corrections, education outcomes and state workforce culture is thinner than for behavioral health, and several subdimensions carry medium rather than high confidence as a result.

## Recommended Next Steps

Oregon lands in the **Functional** band: systems exist but have significant gaps. The specific gap is measurement and delivery, not intent or design.

- Consider [Advisory Support](/advisory) to translate these benchmark insights into strategic action, focusing on the Action and Boundaries dimensions.
- The highest-leverage single change is implementing the Secretary of State's recommendation to standardize data reporting and publish annual public performance reports. Three of the auditor's six recommendations remain unaccepted by the Oregon Health Authority; accepting and implementing them would lift AC3, A1 and AB1 together.

## Methodology and Limitations

Assessed under Compassion Benchmark methodology v1.2, all 8 dimensions and all 40 subdimensions, with a five-year trajectory window of 2021–2026 and the twelve months to 2026-07-19 weighted most heavily. Composite computed by `computeCompositeFromDimensions` in `site/scripts/lib/scoring.mjs`; no figure in this report, including the sensitivity case, was computed by hand.

Sensitivity case, run through the same function: raising Action to 3.0 and Boundaries to 2.8 yields `{"composite":53.1,"band":"Functional","integrationPremium":0}`. The band does not turn on the two weakest dimensions.

Scoring covers Oregon state conduct. HB 4002 deflection is delivered by counties at law-enforcement discretion; individual county program quality is not scored against the state, but the state's choice of an opt-in design that leaves 8 of 36 counties without any deflection pathway is scored, under EQ1, EQ4 and B4. Federal court orders (Lane v. Brown, Mink/Bowman) are scored on Oregon's compliance conduct, not their existence. The full attribution reasoning is recorded in the `attribution_note` field of the change proposal.
