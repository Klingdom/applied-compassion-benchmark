## Key Evidence (in-window, 2026)

- **ITUC 2026 Global Rights Index (Tier 4):** Bangladesh "moved out of the list of the world's 10 worst countries for workers' rights in 2026 for the first time since 2017," though it "remained in rating 5, 'no guarantee of rights'." The improvement was credited to November 2025 reforms permitting "unions to be established with the support of as few as 20 workers, replacing the previous requirement for approval from 20 per cent of a company's total workforce."
- **Labour reform substance (Tier 3-4):** 120 days paid maternity leave; anti-discrimination protections; right to refuse unsafe work; coverage extended to domestic, agricultural and shipbreaking workers; ratification of ILO Conventions C155 (OSH), C187, and C190 (violence and harassment); highlighted at the 114th ILC in Geneva, June 2026.
- **Persistent gaps (Tier 4):** ITUC notes "freedom of association remained heavily restricted because of regulatory barriers and strong resistance from employers," and that Special Economic Zone workers "were still systematically deprived of their right to form and join trade unions." April 2025 protests were dispersed with tear gas.

## Dimension Rationale (delta vs published)

- **BND 2.5 → 3.0 (B2 Autonomy / B-cluster):** Easier union formation and the right to refuse unsafe work materially strengthen worker autonomy and self-determination — a clear structural improvement, not merely stated commitment. Anchored at 3 ("≥1 program designed to build capacity"), not higher, because SEZ exclusion and employer resistance cap the gain.
- **ACC 2.5 → 2.75:** Anti-discrimination protections and harassment-convention alignment (C190) create new formal accountability mechanisms; verification by an independent international body (ITUC/ILO) supports a partial lift, but enforcement is unproven.
- **SYS 2.5 → 2.75 (S1 Root Cause / S4 Structural Critique):** The reform addresses structural drivers of worker suffering (registration thresholds, OSH) rather than symptoms — a genuine upstream intervention.
- **EQU 2.0 → 2.25 (EQ1 Universality):** Coverage extended to previously excluded categories (domestic, agricultural, shipbreaking) is a universality gain, tempered by continued SEZ exclusion.
- **INT 2.0 → 2.5 (I2 Non-Performance):** Reform enacted by an interim administration without an obvious reputational driver suggests genuine, not purely performative, commitment — but durability across the next elected government is untested (I5 caps the score).
- **AWR / EMP / ACT held at 2.5:** No in-window evidence of improved suffering detection, affective resonance, or service delivery; these remain at published levels.

### Recommendation
The published score appears understated relative to the verified 2026 labour reforms. A modest upgrade to ~39.8 is warranted, remaining within the Developing band given that ITUC retained Rating 5 ("no guarantee of rights") and SEZ workers remain excluded. No band crossing.

## Strongest Dimensions
- Boundaries (worker autonomy gains) and Accountability/Systemic (anti-discrimination, structural reform).

## Weakest Dimensions
- Equity (SEZ exclusion, marginalized-worker gaps) and Empathy (no new evidence).

## Evidence Gaps
- Implementation and enforcement data for the November 2025 reforms is not yet available; scores reflect the legal framework, not yet measured outcomes.
- Durability under the next elected government is untested (caps INT/I5).

## Recommended Next Steps
- **Developing band:** Re-assess after first enforcement data and a leadership transition to test resilience of the reform.
