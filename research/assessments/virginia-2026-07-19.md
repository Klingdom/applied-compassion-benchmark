## Key Findings

- **Virginia investigates itself unusually well, and sometimes acts on what it finds.** JLARC, the legislature's independent audit body, told the state in July 2023 that its school divisions get about 14% less than the national average — roughly $1,900 per student. Virginia then raised recurring K-12 funding by $1.85 billion, or 21.4%, between FY2023 and FY2026. Few states commission a report that damaging and then pay for the fix.

- **A crisis response system promised in 2020 still does not reach most of the state.** The Marcus Alert law was meant to send mental health responders instead of police. Six years later, 89 of Virginia's 133 localities are exempt, only one Community Services Board area has final approval, and the deadline has moved to 1 July 2028. The 2026 budget did add $11.4 million to fund the remaining 13 boards, which is real progress on a long failure.

- **Virginia spent its own money to soften federal cuts.** When enhanced federal health insurance subsidies expired, marketplace enrollment fell by 33,000 and the uninsured rate rose from 7.6% to 8.1%. The state put $150 million into premium assistance for up to 200,000 people, plus $3.5 million to help caseworkers handle Medicaid paperwork. That is a state choosing cost over convenience.

- **The state protects its own records better than it protects prisoners.** The working-papers exemption let the Governor's Office withhold records in a 2025 court case, and 133 localities run 133 different records systems. Meanwhile Charles Givens died at Marion Correctional in February 2022, other people there were hospitalized for hypothermia at least 13 times in three years, and the Commonwealth litigated for three years to a defence verdict rather than acknowledging harm.

- **The new administration's biggest single decision cut against its own workers.** On 14 May 2026 Governor Spanberger vetoed a bill giving collective bargaining rights to about 500,000 public employees, after campaigning for it. AFSCME called it a broken promise. This is the clearest gap between what Virginia says and what it does.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Awareness (65/100)** and **Systemic Thinking (65/100)**. Both rest on the same asset: JLARC produces independent, disaggregated, publicly damaging analysis, and the state's opioid and housing work is aimed upstream rather than only at symptoms.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Empathy (45/100)** and **Boundaries (45/100)**. State psychiatric hospitals run above safe capacity, people in crisis can lawfully be released after eight hours with nowhere to go, and the state's response to a death in custody was three years of litigation.

## Evidence Gaps

- No Virginia-specific published outcome data was found for 988 and mobile crisis response, which limits confidence on AC1 and E1.
- The Corrections Ombudsman office has produced no public report yet, so A4 is scored on its creation rather than its output.

## Cross-State Consistency Flag — FOIA

Virginia's public records regime is weak in the same specific way flagged in the open Michigan and Massachusetts consistency question: a broad exemption shields the executive and the legislature from disclosure. Virginia's working-papers exemption covers the Governor's Office and legislators, and Virginia is one of only two states that has never defined "personnel records." Unlike Michigan and Massachusetts, however, Virginia's legislature funds an independent audit body that publishes damaging findings about the executive, and agencies file mandatory public reports to the General Assembly.

**Scoring effect:** AB3 Transparency is held at 3, not 4, because the disclosure regime is genuinely restrictive. It is not dropped to 2, because JLARC and the RD-series reporting give the public real access to unflattering performance data through a different route. Assessors scoring Michigan or Massachusetts should apply the same test: ask whether an independent body publishes damaging findings, not only whether the records law is broad.
