## Key Findings

- **Wisconsin scores 53.1 out of 100 — the middle of the scale.** That places it in the Functional band, where systems exist but have significant gaps. Among states assessed at full depth on the same date it sits just above Colorado at 52.5 and just below Michigan and Delaware, both at 53.8.

- **The state broke a three-year deadlock on contaminated drinking water — but only after three years.** Governor Tony Evers signed 2025 Wisconsin Acts 200 and 201 on 2026-04-06, releasing about $125 million that the legislature's budget committee had withheld since 2023. The money funds roughly $80 million in local cleanup grants and $35 million to compensate people with poisoned wells. Getting there took a 2024 veto and two more years of exposure.

- **Wisconsin imprisons Black adults at the highest rate in the United States, and that has not changed.** One in 36 Black adults in Wisconsin is in prison. Black residents are 6% of the population and 42% of the prison population, imprisoned at nearly 12 times the white rate against a national figure near 5 times. The state measures this disparity carefully and has not narrowed it. This single fact is the main drag on the Empathy score of 2.8 out of 5.

- **The state finished a nine-year prison reform and then left the prison open.** A federal judge ended court oversight of the Lincoln Hills and Copper Lake youth facilities on 2026-01-29 after independent monitors confirmed all 50 required reforms were met. The same facilities were supposed to close by 1 January 2021 under state law. They are still open, five years late, with no firm closing date as of June 2026.

- **Wisconsin publishes evidence against itself, then lets its lawmakers delete theirs.** The Legislative Audit Bureau's March 2026 audit disclosed 3 material weaknesses, 17 significant deficiencies and $2.6 million in questioned costs. Meanwhile state legislators remain exempt from the records-retention part of the open records law and may delete emails. The governor's 2025 budget fix was stripped out by the budget committee and the loophole was still open in March 2026.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Systemic Thinking (3.6 / 65.0, Established)** — the only dimension above the Functional band. Wisconsin spends upstream (PFAS source remediation, opioid prevention), plans across agencies (the DHS–DOC reentry partnership, cabinet-wide tribal coordination), and shares resources downward to 87 local governments and 72 county agencies.
- **Equity (3.4 / 60.0)** — driven by two concrete wins: special education reimbursement rising from 30% to 45%, and the children's disability waitlist falling from about 2,600 children waiting three years to under 1,000 waiting about 70 days.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Empathy (2.8 / 45.0)** — worst-in-nation racial incarceration disparity plus a death from malnutrition and dehydration inside a state prison.
- **Boundaries (2.8 / 45.0)** — Wisconsin turns people away without alternatives. The January 2025 homeless count of 5,225 was the highest since 2016 while state shelter funding stayed flat after the proposed $24 million increase was cut.

## Evidence Gaps

- **Consent Orientation (B5) is scored at low confidence.** No independent review of consent quality in Wisconsin public programs was found. The score of 3 rests on published plain-language enrollment guidance rather than verified understanding.
- **Boundaries generally is thinly evidenced.** States rarely publish refusal protocols, warm-referral rates, or burnout data outside corrections. B2, B3 and B4 rest on program design documents rather than outcome data.
- **Act 10 litigation is unresolved and therefore not scored as an outcome.** A Dane County judge struck down parts of the 2011 collective bargaining law in December 2024. The Court of Appeals took the matter under submission on 2025-10-28 and a final decision is not expected in 2026. Note: the widely-surfaced WisBar article reporting that the Wisconsin Supreme Court upheld Act 10 is dated 2014-08-04 and does not describe current status.
- **Opioid decline attribution is contested.** National overdose deaths also fell, and most Wisconsin settlement money was unspent when the decline occurred. AC1 and S1 were scored accordingly and not credited with full causation.

This assessment scores Wisconsin's own conduct. Medicaid expansion is an optional state choice under federal law and the May 2025 rejection is fully scored. Federal cuts to homelessness and lead-screening funding are noted as context but not scored against the state; the state's own decision to hold shelter funding flat is scored. The Lincoln Hills consent decree was federally imposed, so compliance is scored as correction under external pressure rather than self-initiated correction. Opioid settlement funds arise from national litigation, but allocation decisions are Wisconsin's and are scored. The Milwaukee schools lead crisis was primarily a city and school-district matter; only the state DHS noncompliance order of 2025-03-19 and state screening support are scored.
