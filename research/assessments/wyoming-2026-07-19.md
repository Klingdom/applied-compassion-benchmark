## A note on scale, before the scores

Wyoming has about 585,000 residents. It is the smallest state in the country. Three things follow, and they shape every score below.

First, small absolute numbers are not evidence of small problems. Wyoming recorded 168 suicide deaths in 2024. In a state of 585,000 people that is a rate near the worst in the nation, not a small number.

Second, Wyoming has a very thin press market. **Absence of reporting is not evidence of good conduct.** Where no evidence exists, this assessment marks the subdimension low-confidence and says the evidence is missing. It does not infer good performance from silence, and it does not park the score in the middle to be safe.

Third, this assessment carries more low-confidence subdimensions than most. Nine of the 40 are low confidence. That is the honest outcome of researching a jurisdiction that generates less public documentation than larger states. It is a limitation of the evidence base, not a finding about Wyoming.

## Cross-state rule disposition: AB3 Transparency (v3)

**Disposition: declined. The precondition is not met on either limb.**

**Limb (a), structural transparency failure — not met.** No Wyoming branch has exempted itself from the Wyoming Public Records Act. The Public Records Ombudsman, created by the Legislature in 2019, has always sat inside the Governor's Office and has never held independence protections, so it has not been curtailed. Movement in 2025-2026 ran the other way: the Joint Corporations Committee voted to move the office to the State Auditor with a six-year term, and that bill failed introduction on a 16-15 vote in the 2026 budget session. A reform that fails is not an attack on the watchdog.

**Limb (b), surviving output from an independently funded oversight body — not met, and this is decisive.** The Legislative Service Office program evaluation function has produced no comprehensive evaluation since 2018. Staff were reduced and resources redirected to the budget fiscal division, which grew from four to nine over a decade. Under the Massachusetts output contingency, the cap of 3 holds only while an oversight body is still producing findings. Wyoming's is not.

The Idaho refinement also applies in reverse. Wyoming's problem is not a long list of enumerated carve-outs; it is the near-absence of a functioning independent evaluator.

**Scoring effect.** AB3 is therefore scored on the merits and lands at 3, not the floor of 2, because the executive branch does publish genuinely unflattering findings about itself without being compelled to. The cap and the merits converge on the same value, so the composite is 35.0 either way. Had the floor of 2 been imposed, ACC would fall from 2.6 to 2.4 and the composite to 34.4 — still Developing. The disposition is not band-determinative.

## Key Findings

- **Wyoming leaves its poorest working-age adults with no route to health coverage at all.** The state has not adopted Medicaid expansion. About 19,000 people would gain coverage; roughly 9,000 sit in the coverage gap, earning too much for unexpanded Medicaid and too little for Marketplace subsidies. This single choice is the largest driver of the score, producing the only 1-out-of-5 in the Equity dimension.

- **Access to childbirth is collapsing county by county, and the state has watched it happen.** Nine of Wyoming's 23 counties had no labor and delivery capacity as of 15 October 2025. Four hospitals — Evanston, Rawlins, Kemmerer and Wheatland — closed obstetric wards after 2022, leaving 16 birthing hospitals for 97,000 square miles. Wyoming detected the problem in a 2025 legislative study but has not reversed it.

- **Wyoming knows its suicide crisis in detail and has funded the response at a fraction of what it proposed.** The state recorded 168 suicide deaths in 2024, up from 156 in 2023, with the largest 10-year rate increase in the nation at 35 percent. In the 2024 budget session lawmakers cut a proposed 988 crisis-line trust fund from $40 million to $10 million.

- **Nobody in Wyoming is legally required to send an ambulance.** Emergency medical services carry no essential-service designation, unlike law enforcement and fire. The state's own 2025 Department of Health report put the EMS revenue gap at $30 million, and frontier residents can wait over 30 minutes. Thirteen states and Washington DC have made EMS essential; Wyoming has not.

- **The state's ability to check its own work has largely stopped functioning.** The Legislative Service Office has completed no comprehensive program evaluation since 2018. The Public Records Ombudsman is one person with 49 open cases who described herself as "so overloaded". A 2026 bill to give that office independence failed introduction by one vote, 16-15.

- **Wyoming tells the truth about itself more readily than it acts on it.** The Department of Health published its own $30 million EMS shortfall and its own 25 percent staff turnover. The state MMIP Task Force published the finding that Native residents are 8 times more likely to be murdered than white residents. These are uncompelled disclosures of damaging facts. What is missing is the funded response.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Accountability and Systemic Thinking, both 40/100.** Wyoming discloses unflattering findings about itself without being forced to, has run a five-year behavioural health redesign to completion, and built a long-horizon perpetuity fund into its 2026 health legislation. These are genuine strengths in an otherwise low-scoring profile — but 40 is still the top of the Developing band, not a good score.

- **Awareness, 40/100.** Detection is Wyoming's most functional capability. The state repeatedly finds and documents the harm it is not fixing.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Empathy, 25/100 — the lowest.** The 2026 budget bars the health department from spending any funds on abortion or gender transition care, including facilities, equipment and training. That is documented differential treatment by identity, written into law, and it scores at the floor. The state's first response to losing an abortion case in its own Supreme Court was to pass a new ban.

- **Equity, 30/100.** Non-expansion of Medicaid, nine counties without obstetric care, and no essential-service guarantee for ambulances combine into a pattern where the most access-challenged residents carry the most risk.

## Evidence Gaps

- **Nine of 40 subdimensions carry low confidence.** These are E1 Affective Resonance, B2 Autonomy Preservation, B4 Refusal Ethics, B5 Consent Orientation and AB5 Reparative Action, among others. In each case, evidence was absent rather than negative, and the score defaults to the lower anchor as the methodology requires.

- **Corrections is close to unresearchable from public sources.** No systematic data on deaths in custody at the Wyoming State Penitentiary, and no independent corrections oversight body, was found. Wyoming publishes inmate death notices individually. This is a genuine gap; it is not scored as either good or bad conduct, and no corrections-specific subdimension score rests on it.

- **Wyoming's thin press market limits verification.** Several findings rest on a single outlet. Where a claim could not be dated from a publisher's own URL, the evidence sidecar records month or year precision rather than inventing a day.

- **Community testimony is largely unavailable.** No Tier 5 community-verification evidence was located for any dimension except through the MMIP Task Force interviews. This caps several subdimensions at 3 that might otherwise be assessed higher with direct engagement.

## Recommended Next Steps

Wyoming scores 35.0/100 and sits in the **Developing** band, meaning key structures exist but are inconsistent or reactive. Consider a [Certified Assessment](/certified-assessments) for a structured improvement roadmap.

The highest-leverage single change is closing the coverage gap. Modelling that improvement alone — raising Equity from 2.2 to 3.2 — returns a composite of 38.1, still Developing. Wyoming cannot reach the Functional band on any single-dimension correction; it would require broad improvement across all eight.
