The assessed composite of 47.5 is 12.7 points below the published 60.2. The gap is concentrated in Integrity (published 3.0 raw, assessed 2.2) and Equity (published 3.3, assessed 2.6). Both movements trace to a single event — State Street Global Advisors removing its board gender-diversity requirement from its proxy voting policy after the 2024 US election — which the April 2026 baseline assessment had already reviewed and scored. Applying the published anchors strictly, abandoning a stated commitment under political pressure sits at or near anchor 1 for Consistency Under Pressure; the April assessment scored the dimension at 3.0. This is a disagreement about anchor application between two assessments of the same facts, not new information, and it is being reported as such rather than pushed through as a score change.

## Key Findings

- State Street dropped its board diversity requirement after political pressure, and that is the central compassion finding. Its asset management arm removed the requirement from its 2025 proxy voting policy following the change in US administration.
- The company gave away real money in 2025. The State Street Foundation made $26.8 million in total global giving, including $18.9 million to nonprofits working on education and employment outcomes, and employees volunteered 136,655 hours.
- The July 2026 antitrust lawsuit is an allegation, not a finding. Texas and ten other states allege coal-market manipulation through ESG practices; no court has decided the claim against State Street, and it is weighted accordingly.
- The lawsuit cuts both ways on compassion. Being sued by eleven states for maintaining climate-related stewardship is evidence that State Street holds at least some positions against its own short-term commercial interest.
- This reassessment disagrees with State Street's own three-month-old baseline by 12.7 points on essentially the same evidence. That is flagged for human review rather than applied, because assessor disagreement is not the same as institutional decline.

## Strongest Dimensions

Systemic Thinking (raw 3.4) and Awareness (raw 3.2). State Street publishes structured sustainability reporting and proxy voting disclosures, sustains multi-year upstream investment — over $8 million since 2020 in its Boston-area Early College initiative — and participates in industry coalitions. Long-Term Impact and Coalitional Compassion both score 4 of 5.

## Weakest Dimensions

Integrity (raw 2.2) and Equity (raw 2.6). Consistency Under Pressure, Internal Consistency, Values Alignment and Resilience of Care all score 2 of 5. The board-diversity retreat, framed as a decision "not to be prescriptive," reads as a commitment withdrawn when it became politically costly rather than a considered change of view.

## Evidence Gaps

Employee-side evidence rests on Glassdoor review aggregates, which are tier-2 at best and cannot be verified individually; Boundaries B1 and Integrity I3 are scored with low confidence as a result, and this is a material limitation given how much of the delta they carry. State Street's actual workforce demographic disclosures and any pay-equity audit results were not obtained. No community testimony about the Early College initiative's outcomes was available, so Efficacy (AC3) is scored on inputs rather than results. The antitrust case has produced no merits ruling, so no scoring weight is placed on the underlying conduct allegations.

## Calibration Note

DO NOT APPLY AS A ONE-OFF DOWNGRADE. State Street's published 60.2 is NOT a bulk-import placeholder. It was set on 2026-04-20 by a documented first-time baseline assessment that already corrected the score down 32.3 points from an erroneous 92.5, and that assessment explicitly weighed BOTH the board-diversity retreat AND the Glassdoor layoff-culture evidence that drive this reassessment's lower Integrity and Boundaries scores. The only genuinely NEW within-window evidence is the 2026-07-17 Texas-led antitrust suit, which is an unproven allegation, not a finding — and under the benchmark's own allegation-versus-ruling standard it should carry limited weight before a court decides. A -12.7 point move three months after a recent, evidence-based baseline, on substantially the same evidence, is far more likely to be assessor-to-assessor variance than a real change in State Street's conduct. Per screening rule 5, that is a calibration concern and must be routed to review, not encoded as a one-off reset. Recommendation is flag-for-review, and the published 60.2 should stand until a reviewer reconciles the two assessments.

## Recommendation

**FLAG-FOR-REVIEW** (confidence: low). Composite delta -12.7 points vs published. Change proposal filed.
