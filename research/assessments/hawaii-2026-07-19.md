### Score Difference Analysis

The gap is a data defect, not institutional decline. The published 95.9 was never produced by an assessor. It came from a broken legacy extraction that assigned near-uniform 4.5s, and those uniform values triggered the full 10-point integration premium, which is why the published number sits in the Exemplary band. Under the evidence, all 8 dimensions fall below 4.0, the weakness factor drops to 0, and the premium disappears entirely. About 10 of the 39.6-point difference is the premium alone.

**BND, the largest single gap at -52.5.** A published 4.5 implies Hawaii helps in ways that build capacity, communicates its limits honestly, and refuses with dignity. The evidence contradicts each element. Front Street owners were asked to commit to rebuilding without final rules or timelines. State hospital patients are released before treatment finishes because of a statutory clock. Corrections workers describe a working environment that is making them ill. The 2,600 Hawaiian Home Lands awards in 2025 were largely leases on lots that do not yet exist.

**ACT and INT, both -37.5.** The published 4.5s imply timely, proportionate, values-consistent delivery. Hawaii took 19 months to make the first payment from its own victims' fund, and had completed 234 of roughly 2,200 destroyed Lahaina structures at the three-year mark. On integrity, the state continues to hold about 23% of its incarcerated population in a private prison in Arizona while framing its social policy in the language of ohana and kauhale. A bill under consideration in 2026 would not require even a quarter of them home until 2029.

**ACC and SYS, the smallest gaps at -17.5 each.** These are Hawaii's genuinely strong areas and the placeholder was closest to right here, though still too high. The Attorney General commissioned an independent investigation of the state's own conduct and published all three phases including the unflattering ones. Act 96 taxes tourism, the state's dominant industry, to pay for climate resilience. Both are real, verifiable, and rare among US states.

## Key Findings

- **Hawaii's published rank of #1 in the nation rests on a number no one ever calculated.** The evidence-based composite is 56.3 out of 100, which is Functional, not Exemplary. The 39.6-point drop corrects a data error. It does not describe any change in how Hawaii treats people.

- **Hawaii investigated its own failure in the Lahaina fire and published what it found.** Attorney General Anne Lopez hired an outside fire research institute to examine the state's own response. The result was 84 findings and 140 recommendations, released in three public phases through January 2025. Very few US states have voluntarily documented their own conduct in a disaster that killed 102 people.

- **The state paid before a court made it pay.** Hawaii committed $808 million to the $4.04 billion Maui settlement and put $65 million into the One 'Ohana Fund while liability was still unresolved. As of June 2026, 79 death and serious-injury claims had been paid, totalling $111.5 million.

- **Hawaii sends about 870 of its own residents to a private prison in Arizona, 2,600 miles away.** That is close to 23% of everyone the state incarcerates. A 2026 bill would require only a quarter of them returned by July 2029. This is the sharpest gap between what Hawaii says about family and what it does.

- **Delivery is slow even when the money is committed.** The first payment from the state's own victims' fund came 19 months after the fire. At the three-year mark, 234 of roughly 2,200 destroyed Lahaina structures had been rebuilt.

- **Hawaii taxed its biggest industry to pay for climate resilience.** Act 96, signed May 2025, created the first climate impact fee in the United States, raising about $100 million a year. The state defended it in federal court against a cruise industry challenge and won in December 2025.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Accountability (70.0)** and **Systemic Thinking (70.0)**. Hawaii self-investigated a mass-casualty failure, published the findings, shared them with the national fire service through the US Fire Administration, and paid substantial compensation ahead of adjudication. It also spends upstream: the Green Fee, sea level rise planning to 2100, and an explicit housing-as-health-care model that links homelessness spending to emergency room cost.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Boundaries (35.0)**, the only dimension in the Developing band. Hawaii struggles to tell people honestly what it can and cannot do, to decline with alternatives, and to sustain the workforce doing the helping. Corrections staff conditions, the state hospital revolving door, the Front Street rules vacuum, and the paper-lease approach to the homestead waitlist all point the same way.
- **Action (50.0)** and **Integrity (50.0)**. Hawaii commits real money and then delivers slowly, and its mainland incarceration practice sits directly against its stated cultural values.

## Evidence Gaps

- No independent statewide assessment of frontline public-sector wellbeing exists outside corrections, so B1 rests on HCSOC and workforce vacancy data rather than a dedicated study.
- Consent practices in state service delivery (B5) are thinly documented. The sea level rise disclosure law is the clearest available signal; the score is marked low confidence.
- No recent independent audit of the Office of Language Access was found after 2022, so A2 relies on that finding plus the absence of contrary evidence.
- Community testimony about whether the One 'Ohana Fund process felt fair to claimants is not systematically published, which caps AB5 at 4 rather than 5.

## Recommended Next Steps

Hawaii sits in the **Functional** band. Systems exist and several are genuinely distinctive, but they have significant gaps in delivery speed, boundary-setting, and internal consistency. Consider [Advisory Support](/advisory) to translate these benchmark findings into strategic action, particularly on the Boundaries dimension, which is the single largest correctable weakness.
