### Score Difference Analysis

**ACC (+42.5, the largest gap).** The placeholder implied Texas has almost no accountability infrastructure. That is wrong. The Sunset Advisory Commission has reviewed state agencies on a statutory cycle since 1977 and publishes staff reports critical of the agencies under review. The State Auditor publishes annual workforce data including unflattering figures such as a 21.8% turnover rate in one salary schedule. House Bill 30 in 2023 closed the "dead suspect loophole," making law-enforcement records available even where no conviction occurred, and it became law without the governor's signature. Texas also learned measurably from two catastrophes: five consecutive winters of mandatory weatherization inspections after the 2021 grid failure, and a flood siren regime whose first real test in July 2026 coincided with 2 deaths in the same region that lost 139 people in July 2025. These are Tier 4 findings and they are not close calls. The offsetting evidence is equally real — falsified prison heat logs, DPS withholding Uvalde records, $118 million against a $1.5 billion cooling need — which is why the dimension lands at 3.0 rather than higher.

**INT (+32.5).** The placeholder implied compassionate practice in Texas is entirely personality-dependent. The Sunset Commission and the Public Information Act, dating to 1977 and 1973, have survived many governors without degradation. Proposition 4 binds $1 billion a year of sales tax revenue through 2047, a cost accepted rather than avoided. Values alignment and internal consistency remain weak, which holds the dimension to 2.6.

**AWR (+30.0) and SYS (+25.0).** The placeholder gave no credit for detection or long-horizon thinking. Texas runs mandatory pre-season grid inspections, a statutory maternal mortality committee, and now a Water Development Board assessment of 30 flood-prone counties. Proposition 4 is a 20-year dedicated revenue stream tied to a periodically revised State Water Plan. Both dimensions are nonetheless capped below 4.0 by a large and specific failure: the state declined to review the 2022-2023 pregnancy-related deaths, and it does not question the structures producing its own uninsured population.

**ACT (+25.0).** House Bill 2 delivered $8.5 billion to public schools in 2025, including nearly $2 billion restructuring special education around service intensity. Proposition 4 committed $20 billion to water. The placeholder captured none of this. Benefits-delivery failure — 90,000 backlogged applications, 38-day average determinations against a 30-day standard — keeps the dimension below Functional-high.

**EQU (+12.5, smallest gap).** Here the placeholder was closest to right, and this assessment confirms near-floor performance. Texas has the highest uninsured rate in the United States at 16.7% and accounts for roughly 42% of everyone in the national coverage gap. EQ1 scores 1 of 5 on the evidence, the lowest single subdimension score in this assessment.

### Recommendation

The published Texas score of 14.1 is substantially understated and should be revised to 38.8, band Developing. The revision reflects the replacement of an unevidenced placeholder with a first evidence-based baseline. It does not represent measured institutional improvement, and it must not be reported as such.

## Key Findings

- **Texas is not a bottom-of-the-table state, and the published score saying it was had no evidence behind it.** The evidence-based composite is 38.8 of 100, band Developing — above Arizona at 36.2 and Ohio at 38.1, and well below North Carolina at 45.0. The published 14.1 was a bulk-import placeholder.

- **Texas has genuinely strong public accountability machinery, which is the single most counter-intuitive finding here.** The Sunset Advisory Commission has reviewed state agencies on a statutory cycle since 1977. The State Auditor publishes unflattering agency turnover data every year. In 2023, House Bill 30 closed a loophole that had let police withhold records when a suspect died. Accountability scores 3.0 of 5, one of Texas's two highest dimensions.

- **When Texas is shown a catastrophe, it does change — and the change is measurable.** After 139 people died in the July 2025 Hill Country floods, the legislature passed Senate Bill 3, funding sirens and forecasting. In July 2026, flooding hit the same region and killed 2. After the 2021 grid collapse, mandatory weatherization inspections have now run five consecutive winters, and 2026 storm outages tracked at or below 2022 levels.

- **On equity, Texas is near the bottom of the scale, and this is a matter of state choice.** Texas has the highest uninsured rate in the United States at 16.7%, and 21.6% among working-age adults. Roughly 42% of all Americans in the Medicaid coverage gap live in Texas. Multiple expansion bills failed in the 2025 session. Equity scores 1.8 of 5.

- **The state makes people wait for help without telling them whether help will ever come.** As of March 2026, more than 198,000 people sat on state interest lists for home and community-based care, with waits of 17 to 18 years. State officials confirmed they do not check eligibility before adding someone to a list. Scope Clarity scores 1 of 5, the lowest subdimension alongside universality.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Accountability (3.0/5)** and **Awareness (3.0/5)**. Both rest on statutory review bodies — the Sunset Commission and the State Auditor — that produce and publish critical findings on a fixed schedule regardless of who holds office. Systemic learning after the 2021 grid failure and the 2025 floods scored 4 of 5.
- **Systemic Thinking (2.8/5)**, carried by Proposition 4's binding 20-year, $20 billion water commitment — a rare long-horizon structural investment among the states assessed so far.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Equity (1.8/5)**. Driven by the highest uninsured rate in the nation, a coverage gap of roughly 617,000 adults with no state pathway, and interest lists that grew while reserves hit their statutory cap.
- **Empathy (2.2/5)** and **Boundaries (2.2/5)**. Benefits backlogs dating to August 2023, and a queue system that state officials confirm does not screen for eligibility before people join it.

## Evidence Gaps

- No direct community testimony was available at Tier 5 for most subdimensions. Affective Resonance, Cultural Empathy and Refusal Ethics rest on outcome and process data rather than the accounts of those served, and are marked medium or high confidence on that narrower basis.
- The federal prison heat trial concluded in April 2026 with no ruling issued as of this assessment date. Scores reflect established facts in the record, not a predicted outcome.
- The current Sunset review cycle for the Department of State Health Services and the Texas Workforce Commission runs through December 2026, so its findings could not be scored.
- The 2026 court monitor reports in the foster care litigation cover 2023-2024 activity, so the most recent foster care conduct is assessed with a lag.
- Self-Sustainability carries the widest internal spread of any subdimension: the State Auditor's improving turnover data and the National Guard deaths on Operation Lone Star point in opposite directions. It is marked medium confidence.

## Band Stability

The composite of 38.8 sits 1.2 points below the Developing/Functional boundary at 40. That is outside the 1.0-point instability threshold, but close enough to note. Sensitivity cases, all run through `computeCompositeFromDimensions` rather than computed by hand: raising Equity from 1.8 to 2.8 returns `{"composite":41.9,"band":"Functional"}`; raising Accountability from 3.0 to 4.0 returns `{"composite":41.9,"band":"Functional"}`; adding 0.5 to every dimension returns `{"composite":51.3,"band":"Functional"}`. A single dimension moving one full point in either direction would change the band, so the Developing classification should be treated as firm but not comfortable.

## Recommended Next Steps

- **Developing band**: Consider a [Certified Assessment](/certified-assessments) for a structured improvement roadmap. The evidence points to two tractable priorities: closing the scope-clarity failure in interest-list administration, which requires process change rather than appropriation, and extending the demonstrated disaster-learning loop into benefits delivery, where the state has the fiscal capacity but not yet the feedback mechanism.
