## Own-conduct evidence (within window)

- **Transparent disclosure.** Anthropic published a public statement (anthropic.com/news/fable-mythos-access) and an X post the same day, plainly describing the directive, its scope (all foreign nationals incl. its own foreign-national employees), and the timing (directive received 5:21pm ET on launch day). This is high-transparency conduct (supports ACC/AB3).
- **Principled, public dispute of proportionality.** Anthropic disagreed that a "narrow potential jailbreak" should trigger recall of a model deployed to hundreds of millions, and argued government should be able to block unsafe deployments only via a "transparent, fair, and technically grounded statutory process" (Fortune, Time, Jun 13). Consistency-under-pressure signal (supports INT/I1).
- **Compliance despite cost.** Anthropic complied with the directive (disabled the models) while contesting it — bearing immediate enterprise/customer cost. Four bipartisan Congress members sent a letter Jun 18 to Commerce demanding explanation by Jun 26. Dario Amodei joined G7 voluntary AI safety commitments (Jun 17).
- **Customer-harm exposure.** Enterprises that moved production workloads onto the models in the 3-day window faced abrupt disruption "without notice" (Qz/ebuildersecurity) — but this disruption flows from the government directive, not an Anthropic choice; access to all other models was preserved.
