## Key Findings

- A major bank is walking away, and that is the news. Citizens Financial Group will exit credit facilities to CoreCivic and GEO Group, ending a relationship with CoreCivic that dates to 2011.
- The campaign that pushed the bank cites deaths. Organisers of the De-ICE Citizens Bank Coalition say Citizens financed about $2.5 billion for the two companies, and that more than 20 people have died in ICE facilities they own.
- The bank did not say it was a moral decision. Citizens cited "changed commercial circumstances," and CoreCivic has been selling facilities directly to the federal government, so the financing may simply be less needed.
- CoreCivic's score does not move, because nothing about CoreCivic changed. A lender leaving is a judgment about CoreCivic, not an action by CoreCivic, and this assessment scores conduct.
- The bottom of the index is evidentially earned. Thirty of forty subdimensions score 1 out of 5, and the confirmation at 7.0 reflects a business whose core product is detention capacity sold by the bed.

## Strongest Dimensions

Action (raw 1.4) at 1.4, and within it Resource Mobilization at 2 of 5. CoreCivic operates large facilities at scale and meets its contractual staffing and capacity obligations to federal agencies. Transparency (AB3) also reaches 2 of 5 through mandatory SEC disclosure. These are the only areas where anything above the floor can be evidenced, and none of them reflects compassion — they reflect operational and regulatory competence.

## Weakest Dimensions

Equity (raw 1) and Systemic Thinking (raw 1), both at the floor of 1.0. All ten subdimensions in those two dimensions score 1 of 5. Structural Critique scores 1 because CoreCivic's commercial interest runs directly against reducing the detained population — the company cannot question the structure that generates its revenue. Autonomy Preservation, Refusal Ethics and Consent Orientation are all 1: detained people cannot decline the service.

## Evidence Gaps

The "more than 20 deaths" figure comes from an advocacy coalition and is a tier-1 source; it was not independently verified against ICE detention death records in this cycle, and the assessment treats it as corroborating rather than determinative. CoreCivic's own facility-level incident data, medical staffing ratios and grievance resolution rates are not public. No detainee testimony was obtained directly. Employee treatment evidence is essentially absent, so Self-Sustainability and Internal Consistency are scored 2 on absence rather than on findings. A fuller assessment would require the facility inspection reports produced by the DHS Office of Inspector General.

## Calibration Note

CoreCivic's published 7.0 was set on 2026-04-19 by a documented assessment, not a bulk import, and the current reassessment reproduces it within 2.6 points. This is a genuine confirmation. Reviewers should note the directional discipline applied here: the within-window trigger is a bank withdrawing, which is negative sentiment toward CoreCivic but not new CoreCivic conduct, and it was therefore NOT used to push the score lower. Equally, CoreCivic's sale of facilities to the federal government — arguably a reduction in its own role — was not treated as positive conduct, because divesting an asset is a commercial transaction rather than compassionate action. The score holds at 7.0.
