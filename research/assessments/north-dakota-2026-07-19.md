## What this assessment found, in plain terms

North Dakota was published at 25 out of 100. That number was never researched. It was a placeholder copied across five states at once. This is North Dakota's first assessment built on actual evidence, and it scores 52.5 out of 100.

The state does two things unusually well for its size. It hired outside researchers to examine its own mental health system, published the unflattering result, and has kept working on it for eight years across two governors. And it rebuilt its prisons around treating people humanely, ending solitary confinement on its own, before any court made it.

It also carries one of the widest health gaps in the country. American Indians in North Dakota die 22.5 years younger than white residents.

### Score Difference Analysis

**Every difference above has the same cause, and it is not institutional change.** The published 25.0 is a bulk-import placeholder with `last_assessed: null`. Idaho, Indiana, Missouri, North Dakota and South Dakota all carry composite exactly 25.0 with byte-identical dimension vectors. Five states, one invented number. It was never about North Dakota, so there is nothing in it to agree or disagree with, dimension by dimension.

The only useful comparison is therefore whether 52.5 is defensible on its own terms. Three findings do most of the work in lifting the state well above the placeholder.

**The behavioral health learning loop (AWR, ACC, ACT).** In 2018 North Dakota paid the Human Services Research Institute to examine its own mental health system. The report said the state had too little prevention and leaned too hard on inpatient and residential care. The state published it. The Legislature's interim Human Services Committee then formally studied whether the recommendations had actually been implemented, commissioned a second study in 2022 on acute psychiatric need, and sized a $300 million State Hospital replacement to that second study. The Behavioral Health Planning Council was still meeting and publishing dashboards on 15 April 2026. That is an eight-year chain from uncompelled self-criticism to capital allocation, spanning two governors. Placeholder scores of 2.0 on AWR and ACC cannot survive it.

**Corrections (ACC, EMP, INT, SYS).** North Dakota ended solitary confinement and rebuilt officer training around treating people humanely, under a programme it named Increasing Humanity for People in Prison. A peer-reviewed case study in *Health & Justice* documents that this was self-initiated rather than court-ordered. The penitentiary population fell 6.5%. Other states have since copied the model. This is the strongest single piece of evidence in the assessment, and it is precisely the kind of conduct a placeholder cannot see.

**The equity picture is genuinely mixed, and that is why EQU lands at 3.0 rather than higher.** North Dakota adopted Medicaid expansion in 2014 and has defended it through repeated sunset votes — a real cost borne. At the same time, American Indians in the state die 22.5 years younger than white residents, and are 19% of the prison population while being about 5% of adults. The state measures these gaps well and has begun to legislate against them, notably the three 2025 MMIP laws. It has not yet moved them.

**Small-state caution was applied and mattered.** North Dakota has under 800,000 residents. Its absolute numbers are small — 3,835 people experiencing homelessness, 47 open prison security posts — but the assessment read these per capita and against state fiscal capacity, not as evidence of low need. Thin press coverage was treated as an evidence gap, not as good conduct: six of forty subdimensions carry low confidence for exactly this reason.

**The published score is a data defect and should be replaced, not adjusted.** North Dakota should be updated to composite 52.5, band Functional, with dimensions AWR 3.4, EMP 2.8, ACT 3.2, EQU 3.0, BND 2.6, ACC 3.4, SYS 3.4, INT 3.0.

## Key Findings

- **North Dakota's published score of 25 out of 100 was never researched.** It is a placeholder shared byte-for-byte with Idaho, Indiana, Missouri and South Dakota. This assessment is the state's first evidence-based baseline, at 52.5 out of 100. The 27.5-point rise corrects a data error and means nothing about whether the state improved.

- **The state criticized itself in public before anyone made it.** North Dakota hired the Human Services Research Institute to review its mental health system, published the 2018 finding that it underinvested in prevention and overused inpatient care, and has kept a council working on that plan through April 2026 — eight years and two governors later.

- **North Dakota ended solitary confinement on its own initiative.** The Department of Corrections rebuilt training around treating incarcerated people humanely, a change documented in peer-reviewed research as self-started rather than court-ordered. The penitentiary population fell 6.5%, and other states have copied the model.

- **American Indians in North Dakota die 22.5 years younger than white residents.** Peer-reviewed analysis of state data for 2010–2019 found 57% of American Indian deaths came before age 65, against 21% statewide. Native people are about 5% of adults and 19% of prisoners. The state measures this gap unusually well and has not closed it.

- **The state's biggest 2025 spending decision was untargeted.** The Legislature sent roughly $409 million of Legacy Fund earnings to a property tax credit paid on primary residences regardless of income, while allocating $10 million for homelessness — half of it spent within the first year against 3,835 people experiencing homelessness.

## Strongest Dimensions

- **Accountability (60.0) and Systemic Thinking (60.0).** Both rest on the same two facts: a documented eight-year learning loop in behavioral health, and a self-initiated correction in corrections that no court required. The Legacy Fund adds a genuine multi-decade planning horizon that few states of any size can match.
- **Awareness (60.0).** North Dakota detects suffering well. It commissions outside reviews of itself, runs an annual suicide fatality review, and keeps mortality data detailed enough for outside researchers to quantify a 22.5-year life expectancy gap.

## Weakest Dimensions

- **Boundaries (40.0).** The lowest dimension. Emergency shelter capacity falls well short of need, there is no evidence of structured alternatives when the state cannot help, and consent practice is largely unevidenced — with a gender-affirming care ban that criminalizes clinicians for providing care families consent to.
- **Empathy (45.0).** Real tribal consultation structures exist and produced law in 2025. They sit alongside two categories of care removed by statute and a carceral disparity the state documents but has not reduced.

## Evidence Gaps

- **Six of forty subdimensions carry low confidence** (A5, E4, AC1, B3, B4, B5). North Dakota's small press market produces thinner coverage than larger states, and the assessment treated that thinness as missing evidence rather than as evidence of good conduct.
- **No published response-time standards** were found for state services, which capped AC1 at 3.
- **No independent audit of state workforce sustainability** was found. B1 rests on agency budget documents and sector reporting rather than third-party assessment.
- **No community testimony at Tier 5** was located confirming whether tribal nations regard the 2025 MMIP laws and repatriation funding as adequate repair. This is the single most valuable missing evidence; it caps EQ5, AB5 and A4 at 3 and would move several scores if obtained.
- **Free Through Recovery outcome data is reported by the state itself.** No independent evaluation was found, which caps AC3 at 3.

## Recommended Next Steps

North Dakota lands in the **Functional** band: systems exist but have significant gaps.

- Consider [Advisory Support](/advisory) to translate these benchmark insights into strategic action, particularly on the Boundaries dimension, where the gap between the state's fiscal capacity and its refusal-ethics and consent practice is widest.
- The single highest-value action for the score and for the population is to pair the state's strong measurement of the Native health and incarceration gaps with a published, resourced closure target. North Dakota already does the hard part — it measures honestly. EQ3, EQ5 and E3 all move on evidence of correction, not detection.
