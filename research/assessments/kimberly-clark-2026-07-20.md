## Assessment Context

Kimberly-Clark was surfaced by the 2026-07-20 scan on a fairness-hearing step in its $2.25 million settlement of a 401(k) excessive-fee class action covering about 25,000 plan participants, and on roughly 770 job cuts at Kenvue ahead of Kimberly-Clark's more than $40 billion acquisition. Both are modest signals. The more substantive compassion evidence for a company that makes toilet paper, nappies and menstrual products lies in whether it extends access to those essentials to people who cannot afford them — and on that measure Kimberly-Clark performs comparatively well.

The assessed composite of 50 is 10.9 points below the published 60.9. Action (assessed 3.4) and Systemic Thinking (assessed 3.4) land close to the placeholder and are the strongest dimensions. Equity (assessed 3) actually exceeds the published 3.0 raw, because the placeholder undervalued programmes that remove real access barriers — the Toilets Change Lives sanitation programme and 11.8 million donated period products. Boundaries (assessed 2.6) and Accountability (assessed 2.8) fall furthest, driven by the retirement-plan fee failure and by the workforce reduction at Kenvue.

## Key Findings

- Kimberly-Clark reaches people who cannot afford basics, which is the heart of its compassion profile. Its Toilets Change Lives programme has improved sanitation access for nearly four million people since 2014, and it has donated 11.8 million period products.
- It let its own employees overpay for their retirement plan. Participants alleged recordkeeping fees of about $78 each when comparable services were available at roughly a third of that price; Kimberly-Clark settled for $2.25 million, about 15 percent of estimated maximum losses.
- The settlement is small relative to the harm, which caps Accountability. Paying 15 cents on the dollar without admission is closer to a minimal legal resolution than to genuine repair.
- Job cuts are coming through the merger, not the core business. Kenvue, which Kimberly-Clark is acquiring for more than $40 billion, announced a roughly 3.5 percent global workforce reduction.
- The 10.9 point gap is a measurement artifact. Kimberly-Clark had a flat 3.5 placeholder in all eight areas; genuine review found variation from 2.6 to 3.4, with Equity scoring above the placeholder.

## Strongest Dimensions

Action (raw 3.4) and Systemic Thinking (raw 3.4). Resource Mobilization, Follow-Through, Root Cause Orientation, Long-Term Impact and Coalitional Compassion all score 4 of 5. The Toilets Change Lives programme has run continuously since 2014, and the partnership with UNICEF Indonesia on menstrual health and WASH facilities is co-designed rather than donated — which is why Cultural Empathy and Access Design also reach 4.

## Weakest Dimensions

Boundaries (raw 2.6) and Accountability (raw 2.8). Consent Orientation, Self-Sustainability, Harm Acknowledgment and Reparative Action all score 2 of 5. The common thread is that Kimberly-Clark treats people outside the company better than people inside it: plan participants had to sue to surface a fee problem the company was positioned to see first.

## Evidence Gaps

Independent verification of the Toilets Change Lives beneficiary figures was not available; those numbers come from company reporting, so Efficacy (AC3) is credited at 3 rather than higher. No recent employee engagement or Glassdoor aggregate was verified, leaving Internal Consistency (I3) scored primarily on the ERISA case. Kimberly-Clark's supply-chain labour auditing results and any disaggregated workforce equity data were not obtained, so Bias Awareness (EQ3) and Historical Harm Acknowledgment (EQ5) are scored 2 on absence of evidence. Several sustainability sources date to 2022 or earlier and may understate current progress.

## Calibration Note

MEASUREMENT GAP, NOT A DECLINE — and note the trigger is weaker than the scan implies. Kimberly-Clark has never been individually assessed; the published 60.9 is a bulk-import placeholder with a near-flat 3.5. The -10.9 gap is largely that arithmetic difference. On the trigger itself: the $2.25 million 401(k) excessive-fee settlement is real, but it was finally approved in July 2025, and the July 2026 item the scan surfaced is a subsequent procedural step rather than a new finding. A recordkeeping-fee settlement covering roughly 25,000 participants at about 15 percent of estimated maximum losses is a genuine but minor governance failure — it is one data point in a forty-part assessment, not the assessment. Kimberly-Clark's actual conduct record in the window is mixed-to-positive: sustained sanitation and menstrual-health access programmes against a workforce reduction at the company it is acquiring. Recommendation is flag-for-review for cohort calibration, not a conduct-based downgrade.
