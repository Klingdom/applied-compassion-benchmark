## Key Findings

- Why it matters: Kuwait City is a wealthy capital with strong services for citizens, but its non-citizen majority — migrant and domestic workers — remains trapped in the kafala sponsorship system that Human Rights Watch says leaves them exposed to abuse and forced labor.
- In July 2025 Kuwait tightened, not loosened, control: migrant workers now need employer permission to leave the country. That deepens dependency and drives the low Boundaries score.
- The July 2026 Iranian strike on a Kuwaiti desalination plant, which killed an 11-year-old girl, is harm inflicted on the city from outside; it is screened out of the city’s own conduct score.

## Strongest Dimensions

- Awareness and Systemic Thinking — well-resourced utilities and long-horizon capital planning for citizens.

## Weakest Dimensions

- Boundaries and Integrity — the kafala system and the 2025 exit-permit rule entrench dependency for the non-citizen majority.

## Evidence Gaps

- Distinguishing municipal from national policy in a small city-state is difficult; kafala is national policy administered locally. No independent municipal service-equity audit was available.
