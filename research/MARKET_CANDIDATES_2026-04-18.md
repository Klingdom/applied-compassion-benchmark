## Part 1: Who Is Likely to Pay

### Buyer Segment Analysis

**ESG / Sustainability Teams at Corporations**
Signal: Moderate. These teams are budget-constrained in 2025-2026 due to ESG rollback pressure (Target, Walmart, Ford DEI programs scaled back). However, the segment still exists at Fortune 500 companies that have *not* rolled back DEI — they face competitive and reputational pressure and need third-party validation. The Compassion Benchmark's independence is a genuine asset here. Weakness: these teams typically consume Bloomberg/Refinitiv/MSCI feeds, not boutique research PDFs.

**Activist Investors / Mission-Driven Funds**
Signal: Strong for the right ICP. Institutions like Calvert Research, Trillium Asset Management, Parnassus Investments, Zevin Asset Management, and similar ESG-integrated equity funds need differentiated data. MSCI ESG Ratings and Sustainalytics both measure governance and environment through a risk lens. The Compassion Benchmark measures something different: institutional character under pressure, using primary-source evidence (court filings, enforcement actions, regulatory outcomes) rather than ESG questionnaires. This is the most credible gap to exploit. No direct competitor holds this position.

**Institutional Risk Teams**
Signal: Moderate-to-strong. The pipeline's AI Labs findings (OpenAI compound -30.3 pts in 72 hours, Illinois SB 3444) are directly relevant to enterprise risk teams at companies deploying AI tools. The finding that ChatGPT was queried immediately before a mass-casualty event — and the company's simultaneous lobbying for a liability shield — is the kind of signal that should show up in vendor risk assessments. These teams buy from Bloomberg, Refinitiv, and RepRisk. Entering that channel is a medium-term play, not immediate.

**Journalists / Think Tanks / Academic**
Signal: Strong for volume, weak for revenue. The daily digest content (CVS -18.7, Target -10.0, OpenAI -30.3 compound) is exactly what investigative journalists use. Think tanks (Brookings, Roosevelt Institute, Data & Society, AI Now) cite benchmark data. This segment pays $0-195. Value: builds citation authority and earned media that converts to institutional buyers. Priority: high for visibility, low for direct revenue.

**Entities Themselves (Right of Reply / Improvement Intelligence)**
Signal: Emerging and underexplored. The Target finding is the clearest illustration: Target published a score of 92.5, gets a -10.0 on the DEI rollback. That is information Target's ESG or communications team would pay to understand *before* it becomes public. The right product is not a "change your score" service (which violates the independence policy) but a "know your score before publication" early-access or a "peer benchmarking brief" that helps them understand where they rank. Needs careful framing.

**HR / DEI Leaders Post-Target Signal**
Signal: Concentrated and timely. The Target DEI rollback finding is a specific, named event in an Exemplary-band company that produces a -10.0 delta. HR and DEI leaders at Fortune 500 companies who did *not* roll back DEI have a direct interest in that analysis — it validates their decision. HR leaders at companies that *did* roll back may want to understand the score implications. This is the highest-immediacy ICP for the DEI angle.

**AI Governance Teams**
Signal: Strong and growing. Illinois SB 3444 (April 24 vote), EU AI Act enforcement (August 2, 2026), and the OpenAI compound decline make AI governance the most active topic in the AI Labs index. Enterprise AI governance leads, trust and safety teams, and AI ethics officers at deploying organizations (not just labs) are the buyers. They have budget (these are typically Risk or Legal budget lines), they need independent data, and no other source produces primary-source evidence-based AI lab scores updated in near-real time.

## Evidence Gaps and Open Questions

- **LinkedIn audience composition is unknown.** Engagement signal is real but the ICP is inferred. Who specifically is engaging? If the engaged audience is other researchers and journalists, Candidate 3 is the right first test. If it includes ESG professionals and fund staff, Candidate 1 and 5 become higher priority.
- **No institutional relationship has been tested.** All five candidates assume buyers exist; none have been validated through an actual sales conversation. One warm outreach to a socially responsible investment firm this week would resolve the most important uncertainty for Candidate 5.
- **Enterprise AI governance as a buyer vertical is inferred from the research topic, not from direct buyer evidence.** The OpenAI and AI Labs findings are clearly decision-relevant for enterprise risk; whether those teams have budget for a boutique subscription is unknown.
- **Healthcare sector: state AG procurement cycle is slow.** The evidence base for Candidate 4 is strong, but state government procurement typically takes 6-18 months. The faster buyer for that product is likely the healthcare ESG fund segment, which has shorter decision cycles.
- **Gumroad subscription functionality.** Before launching Candidates 1 and 3, confirm Gumroad supports the recurring delivery and notification format needed, or identify whether a Ghost/Substack hybrid is required.
