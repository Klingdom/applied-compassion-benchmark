# Editorial Style Guide

## Core Voice

Write like a serious human author with experience inside institutions.

The voice should be clear, sharp, grounded, readable, and occasionally memorable.

It should sound like the author has spent twenty years improving systems and finally noticed that we measure almost everything except whether institutions reduce human suffering.

The voice should not sound like a grant template, startup pitch, ESG report, AI whitepaper, or consultant deck.

## Public Brand Rules

Use "Compassion Benchmark" consistently.

Do not use "ACB" in public-facing prose except in appendix references to legacy document codes if unavoidable.

Do not use "Applied Compassion Benchmark" unless quoting source document titles.

Never refer to the project as "Applied Compassion Benchmark" in narrative sections.

Never use em dashes.

## Sentence Rhythm

Avoid mechanical cadence.

Use a natural mix of short, medium, and longer sentences.

Do not rely on one-line paragraphs as drama. Use them only when earned.

Do not stack declarative aphorisms.

Do not make every paragraph end like a slogan.

Good prose varies pressure. Some paragraphs should develop an idea slowly. Others should move quickly.

## Paragraphing

Prefer developed paragraphs over fragmentary lists.

Bullets are allowed when they improve comprehension, especially for:

- source material inventories
- chapter structures
- deliverable lists
- comparison tables
- funding package summaries
- governance safeguards
- quality gates

Do not use bullets to avoid writing.

## Banned Phrases

Avoid the following unless quoting source material:

- in today's rapidly changing world
- now more than ever
- at its core
- the reality is
- the truth is
- it is important to note
- this highlights
- this underscores
- taken together
- ultimately
- as we have seen
- this publication explores
- this chapter examines
- the following section
- imagine a world
- one thing is clear
- unlock
- leverage
- ecosystem
- stakeholder, unless no better word exists
- paradigm
- holistic
- innovative
- transformative
- scalable solution
- game changer
- mission-driven when used as filler

## Banned Patterns

Do not use:

- three consecutive paragraphs of similar length
- more than two consecutive paragraphs beginning with the same grammatical structure
- more than three rhetorical questions per chapter
- section endings that merely summarize
- excessive contrast pairs like "not X, but Y"
- repeated "society needs" constructions
- false profundity
- unexplained claims of first-of-kind status
- generic praise for philanthropy
- vague "impact" language without specifying who benefits and how

## Preferred Moves

Use:

- precise institutional examples
- quiet confidence
- intellectual honesty
- concrete scenarios
- direct language
- evidence where available
- modest claims where evidence is emerging
- strong transitions that carry thought forward
- human implications before technical details

## Examples of Better Framing

Weak:

"Compassion Benchmark unlocks a new paradigm for institutional accountability."

Better:

"Compassion Benchmark gives researchers, journalists, policymakers, and institutions a shared way to ask whether organizational decisions reduce suffering or merely manage reputation."

Weak:

"Institutional compassion is critical now more than ever."

Better:

"When an AI system can affect millions of people before anyone reviews its behavior, the old model of waiting for harm to become visible is no longer enough."

Weak:

"Existing frameworks do not holistically address compassion."

Better:

"ESG can tell us something about disclosure. Compliance can tell us whether a rule was followed. Customer satisfaction can tell us whether an interaction was liked. None of those, by itself, tells us whether an institution recognized suffering, responded proportionally, and learned from the harm it caused."

## Tone Around Funders

Do not flatter funders.

Respect them.

Make clear why philanthropy is needed:

- early public infrastructure is difficult to fund through earned revenue
- independence is compromised if scored entities become the primary revenue source too early
- open methodology, public indexes, and academic validation are public goods
- grants can de-risk the work before commercial revenue matures

## Tone Around Institutions

Avoid cheap outrage.

Do not imply institutions fail because people do not care.

The core argument is stronger:

Institutions often fail because systems turn care into process, process into metrics, and metrics into behavior that no individual would defend in isolation.

## Tone Around Compassion

Do not make compassion sound soft.

Define it as observable institutional behavior:

- detection of suffering
- accurate understanding of need
- timely and proportional action
- fair distribution of care
- protection of autonomy
- accountability for harm
- attention to root causes
- integrity under pressure

At institutional scale, compassion is not sentiment. It is a pattern of decisions.
