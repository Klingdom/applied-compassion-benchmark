# Chapter 26: Governance and Independence

Every funder who has spent time near this field asks the same question, usually early and usually in writing: can a lab buy a better score? It is the right question, and the honest answer has to be more than a promise. A promise is only as durable as the person making it. The answer Compassion Benchmark is building is a layered one, in which independence is engineered into the software first, written into law second, and governed by an independent board third, so that defeating it would require breaking all three layers at once rather than persuading one founder.

Start with the layer that already exists, because it is the most concrete and the least common. Independence here is structural before it is legal. As the previous chapter described, the platform runs on two separated planes. The assessment plane reads the world and writes scores and cannot see who pays the organization anything. The commercial plane runs the payments, the subscriber list, and the email, and it can read published scores but cannot write a single character back into the scoring data. The separation is enforced by missing credentials and write-protected files, not by a policy document. This is the strongest available proof that the firewall is real, because it does not depend on anyone's restraint. A person inside the organization who wanted to sell a score could not route the transaction through the system, because the part of the system that handles money has no reach into the part that sets numbers. Most independence claims are assertions. This one is a property of the architecture, and it is the foundation the rest is built on.

The legal layer is the next to come, and it is the central reason for the nonprofit conversion. Today Compassion Benchmark is a for-profit solo venture, which means the independence rule holds exactly as long as the founder's discipline holds it. That is a real vulnerability, and converting to a 501(c)(3) public charity is how it gets closed. The rule becomes a clause in the Articles of Incorporation and the bylaws, and the bylaw is locked behind a supermajority. The intended language is direct: the organization shall never accept payment from any entity in exchange for inclusion in a benchmark, a change to a published score, or the suppression of a finding, and that policy may not be modified, suspended, or waived except by an affirmative vote of at least two-thirds of all directors then in office, with any such amendment disclosed publicly within thirty days. The mechanism matters more than the sentiment. Once it is in the bylaws, selling a score is not merely against policy; it is outside the organization's legal power, a breach of the directors' fiduciary duty, and ultra vires in the strict sense. An entity that wanted to buy a finding would have to corrupt a supermajority of an independent board and accept that the amendment becomes public within a month. The thirty-day disclosure turns any attempt to weaken the rule into a reputational trip wire that fires before the change can take effect quietly. To be clear about status: the 501(c)(3) is planned, not yet established. The bylaw described here is drafted intent, not enacted law. Chapter 27 lays out the formation path and timeline.

The third layer is the board, and its composition is the point of it. A public charity is required by the IRS to seat a majority of directors unrelated to one another and to the founder, which is, conveniently, also the single best defense against capture. The design calls for three to five independent directors chosen for independence credibility rather than industry connections or a fundraising network, following the model of organizations that have refused corporate money on principle. The founder serves initially as executive director and recuses from any vote touching the independence rule. The other seats are sized to the trust they protect: an ethics or welfare researcher for subject-matter credibility, a nonprofit-governance or legal expert for fiduciary rigor and conflict enforcement, a data-methods or measurement researcher for scoring defensibility, and a journalist or transparency advocate for editorial-independence credibility. Terms are staggered over two years so that no single moment can replace the whole board. No director may be connected to a scored entity, and that bar is enforced through the conflict-of-interest policy, which requires annual written disclosure and recusal and extends explicitly to funders: a director or major donor tied to a rated institution recuses from anything that touches that institution's score or coverage. Alongside the fiduciary board sits a separate external methodology advisory board, three to five credentialed experts serving as volunteers or for a small honorarium, with no fiduciary liability, who review the method each year and publicly attest to what they find. It is cheap to run and high in legitimacy, and it puts named outside experts on record about the soundness of the rubric.

Then there is the funder firewall, which is where the abstract commitment to independence becomes a set of bright lines a program officer can check. The policy is assembled from the strongest clauses of the organizations that have survived this test before. No scored entity may be a material funder, ideally none at all, the categorical line AI Now draws. No single funder may exceed ten percent of annual revenue, the diversification norm Transparency International holds. A written clause states that no funding relationship constrains findings about the funder, the commitment Bellingcat puts in writing as the right to criticize its own funders. Scoring decisions are made by the research staff and the pipeline, independent of any donor, the editorial firewall Freedom House runs between its money and its flagship index. Every funder above one thousand dollars is disclosed publicly, and funders above five thousand are screened with reverse due diligence before the money is accepted. Grants for the core scoring work must be general-operating or unrestricted, so that no earmark can quietly shape what gets measured. These are not aspirations; they are the written rules the board adopts and the website publishes, and several of them, the disclosure threshold and the no-material-funder line in particular, also happen to protect the public-support test that keeps the charity a charity.

The deepest safeguard is the one built into the method rather than the org chart, and it is the answer to the question of what happens if a single person is compromised. The full rubric, the weights, and the evidence sources are published. Scoring is rules-based and logged against that rubric. The evidence behind each score is retained and auditable. Score changes are documented with their rationale and dated. The standard the whole design is held to is a reproducibility test: if the founder were replaced tomorrow, a successor with the published method and the evidence record must be able to reproduce any score and reach the same number. When that is true, no individual's quiet judgment can move a number, because the number is a function of disclosed inputs anyone can recompute. One piece of this machinery is still being finished and should not be overstated: a weekly automated integrity audit, designed to verify mechanically that only the assessment pipeline touches the index files and that the commercial side never reaches the scores, is specified and partially built but not yet running on its own schedule. It belongs on the roadmap, not in the list of things already operating. Naming that gap plainly is itself part of the independence posture, because an institution that rounds up its own audit status has already started down the road it claims to be guarding against.
