# Chapter 14: Artificial Intelligence and Robotics

A frontier model declines a coverage appeal, ranks a job applicant, moderates a post, or steers a robot across a warehouse floor. The decision lands in milliseconds, on a person who will never see the rule that produced it, repeated across millions of cases before any human reviews one. Earlier chapters described institutional decisions that are hard to see because no one is looking. Here they are hard to see because they happen faster than oversight can follow, at a scale where one design choice reaches more people in a day than a hospital reaches in a decade.

That is the case for timing, and it is the most fundable framing in this document. AI and robotics labs are among the most consequential institutions of the decade. They face audited finances, a thickening layer of capability and safety benchmarks, and growing regulatory attention to what their systems can do. They face almost nothing that measures how those systems treat the people on the other side: whether the model recognizes distress in the case it is deciding, whether harm is logged before or after a news cycle, whether the lab corrects course when shown its system hurt someone. That accountability infrastructure is being built dimension by dimension, the way it was for finance and product safety, and the compassion dimension is the one still missing while the field is young enough that a measurement norm can still be set.

Compassion Benchmark scores 50 AI labs and 50 robotics labs against the same eight dimensions used for every other entity. What changes is not the definition of compassion but its subject: for a lab, the population served is its users and the broader society its systems reach. Integrity asks whether safety commitments hold when they become expensive, which is the only condition under which a safety commitment means anything. The Schmidt Sciences framing of multi-agent oversight maps directly onto the robotics index: scoring labs that build autonomous physical systems is oversight of agents that act in the world, not only in text.

Consider the single most-cited example the indexes have produced. OpenAI scores 26.7, in the Developing band, while it is the subject of a multistate attorney-general inquiry into how its systems affect users. The number is not a verdict on the technology or the people who built it. It is a sum of eight dimension scores, each anchored in sourced evidence, and like every published composite it can be reconstructed from those eight numbers by anyone who reads the method. A 26.7 says something specific: documented practices for recognizing and repairing harm to users exist but remain inconsistent, reactive, or unevenly applied. It is a measurement, not an accusation, and the difference is the entire point of doing this independently.

The robotics index makes a different problem visible. A humanoid robotics lab can look exemplary in a demonstration, where the conditions are chosen and the cameras are running, but the benchmark does not score the demonstration. Under the pressure-test rule, a high mark requires at least one documented case where the compassionate choice was costly, and a lab that has only ever performed well when performing well is capped at Developing. For a sector whose public face is the controlled demo, that distinction is most of what an outside observer needs to know.

None of this requires a human to sit with each lab for a week. The 100 labs in these two indexes are maintained by the same automated nightly pipeline that monitors the rest of the 1,256 entities: a scanner sweeps for recent material evidence, an assessor runs full assessments against the eight dimensions and forty subdimensions, and no score moves until a person approves it. The marginal cost of watching one more lab through another news cycle is close to nothing. The deeper, human-administered certification battery is a separate and future pathway; it is not what produces these public scores.

What a funder underwrites here is a clock. The labs are deploying now, and the norms for how their conduct gets measured will harden into whatever fills the vacuum, whether an independent standard or the labs' own self-reporting. A capture-resistant measure of how the most powerful new institutions treat the people their systems touch is worth most before those institutions decide the question cannot be answered.
