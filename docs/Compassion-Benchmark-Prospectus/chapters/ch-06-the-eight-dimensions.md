# Chapter 6: The Eight Dimensions

Take a single institution and hold it still for a moment. Picture a frontier AI lab, well-funded, technically excellent, releasing systems that millions of people now use to ask for help with their health, their work, and their worst days. This lab will run through the rest of Part II as a worked example. Its scores are illustrative, built to demonstrate the standard rather than to report a published result, and they are flagged as illustrative every time they appear. What it offers here is a face. The eight dimensions are abstract until you ask them about something specific, so we will ask them about this lab.

The decomposition matters because "does it reduce suffering" is one question wearing the clothes of eight. An institution can be acutely aware of distress and useless at responding to it. It can respond generously to the people who ask loudly and never reach the people who cannot ask at all. It can help in ways that build dependency, or own its failures, or never do either. These are different capacities, they fail independently, and a standard that collapses them into one number too early learns nothing. Compassion Benchmark keeps them separate all the way down, and each one is phrased as a question a reasonable outsider could ask about any institution, in any sector, without specialist training.

[GRAPHIC: The eight-dimension wheel. Eight colored arcs forming a ring, each labeled with its code, name, and one-line question, in the canonical order AWR through INT. Colors from the locked dimension palette; cyan reserved for AWR/Awareness only, never used elsewhere as a general accent.]

**AWR Awareness.** Does this entity reliably detect when others are in pain or need, before they have to name it? For our illustrative lab, awareness is the question of whether harms from its systems get noticed by the lab before they surface in the press, and whether it sees the distress of a user in crisis that a model was never designed to recognize.

**EMP Empathy.** Does this entity genuinely connect with the inner experience of those it serves, or does it process them? Empathy at institutional scale is not warmth for its own sake. It is whether the people on the other side feel understood rather than handled, and whether that holds across cultural difference and under pressure.

**ACT Action.** Does compassionate understanding translate into real, proportional, effective help? This is the dimension where awareness either becomes useful or evaporates, and we will open it up in full below.

**EQU Equity.** Is care distributed fairly, especially toward those with the greatest need and the least power? An institution can perform well on average and still fail the specific people who most depend on it. Equity asks where the help actually lands.

**BND Boundaries.** Is helping sustainable, ethical, and autonomy-preserving rather than dependency-creating? For an AI lab this is unusually sharp: a system designed for maximum engagement and one designed to build a user's own capacity are not the same product, and the difference is a Boundaries question.

**ACC Accountability.** Does this entity own its failures, correct course, and make genuine repair? Every institution causes harm sometimes. Accountability asks what happens next: acknowledgment or deflection, correction or repetition, repair or a press statement.

**SYS Systemic Thinking.** Does compassion extend to root causes and structural change, not only symptom relief? An institution can treat the visible case and leave intact the structure that produces a thousand more. Systemic Thinking asks whether it works upstream.

**INT Integrity.** Is compassion genuine, consistent, and non-performative, especially when it costs something? This is the dimension that asks whether any of the other seven survive contact with a hard quarter, a leadership change, or a moment when doing the right thing is expensive.

Eight questions. None requires you to read the institution's mind, and each can be investigated in its conduct. That is the whole claim of the wheel: compassion at scale is not one vague virtue but eight separate, observable capacities, and an institution has a different answer for each.

To see what sits beneath a single arc, open up Action. Each of the eight dimensions is itself made of five subdimensions, the concrete behaviors an assessor actually goes looking for, and Action's five show how the zoom works.

- **AC1 Responsiveness.** Whether identified needs receive timely, appropriately prioritized responses, or sit in a queue.
- **AC2 Proportionality.** Whether help is calibrated to actual need rather than to what is easiest to provide.
- **AC3 Efficacy.** Whether the help actually works, as against activity that only looks like help.
- **AC4 Resource Mobilization.** Whether the entity brings genuinely adequate resources to bear, or token ones.
- **AC5 Follow-Through.** Whether the entity persists rather than disengaging once attention moves on.

Asked of the illustrative lab, these stop being abstractions. Responsiveness is how fast a reported safety failure gets a fix rather than a statement. Proportionality is whether a serious harm gets a serious response or the same templated reply as a feature request. Efficacy is whether the fix works in the wild or only in the demo. Resource Mobilization is whether the safety team is staffed to the scale of the deployment or to the scale of the press release. Follow-Through is whether the fix survives the next launch cycle, when attention and engineers have moved on. A lab can score well on Responsiveness and badly on Follow-Through, and the subdimensions are what let the standard say so instead of averaging the distinction away.

Forty subdimensions span the eight dimensions, five each, and they are the level at which scoring happens: every one is rated on a behavioral scale before anything rolls up into a dimension or a composite. The full set, with the plain-English definition of each, is the subject of Appendix A, and there is no need to march through all forty here. The pattern established by Action holds across the wheel. Awareness has its five, Equity has its five, Integrity has its five, and in each case the dimension is the question while the subdimensions are the places you look for the answer.

This is also where the standard quietly resists capture. An institution can polish the one or two dimensions that draw public attention and let the rest decay, and a single headline number would let it. Forty named behaviors do not. A lab that is excellent at Awareness and hollow at Accountability has nowhere for the hollowness to hide once the profile is broken out, which is why the next problem is not what to measure but how to turn forty behaviors, observed in messy real-world evidence, into one defensible score. That requires deciding which evidence is allowed to count.
