# Chapter 17: Corporations and Employers

A large employer can publish a values statement about dignity and belonging on the same morning it closes a plant by email, and nothing in the existing accountability apparatus will register the contradiction as a contradiction. The disclosure was filed. The press release was issued. The ESG questionnaire was answered. What none of those instruments measures is whether the people on the receiving end of the company's decisions were recognized, responded to proportionally, and the harm to them reduced. That gap, between what a corporation discloses and how it behaves toward the people in its care, is what the benchmark is built to close.

Compassion Benchmark scores 448 of the Fortune 500 against the eight dimensions. For a company, the population that suffers or is served is wider than any one constituency: its workers, its customers, and the communities where it operates. A corporation can score well on one and badly on another, and the benchmark is designed to catch exactly that asymmetry. Integrity includes a subdimension, Internal Consistency, that asks whether an institution treats the people inside it with the same compassion it extends to the people it markets to. A company whose customer experience is gracious and whose warehouse conditions are punitive does not average out to fine. The inconsistency is itself the finding.

The contrast with ESG ratings is the heart of why this index exists. ESG can tell you whether a company filed the right disclosures, set a target, and published a policy. Those are real things, but they are not this one. A policy is a Tier 4 document in the benchmark's evidence hierarchy, weaker than verified outcome data and community testimony and far weaker than independent audit, because a policy describes an intention and the benchmark scores conduct. Two companies can hold identical human-rights policies and earn very different scores, because one acted on it when acting was expensive and the other did not. Measured by disclosure, corporate responsibility rewards the company with the best documents; measured by behavioral evidence, it rewards the company whose documents turned out to be true.

That distinction is enforced by the rule that governs every dimension: a high mark requires at least one documented case where the compassionate choice was costly, legally risky, or commercially inconvenient. Almost every company looks compassionate in a quarter when compassion is free, so the benchmark caps a subdimension at Developing where no costly case exists. The question the score answers is what the company did the quarter the right thing cost money. The histogram of where the Fortune 500 actually lands is its own argument: a benchmark earns trust by discriminating, placing companies across the bands on evidence and defending each placement from its dimension scores. Every composite is reconstructable from its eight numbers, so a company that disputes its score is arguing about evidence, not about a verdict handed down from nowhere.

None of this depends on the cooperation of the companies being scored, and that independence is the product. A corporation cannot pay for a better placement, cannot pay to be left out, and cannot pay to soften a finding, because the systems that produce the scores have no access to the systems that handle commercial relationships, and the governing rule that forbids pay-for-score is intended for a supermajority-locked bylaw. The buyers of the benchmark's deeper research are investors and analysts who need behavioral risk signals that disclosure does not give them, never the rated companies themselves. An ESG-exposed investor reading a 448-company distribution wants what a journalist does: the company whose conduct and whose disclosures have quietly come apart, named in terms that can be cited and contested. That is a service to the people a corporation affects, not to the corporation.
