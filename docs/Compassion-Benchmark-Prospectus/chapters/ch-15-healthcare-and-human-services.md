# Chapter 15: Healthcare and Human Services

UnitedHealth Group scores 10.2 out of 100.

The number sits in Critical, the lowest of the five bands, the range reserved for institutions where foundational compassion practices are absent or documented active harm is present. Healthcare is the sector where the benchmark's subject matter and the institution's stated purpose are supposed to be the same thing: a health insurer exists to relieve a specific kind of suffering. When it scores 10.2, the gap being measured is between what the institution says it is for and what its decisions do to the people it covers.

This is the sector where the eight dimensions need almost no translation. Awareness asks whether the system detects distress in the case in front of it, or processes it. Action asks whether an identified need receives a timely, proportional response or a denial calibrated to a model. Equity asks whether care reaches the people with the greatest need and the least power to demand it. In a claims operation these are not virtues; they are the daily output of decision rules, review windows, and appeal pathways, each a documented choice with a documented author, each leaving evidence that can be scored.

The provenance matters as much as the number, because a score this low invites the suspicion that it is an opinion dressed as a measurement. It is not. The composite is a sum of eight dimension scores, each built from subdimensions anchored in sourced evidence and weighted by the five-tier hierarchy in which the company's own statements are the weakest input and independent audit and regulatory findings the strongest. Where the institution's account of itself and the documented experience of the people it covers diverge, the benchmark scores the world as encountered. Evidence beats aspiration is the rule that produces a 10.2 instead of the number the annual report would imply.

UnitedHealth Group also shows the mechanism that keeps the benchmark honest near the bottom. As its regulatory exposure widened, the new adverse evidence did not push the composite lower. An entity already in Critical has almost no scorable room left to fall, and unadjudicated allegations cannot accumulate into a score change on their own. So the expansion of the federal probe was logged where it belonged, as an upgrade in the strength of the evidence behind the existing score, while the composite held at 10.2. The score moves on evidence, not on news pressure, in both directions. That same discipline makes the rare downgrade meaningful: when the pipeline logged one for another large managed-care insurer in the same period, it was because the evidence behind specific dimensions had shifted, not because the sector was having a bad month. A score that can fall only on documented behavior is one that can also be defended when the institution objects, which insurers with substantial legal departments reliably do.

Healthcare is also where the easy story about intentions breaks down most completely. The reviewer with ninety seconds per case did not set the review window. The harm is an emergent property of incentives and defaults that no single caring person controls, which is why asking whether the institution means well predicts so little about what it does. The benchmark scores the rule, the window, and the appeal path, because that is where care either survives the system or does not.

The same logic extends across human services, where the subject of care is rarely able to shop for a better provider. A benefits office, a long-term-care operator, a behavioral-health network: each serves a population defined by need and constrained in its ability to walk away, which raises the stakes on every dimension and especially on Equity and Boundaries. These are exactly the institutions for which no comparable external measure of conduct exists, and exactly the ones where a public, reconstructable score gives a journalist, a regulator, or an affected family a common reference point. A 10.2 is not the end of an argument about UnitedHealth Group. It is the start of one that can finally be conducted in shared, evidence-anchored terms.
