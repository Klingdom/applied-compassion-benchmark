# Chapter 28: Financial Sustainability

The whole institution runs on something between two hundred thousand and three hundred thirty thousand dollars a year. That number is the most important fact in this chapter, because it sets the scale of everything else. This is not an organization that needs a large endowment or a sprawling staff to do what it does. It needs roughly the cost of two or three professional salaries to monitor 1,256 institutions every working night and publish the results for free. The financial question is therefore not whether the model can ever be cheap enough; it already is. The question is how to fund a public good that, by design, cannot charge the institutions it measures and will not paywall its core output.

Before the model, a caution that governs the entire chapter. None of the figures here are booked revenue. There are no audited financial statements behind them, because the nonprofit does not yet exist and the commercial products are early. What follows is a model: recommended prices, projected ranges, and a cost base estimated from real current spending on compute and infrastructure. It should be read as a plan that is internally consistent and grounded in comparable organizations, not as a track record. Where a number is a target, this chapter says so.

The cost base is the solid ground, because most of it is already being spent. Founder compensation is the largest line, between one hundred twenty and one hundred eighty thousand dollars, and it is deliberately below what an equivalent research-director or data-science role would command, justified by the fact that the automation does the work a team of four to six would otherwise require. The pipeline's compute and model-inference costs run twenty to forty-five thousand dollars a year, the single line most directly tied to the work itself, and partly offsettable by the compute credits some funders provide. Hosting and infrastructure are modest, five to ten thousand, because the platform is a static site, a single virtual server, and one Cloudflare Worker on a free tier rather than a fleet. A part-time research contractor, phased in once funding is secured, adds zero to sixty thousand. Legal, accounting, and the external methodology review run five to thirty thousand, higher in the formation year. Directors-and-officers and media-liability insurance, which is not optional for an organization that publishes named scores, adds three to ten thousand. A fiscal-sponsor fee of roughly five to ten percent applies to grant income during the bridge period and falls to zero once the standalone charity is established. Add it up and the annual operating need lands in that two-hundred-to-three-hundred-thirty-thousand band, with the low end describing a lean solo year and the high end a fully staffed and fully compliant one.

The revenue side rests on three legs, and the design intent is that no single leg can dominate. The first leg is grants, and in the early years it is the largest. Foundation funding is what the public-good core is built to attract, because the free rankings and the open methodology are precisely the kind of field infrastructure that grantmakers in AI accountability and institutional transparency exist to support. Chapter 29 details the specific funders and asks; the financial point is that grants are expected to carry the majority of revenue in Year 1, in the range of fifty to three hundred thousand dollars depending on how many of the early applications land.

The second leg is related earned revenue, and its role is to make the organization less dependent on grants over time without compromising what makes it fundable. The commercial products are derived depth sold to data users, never access sold to rated entities: research reports and datasets, a per-entity alert subscription, institutional interpretation and briefing services, and an institutional data license. The hard rule underneath all of them is that every dollar comes from someone using the data, an investor, a journalist, a researcher, a procurement team, and never from an institution paying to change or suppress its own score. Structured this way, the revenue is not a compromise of the mission; under the tax rules it is the exempt educational activity itself, the same logic by which a university sells its course materials or an investigative newsroom sells its data store. The projected range is modest and deliberately so, twenty to eighty thousand in Year 1, because the priority while the brand is young is credibility, not revenue maximization, and the pricing is tiered to stay below the institutional monitoring services it sits beside.

The third leg is small donors, and although it is the smallest in dollars, it does disproportionate work. A base of individual supporters, with the alert subscribers as the natural pipeline, signals to the IRS and to funders that the organization has genuine public support, and it diversifies the revenue away from any single large source. The projected five to twenty thousand dollars in Year 1 matters less for its size than for what it protects.

That protection is the real reason the three-leg structure is not arbitrary, and it connects the finances back to the independence that the rest of this Part has been about. A public charity must pass a public-support test over a rolling five-year window, broadly meaning that enough of its support comes from the general public rather than a single source. An organization whose revenue came to be dominated by a handful of large institutional contracts could fail that test and be reclassified, and a single very large grant can be capped for the purpose of the test. The mitigation is exactly the diversified mix described above: a healthy balance of grants, earned revenue, and small donors, with no source allowed to dominate. The same Transparency International norm that serves independence, no single funder above ten percent of revenue, happens to serve the public-support test at the same time. The constraint that keeps the organization independent is the same constraint that keeps it a charity, which is a convenient alignment rather than a coincidence: both are defenses against any one party gaining too much hold.

Why philanthropy is needed now, and not indefinitely, is the question a funder is entitled to ask of any organization with earned revenue. The answer is timing. The earned-revenue leg is real but young, and growing it faster would mean either paywalling the core rankings, which would destroy the public good that justifies the work, or selling harder to rated entities, which would destroy the independence that gives the scores their value. Neither is acceptable, so the commercial leg grows at the pace credibility allows. Grant funding bridges the years between an instrument that already works and an earned-revenue base mature enough to carry more of the load. The pipeline's low cost is what makes that bridge short: an organization this inexpensive to run needs only a modest and diversified revenue base to sustain itself indefinitely, which means philanthropy here buys durable independence rather than a permanent subsidy.
