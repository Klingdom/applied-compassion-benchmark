# Chapter 25: Technology Platform

A research team that wanted to monitor 1,256 institutions for changes in how they treat human suffering, reassess the most consequential ones against a forty-part rubric, and publish a defensible result every morning would need to be large, expensive, and slow. Compassion Benchmark does it for roughly the price of a working dinner a night, and the reason is the platform. This chapter describes that platform as it actually is: what it does without supervision, what still requires a person, and where its limits are. The cost-efficiency case funders respond to lives here, but so does the honest accounting of what automation cannot do.

The center of the platform is a four-stage pipeline that runs Monday through Saturday, roughly overnight. Each stage is a defined job with a defined cost, and the stages hand off to one another in sequence.

The first stage is the scanner. It sweeps all of the entities, around 1,256, looking for material evidence inside a strict 14-day window, working within a budget of about 250 searches. It is not trying to reassess everything; it is triaging. For every entity it writes a short provenance record of what it looked at and found, so that even an entity with no news has a dated note saying so. It outputs a prioritized list, roughly the top fifteen entities that warrant a full look plus about five more pulled in on rotation so that quiet entities are not ignored forever. The scan takes about half an hour and costs around two dollars.

The second stage is the assessor, and it is the expensive one. It runs full assessments on the fifteen to twenty priority entities using the same eight-dimension, forty-subdimension method published on the site, producing an evidence table across all forty subdimensions recorded both as raw 0 to 5 anchors and as the scaled 0 to 100 values. Crucially, it does not propose a score change for every wobble. A change proposal is emitted only when the composite would move by five points or more, and only after the proposal survives a set of screens: a check that the baseline it is moving from is real, a check that the direction of the move matches the direction of the evidence, a check that the new rationale is consistent with the entity's history, and a validation of the arithmetic against the canonical formula. A proposal that passes all of that is recorded with the status pending. It is never applied on its own. The assessor runs a little over two hours and costs somewhere between twenty-five and sixty dollars a night, depending on how much it finds worth assessing.

The third stage is the digest. It takes the night's findings and writes the public daily briefing, the structured record of top signals, boundary cases, recent assessments, emerging risks, sector alerts, and forward triggers, and it updates the internal log of changes awaiting approval. A pair of validators enforces a plain-language contract on that briefing and is wired into the site build, so a malformed or jargon-choked briefing fails rather than ships. This stage takes about five minutes and costs around fifty cents.

The fourth stage is not automated, and that is the design, not a gap. The score-updater applies approved changes to the public indexes, and it runs only when a human triggers it. It is the approval gate. When the founder approves a pending proposal, the updater writes it to the index files under a drift guard: if the baseline has moved by more than 2.0 since the proposal was made, the update refuses and holds, flagging a stale baseline rather than writing a number built on outdated ground. It runs a direction-inversion check, recalculates the rankings, and logs the change to a permanent applied-changes record. Nothing reaches the public scores without passing through this stage, and this stage does not move without a person.

Add the stages and a night costs roughly $27.50 to $62.50, which is about $700 to $1,600 a month in research compute. That figure is the whole cost argument compressed into a number. Continuous coverage of 1,256 institutions, reassessed in priority order every working night, for a monthly cost a single mid-size foundation grant covers many times over. The cost per entity falls as the catalog grows, because the scanner's budget and the assessor's triage do not scale linearly with the number of entities watched. This is the sense in which the marginal cost of one more monitored institution is close to zero.

Underneath the pipeline sits the rest of the platform, and it is deliberately ordinary. The public site is a statically exported Next.js application served from a single virtual server, with the scores living in structured data files rather than a database. A single Cloudflare Worker handles everything that needs to respond to the outside world: it serves a score badge any site can embed, processes the payment webhook and subscriber records for the commercial products, and handles unsubscribe requests. The Worker reads published scores and writes subscriber and payment state; it has no path back into the scoring data. The free tier of that service covers the expected traffic for years. There is no fleet of servers here and no large standing bill, which is part of why the cost base in Chapter 28 is as low as it is.

The architecture also carries the independence claim, and this is the part worth dwelling on. The platform runs on two separated planes. The assessment plane reads the world and writes scores, and it has no access to subscriber lists, payment data, or any commercial state. The commercial plane runs the Worker, the email system, and the payment integration, and it can read only the already-published scores while writing nothing back to the assessment record. The separation is enforced by structure, not by policy: the planes hold different credentials, the score files are write-protected against the commercial side, and the Worker has no key that would let it alter an index. A person who wanted to sell a score change could not do it through the software, because the software that talks to paying customers cannot reach the software that sets scores. Chapter 26 builds the governance on top of this; the point here is that the firewall is physical before it is procedural.

What the platform cannot do is as important as what it can. It cannot conduct an interview, sit in a waiting room, or hear directly from an affected community, which is why the automated scores rely on the public evidence record and why the certified human assessment described elsewhere remains the more rigorous and still-aspirational pathway. It cannot judge a genuinely novel situation the rubric never anticipated; it flags such cases for the human gate rather than guessing. It over-finds bad news, because adverse events are reported more loudly than steady competent care, which is why the method explicitly instructs the search toward durable positive evidence and why a human reviews the consequential moves. And it cannot, by itself, make any number public. The pipeline is powerful at scale and narrow at judgment, and the platform is built so that the judgment it lacks is supplied by a person before anything is published, not after.
