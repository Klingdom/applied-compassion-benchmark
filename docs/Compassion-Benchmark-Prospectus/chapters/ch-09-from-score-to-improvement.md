# Chapter 9: From Score to Improvement

A single composite is almost useless to the institution that receives it. The illustrative lab's 46.9 tells its leadership that it sits in the middle band and nothing about what to do on Monday. If the benchmark stopped at the composite it would be a verdict, and verdicts do not improve anything; they only rank. The composite is not where the standard's value to an institution lives. The value lives one level down, in the profile the composite was built from, because that profile names the specific behaviors a score is made of and therefore the specific behaviors that would move it.

Break the illustrative lab's 46.9 back into its parts and the flat number becomes a map. Awareness and Action sit at 3.5, the lab's relative strengths. Empathy, Equity, Accountability, and Integrity sit at 2.5, dragging the mean down. The lab is not uniformly mediocre. It is good at noticing problems and acting on them, and weak at connecting with the people affected, distributing help fairly, owning its failures, and holding its conduct when it costs something. That is not a grade. It is a diagnosis, and it points at four dimensions rather than a vague exhortation to do better.

The map gets more precise still. Each of those four weak dimensions decomposes into five subdimensions, and a 2.5 on Accountability is itself a story: perhaps the lab acknowledges harm but does not repair it, or corrects course privately but resists transparency. The standard does not hand an institution a number and walk away. It hands over the forty behaviors the number was assembled from, and an institution that wanted to raise its score would find, in that breakdown, the actual conduct it would have to change. Improvement is specified, not wished for.

Specified is not the same as easy, and the standard is built to make sure the distinction holds. Raising a subdimension from Developing to Established is not a matter of issuing a better policy; it requires evidence that the new behavior held across most cases and was confirmed from outside the institution. A press release does not move a score. Because the higher anchors demand pressure-tested evidence, the only route from 46.9 to a higher band runs through conduct that an outsider can verify, sustained long enough to show it was not a one-week performance. The benchmark makes improvement legible and keeps it expensive, which is the combination that makes a gain mean something.

Reversibility runs in both directions, and the floor is where it is starkest. UnitedHealth Group is scored at 10.2, in the Critical band, the lowest in the benchmark's healthcare coverage. A score that low is not a life sentence and not a single bad headline frozen into a number. It can be earned down further, and it can be climbed back. The mechanics of the bottom are worth understanding precisely, because they show the standard refusing to be either vindictive or naive. When an entity already sits at or near the floor, new adverse evidence has no scorable room left to move the composite; there is nowhere lower for most of the profile to go. Such evidence is logged, but as an upgrade to the strength of the evidence record rather than as a drop in the number. When a Department of Justice probe against UnitedHealth Group expanded, it was recorded exactly that way: a tier upgrade with no change to the 10.2. The score did not move because the score was already at the bottom, and the standard declines to manufacture motion it cannot justify.

Climbing back is governed by an exit protocol, and the protocol is demanding by design. A floored entity recovers only on evidence-of-care behavior at the dimension level, sustained across at least two consecutive assessment cycles, drawn from sources outside the entity's own control. Performative statements do not register; a campaign announcing reform is precisely the kind of self-report the evidence hierarchy ranks lowest. What lifts a floor is durable, externally visible change in conduct, observed over time, which is the same standard that gates every other band, applied at the place where the temptation to accept a good story is strongest.

This is what keeps a published score from being a one-time grade stamped on an institution and left there. The pipeline reassesses, evidence accumulates and decays, and a score is a current reading that the next cycle can revise in either direction. A number that can be reconstructed can also be moved, and the only way to move it is to change what the institution actually does. Whether that prospect changes the institution's calculus is the question the rest of this publication takes up.
