# Chapter 21: Journalists, Policymakers, Researchers, and Communities

A benchmark is worth exactly what other people do with it. The scores can be precise, the methodology open, the pipeline humming every night, and none of it matters if the numbers sit unread on a website. The value of a shared measure is created downstream, by the people who pick it up, cite it, argue with it, and act on it. This chapter is about those people, what the benchmark lets each of them do, and an honest account of which parts are built and which are still being built.

Start with the journalist, because the journalist is the first mover. An investigative reporter covering a hospital system, an AI lab, or a state agency spends much of an investigation establishing that a problem is real and significant. A score does part of that work before the reporting starts. It says this institution sits in the Critical band, here are the eight dimensions that put it there, and here is the evidence trail beneath each one. The score is not the story; it is the lead and the spine. When the benchmark's nightly research moves an entity or crosses a band boundary, that movement is itself a tip: something changed, here is what, here is the dated record. Newsrooms that work this territory, the ones covering health care, technology accountability, and public services, are the natural first citers, and the benchmark is designed to make a citation easy: a public score, a reconstructable composite, an evidence trail a fact-checker can walk.

The policymaker uses the same number for a different purpose. A legislator or a regulator writing oversight needs comparison and standing. It is one thing to say an agency is failing the residents it serves; it is another to say it scores in the Developing band while comparable states score higher, on a public scale the agency cannot dispute the arithmetic of. The benchmark scores governments, states, and cities precisely so that public institutions can be held to the same standard as the corporations they regulate. A score gives an oversight letter a citable anchor and gives a hearing a common reference that the institution under question is already on record against.

The researcher is the user who makes the standard durable. An academic studying institutional behavior, or a measurement specialist stress-testing the method, does something the other three cannot: they reproduce it, contest it, and extend it. Because the rubric, the anchors, and the formula are published, a researcher can take an entity's evidence record and rebuild its composite, then challenge a judgment in public. That adversarial use is not a threat to the benchmark; it is how a measure earns the trust that makes it citable in the first place. The capture-resistant indices the method borrows from were hardened exactly this way, by surviving people trying to break them.

The fourth audience is the one most measurement systems forget. The communities affected by an institution's conduct are not only readers of the score; they are part of its evidence base. Community testimony is a named tier in the evidence hierarchy, and where the people an institution serves report harm that its policy documents deny, the standard already says the testimony outranks the document. For an affected community, the benchmark offers two things at once: a public number that validates what they have been describing, and a method that counts their account as evidence rather than anecdote. That is a structural inversion of the usual arrangement, in which the institution's paperwork is presumed true until a community can prove otherwise.

The connective tissue between these audiences and the scores is partly built and partly planned, and the distinction matters. The badge endpoint is live: any site can embed an entity's current score as a graphic that updates when the score does. Score Watch, the per-entity alert that notifies a subscriber when a tracked institution's composite moves or changes band, is sold today at a per-entity annual price, and its independence is structural: paying to watch a score cannot affect the score, and a subscriber sees nothing the public briefing does not. Automated delivery of those alerts, though, is still being finished; the product is real and the fulfillment pipeline is in progress, and this document does not claim a fully automated alert service that is not yet running. The bulk data feed researchers want exists as an export today and is specified to become a programmatic interface later.

Honesty requires the baseline. As of today, the documented count of external citations of these scores is zero. No newsroom has yet cited one in print, no legislature has yet quoted one, no third-party tool beyond the benchmark's own runs on the badge. Everything in this chapter describes designed and intended use, with the connective infrastructure partly shipped, not a record of adoption already achieved. The next two chapters take that gap seriously: Chapter 22 lays out why third-party use is the load-bearing assumption of the whole model, and Chapter 23 commits to the specific numbers by which a funder should judge whether the use is materializing.

What changes as the users arrive. Each audience makes the benchmark more valuable to the others. A journalist's citation gives a policymaker cover to quote the score; a policymaker's use gives a researcher a reason to scrutinize it; a researcher's validation gives the next journalist confidence to cite it; a community's corroboration strengthens the evidence the whole chain rests on. A standard does not scale by being promoted. It scales by being used, each use lowering the cost of the next, until reaching for the score is simply what one does.
