# Chapter 22: Theory of Change

The honest version of a theory of change names the place where the causal chain is strongest, the place where it is weakest, and the assumptions holding the two together. This is that version. It is also the chapter where a funder is entitled to ask the blunt question that the rest of the document has been building toward: if you do the work, what actually changes, and how much of that is within your control?

Start with what is fully within our control, because that is where the chain is strongest. The inputs are concrete: a working assessment framework, an automated pipeline, a published methodology, and a modest compute budget. The activities those inputs drive are equally concrete. Every night, the pipeline scans 1,256 entities for recent evidence, reassesses the highest-priority ones against the eight dimensions, and a human approves any score that moves. The findings are published. From those activities come outputs we can count and already produce: entities scored and maintained, free public rankings, logged and dated score-change events, and the daily briefing that records what moved and why. None of this depends on anyone outside the institution cooperating. If the lights stay on, the outputs happen. This is the part of the theory of change that is not a theory at all. It is operations.

The causal chain gets more interesting, and less certain, at the next link.

| Stage | What it is | Who controls it |
|---|---|---|
| Inputs | Framework, pipeline, methodology, compute, funding | Us |
| Activities | Nightly scanning, reassessment, human-gated updates, publishing | Us |
| Outputs | Entities scored, public rankings, logged score changes, impact reports | Us |
| Outcomes | A shared, citable standard that third parties use and contest | Shared |
| Impact | Institutions adjust conduct; the measure enters standard practice | Mostly them |

The outputs are ours. The impact is not, and any prospectus that pretends otherwise should be distrusted. Between the two sits the load-bearing span of the entire model: the move from publishing a credible score to that score being used by someone with leverage over the institution it describes. We do not control that adoption. We can only make it as likely as careful work allows.

The mechanism we are betting on is not shame. Institutions absorb bad press routinely and move on. The mechanism is a shared reference point. When a measure becomes common vocabulary, it changes the cost of ignoring it. Freedom House scores and Transparency International's Corruption Perceptions Index did not reform governments by embarrassing them once. They became the language in which journalists framed stories, investors priced country risk, and policymakers justified decisions, until maintaining a defensible position on the measure became part of how a serious institution operated. The wager here is that a credible, independent, reconstructable measure of how institutions treat suffering can travel the same path: cited in an investigation, folded into an investor's due diligence, raised in a policy hearing, pointed to by an affected community, until an institution that expects to be measured maintains its conduct the way it now maintains its compliance posture.

Stated as a sequence, the bet runs: research creates measurement, measurement creates transparency, transparency creates a shared standard, a shared standard creates accountability, and accountability creates pressure to improve. Each arrow in that sentence is an assumption, and a funder should see them as assumptions rather than findings.

The first is that transparency drives accountability, that a public, credible score changes behavior more than a private one or none at all. The comparable indices support this, but the evidence is strongest for governments and corporations and thinnest for the newer subjects, the AI and robotics labs, where no measurement norm yet exists to point to. The second assumption is that the scores are credible enough to be cited, which depends on the methodology holding up under expert scrutiny and the pipeline producing defensible results at scale. The third is that independence is preserved and, just as important, trusted, because a measure suspected of capture will be used as ammunition by partisans rather than as a reference by neutrals, and that is a failure mode, not a success. The fourth assumption is the one most outside our control: that third parties actually pick the scores up. We can lower the barriers, by keeping the rankings free, the methodology open, the data citable, and the score-change events legible to a working journalist. We cannot manufacture the citation.

This is the appropriate place to be clear about the present state of the evidence. As of today, the count of documented external citations is zero. No institution has visibly altered its conduct in response to a published score. Those are not embarrassing admissions to be buried; they are the honest baseline against which the next two years should be judged. The theory of change predicts that the early outcomes, the first citations, the first documented institutional responses, the first third-party tools embedding the scores, are achievable within that horizon. It does not predict measurable behavior change at scale on that timeline, and it would be a warning sign if anyone claimed it did. Long-horizon impact, the kind where the measure has entered standard due-diligence vocabulary and institutions reliably move because of it, is a decade-scale ambition, not a grant deliverable.

What philanthropic capital buys, then, is not the impact directly. It buys the conditions under which the impact becomes possible: a credible measure already exists, and the work funded here, the citable research, the independent governance, the verified integrity of the process, is what gives third parties a reason to trust it enough to use it. The instrument is built. The next chapter asks how we will know whether it is working, and answers it with the metrics the pipeline can already track rather than the ones that would flatter us.
