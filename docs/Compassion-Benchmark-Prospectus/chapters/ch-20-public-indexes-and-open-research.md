# Chapter 20: Public Indexes and Open Research

The most consequential decision in this institution's business model is the thing it gives away. Every one of the 1,256 scores is free to read. All eight indexes, the full rankings, the band each entity sits in, the daily briefing that records what moved overnight: free, with no login, no metered preview, no paywall waiting three articles in. The methodology that produces the scores is free to read in full, down to the eight dimensions, the forty subdimensions, the behavioral anchors, the five-tier evidence hierarchy, and the exact formula that turns dimension scores into a 0 to 100 composite. An organization that intends to earn revenue has put its entire core product, and the recipe for it, in public view. That is not generosity. It is the only configuration under which the rest of the model means anything.

Consider what would happen if the scores were paywalled. A score behind a paywall is a private accusation sold to whoever pays, which is exactly the posture that makes a rating capturable: the institution being rated has the most reason and the most money to control who sees what. The moment access becomes the product, the door opens to the rated entity buying its way to silence. Keeping the rankings free closes that door by construction. It also serves the people the benchmark exists for. A patient, a worker, a resident, a community organizer affected by an institution's conduct is rarely the person with a research budget. If the score that describes their treatment were sold, it would reach everyone except them. Free public indexes are the difference between a tool for the watched and a tool for the watchers.

What, then, is sold. The depth, never the verdict. The free indexes tell you that a company sits in the Critical band and show you its eight dimension scores. The paid research tells you the evidence trail beneath each of the forty subdimensions, the sector comparison, the custom analysis an investor or a newsroom needs to act. This is the model libraries of public-interest data already run: Our World in Data gives away its charts and its data and sustains itself on a base of small donors; ProPublica publishes its investigations free and runs a Data Store for the derived datasets researchers will pay to skip building themselves. The principle is the same one a museum runs on. The collection is public; the deep guided study is the related, supporting activity. Keep the core free, sell only what is genuinely derived from it, and the commerce stays subordinate to the public good instead of competing with it.

Open methodology is the second half of the public good, and it is worth being precise about what "open" covers here, because the word is often used loosely. Published, today, in full: the rubric, the dimension and subdimension definitions, the 0 to 5 anchors, the evidence hierarchy, the composite formula, and the rules that govern how a score may change. The reproducibility test follows directly from this: a competent successor, handed the published method and an entity's evidence record, must be able to rebuild that entity's score and arrive at the same number. A capturable index cannot survive that test, because its weighting is private and its judgments are unreviewable. A separate, more rigorous human assessment protocol exists as a specification and underpins a future certified product, but it is not how the 1,256 public scores are produced and is not claimed as operational here. The open part is the part that scores the public indexes, and it is genuinely open.

There is a machine-readable layer to the openness as well. A built badge endpoint serves any entity's current score as an embeddable graphic, so a third-party site can display a live composite that updates when the underlying score does. A broader data feed for researchers who want the scores in bulk exists today as an exportable file and is specified to grow into a programmatic interface; the interface is planned, not yet shipped, and this document does not claim otherwise. The direction is consistent: lower the cost of citing, embedding, and contesting the scores until doing so is nearly free, because a public good that is hard to use is a public good in name only.

What changes if the openness holds. An index that anyone can read, rebuild, and embed becomes infrastructure rather than a publication. A researcher who can reproduce a score will defend it; one who cannot will ignore it. A newsroom that can embed a live badge will return to it; one that has to screenshot a PDF will not. The free indexes and the open method are not the charitable wrapper around a business. They are the asset, and the business is what the asset can responsibly support without ever being allowed to own it.
