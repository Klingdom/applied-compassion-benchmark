# How to Read This Publication

The argument runs in six parts, and each one inherits a question from the part before it.

**Part I asks whether the gap is real.** It establishes that every other domain of institutional performance now has external accountability infrastructure, and this one does not. Read it for the problem, stated without exaggeration.

**Part II asks whether the gap can be measured.** It defines institutional compassion as a pattern of conduct, lays out the eight dimensions and the evidence hierarchy, and shows how a 0 to 100 score is built from documented behavior by one published formula. Read it for the standard.

**Part III asks whether the measurement is credible.** It grounds the framework in established measurement methods, explains why a score of 4 is different from a 3, and shows the pressure test that gates the top bands. Read it for the intellectual defense.

**Part IV asks whether the standard finds anything meaningful.** It applies the benchmark to AI and robotics labs, healthcare, governments, corporations, and civil society, with named institutions and real scores. Read it for the proof in practice.

**Part V asks whether public measurement changes anything.** It sets out the theory of change, the public-good case, and how journalists, investors, policymakers, and communities use the scores. Read it for the public benefit, and for the honest limits of what the work can yet claim.

**Part VI asks whether this institution can actually do it.** It covers what is already built, the technology platform, governance and engineered independence, the roadmap to 501(c)(3) status, the financial model, and the funding ask. Read it for the case that the instrument exists and the institution is fundable.

A reader short on time can start with the Executive Summary, then read Part IV for the evidence and Part VI for the institution. A reader weighing credibility should read Parts II and III in full. Six appendices hold the full reference: every dimension and subdimension, the evidence tiers, the assessment lifecycle, the governance safeguards, the funding packages, and the visual system.

Throughout, two distinctions are kept strict. What is built is separated from what is planned or aspirational. And the scores are produced by an automated, human-gated pipeline, never hand-assessed one institution at a time.
