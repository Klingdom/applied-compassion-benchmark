## Core belief

Compassion at institutional scale is not a sentiment that resists measurement. It is a pattern of decisions: whether a complaint triggered a real review, whether a coverage denial got a second look, whether a system's harm was logged before or after the news cycle. Decisions like these leave evidence. Evidence can be scored against a published standard. A score can be made public, and a public score can be cited, contested, and acted on.

The conviction underneath the work is that the treatment of suffering is not too soft to measure. It is simply unmeasured, because no one independent has decided to measure it at scale and hold the result to the same standard of evidence we already demand of an institution's finances, its safety record, and its products.

This institution exists to do that measuring, to publish what it finds, and to make the scores worth trusting by making them impossible to buy.



# Founder Letter

I want to tell you why this exists, and I want to be honest about what it cannot yet do.

The idea did not arrive as a thesis. It arrived as a question I could not answer. We can find out, in an afternoon, whether a company passed its financial audit, whether a building met fire code, whether a model cleared a safety benchmark. We cannot find out, by any comparable public measure, whether the institution on the other side of those systems noticed that a person was in distress and did something proportional about it. Every domain of institutional performance has external accountability infrastructure. This one has almost none.

I could have written about that gap. People have written about it well. I decided the more useful thing was to build the measurement, make it public, and let it be argued with. Compassion Benchmark scores 1,256 institutions on how they recognize, respond to, and reduce suffering, across eight dimensions of conduct. The scores come from evidence, not reputation. Each one is reconstructable from a published formula, so anyone who disagrees can redo the arithmetic. An automated pipeline reviews those institutions every night, and a person approves every change before it goes live. None of it depends on an institution's cooperation, and none of it is for sale.

Then came the hardest decision.

I made independence a property of the architecture rather than a line in a policy. The systems that score the world cannot see who pays us. The systems that handle payment cannot touch a score. A scored entity cannot buy inclusion, a better number, or silence, because the machine is built so that it cannot sell those things even if someone inside wanted to. Independence is locked, not promised. That sentence is closer to the whole product than anything else I could write.

There was a doubt to get past first, the oldest objection to this whole idea: that compassion is too soft, too subjective, too interior to measure, and that any attempt to score it would be performance dressed as rigor.

What resolved it for me was specificity. When UnitedHealth Group scores 10.2 out of 100 and lands in our Critical band, that number is not a mood or a verdict handed down. It is the sum of eight dimension scores, each assembled from documented evidence, each reproducible by anyone who reads the method. A number you can take apart is a number you can trust, or contest, on its merits.

Here is what I will not claim. No journalist has yet cited these scores in an investigation I can point to. No institution has visibly changed because of a figure we published. The weekly integrity audit is designed and partly built, not yet running on its own. What I am asking for is the capital to turn a working instrument into a durable institution: an independent board, legal formation, the first genuinely citable research, the integrity audit finished and running on its own. To a first funder, I commit to the discipline I ask of every institution I score: quarterly reports that state plainly what moved and what did not, the independence indicators published intact at every interval, and no projection dressed up as a result.

We measure almost everything except whether institutions reduce suffering. I believe that is worth changing, and I believe it can be.

# Executive Summary

We have built public accountability infrastructure for almost everything that determines an institution's standing. Financial performance has audited statements and credit ratings. Product safety has recalls and inspections. Cybersecurity has disclosure rules and breach registries. Environmental and labor conduct sit inside a thicket of indices, certifications, and ratings. Artificial intelligence now has a fast-growing set of capability and safety benchmarks. Across all of it, one question has no comparable public measure: when an institution's decisions reach a person who is suffering, does it recognize that, respond proportionally, and reduce the harm, or not.

That is the gap Compassion Benchmark exists to close. It is an independent benchmark institution that measures how institutions recognize, respond to, and reduce suffering, and makes the scores public. The premise underneath it is that compassion at institutional scale is not a sentiment that resists measurement. It is a pattern of decisions: whether a complaint triggered a real review, whether a coverage denial got a second look, whether an AI system's harm was logged before or after the news cycle. Decisions like these leave evidence. Evidence can be scored. A score can be made public, and a public score can be argued with, cited, and acted on.

## What exists today

Compassion Benchmark is not a proposal. The framework, the public indexes, and the machinery that maintains them are built and operating.

- **1,256 entities scored**, across eight public indexes: 193 countries, 448 Fortune 500 companies, 250 global cities, 50 AI labs, 50 robotics labs, 21 US states, 144 US cities, and 100 universities. The rankings are free to read.
- **A published standard.** Every entity is scored on eight dimensions of institutional compassion, from Awareness through Integrity, and forty subdimensions beneath them. Each subdimension is rated against documented behavior on a five-tier evidence hierarchy in which an institution's own statements are the weakest input and independent audit the strongest.
- **A reconstructable number.** Scoring model v1.2 builds a 0 to 100 composite from the eight dimension scores by one published formula, with an integration premium of up to ten points that rewards even, sustained performance over a single brilliant spike. Anyone who reads the method can redo the math and reach the same number. Reproducibility is the honesty proof.
- **An automated nightly pipeline.** A scanner sweeps all 1,256 entities for recent material evidence, an assessor runs full assessments on the highest-priority entities, and a digest publishes the night's findings. The pipeline runs six nights a week on a compute budget in the low tens of dollars a night. Continuous monitoring of more than a thousand institutions costs less per night than a working dinner.
- **A human gate.** No score changes automatically. Proposed changes wait, flagged pending, until a person approves them. The public scores are produced by an automated, human-gated process, not hand-assessed one institution at a time.

The findings are already specific enough to matter. The hundred universities we scored average 46.2 out of 100, in the Functional band, and not one reached Exemplary. Harvard, at 52.3, is the highest in the set. UnitedHealth Group scores 10.2 and sits in Critical. These are not impressions. Each is a sum of dimension scores anchored in sourced evidence.

## What we do not yet claim

A prospectus that overstates its evidence forfeits the trust the whole project depends on, so the limits belong here, not in a footnote. No external citation of these scores has yet been documented; the baseline is zero. No institution has visibly changed its conduct because of a published score. The weekly integrity audit is specified and partly built, not yet running on its own. Automated delivery of the paid alert product is partial. The seven-session Human Assessment Battery, the deep certified pathway, is an aspirational standard that underpins a future paid service, not the method behind the 1,256 public scores. The organization itself is a fiscal-sponsorship-to-501(c)(3) plan, not yet an established charity. The impact case in this document is forward-looking, and stated that way throughout.

## The public-benefit case

The free rankings and the open methodology are the public good. A shared, comparable measure of institutional conduct is the kind of infrastructure that civil society uses the way it already uses Freedom House scores or Transparency International's Corruption Perceptions Index: a common reference point that journalists can cite, investors can fold into due diligence, policymakers can question institutions against, and affected communities can point to. The value compounds as third parties use it. A standard becomes a standard by being adopted, contested, and cited, and that is the trajectory this work is built to enable rather than a result it can yet report.

## Why independence is the product, and how it is protected

A benchmark that can be bought is worthless, and a benchmark that cannot criticize its funders is a mirror. Independence here is engineered before it is governed. The assessment systems that score the world have no access to subscriber, payment, or commercial data. The commercial systems read only published scores and can write nothing back. The separation is enforced by missing credentials and file-system permissions, not by good intentions. On top of that architecture sits the governing rule, intended for a supermajority-locked bylaw: no scored entity may pay for inclusion, a score change, or suppression of findings. Revenue comes only from data users, never from the institutions being scored. Funding this work does not buy a better score, and that is not modesty. It is the only arrangement under which a score is worth anything.

## Why philanthropy, and why now

This is early institutional infrastructure with a long public-good horizon and a deliberately constrained commercial model. Earned revenue is designed to stay subordinate to the free public mission, which means it cannot, and should not, carry the cost of building the institution in its first years. The work that establishes durability and trust, an independent board, legal formation, the first citable research, a finished integrity audit, is exactly the work that grant capital exists to fund. The timing argument is sharpest where the stakes are highest. AI and robotics labs are among the most consequential institutions of the decade and face almost no standardized external accountability on how their systems treat the people they affect. The window to set a measurement norm is open now, while the field is still forming.

# Part I: Why Compassion Needs a Standard


# Chapter 1: The Decisions We Never See

A claim arrives at a health insurer at 6:40 in the morning. It is a request for continued coverage of a rehabilitation stay for an elderly woman recovering from a stroke. No human reads it first. An algorithm scores the case against a predicted length of stay, finds the patient three days past the model's estimate, and flags the claim for denial. A reviewer who has ninety seconds per case approves the flag. A letter goes out. Somewhere, a family that assumed the next week was covered learns that it is not, and begins making calls it does not know how to make.

[GRAPHIC: Spot illustration. One institutional decision at the center, rippling outward to the people it reaches: the claim, the letter, the family on the phone.]

That scene is a composite, drawn to be representative rather than to describe one named case. Pick almost any large institution and you can assemble its equivalent. A content-moderation system that suppresses a user's appeal before anyone reads it. A municipal benefits office that closes a case for a missing form the applicant never received. A university that quietly declines to renew the contracts of its lowest-paid staff in a budget cycle no student will ever see. None of these decisions is dramatic. None will be reported. Each one determines, for a specific person, whether an institution recognized that they were in trouble and did something proportional about it.

This is where institutional compassion actually lives. Not in the mission statement, not in the foundation's annual report, not in the chief executive's letter about the company's values. It lives in the decision rule that fires at 6:40 in the morning, in who set the ninety-second review window, in whether anyone built a path for the case the model gets wrong. Compassion at institutional scale is not a feeling that an organization has. It is a pattern of decisions it makes, repeated thousands of times a day, mostly by people and systems acting exactly as the institution designed them to act.

The people inside these systems are not, as a rule, cruel. The reviewer with ninety seconds per case did not choose ninety seconds. The engineer who tuned the length-of-stay model was solving an accuracy problem, not deciding to deny a grandmother her recovery. This is the part that resists the easy story. Good people work inside systems that still produce harm, because the harm is an emergent property of incentives, defaults, and pressures that no single person fully controls. Asking whether the individuals care is the wrong question. It is almost always answered yes, and it almost never predicts what the institution does. The better question is whether care survives the system: whether it holds up under cost, under scale, under the quarter's numbers, under the automation that now sits between most institutions and most of the people they affect.

Here is the strange thing about those decisions. They are, in principle, observable. The denial rate exists. The appeal-reversal rate exists. The gap between what the leadership says about patient dignity and what the claims data shows exists, and it is measurable. The decision to staff a review queue at ninety seconds a case is a documented decision with a documented author. The behavior is not hidden because it is unknowable. It is hidden because no one whose job is to look across institutions is looking at it, and no shared standard exists for what they would be looking for.

Consider what we do measure. A public company's finances are audited and rated, its products recalled when they injure people, its data breaches disclosed by law, its environmental and labor practices scored by a dozen overlapping indices, and its AI models run against a growing battery of public benchmarks. We have constructed, domain by domain, an entire apparatus of external accountability, precisely because we learned that institutions optimize for what is measured and neglect what is not. Then we left out the one measure that asks whether the institution, in the ordinary course of its decisions, reduces or compounds the suffering of the people on the other side.

That omission is not a moral failure on anyone's part. It is a gap in infrastructure. The denial letter, the closed case, the unrenewed contract, the suppressed appeal: each is a data point that no instrument currently collects, on a dimension no institution is currently graded against, by anyone with the independence to grade it. The cost of leaving them unmeasured is that institutions get to manage the appearance of compassion while the conduct goes unwatched, and the rest of us have no common way to tell the two apart.

The instinctive objection is that this gap exists because the thing itself cannot be measured, that compassion is too soft, too subjective, too internal to score. It is the first objection any serious funder will raise, and it deserves a serious answer rather than a reassurance. But the gap is not, first, a measurement problem. It is the absence of anyone independent deciding to measure at all.



# Chapter 2: The Problem Is Not That Institutions Do Not Care

Read the values pages of the institutions responsible for the most documented harm of the last decade and you will not find indifference. You will find the opposite. Mission statements about dignity. Patient-first charters. Codes of ethics signed by every employee. Chief executives who talk, often sincerely, about the people their organizations exist to serve. The premise that institutions harm people because the people inside them do not care is not just uncharitable. It is wrong often enough to be useless as an explanation, and any reform built on it will aim at the wrong target.

[GRAPHIC: Split panel. Left, "What the institution says": a column of representative values-statement language. Right, "What the evidence shows": the corresponding conduct, scored. The gap between the two columns is the chapter.]

The more uncomfortable account is that care is real and harm is also real, and the two coexist because care is not what does the work. What does the work is the system: the incentives that decide which outcomes get rewarded, the defaults that decide what happens when no one is paying attention, the metrics that decide what counts as a job well done, and the pressures that decide which corners get cut when the quarter is tight. An individual can hold a value. An institution runs on a structure, and the structure has its own behavior, which is frequently at odds with the values of the individuals inside it.

This is why the useful question is not whether an institution cares. It is whether care survives. Care has to pass through process, and process flattens the particular case into the standard one. It has to hold up under pressure, and pressure is exactly the moment that tests whether a value is load-bearing or decorative. It has to scale, and at scale the human who might have noticed the exception is replaced by a rule that cannot. It has to compete with incentives, and incentives are patient. And it has to persist over time, across leadership changes and budget cycles and the slow drift of an organization toward whatever its metrics actually measure. Each of those is a filter. Compassion that is genuine at the level of intention can fail at any one of them and never reach the person on the other side.

Consider the pressures one at a time, because they fail differently. Process failure is the benefits office that closes a case for a missing form, not from malice but because the workflow has no step for "this applicant never received the form." Scale failure is the moderation system that suppresses an appeal before a human reads it, because there are ten million appeals and four hundred reviewers. Incentive failure is the hospital that books the profitable procedure and lets the unprofitable follow-up slide, with no one ever deciding, in so many words, to neglect the patient. Time failure is the program that was genuinely compassionate under its founder and is now a brand, its original purpose surviving in the language but not the conduct. None of these requires a villain. Each produces a person who was not helped.

The institutions themselves often cannot see it, and that is part of the problem rather than an excuse for it. From the inside, an organization sees its intentions, its policies, its training materials, and the cases that go right. The cases that go wrong are diffuse, distributed across thousands of individual decisions, each defensible on its own terms. The denial rate is a number on a dashboard, not a grandmother on the phone. Without an outside instrument pointed at the conduct rather than the intention, the gap between what an institution means and what it does is nearly invisible to the institution, and entirely invisible to everyone else.

Which is the whole reason this gap can only be closed from the outside, and only by measuring behavior rather than asking about belief. The benchmark this document describes does not score an institution's values. It scores whether its compassion holds when holding it costs something. Its Integrity dimension asks, in part, whether compassionate behavior survives when it is legally risky or financially inconvenient, and its scoring rule is deliberately severe about this: a subdimension cannot be rated above the Developing band unless assessors can find at least one documented case where the institution did the costly thing anyway. Looking good when looking good is free is not evidence of anything. The measure is built around the one question that intention cannot answer for itself.

That severity is only meaningful if someone is actually applying it, consistently, across institutions, on a standard the public can see. No one is. The conduct described here is real, it is observable, and it is graded against nothing. The next chapter is about how strange that absence is, given everything else we have decided to measure.



# Chapter 3: We Measure Almost Everything Except This

A mid-sized public company carries more external scorekeepers than most people realize. Its financial statements are audited to a common standard and rated by agencies whose ratings move its cost of capital. Its physical products can be recalled by regulators and are tracked in public injury databases. A serious data breach must be disclosed by law, often within days, into registries anyone can search. Its emissions, its labor practices, and its supply chain are scored by a thicket of overlapping indices and certifications. If it trains artificial intelligence, its models are run against a fast-growing battery of public capability and safety benchmarks. None of this infrastructure existed by accident. Each piece was built, usually after a failure, on a hard-won lesson: institutions optimize for what is measured and neglect what is not, so if you want an outcome to matter, you build a standard for it and put the result where outsiders can see it.

Now name the standard for whether that same company, in the ordinary course of its decisions, recognizes when a person is suffering and reduces the harm rather than compounding it. There isn't one. Not a missing line item in an existing framework. A whole domain of institutional conduct with no comparable, independent, public measure at all.

The pattern holds across the domains we take for granted. Financial integrity has audited statements, a shared accounting standard, and credit ratings produced by parties other than the company being rated. Product safety has recalls, inspections, and injury reporting. Cybersecurity has breach-disclosure law and shared control frameworks. Environmental and labor conduct sit inside decades of indices, ratings, and certifications, imperfect and contested, but present and public. Artificial intelligence, the newest entrant, already has more public benchmarks for what a model can do, and increasingly for how safely it behaves, than exist for how the company building it treats the people its systems reach. The accountability apparatus is dense, redundant, and uneven, and it covers nearly everything an institution does except the one thing this benchmark measures.

It is worth being precise about the claim, because the strong version is false and the true version is strong enough. The claim is not that nobody has ever measured anything adjacent to institutional compassion. Human-rights organizations rank governments. Wellbeing researchers survey populations. Worker-voice and patient-experience surveys exist. The claim is narrower and harder to dismiss: there is no independent, standardized, comparable, public benchmark of how institutions across sectors recognize, respond to, and reduce suffering, scored against documented behavior and held to a consistent evidence standard the way an audit is. The adjacent efforts are siloed by sector, inconsistent in method, frequently funded by the parties they assess, or locked behind institutional paywalls. None of them lets a journalist compare a hospital system, a national government, and a robotics lab on the same scale, using a method anyone can read.

The absence is most glaring exactly where the stakes are climbing fastest. Artificial intelligence and robotics labs are among the most consequential institutions of the decade, building systems that can reach millions of people before any outside party has reviewed how those systems treat them. On the question of whether the institution recognizes the suffering its products cause and responds to it, there is, at present, almost no standardized external measurement. We are building the most powerful instruments of influence in modern history inside an accountability vacuum on the one dimension that asks what those instruments do to the people on the other side.

This is a gap in infrastructure, not a failure of will, and it has a familiar shape. We did not have breach-disclosure registries, then a series of disasters produced them. The measurement of institutional compassion sits today roughly where financial auditing sat before it became standard practice: obviously needed in hindsight, technically demanding, and waiting for someone independent to decide that the difficulty is not a reason to leave it unmeasured.

The obvious objection is that the gap is already filled, that ESG ratings and corporate-responsibility frameworks and AI-ethics codes are precisely this measurement under other names. They are not, and the reasons they are not are specific. That is the subject of the next chapter.



# Chapter 4: Why Existing Frameworks Fall Short

The reasonable first reaction to a new benchmark is suspicion that it duplicates an old one. The space around institutional conduct is crowded: ESG ratings, corporate-responsibility frameworks, social audits, certifications, internal ethics codes, and a young generation of AI-ethics statements. Each is useful. Each answers a real question. The trouble is that none of them answers this one, and several are built in ways that would prevent them from answering it honestly even if they tried.

Start with what each adjacent tool is actually good at, because the case here is not that they are worthless. ESG ratings can tell you whether a company disclosed the right things and how its disclosed practices compare to a sector norm. A compliance audit can tell you whether a specific rule was followed. A certification can tell you whether a process met a published bar at the moment of inspection. A customer-satisfaction score can tell you whether an interaction was liked. An AI-ethics statement can tell you what an organization says it intends. Every one of those is a genuine signal. Not one of them, by itself, tells you whether an institution recognized that a person was suffering, responded in proportion, and learned from the harm it caused. They measure disclosure, conformance, intention, and sentiment. They do not measure recognition, response, and repair as observed behavior, which is the thing.

[GRAPHIC: Comparison table. Rows: ESG ratings, Human-rights and wellbeing indices, Corporate audits and certifications, Internal ethics codes, AI-ethics statements, Compassion Benchmark. Three structural problems recur, and they are worth separating because they have different fixes. The first is narrowness. Each framework was designed to answer its own question and answers it within a single domain. An emissions index does not speak to patient dignity. A labor-rights audit does not speak to whether an algorithm suppressed someone's appeal. The frameworks are siloed by sector and by subject, so no two of them can place a hospital system, a national government, and a robotics lab on one comparable scale. The conduct this benchmark cares about, the recognition of suffering and the response to it, runs across all of those institutions and is captured whole by none of the existing tools.

The second problem is capturability, and here precision matters both for fairness and because the failure mode is instructive. In much of the ESG-ratings world, the entity being rated participates in the rating: it supplies data, reviews drafts, and in some commercial arrangements pays for the assessment or for the consulting that improves it. That is not a hidden scandal; it is the disclosed business model of parts of the industry, and it produces the documented oddity that the major raters frequently disagree with each other about the same company, in a way bond-rating agencies, for all their flaws, mostly do not. When the rated party shapes the rating, the rating drifts toward what the rated party can live with. The lesson the credible measurement institutions learned long ago is that independence cannot be a promise of good behavior; it has to be structural. Freedom House keeps its flagship index free of government money even though most of the organization's budget is government-funded. The cautionary cases are equally clear: an AI-research organization recently drew a credibility crisis when undisclosed funding from a lab it benchmarked, paired with privileged access, came to light. A measure of institutional compassion that could be purchased, softened, or pre-reviewed by the institutions it grades would be worse than no measure, because it would launder reputation while appearing to scrutinize it.

The third problem is access. Several of the most rigorous existing tools live behind institutional paywalls priced for procurement departments, not for the public. Enterprise ESG monitoring runs from several thousand dollars a year at the low end to well over twenty thousand at the high end. That pricing is defensible as a business, but it means the very people with the most at stake, the journalist, the affected community, the policymaker without a research budget, cannot see the analysis, cannot check it, and cannot cite it. A finding locked inside a subscription is not accountability. It is a private service that happens to be about ethics.

Put the three together and the shape of what is missing becomes exact. The gap is not a better ESG rating. It is an instrument that is broad enough to compare institutions across sectors on one scale, independent enough that the rated entity cannot touch the result, and open enough that anyone can read the method, check the math, and contest the score. None of the existing frameworks is all three at once, and the ones that are strongest on rigor are often weakest on independence or access, because the business model that funds the rigor is the thing that compromises it.

That is the brief for everything in Part II. If the existing tools cannot answer the question, the burden falls on the new one to define institutional compassion precisely enough to be observable, score it transparently enough to be reconstructable, and structure itself so that the answer cannot be bought. The next part begins with the definition: what, exactly, this benchmark measures.

# Part II: The Standard


# Chapter 5: What Compassion Benchmark Measures

The word "compassion" is doing a lot of work, and most of it is the wrong work. In ordinary use it names a feeling: the inward turn of fellow-feeling when we meet someone in pain. An institution does not have that feeling. A health insurer does not ache. A government does not grieve. A frontier AI lab does not lie awake. If compassion were only the feeling, then asking whether an institution has it would be a category error, and there would be nothing to measure. The skeptic who says compassion is too soft to score is usually picturing the feeling, and on that picture the skeptic is right.

Compassion Benchmark does not measure the feeling. It measures conduct, and the distinction is the foundation of everything that follows.

What an institution does when someone it is responsible for is in trouble is not a private interior state. It is a sequence of decisions, made by people and systems acting as the institution designed them to act, and decisions leave evidence. A denial rate, an appeals path, whether a coverage decision the model got wrong ever reached a human: all of it is conduct. The benchmark is built on a single working premise: at institutional scale, compassion is a pattern of decisions, not a sentiment, and a pattern of decisions can be observed, compared, and scored.

That premise narrows the question to something answerable. We are not asking whether an institution is kind. We are asking whether, across the decisions it actually makes, it does three things in order.

[GRAPHIC: The recognize → respond → reduce loop as the spine of the standard. Three linked stages around a return arrow, each labeled with its plain question, the whole loop closing back on itself to mark that it repeats with every case.]

**Recognize.** Does the institution detect that someone is in distress or need, often before that person can name it cleanly or file it correctly? Recognition is the first thing to fail, because an institution that never registers the suffering in front of it has no occasion to do anything about it. The missing form the applicant never received, the appeal suppressed before anyone read it, the worker whose complaint routed into a queue no one staffs: each is a failure to recognize, and each forecloses everything downstream.

**Respond.** Once need is detected, does the institution do something real and proportional about it, or does it generate activity that resembles help without being help? Response is where good intentions most often dissolve into process. A benefits office can acknowledge a hardship and still close the case on a technicality. A company can run an ethics review that reaches the wrong people too late. Recognition without proportional response is awareness that changes nothing.

**Reduce.** Across time and at the level of the whole population it serves, does the institution actually leave less suffering than it found, or does it relieve the visible case while leaving the conditions that produced it untouched? Reduction is the hardest of the three to fake and the slowest to show, because it asks about outcomes and second-order effects rather than gestures. An institution can recognize and respond to one family on the phone and still run a system that produces a thousand more such calls by Friday.

Recognize, respond to, and reduce suffering. The triad is the spine of the standard, and it is deliberately ordered: each stage depends on the one before it, and an institution can pass the first and fail the rest. This is why a single warm anecdote proves so little: one generously handled case tells you nothing about the pattern, and the pattern is the subject.

The narrowing has a second consequence, easy to miss and central to the benchmark's independence. If compassion is conduct, then what an institution says about its own compassion is not the measurement. It is one input, and a weak one. The mission statement, the values page, the chief executive's letter about dignity: these describe the story the institution tells about itself. The benchmark scores the world as encountered, not the story as presented. Where the two diverge, and they frequently diverge, the conduct wins. The denial data and the dignity statement can both be real; they are simply not the same object, and only one of them is behavior.

Measuring conduct rather than sentiment still requires deciding, precisely, which behaviors count and how to read them, because "does it reduce suffering" is too large a question to score directly. An institution that recognizes financial distress may be blind to medical distress. One that responds quickly may respond unfairly, reaching the articulate and missing the people with the greatest need and the least power to ask. The single question has to be decomposed into separate, nameable capacities that can each be looked for in the evidence and rated on their own.

There are eight of them.



# Chapter 6: The Eight Dimensions

Take a single institution and hold it still for a moment. Picture a frontier AI lab, well-funded, technically excellent, releasing systems that millions of people now use to ask for help with their health, their work, and their worst days. This lab will run through the rest of Part II as a worked example. Its scores are illustrative, built to demonstrate the standard rather than to report a published result, and they are flagged as illustrative every time they appear. What it offers here is a face. The eight dimensions are abstract until you ask them about something specific, so we will ask them about this lab.

The decomposition matters because "does it reduce suffering" is one question wearing the clothes of eight. An institution can be acutely aware of distress and useless at responding to it. It can respond generously to the people who ask loudly and never reach the people who cannot ask at all. It can help in ways that build dependency, or own its failures, or never do either. These are different capacities that fail independently, and a standard collapsing them into one number too early learns nothing. Compassion Benchmark keeps them separate, each phrased as a question a reasonable outsider could ask about any institution, in any sector, without specialist training.

[GRAPHIC: The eight-dimension wheel. Eight colored arcs forming a ring, each labeled with its code, name, and one-line question, in the canonical order AWR through INT. Colors from the locked dimension palette; cyan reserved for AWR/Awareness only, never used elsewhere as a general accent.]

**AWR Awareness.** Does this entity reliably detect when others are in pain or need, before they have to name it? For our illustrative lab, awareness is the question of whether harms from its systems get noticed by the lab before they surface in the press, and whether it sees the distress of a user in crisis that a model was never designed to recognize.

**EMP Empathy.** Does this entity genuinely connect with the inner experience of those it serves, or does it process them? Empathy at institutional scale is not warmth for its own sake. It is whether the people on the other side feel understood rather than handled, and whether that holds across cultural difference and under pressure.

**ACT Action.** Does compassionate understanding translate into real, proportional, effective help? This is the dimension where awareness either becomes useful or evaporates, and we will open it up in full below.

**EQU Equity.** Is care distributed fairly, especially toward those with the greatest need and the least power? An institution can perform well on average and still fail the specific people who most depend on it. Equity asks where the help actually lands.

**BND Boundaries.** Is helping sustainable, ethical, and autonomy-preserving rather than dependency-creating? For an AI lab this is unusually sharp: a system designed for maximum engagement and one designed to build a user's own capacity are not the same product, and the difference is a Boundaries question.

**ACC Accountability.** Does this entity own its failures, correct course, and make genuine repair? Every institution causes harm sometimes. Accountability asks what happens next: acknowledgment or deflection, correction or repetition, repair or a press statement.

**SYS Systemic Thinking.** Does compassion extend to root causes and structural change, not only symptom relief? An institution can treat the visible case and leave intact the structure that produces a thousand more. Systemic Thinking asks whether it works upstream.

**INT Integrity.** Is compassion genuine, consistent, and non-performative, especially when it costs something? This is the dimension that asks whether any of the other seven survive contact with a hard quarter, a leadership change, or a moment when doing the right thing is expensive.

Eight questions. That is the whole claim of the wheel: compassion at scale is not one vague virtue but eight separate, observable capacities, and an institution has a different answer for each.

To see what sits beneath a single arc, open up Action. Each of the eight dimensions is itself made of five subdimensions, the concrete behaviors an assessor actually goes looking for, and Action's five show how the zoom works.

- **AC1 Responsiveness.** Whether identified needs receive timely, appropriately prioritized responses, or sit in a queue.
- **AC2 Proportionality.** Whether help is calibrated to actual need rather than to what is easiest to provide.
- **AC3 Efficacy.** Whether the help actually works, as against activity that only looks like help.
- **AC4 Resource Mobilization.** Whether the entity brings genuinely adequate resources to bear, or token ones.
- **AC5 Follow-Through.** Whether the entity persists rather than disengaging once attention moves on.

Asked of the illustrative lab, these stop being abstractions. Responsiveness is how fast a reported safety failure gets a fix rather than a statement. Proportionality is whether a serious harm gets a serious response or the same templated reply as a feature request. Efficacy is whether the fix works in the wild or only in the demo. Resource Mobilization is whether the safety team is staffed to the scale of the deployment or to the scale of the press release. Follow-Through is whether the fix survives the next launch cycle, when attention and engineers have moved on. A lab can score well on Responsiveness and badly on Follow-Through, and the subdimensions are what let the standard say so instead of averaging the distinction away.

Forty subdimensions span the eight dimensions, five each, and they are the level at which scoring happens: every one is rated on a behavioral scale before anything rolls up into a dimension or a composite. The full set, with the plain-English definition of each, is the subject of Appendix A, and there is no need to march through all forty here. The pattern established by Action holds across the wheel: in each dimension, the dimension is the question and the subdimensions are the places you look for the answer.

This is also where the standard quietly resists capture. An institution can polish the one or two dimensions that draw public attention and let the rest decay, and a single headline number would let it. Forty named behaviors do not. A lab that is excellent at Awareness and hollow at Accountability has nowhere for the hollowness to hide once the profile is broken out, which is why the next problem is not what to measure but how to turn forty behaviors, observed in messy real-world evidence, into one defensible score. That requires deciding which evidence is allowed to count.



# Chapter 7: Evidence Before Assertion

Two documents arrive describing the same institution on the same week. One is the company's annual report, which devotes a glossy spread to its commitment to the people it serves. The other is a regulator's finding that the same company systematically denied them what they were owed. Both are real. Both are about the same conduct. They point in opposite directions. A measurement system has to decide, before it ever assigns a number, which of the two it believes, and why. Get that decision wrong and every score downstream inherits the error.

Compassion Benchmark decides it the same way every time. Evidence is sorted into five tiers, strongest to weakest, and one rule governs all of them: strong scores require stronger evidence. A high mark on any behavior cannot rest on what an institution says about itself. The annual report and the regulatory finding are not weighed as equals and split down the middle. They occupy different tiers, and the tier decides which one moves the score.

[GRAPHIC: The five-tier evidence pyramid. A narrow apex widening to a broad base, each band labeled with its tier and one real source type: independent audit at the top, entity self-report at the bottom. The visual encodes that the strongest evidence is the rarest and the weakest is the most abundant.]

**Tier 1, independent external audit, sits at the top.** An assessment produced by a party with no stake in the result: a third-party audit, a regulatory finding, an academic study. This is the only tier strong enough to confirm the highest behavioral anchors without corroboration from below. It is also the scarcest, which is the first honest thing to say about the pyramid. The strongest evidence is the rarest, and a great deal of institutional conduct has never been independently audited at all.

**Tier 2 is verifiable outcome data.** Measured results that can be checked against a defined population: disaggregated service data, longitudinal surveys, resolution rates. The defining word is outcomes. Not what the institution intended, not what it offered, but what measurably happened to the people on the receiving end.

**Tier 3 is community testimony.** The account of the people the institution is actually responsible for, gathered through affected populations, independent focus groups, and structured interviews. Its placement is deliberate and, for some readers, surprising: the testimony of the served sits above the institution's own paperwork. Where leadership's narrative and the community's account conflict, the community account is the primary reference point. The people on the other side of a decision are treated as authorities on what that decision did to them.

**Tier 4 is policy and process documents.** Governing documents, training records, budget allocations. This tier shows that a practice is written down and resourced. It does not show that the practice works. A well-drafted policy is evidence of intent and capacity, not of outcome, and it is rated accordingly.

**Tier 5, entity self-report, sits at the bottom.** Mission statements, annual reports, the values page. This is the most abundant evidence in the world and the weakest. Self-report cannot move a score upward on its own; it requires corroboration from a higher tier first. Stated plainly, in the form the assessors actually use: a mission statement is not evidence of compassion. It is evidence that the institution wished to be seen as compassionate, which is a different fact about a different object.

The ordering inverts the instinct of public relations, and that inversion is the whole point. An institution controls its self-report completely and its independent audit not at all. A standard that let the controllable evidence set the score would be measuring communications budgets. By placing the institution's own account at the foundation of the pyramid rather than its peak, the benchmark scores what is hardest for the institution to manufacture and easiest for an outsider to check. This is the operating meaning of the rule that runs beneath all five tiers: evidence beats aspiration. Where the paper claim and the lived experience diverge, the assessor scores the world as encountered, not the story as presented.

Two further consequences follow, and both protect the score's honesty rather than the institution's image. First, the tier of evidence governs not only the score but the confidence in it. The higher behavioral anchors cannot be reached on self-report alone; a top mark recorded without strong, pressure-tested evidence is flagged provisional and held for review rather than published as settled. Confidence is disclosed, not hidden. In the universities index it is published per entity, and a thin evidentiary record yields a Low-confidence flag carried in the open. Second, evidence has a lifespan. A regulator's adjudicated finding persists until something supersedes it. An uncorroborated allegation decays and cannot accumulate, on its own, into a score change. And because desk research naturally over-reports bad news, assessors are required to go looking for the structural good news that press cycles miss: outcome data acted upon, audits followed by correction, durable access built and maintained, commitments held when holding them cost something.

The pyramid says nothing yet about how a stack of tiered evidence becomes a single number between 0 and 100. It establishes the prior question, the one that has to be settled before any arithmetic is allowed to begin: not how much an institution has to say for itself, but how much of what it says anyone has reason to believe.



# Chapter 8: How Assessment Works

A composite score of 46.9 looks like a precise thing, and precision invites suspicion. Where does the .9 come from? Who decided it was not 51, or 38? The honest answer, and the one that separates this benchmark from the indices a reader has learned to distrust, is that no one decided it. The number is built from the bottom up by a published formula, and anyone holding the eight underlying scores can reconstruct it exactly. Every number below is illustrative, built to demonstrate the mechanism rather than to report a published score, using the illustrative AI lab from the last two chapters.

Scoring starts not at the composite but at the floor, with a single behavior. Each of the forty subdimensions is rated on the same six-point anchor scale, where each level is a described pattern of conduct rather than a vibe.

[GRAPHIC: The 0–5 anchor strip. Six labeled steps left to right with their behavioral descriptions: 0 Active Harm, 1 Absent, 2 Minimal, 3 Developing, 4 Established, 5 Exemplary. The 0 step is set apart to mark it as a separate documented-harm floor rather than a continuation of the scale.]

- **0 Active Harm.** Specific, documented harm. A separate floor, requiring written evidence and a second assessor's sign-off.
- **1 Absent.** No meaningful capacity.
- **2 Minimal.** A nominal capacity that fails under any pressure.
- **3 Developing.** Genuine capacity in some cases, but inconsistent.
- **4 Established.** Consistent capacity across most cases, confirmed by the people served.
- **5 Exemplary.** Outstanding, independently verified, and sustained when it was costly.

The anchors are written so that two assessors reading the same evidence land in the same place, and the gap between a 3 and a 4 is not a matter of generosity. A 3 is real but uneven. A 4 requires evidence that the behavior held up across most cases and was confirmed from outside the institution. The higher anchors carry an evidentiary cost that the lower ones do not, which is the first place the evidence hierarchy from the previous chapter does its work.

The five subdimension ratings within a dimension average into that dimension's score, so each of the eight dimensions arrives as a number on the same 1-to-5 scale. For the illustrative lab, assessment produces these eight:

| AWR | EMP | ACT | EQU | BND | ACC | SYS | INT |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 3.5 | 2.5 | 3.5 | 2.5 | 3.0 | 2.5 | 3.0 | 2.5 |

Now the composite. Scoring model v1.2 turns these eight numbers into a 0-to-100 score in two moves.

**The base.** Take the mean of the eight dimension scores, then stretch the 1-to-5 range onto a 0-to-100 scale. The formula is `((mean − 1) / 4) × 100`. The subtraction of 1 sets the bottom of the behavioral scale to zero, so that an institution sitting at the lowest anchor on every dimension scores 0 rather than 20.

- Mean = (3.5 + 2.5 + 3.5 + 2.5 + 3.0 + 2.5 + 3.0 + 2.5) ÷ 8 = 2.875.
- Base = ((2.875 − 1) ÷ 4) × 100 = 46.9.

**The integration premium.** On top of the base, an entity can earn up to ten additional points, and the premium is where the standard encodes what it values. It is `10 × a consistency factor × a balance factor`. The consistency factor rewards an even profile: low variation across the eight dimensions earns the full multiplier, high variation shrinks it toward nothing. The balance factor subtracts 0.2 for every dimension that falls below the exemplary threshold of 4.0, with a floor of zero. And one override governs both: if any single dimension sits at exactly 0, active documented harm, the premium is canceled entirely, no matter how even the rest of the profile looks.

For the illustrative lab, the eight scores are tightly clustered, so the consistency factor is at its maximum of 1.0. But not one dimension reaches 4.0, so the balance factor is `max(0, 1 − 8 × 0.2)`, which is 0. The premium is `10 × 1.0 × 0 = 0`.

[GRAPHIC: The composite-formula callout. The two-line equation set large: `base = ((mean of 8 dimension scores − 1) / 4) × 100`, then `final = base + integration premium (0 to +10)`, with the illustrative lab's numbers substituted beneath each term.]

The lab's final composite is `46.9 + 0 = 46.9`. That lands it in the third of five bands, each twenty points wide: **Critical 0–20, Developing 20–40, Functional 40–60, Established 60–80, Exemplary 80–100.** At 46.9 the lab is Functional: core practices exist and clear a basic bar, with significant gaps still open. It earned no premium because no single dimension reached the exemplary threshold, and that is the common case, not a glitch. The premium is hard to earn on purpose.

How hard, and why it is shaped this way, is clearest when two entities with very different profiles are set side by side.

| Profile | Dimension scores | Base | Premium | Final | Band |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Balanced | all eight at 4.0 | 75.0 | +10.0 | 85.0 | Exemplary |
| Spiky | four at 5.0, four at 2.0 | 62.5 | +2.0 | 64.5 | Established |

The spiky entity has four perfect scores. The balanced entity has none. The balanced entity wins, by twenty points and a full band, because its care is even and sustained while the spiky entity is brilliant in four places and failing in four others. A standard that simply averaged the eight would rank these two far closer together. The premium is the benchmark's deliberate statement that consistency is itself a form of compassion: an institution strong everywhere is doing something an institution with four spikes and four holes is not.

Every composite on this page is reconstructable. Hand any reader the eight dimension scores and the published formula and they will arrive at the same final number, which is the honesty proof.

One distinction has to be stated plainly before this is mistaken for something it is not. The 1,256 public scores are produced by an automated nightly pipeline that applies this exact framework and formula at scale, with a human approving any change before it is published. That pipeline is built and running. It is not a room of analysts hand-assessing twelve hundred institutions. A separate and more intensive method, the seven-session Human Assessment Battery conducted by credentialed assessors in the field, is the aspirational standard that will underpin a future paid certification. The two pathways share these anchors and this formula but are not the same process; the next chapter, about what a score is for, applies to both.



# Chapter 9: From Score to Improvement

A single composite is almost useless to the institution that receives it. The illustrative lab's 46.9 tells its leadership that it sits in the middle band and nothing about what to do on Monday. If the benchmark stopped at the composite it would be a verdict, and verdicts do not improve anything; they only rank. The value lives one level down, in the profile the composite was built from, because that profile names the specific behaviors a score is made of and therefore the specific behaviors that would move it.

Break the illustrative lab's 46.9 back into its parts and the flat number becomes a map. Awareness and Action sit at 3.5, the lab's relative strengths. Empathy, Equity, Accountability, and Integrity sit at 2.5, dragging the mean down. The lab is not uniformly mediocre: it is good at noticing problems and acting on them, and weak at connecting with the people affected, distributing help fairly, and owning its failures. That is not a grade. It is a diagnosis, and it points at four dimensions rather than a vague exhortation to do better.

[GRAPHIC: Before/after dimension profile for the illustrative lab, drawn as a grouped bar chart across the eight dimensions rather than a radar. A radar would let the eye read improvement as enclosed area, which overstates change; paired bars per dimension show movement honestly, dimension by dimension. The "after" bars lift the four weak dimensions toward 4.0 and leave the two strong ones roughly flat.]

The map gets more precise still. Each of those four weak dimensions decomposes into five subdimensions, and a 2.5 on Accountability is itself a story: perhaps the lab acknowledges harm but does not repair it, or corrects course privately but resists transparency. The standard does not hand an institution a number and walk away. It hands over the forty behaviors the number was assembled from, and an institution that wanted to raise its score would find, in that breakdown, the actual conduct it would have to change. Improvement is specified, not wished for.

Specified is not the same as easy, and the standard is built to make sure the distinction holds. Raising a subdimension from Developing to Established is not a matter of issuing a better policy; a press release does not move a score. Because the higher anchors demand pressure-tested evidence, the only route from 46.9 to a higher band runs through conduct that an outsider can verify, sustained long enough to show it was not a one-week performance. The benchmark makes improvement legible and keeps it expensive, which is the combination that makes a gain mean something.

Reversibility runs in both directions, and the floor is where it is starkest. UnitedHealth Group is scored at 10.2, in the Critical band, the lowest in the benchmark's healthcare coverage. A score that low is not a life sentence and not a single bad headline frozen into a number: it can be earned down further, and it can be climbed back. When an entity already sits at or near the floor, new adverse evidence has no scorable room left to move the composite, so it is logged as an upgrade to the strength of the evidence record rather than as a drop in the number. When a Department of Justice probe against UnitedHealth Group expanded, it was recorded exactly that way: a tier upgrade with no change to the 10.2, because the standard declines to manufacture motion it cannot justify.

Climbing back is governed by an exit protocol that is demanding by design. A floored entity recovers only on evidence-of-care behavior at the dimension level, sustained across at least two consecutive assessment cycles, drawn from sources outside the entity's own control. Performative statements do not register; a campaign announcing reform is precisely the kind of self-report the evidence hierarchy ranks lowest. What lifts a floor is durable, externally visible change in conduct, observed over time.

This is what keeps a published score from being a one-time grade stamped on an institution and left there. The pipeline reassesses, evidence accumulates and decays, and a score is a current reading that the next cycle can revise in either direction. The only way to move it is to change what the institution actually does. Whether that prospect changes the institution's calculus is the question the rest of this publication takes up.

# Part III: Research Foundation


# Chapter 10: Compassion as Observable Institutional Behavior

Two hospital systems publish patient commitments that read almost identically. Both promise dignity, both promise to treat the whole person, both use the word compassion in the first sentence. One scores meaningfully higher than the other on Compassion Benchmark. The difference between them is not the sincerity of the language, which no instrument can read, and which the benchmark never tries to. The difference is that one system's complaint data shows that grievances from non-English-speaking patients get resolved at the same rate as everyone else's, and the other's shows they do not.

That is the whole move of this chapter, and of the standard underneath it. Compassion Benchmark does not score what an institution feels; it scores what an institution does, because conduct is observable and feeling is not, and because feeling, even when genuine, is a poor predictor of what happens to the person on the other side of a decision. The mission statement is the institution describing itself; the conduct is the institution. When the two diverge, the rule is the one stated in the evidence hierarchy: score the world as encountered, not the story as presented. This is not a philosophical claim about whether compassion is "really" a feeling. It is a methodological one about what an independent party with no privileged access to anyone's interior life can measure from the outside. An assessor cannot verify that a claims supervisor cares about the families on the other end of a denial, but can verify the denial rate, the appeal-reversal rate, the staffing of the review queue, and whether the supervisor's own account survives contact with the people the institution serves.

The framework sorts those facts by how directly they can be shown, on the behavioral-evidence ladder that runs from weakest to strongest.

[GRAPHIC: Behavioral-evidence ladder, four rungs ascending. Sentiment (mission statements, values pages) at the bottom, then Policy (written rules, budgets, training), then Conduct (what the institution actually did in documented cases), then Verified Outcome (independently confirmed results for affected people) at the top. The same source can appear at more than one rung; height is what counts.]

At the bottom is sentiment: what an institution says it stands for, the weakest input because it costs nothing and commits to nothing. One rung up is policy: the written rule, the budget line, the training curriculum, stronger because someone had to resource it, but establishing only that a practice is written down, not that it works. The third rung is conduct: what the institution actually did in a documented case, especially a hard one. The top rung is verified outcome: an independently confirmed result for the people the institution is responsible for. A score resting on the top two rungs is worth more, and allowed to go higher, than one resting on the bottom two.

Climbing the ladder answers one question. A second has to be settled before any conduct can be scored: whose conduct is it? An institution can be the cause of harm or the target of it, and the two are scored in opposite directions. Before any harm event is scored, the assessor settles who the subject is, meaning the population the entity is responsible for: for a country, the people under its control; for a company, its workers, customers, and operating communities; for an AI or robotics lab, its users and the broader society its systems affect; for a university, its students, staff, and surrounding communities. Harm inflicted on an entity by an outside actor is attributed to the perpetrator, not charged against the victim. Harm an entity inflicts by its own choice is charged against it.

The rule has teeth in real cases. When the United States Department of Justice took action against Harvard in June 2026, that external action was not scored against Harvard, because Harvard was its subject, not its author. Harvard's own layoffs in the same window were scored, because those were its own decisions. The composite held at 52.3 through both events: the institution is graded on what it chose, not on what happened to it or near it.

One honest caveat belongs here, because it recurs throughout Part III. The claim that observable conduct predicts how an institution treats people better than its stated sentiment does is a reasonable and defensible premise, and it is the premise the whole framework rests on. It has not yet been put through formal external validation against independent outcome measures. That validation is named, costed work in the plan ahead, not a result already in hand. What can be said now is narrower and still worth saying: the benchmark scores only what can be shown, weights it by how directly it can be shown, and publishes the evidence trail so that anyone can check the rung each score is standing on.



# Chapter 11: The Science Behind the Framework

A single number that claims to capture how an institution treats human suffering invites an obvious suspicion: that it is one person's impression dressed up as a measurement. The framework is built specifically to answer that suspicion. It never asks an assessor to rate compassion as one thing. It breaks the construct into eight capacities, breaks each capacity into five concrete behaviors, and asks the assessor only ever to rate a behavior against a fixed description, with the evidence attached. The composite is assembled from those small judgments by a published formula. No step in the chain asks anyone for a global verdict.

This is a borrowed move. A construct that cannot be observed directly is estimated through indicators that can be: you do not measure "democracy," you measure specified, observable things and aggregate them under a stated rule. The eight dimensions are the high-level capacities; the forty subdimensions are the indicators, each written so that two assessors looking at the same evidence should reach a similar rating. The decomposition converts a soft word into something that can be checked, contested, and reproduced. [VERIFY: cite the construct-and-indicator measurement literature, e.g. a standard text on latent constructs and observable indicators, rather than asserting the principle unsupported.]

Two existing institutions show the same instinct at industrial scale, and the benchmark studied both. V-Dem produces its democracy measures from a broad set of specific indicators, coded by a distributed body of roughly 3,500 coders, so no single hand controls a country's score. Stanford's Foundation Model Transparency Index scores AI developers against roughly one hundred discrete indicators rather than a single overall impression, with an external advisory structure attesting to the method. The lesson is architectural: capture resistance comes from decomposition plus publication, because moving a composite made of many small, rule-bound judgments would mean corrupting many independent indicators at once, in public, against a method anyone can re-run. The benchmark claims kinship with that design philosophy, though not the validation record V-Dem has built over years.

What does an indicator look like up close? Take AC1 Responsiveness, the first subdimension of Action: whether identified needs receive timely, appropriately prioritized responses, rated on the same 0-to-5 anchor scale every subdimension uses. The behavioral anchors below are illustrative, written to show the shape of an anchored scale rather than reproduced verbatim from the live rubric.

| Level | Anchor | What the evidence would show |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Active Harm | Documented cases where the entity received a clear signal of urgent need and acted to suppress, delay, or punish the person who raised it. |
| 1 | Absent | No meaningful capacity to respond to identified needs; signals routinely go nowhere. |
| 2 | Minimal | A nominal response process exists on paper but fails under any real pressure or volume. |
| 3 | Developing | Genuine, timely responses in some documented cases, but not consistently, and not when responding is costly. |
| 4 | Established | Consistent, appropriately prioritized responses across most documented cases, confirmed by the people served, including hard cases. |
| 5 | Exemplary | Outstanding, independently verified responsiveness sustained under significant pressure, including cases where responding cut against the entity's own interest. |

The anchors do the work a single global rating cannot, making the difference between a 3 and a 4 a matter of stated evidence and confirmation by the people served rather than how generous the assessor feels. They are also where the pressure-test gate bites: a 4 or 5 that lacks a documented costly case is flagged provisional and cannot stand, the subject of Chapter 13. The five anchors stored per subdimension represent levels 1 through 5; level 0, active harm, is a separate documented floor requiring a lead assessor's co-sign.

Here is where intellectual honesty is the more persuasive position. The framework is principled and it is operationalized: it rests on a coherent theory of what compassionate institutional conduct consists of, and it has been turned into a running system that scores 1,256 entities against fixed anchors with an auditable evidence trail. It has not yet been externally validated, and that is a real distinction, not a modest one. The eight-dimension structure has not been confirmed by independent factor analysis. The inter-rater reliability of the anchors has not been established through a published study in which multiple trained assessors score the same entities blind. The benchmark's scores have not been tested for convergence with independent outcome measures, the check that would show a low score actually tracks worse real-world treatment of people. [VERIFY: cite standard psychometric validity criteria, e.g. construct, convergent, and discriminant validity, and an inter-rater reliability standard, so these terms are anchored to an external source.] Until that work is done, the right description of the framework is a well-reasoned, fully operational measurement instrument whose validity is argued from first principles and design, not yet demonstrated empirically.

Saying so is not a hedge; it is the funding case stated plainly. A credible measurement instrument already exists and already runs; what it has not yet had is the validation studies that would let a researcher cite it without caveat. Those studies are nameable, costable work, and among the most valuable things philanthropic capital could buy here, because they convert a defensible design into a defended one: the difference between an instrument experts find reasonable and an instrument experts use.



# Chapter 12: Why Systems Matter More Than Intentions

A food bank that hands out more meals every year while doing nothing about why the line keeps growing is not the same institution as one that feeds people today and works to shorten the line. Both are kind. Only one is treating the cause. If a benchmark scored intentions, the two would be indistinguishable, because their intentions are the same. The benchmark scores them differently, and the reason it can is the subject of this chapter.

Good people work inside systems that still produce harm. That is not a cynical observation; it is the ordinary condition of large institutions, where outcomes are set by incentives, defaults, staffing levels, and structure far more than by the goodwill of any individual inside them. The denial rate is a property of the system, not of the reviewer's heart. Any measurement that hopes to predict how an institution actually treats people therefore has to look past motive, which is unobservable and a poor predictor, to the structures that generate conduct whether the people inside mean well or not. Two of the eight dimensions exist precisely to capture those structures.

The first is Systemic Thinking. It asks whether compassion reaches the causes of suffering or stops at the symptoms. Its five subdimensions ask whether the institution thinks structurally: Root Cause Orientation, Long-Term Impact, Interconnection Awareness, Structural Critique of the structures that perpetuate the suffering it addresses, and Coalitional Compassion. An institution can score well on Action, the dimension of timely effective help, while scoring poorly on Systemic Thinking, and that gap is informative. It is the difference between an emergency room that treats the same patients for the same preventable conditions year after year and one that asks why they keep coming back.

[GRAPHIC: Root-cause versus symptom-relief diagram. Two response paths from a single recurring harm. One loops back to the same harm after each intervention (symptom relief). The other branches upstream to the conditions producing it (root cause), where Systemic Thinking is scored. The point is that both paths can look compassionate in a single snapshot; only the structure reveals which is which.]

The second structural dimension is Integrity, which measures not whether the institution thinks about causes but whether its care holds when conditions turn against it. Its five subdimensions are Consistency Under Pressure, Non-Performance, Internal Consistency, Values Alignment, and Resilience of Care. Integrity asks whether a system was built so that care survives the system, rather than depending on whoever happens to be in the building this quarter.

Between them, Systemic Thinking and Integrity encode the chapter's claim into the measurement itself. The benchmark does not reward an institution for meaning well; it rewards being structured so that good outcomes do not depend on continuous individual heroism. That is why the score can separate two institutions with identical mission statements and identical good intentions: one has built the structure, the other is relying on the goodwill of people who will eventually leave, burn out, or be overruled by the quarter's numbers. Structure is also more observable than motive and more stable than mood. A staffing ratio, a documented decision to fund prevention rather than only response, a record of holding a commitment after the executive who made it departed: these are facts that persist and can be checked, where sincerity cannot. An institution's intentions tell you what it hopes to do; its structures tell you what it will do next year when no one is watching and the person of goodwill has moved on.



# Chapter 13: Pressure Tests, Maturity Gates, and Character Over Time

A subdimension cannot earn a 4 on Compassion Benchmark unless the assessor can point to at least one documented case where the compassionate choice was costly, legally risky, or institutionally inconvenient, and the institution made it anyway. If no such case exists, the subdimension is capped at Developing, level 3, no matter how good the institution looks under easy conditions. That single rule is the most important design decision in the standard, and it is the reason a high score is hard to manufacture.

The principle behind it is plain. Looking good when looking good is free tells you almost nothing about character. Any institution can fund the program that also generates favorable press, answer the complaint that arrives with a journalist attached, honor the value that happens to align with this quarter's incentives. What distinguishes compassionate character from compassionate marketing is what an institution does when the two diverge, when care costs money, invites a lawsuit, slows a launch, or embarrasses a leader. So the framework refuses to certify the higher anchors on favorable evidence alone. Established and Exemplary, levels 4 and 5, are assessed under adversarial conditions by design: the assessor goes looking for the costly case, and a 4 or 5 recorded without one is flagged provisional and routed to lead-assessor review rather than published as it stands.

Watch what the gate does to a real-shaped score. The figures below describe an illustrative frontier AI lab, built to demonstrate the mechanism, not a published entity.

[GRAPHIC: Before/after pressure-test bar for one dimension. Left bar, favorable evidence, dimension mean 4.0 (Established). Right bar, after the pressure-test cap is applied to subdimensions lacking a documented costly case, dimension mean 3.2 (Developing). The drop is the gate, not new bad news.]

On its published materials, the lab's Action dimension looks strong. Its documentation describes fast incident response, generous remediation, and sustained follow-through, and an assessor scoring the five Action subdimensions generously might land near 4.0, which reads as Established. Then the pressure test is applied subdimension by subdimension.

|---|---|---|---|
| AC1 Responsiveness | 4 | Yes: throttled a profitable feature after harm reports | 4 |
| AC2 Proportionality | 4 | No costly case on record | 3 |
| AC3 Efficacy | 4 | No costly case on record | 3 |

| AC5 Follow-Through | 4 | No costly case on record | 3 |

Three of the five subdimensions had no documented instance where the lab's responsiveness cost it anything, so each is held at Developing. The dimension mean falls from 4.0 to 3.2, and Action drops from Established to Developing. Nothing bad was discovered to cause that fall: it is the absence of pressure-tested evidence, which the standard treats as a real deficiency rather than a neutral gap, because an unproven capacity looks identical to a genuine one until the moment it is tested.

The maturity gate works the same way wherever evidence is thin: a 0 for active harm requires written documentation and a lead assessor's co-sign, an inter-rater discrepancy greater than 1.5 triggers review, a divergence between leadership narrative and community testimony is resolved toward the community account, and a refusal to provide requested documentation is itself a score-relevant event rather than a blank scored neutrally. Each holds the top bands shut until the evidence earns them.

Pressure under load is one test; consistency across time is the other, and Integrity carries it. Two of its subdimensions are explicitly temporal. Consistency Under Pressure applies the pressure-test logic to the institution's track record rather than a single case; Resilience of Care asks whether compassion persists across leadership change and institutional stress, whether the commitment outlives the executive who announced it. An institution that did the right thing once, under one leader, in one favorable year, has shown less than one that held the same conduct through a transition, a downturn, and a change of incentives.

That temporal logic is enforced at the extreme by the floor. A floor designation, the deliberate decision through the human gate to set every dimension to the bottom and attach a public disclosure of why, is not a snapshot judgment: it requires all four of its conditions, multi-source corroboration at the strongest tiers, a systemic rather than episodic pattern, harm active within the recency window, and no countervailing recognition or response above the floor, documented across at least three independent assessment cycles. Leaving the floor is equally time-tested, requiring evidence-of-care behavior sustained across at least two consecutive cycles from sources outside the entity's control. One good quarter does not reverse a floor.

Pull the threads together and the highest bands describe a specific and demanding thing. An Exemplary score is not an institution that looked good when an assessor happened to check. It is an institution whose care was documented to survive cost, confirmed by the people it serves, and held steady across time and through stress. That is a high bar on purpose. It is also why, across the universities index, with a mean of 46.2 and Harvard highest at 52.3, not one of the hundred institutions reached Exemplary. The top band is reserved for character proven under pressure, and proof of that kind is rare.

# Part IV: Applications


# Chapter 14: Artificial Intelligence and Robotics

A frontier model declines a coverage appeal, ranks a job applicant, moderates a post, or steers a robot across a warehouse floor. The decision lands in milliseconds, on a person who will never see the rule that produced it, repeated across millions of cases before any human reviews one. Earlier chapters described institutional decisions that are hard to see because no one is looking. Here they are hard to see because they happen faster than oversight can follow, at a scale where one design choice reaches more people in a day than a hospital reaches in a decade.

That is the case for timing, and it is the most fundable framing in this document. AI and robotics labs are among the most consequential institutions of the decade. They face audited finances, a thickening layer of capability and safety benchmarks, and growing regulatory attention to what their systems can do. They face almost nothing that measures how those systems treat the people on the other side: whether the model recognizes distress in the case it is deciding, whether harm is logged before or after a news cycle, whether the lab corrects course when shown its system hurt someone. That infrastructure is being built dimension by dimension, the way it was for finance and product safety, and the compassion dimension is the one still missing while the field is young enough to set a measurement norm.

Compassion Benchmark scores 50 AI labs and 50 robotics labs against the same eight dimensions used for every other entity. What changes is not the definition of compassion but its subject: for a lab, the population served is its users and the broader society its systems reach. Integrity asks whether safety commitments hold when they become expensive, which is the only condition under which a safety commitment means anything. The Schmidt Sciences framing of multi-agent oversight maps directly onto the robotics index: scoring labs that build autonomous physical systems is oversight of agents that act in the world, not only in text.

[GRAPHIC: AI and robotics lab leaderboard excerpt, entity rows with band-colored composite scores, the AI Labs index beside the Robotics Labs index.]

Consider the single most-cited example the indexes have produced. OpenAI scores 26.7, in the Developing band, while it is the subject of a multistate attorney-general inquiry into how its systems affect users. The number is not a verdict on the technology or the people who built it. It is a sum of eight dimension scores, each anchored in sourced evidence and reconstructable by anyone who reads the method. A 26.7 says something specific: documented practices for recognizing and repairing harm to users exist but remain inconsistent, reactive, or unevenly applied. It is a measurement, not an accusation, and the difference is the entire point of doing this independently.

The robotics index makes a different problem visible. A humanoid robotics lab can look exemplary in a demonstration, where the conditions are chosen and the cameras are running, but the benchmark does not score the demonstration. Under the pressure-test rule, a lab that has only ever performed well when performing well is capped at Developing. For a sector whose public face is the controlled demo, that distinction is most of what an outside observer needs to know.

None of this requires a human to sit with each lab for a week. The 100 labs in these two indexes are maintained by the same automated, human-gated pipeline that monitors the other 1,256 entities, so the marginal cost of watching one more lab through another news cycle is close to nothing. The deeper, human-administered certification battery is a separate and future pathway, not what produces these public scores.

What a funder underwrites here is a clock. The labs are deploying now, and the norms for how their conduct gets measured will harden into whatever fills the vacuum, whether an independent standard or the labs' own self-reporting. A capture-resistant measure of how the most powerful new institutions treat the people their systems touch is worth most before those institutions decide the question cannot be answered.



# Chapter 15: Healthcare and Human Services

UnitedHealth Group scores 10.2 out of 100.

The number sits in Critical, the lowest of the five bands, the range reserved for institutions where foundational compassion practices are absent or documented active harm is present. Healthcare is the sector where the benchmark's subject matter and the institution's stated purpose are supposed to be the same thing: a health insurer exists to relieve a specific kind of suffering. When it scores 10.2, the gap being measured is between what the institution says it is for and what its decisions do to the people it covers.

This is the sector where the eight dimensions need almost no translation. Awareness asks whether the system detects distress in the case in front of it, or processes it. Action asks whether an identified need receives a timely, proportional response or a denial calibrated to a model. Equity asks whether care reaches the people with the greatest need and the least power to demand it. In a claims operation these are not virtues; they are the daily output of decision rules, review windows, and appeal pathways, each a documented choice with a documented author, each leaving evidence that can be scored.

[GRAPHIC: Annotated entity card. UnitedHealth Group, composite 10.2, Critical band, with the eight dimension scores and the evidence-tier provenance behind the lowest of them.]

The provenance matters as much as the number, because a score this low invites the suspicion that it is an opinion dressed as a measurement. It is not. The composite is a sum of eight dimension scores, each anchored in sourced evidence and weighted by the five-tier hierarchy in which the company's own statements are the weakest input and independent audit the strongest. Where the institution's account and the documented experience of the people it covers diverge, the benchmark scores the world as encountered, which is the rule that produces a 10.2 instead of the number the annual report would imply.

UnitedHealth Group also shows the mechanism that keeps the benchmark honest near the bottom. As its regulatory exposure widened, the adverse evidence did not push the composite lower: an entity already in Critical has almost no scorable room left to fall, and unadjudicated allegations cannot accumulate into a change on their own. So the expansion of the federal probe was logged as an upgrade in the strength of the evidence behind the score, while the composite held at 10.2. The score moves on evidence, not news pressure, in both directions. That same discipline makes the rare downgrade meaningful: when the pipeline logged one for another large managed-care insurer in the same period, it was because the evidence behind specific dimensions had shifted, not because the sector was having a bad month. A score that can fall only on documented behavior is one that can also be defended when the institution objects, which insurers with substantial legal departments reliably do.

Healthcare is also where the easy story about intentions breaks down most completely. The reviewer with ninety seconds per case did not set the review window; the harm is an emergent property of incentives and defaults that no single caring person controls. The benchmark scores the rule, the window, and the appeal path, because that is where care either survives the system or does not.

The same logic extends across human services, where the subject of care is rarely able to shop for a better provider. A benefits office, a long-term-care operator, a behavioral-health network: each serves a population defined by need and constrained in its ability to walk away, which raises the stakes on every dimension and especially on Equity and Boundaries. These are exactly the institutions for which no comparable external measure of conduct exists, and exactly the ones where a public, reconstructable score gives a journalist, a regulator, or an affected family a common reference point. A 10.2 is not the end of an argument about UnitedHealth Group. It is the start of one that can finally be conducted in shared, evidence-anchored terms.



# Chapter 16: Government and Public Service

No one chooses their government the way they choose a bank. A resident cannot decline the state that taxes, polices, schools, and protects them, which makes a government's treatment of the people under its control the most consequential subject the benchmark scores, and the one where the absence of a comparable measure is strangest. We rate countries on freedom, on corruption perception, on press conditions, on ease of doing business. We do not have a shared, independent measure of whether a government recognizes the suffering of the people it is responsible for and does something proportional about it.

Compassion Benchmark scores 193 countries, 21 US states, 144 US cities, and 250 global cities against the eight dimensions. The subject of care is defined by jurisdiction, which settles in advance whose suffering counts when a government is scored, before any particular event has to be judged.

[GRAPHIC: Ranked-bar excerpt of country composite scores, band-colored, drawn from the benchmark's own data with a zero baseline.]

The coverage is honest about its own limits. The country index holds 193 of the world's roughly 207 states; the US States index currently holds 21 of 51. The benchmark scores a representative but incomplete slice in some indexes, and the prospectus says so rather than implying a completeness it has not reached.

Scoring governments forces an answer to a question softer indices can avoid: when terrible things happen inside a jurisdiction, whose conduct do they belong to? The attribution rule settles this before any score is assigned. Harm a government inflicts by its own choice is scored against it; harm inflicted on a population by an external actor is attributed to that actor, not charged to the victim. Missile strikes on Ukraine are attributed to Russia, not counted against the country absorbing them. The same rule, applied honestly, produces the benchmark's hardest cases. Israel and Sudan each carry a composite of 0.0, the floor of the scale.

A 0.0 is not the arithmetic of a bad month. It is a floor designation, a deliberate decision made through the human gate under the four documented conditions described in Chapter 13, met across at least three independent assessment cycles, carrying a public disclosure of why it was made and reversible through a time-tested exit protocol. The floor is engineered to be hard to reach and possible to leave, which is what keeps it a measurement rather than a sentence.

What scoring governments reveals, more than any single placement, is whether services reach the people with the greatest need and the least power to demand them. Equity is where public institutions most often fail quietly: a benefits system technically open to all but reachable only by those who can navigate it, an emergency response calibrated to the neighborhoods that complain loudest, a school-funding formula that tracks property values. These are not scandals; they are design choices, documented and authored, that leave the kind of evidence the benchmark is built to read. A government can publish an unimpeachable statement of values and still distribute care in a way its own service data contradicts, and the score follows the service data.

The point of putting governments on the same eight dimensions as corporations and AI labs is to give civil society a common reference point for an institution it cannot opt out of. Freedom House scores and Transparency International's Corruption Perceptions Index became the language in which journalists framed stories and policymakers justified decisions, until holding a defensible position on the measure was part of how a serious government operated. A reconstructable, independent, capture-resistant measure of how a state treats the suffering of its own people is built to travel the same road, and the residents who cannot choose their government have the most to gain from someone independent keeping that score.



# Chapter 17: Corporations and Employers

A large employer can publish a values statement about dignity and belonging on the same morning it closes a plant by email, and nothing in the existing accountability apparatus will register the contradiction as a contradiction. The disclosure was filed. The press release was issued. The ESG questionnaire was answered. What none of those instruments measures is whether the people on the receiving end of the company's decisions were recognized, responded to proportionally, and the harm to them reduced. That gap, between what a corporation discloses and how it behaves toward the people in its care, is what the benchmark is built to close.

Compassion Benchmark scores 448 of the Fortune 500 against the eight dimensions. For a company, the population that suffers or is served is wider than any one constituency: its workers, its customers, and the communities where it operates. A corporation can score well on one and badly on another, and the benchmark is designed to catch exactly that asymmetry. Integrity includes a subdimension, Internal Consistency, that asks whether an institution treats the people inside it with the same compassion it extends to the people it markets to. A company whose customer experience is gracious and whose warehouse conditions are punitive does not average out to fine. The inconsistency is itself the finding.

[GRAPHIC: Histogram of Fortune 500 composite scores distributed across the five bands, drawn from the benchmark's own data on a zero baseline.]

The contrast with ESG ratings is the heart of why this index exists. ESG can tell you whether a company filed the right disclosures, set a target, and published a policy. Those are real things, but they are not this one. A policy is a Tier 4 document, weaker than outcome data and community testimony and far weaker than independent audit, because it describes an intention and the benchmark scores conduct. Two companies can hold identical human-rights policies and earn very different scores, because one acted on it when acting was expensive and the other did not. Measured by disclosure, corporate responsibility rewards the company with the best documents; measured by behavioral evidence, it rewards the company whose documents turned out to be true.

That distinction is enforced by the rule that governs every dimension: a high mark requires at least one documented case where the compassionate choice was costly, legally risky, or commercially inconvenient, so the benchmark caps a subdimension at Developing where no costly case exists. The question the score answers is what the company did the quarter the right thing cost money. The histogram of where the Fortune 500 actually lands is its own argument: a benchmark earns trust by discriminating, placing companies across the bands on evidence and defending each placement from its dimension scores. Every composite is reconstructable from its eight numbers, so a company that disputes its score is arguing about evidence, not a verdict handed down from nowhere.

None of this depends on the cooperation of the companies being scored, and that independence is the product. A corporation cannot pay for a better placement, to be left out, or to soften a finding, because the systems that produce the scores have no access to the systems that handle commercial relationships. The buyers of the benchmark's deeper research are investors and analysts who need behavioral risk signals that disclosure does not give them, never the rated companies themselves. An ESG-exposed investor reading a 448-company distribution wants what a journalist does: the company whose conduct and whose disclosures have quietly come apart, named in terms that can be cited and contested. That is a service to the people a corporation affects, not to the corporation.



# Chapter 18: Nonprofits, Humanitarian Organizations, and Civil Society

The hardest institution to benchmark is the one that already claims compassion as its purpose. A relief agency, a charity, a movement, a foundation: each is organized around the reduction of suffering and says so in its founding documents, which means the gap the benchmark measures everywhere else, between stated purpose and actual conduct, is in this sector the entire subject. A corporation can fail the compassion test and still be an excellent corporation by the terms it set for itself. A humanitarian organization that fails it has failed at the one thing it exists to do.

Compassion Benchmark does not yet publish a civil-society index. The eight live indexes cover governments, companies, AI and robotics labs, cities, and universities; nonprofits and humanitarian organizations are a planned extension, not a current scoreboard, and this chapter argues for building it rather than reporting what it found. The honesty matters, because the case for scoring institutions that claim compassion as their purpose is undercut the moment it overstates itself. What the benchmark can say today is that the framework was designed to be sector-agnostic: nothing in the eight dimensions assumes a profit motive or a public charter. What changes by entity type is the evidence model and who counts as the subject of care, not the definition of compassion.

[GRAPHIC: Sector-spread panel showing the eight live indexes and the planned civil-society extension on one axis, the eight dimensions on the other, demonstrating that the framework is sector-agnostic.]

For a nonprofit, the subject of care is the population it claims to serve, and the first thing an honest assessment looks for is the distance between that claim and the lived experience of those people. This is where the evidence hierarchy does its sharpest work. A mission statement is the weakest tier the benchmark accepts, requiring corroboration before it moves anything, and a sector whose every member can write a moving mission statement is exactly where that rule earns its place. Community testimony from the served population outranks the organization's account of itself: where the brochure and the beneficiaries disagree, the benchmark scores the world as encountered.

Two dimensions tend to separate genuinely effective civil-society organizations from well-meaning ones. Boundaries asks whether help builds the capacity of the people receiving it or quietly creates dependency; a great deal of humanitarian harm is done by sincere help that was never designed to end. Systemic Thinking asks whether the organization addresses the causes of the suffering it treats or only the symptoms, and whether it collaborates beyond its own reach rather than competing for the same donors and the same credit. The willingness to share work and visibility with others serving the same people is one of the clearest signals that an institution is oriented toward the mission rather than itself.

The objection to scoring this sector is real and deserves a real answer: that holding an underfunded relief agency to the same standard as a trillion-dollar corporation is unfair, even hostile. The answer is that the benchmark scores conduct, not difficulty, and that the populations served by humanitarian organizations are among the least able to choose a better provider or to make their dissatisfaction heard. The greater the vulnerability of the served population and the smaller its voice, the more it matters that someone independent is measuring whether the organization claiming to help is actually helping, on evidence rather than on reputation. Accountability for compassionate practice is not the enemy of compassionate purpose; it is what keeps the purpose honest when the purpose alone stops being enough.

Building the index will take more than extending the scanner. Civil-society organizations vary enormously in size, transparency, and the availability of independent evidence, which is why the benchmark already attaches a per-entity confidence flag where the evidentiary record is thin, and would publish a low-confidence civil-society score with that caveat visible rather than withhold it or overstate it. The institutions that ask the public to trust them with the relief of suffering have the strongest claim on a public, independent account of how well they do it. That account does not exist yet. It is exactly the kind of work this benchmark was built to make possible, and exactly the kind that a funder underwrites before it can be reported as done.

# Part V: Public Benefit


# Chapter 19: A Shared Language for Institutional Accountability

Four people notice the same institutional failure in the same week. A reporter has a source inside a hospital system describing a wave of coverage denials. A pension analyst sees the same company flagged in a portfolio screen and wants to know how worried to be. A state legislator's staffer is drafting an oversight letter and needs to say how this institution compares to its peers. A patient advocate has a stack of testimony from families and no way to make it count as evidence. All four are looking at one institution's treatment of the people in its care. None of them can describe what they are seeing in terms the other three would recognize. The reporter has anecdotes, the analyst has a risk flag with no content, the staffer has outrage, the advocate has stories. They are describing the same thing in four private languages, and the institution, fluent in all four, can dismiss each in turn.

That is the condition this benchmark is built to change, and the change is not more information. It is a shared one. When every one of those four can point to the same number, built from the same eight dimensions, derived by the same published formula, the conversation stops being four monologues and becomes one argument. The hospital system scores in the Critical band on a 0 to 100 scale that means precisely what it means for a robotics lab and a national government. The reporter cites it, the analyst prices it, the staffer quotes it, the advocate's testimony becomes the Tier 3 evidence the number partly rests on. The institution can still contest the score. What it can no longer do is answer each party in a different dialect, because they are now speaking the same one.

This is what a comparative measure does that a single investigation cannot. An investigation tells you that one institution did one thing. A standard tells you where that thing sits on a scale every institution is held to. The difference is the difference between a photograph and a map. Two precedents show how far the difference travels. Freedom House's freedom scores and Transparency International's Corruption Perceptions Index did not begin as authorities. They began as one organization's attempt to put a comparable number on something everyone agreed mattered and no one measured the same way twice. They became the language in which journalists framed stories, investors priced country risk, and policymakers justified decisions, until a government's position on the measure was part of how a serious institution was discussed at all. They worked by becoming the vocabulary, so that to argue about the subject at all was to argue in their terms.

A shared language has one demand that distinguishes it from a slogan: anyone holding it must be able to take it apart. This is where the benchmark's design carries the weight the analogy assigns it. A Freedom House score is trusted partly because its components are public; a Compassion Benchmark composite goes further, because every published score is reconstructable from its eight dimension scores by a formula anyone can read and rerun. The reporter and the institution's general counsel are working from the same arithmetic. When the advocate's families and the company's policy documents disagree, the standard already says which one outranks the other, in public, before either side reaches the table. A common vocabulary that cannot be inspected is just a louder assertion; one that can be inspected lets adversaries disagree productively, because they are finally disagreeing about the same evidence on the same scale.

[GRAPHIC: A single composite score at the center, with four lines radiating outward to a journalist, an investor, a policymaker, and an affected community, each annotated with what the same number lets that party do (cite, price, cite in oversight, corroborate). The lingua-franca diagram: one measure, read four ways.]

It would be dishonest to present this as accomplished. No journalist has yet cited these scores in print, no legislature has yet quoted one, and the indices the analogy leans on earned their standing over decades, not quarters. What exists today is the harder half: a measure precise enough, transparent enough, and consistent enough across sectors that it could become a shared language if it is used. Whether it is used is not within this institution's control, and Chapter 22 treats that honestly as the central bet of the entire model.

What changes if the bet pays. The first time a reporter, an analyst, and an oversight committee all cite the same composite for the same institution in the same month, the institution acquires a problem it did not have before. It can no longer manage four audiences separately, because the four are now coordinated by a number it did not author and cannot pay to move. A shared language does not force an institution to improve. It removes the option of being measured in private, which for an institution that has relied on the absence of a common scale is most of the protection it had.



# Chapter 20: Public Indexes and Open Research

The most consequential decision in this institution's business model is the thing it gives away. Every one of the 1,256 scores is free to read: all eight indexes, the full rankings, the band each entity sits in, and the daily briefing that records what moved overnight, with no login, no metered preview, no paywall waiting three articles in. The methodology is free to read in full, down to the eight dimensions, the forty subdimensions, the behavioral anchors, the five-tier evidence hierarchy, and the exact formula that turns dimension scores into a 0 to 100 composite. An organization that intends to earn revenue has put its entire core product, and the recipe for it, in public view. That is not generosity. It is the only configuration under which the rest of the model means anything.

Consider what would happen if the scores were paywalled. A score behind a paywall is a private accusation sold to whoever pays, exactly the posture that makes a rating capturable: the institution being rated has the most reason and the most money to control who sees what. Keeping the rankings free closes that door by construction. It also serves the people the benchmark exists for. A patient, a worker, a resident, a community organizer affected by an institution's conduct is rarely the person with a research budget; if the score that describes their treatment were sold, it would reach everyone except them. Free public indexes are the difference between a tool for the watched and a tool for the watchers.

What, then, is sold. The depth, never the verdict. The free indexes tell you that a company sits in the Critical band and show you its eight dimension scores. The paid research tells you the evidence trail beneath each of the forty subdimensions, the sector comparison, the custom analysis an investor or a newsroom needs to act. This is the model libraries of public-interest data already run: Our World in Data gives away its charts and its data and sustains itself on a base of small donors; ProPublica publishes its investigations free and runs a Data Store for the derived datasets researchers will pay to skip building themselves. Keep the core free, sell only what is genuinely derived from it, and the commerce stays subordinate to the public good instead of competing with it.

Open methodology is the second half of the public good, and it is worth being precise about what "open" covers, because the word is used loosely. Published today, in full: the rubric, the dimension and subdimension definitions, the 0 to 5 anchors, the evidence hierarchy, the composite formula, and the rules that govern how a score may change. The reproducibility test follows: a competent successor, handed the published method and an entity's evidence record, must be able to rebuild that score and arrive at the same number. A capturable index cannot survive that test, because its weighting is private and its judgments are unreviewable. A separate, more rigorous human assessment protocol exists as a specification and underpins a future certified product, but it is not how the 1,256 public scores are produced. The open part is the part that scores the public indexes, and it is genuinely open.

Right, the open methodology: the formula, the anchors, the evidence tiers, marked "published." A thin line connects a single score on the left to the formula on the right, labeled "reconstructable."]

There is a machine-readable layer to the openness as well. A built badge endpoint serves any entity's current score as an embeddable graphic, so a third-party site can display a live composite that updates when the underlying score does. A broader data feed for researchers who want the scores in bulk is buildable on request today and is specified to grow into a programmatic interface; the interface is planned, not yet shipped, and this document does not claim otherwise. The direction is consistent: lower the cost of citing, embedding, and contesting the scores until doing so is nearly free, because a public good that is hard to use is a public good in name only.

What changes if the openness holds. An index that anyone can read, rebuild, and embed becomes infrastructure rather than a publication. A researcher who can reproduce a score will defend it; one who cannot will ignore it. A newsroom that can embed a live badge will return to it; one that has to screenshot a PDF will not. The free indexes and the open method are not the charitable wrapper around a business. They are the asset, and the business is what the asset can responsibly support without ever being allowed to own it.



# Chapter 21: Journalists, Policymakers, Researchers, and Communities

A benchmark is worth exactly what other people do with it. The scores can be precise, the methodology open, the pipeline humming every night, and none of it matters if the numbers sit unread on a website. The value of a shared measure is created downstream, by the people who pick it up, cite it, argue with it, and act on it.

Start with the journalist, because the journalist is the first mover. An investigative reporter covering a hospital system, an AI lab, or a state agency spends much of an investigation establishing that a problem is real and significant. A score does part of that work before the reporting starts. It says this institution sits in the Critical band, here are the eight dimensions that put it there, and here is the evidence trail beneath each one. The score is not the story; it is the lead and the spine. When the benchmark's nightly research moves an entity or crosses a band boundary, that movement is itself a tip: something changed, here is what, here is the dated record. Newsrooms covering health care, technology accountability, and public services are the natural first citers, and the benchmark is designed to make a citation easy: a public score, a reconstructable composite, an evidence trail a fact-checker can walk.

The policymaker uses the same number for a different purpose. A legislator or a regulator writing oversight needs comparison and standing. It is one thing to say an agency is failing the residents it serves; it is another to say it scores in the Developing band while comparable states score higher, on a public scale the agency cannot dispute the arithmetic of. A score gives an oversight letter a citable anchor and gives a hearing a common reference that the institution under question is already on record against.

The researcher is the user who makes the standard durable. An academic studying institutional behavior, or a measurement specialist stress-testing the method, does something the other three cannot: they reproduce it, contest it, and extend it. Because the rubric, the anchors, and the formula are published, a researcher can take an entity's evidence record and rebuild its composite, then challenge a judgment in public. That adversarial use is not a threat to the benchmark; it is how a measure earns the trust that makes it citable in the first place, the way the capture-resistant indices the method borrows from were hardened, by surviving people trying to break them.

The fourth audience is the one most measurement systems forget. The communities affected by an institution's conduct are not only readers of the score; they are part of its evidence base. Community testimony is a named tier in the evidence hierarchy, and where the people an institution serves report harm that its policy documents deny, the standard already says the testimony outranks the document. For an affected community, the benchmark offers two things at once: a public number that validates what they have been describing, and a method that counts their account as evidence rather than anecdote. That is a structural inversion of the usual arrangement, in which the institution's paperwork is presumed true until a community can prove otherwise.

[GRAPHIC: A four-quadrant usage map, journalist, policymaker, researcher, community, arranged around a central node labeled with the badge endpoint and the public score. Each quadrant lists what that audience does with the number: cite, compare, reproduce, corroborate. Arrows run both ways, the community quadrant feeding evidence in.]

The connective tissue between these audiences and the scores is partly built and partly planned. The badge endpoint is live: any site can embed an entity's current score as a graphic that updates when the score does. Score Watch, the per-entity alert that notifies a subscriber when a tracked institution's composite moves or changes band, is sold today at a per-entity annual price, and its independence is structural: paying to watch a score cannot affect the score, and a subscriber sees nothing the public briefing does not. Automated delivery of those alerts is still being finished; the product is real and the fulfillment pipeline is in progress, and this document does not claim a fully automated alert service that is not yet running. The bulk data feed researchers want is buildable on request today and is specified to become a programmatic interface later.

Honesty requires the baseline. As of today, the documented count of external citations of these scores is zero. No newsroom has yet cited one in print, no legislature has yet quoted one, no third-party tool beyond the benchmark's own runs on the badge. Everything in this chapter describes designed and intended use, with the connective infrastructure partly shipped, not a record of adoption already achieved. The next two chapters take that gap seriously: Chapter 22 lays out why third-party use is the load-bearing assumption of the whole model, and Chapter 23 commits to the specific numbers by which a funder should judge whether the use is materializing.

What changes as the users arrive. Each audience makes the benchmark more valuable to the others. A journalist's citation gives a policymaker cover to quote the score; a policymaker's use gives a researcher a reason to scrutinize it; a researcher's validation gives the next journalist confidence to cite it; a community's corroboration strengthens the evidence the whole chain rests on. A standard does not scale by being promoted. It scales by being used, each use lowering the cost of the next, until reaching for the score is simply what one does.



# Chapter 22: Theory of Change

The honest version of a theory of change names the place where the causal chain is strongest, the place where it is weakest, and the assumptions holding the two together. This is that version. It is also the chapter where a funder is entitled to ask the blunt question that the rest of the document has been building toward: if you do the work, what actually changes, and how much of that is within your control?

Start with what is fully within our control, because that is where the chain is strongest. The inputs are concrete: a working assessment framework, an automated pipeline, a published methodology, and a modest compute budget. The activities those inputs drive are equally concrete. Every night, the pipeline scans 1,256 entities for recent evidence, reassesses the highest-priority ones against the eight dimensions, and a human approves any score that moves. The findings are published. From those activities come outputs we can count and already produce: entities scored and maintained, free public rankings, logged and dated score-change events, and the daily briefing that records what moved and why. None of this depends on anyone outside the institution cooperating. This is the part of the theory of change that is not a theory at all. It is operations.

The causal chain gets more interesting, and less certain, at the next link.

| Stage | What it is | Who controls it |
|---|---|---|
| Inputs | Framework, pipeline, methodology, compute, funding | Us |
| Activities | Nightly scanning, reassessment, human-gated updates, publishing | Us |
| Outputs | Entities scored, public rankings, logged score changes, impact reports | Us |
| Outcomes | A shared, citable standard that third parties use and contest | Shared |
| Impact | Institutions adjust conduct; the measure enters standard practice | Mostly them |

The outputs are ours. The impact is not, and any prospectus that pretends otherwise should be distrusted. Between the two sits the load-bearing span of the entire model: the move from publishing a credible score to that score being used by someone with leverage over the institution it describes. We do not control that adoption. We can only make it as likely as careful work allows.

The mechanism we are betting on is not shame. Institutions absorb bad press routinely and move on. The mechanism is a shared reference point: when a measure becomes common vocabulary, it changes the cost of ignoring it, the way Freedom House scores and the Corruption Perceptions Index became the language in which journalists framed stories, investors priced country risk, and policymakers justified decisions, until a defensible position on the measure was part of how a serious institution operated. The wager here is that a credible, independent, reconstructable measure of how institutions treat suffering can travel the same path: cited in an investigation, folded into an investor's due diligence, raised in a policy hearing, pointed to by an affected community, until an institution that expects to be measured maintains its conduct the way it now maintains its compliance posture.

Stated as a sequence, the bet runs: research creates measurement, measurement creates transparency, transparency creates a shared standard, a shared standard creates accountability, and accountability creates pressure to improve. Each arrow in that sentence is an assumption, and a funder should see them as assumptions rather than findings.

The first is that transparency drives accountability, that a public, credible score changes behavior more than a private one or none at all. The comparable indices support this, but the evidence is strongest for governments and corporations and thinnest for the newer subjects, the AI and robotics labs, where no measurement norm yet exists to point to. The second assumption is that the scores are credible enough to be cited, which depends on the methodology holding up under expert scrutiny and the pipeline producing defensible results at scale. The third is that independence is preserved and, just as important, trusted, because a measure suspected of capture will be used as ammunition by partisans rather than as a reference by neutrals, and that is a failure mode, not a success. The fourth assumption is the one most outside our control: that third parties actually pick the scores up. We can lower the barriers by keeping the rankings free, the methodology open, and the data citable. We cannot manufacture the citation.

This is the appropriate place to be clear about the present state of the evidence. As of today, the count of documented external citations is zero. No institution has visibly altered its conduct in response to a published score. Those are not embarrassing admissions to be buried; they are the honest baseline against which the next two years should be judged. The theory of change predicts that the early outcomes, the first citations, the first documented institutional responses, the first third-party tools embedding the scores, are achievable within that horizon. It does not predict measurable behavior change at scale on that timeline, and it would be a warning sign if anyone claimed it did. Long-horizon impact, the kind where the measure has entered standard due-diligence vocabulary and institutions reliably move because of it, is a decade-scale ambition, not a grant deliverable.

It buys the conditions under which the impact becomes possible: a credible measure already exists, and the work funded here, the citable research, the independent governance, the verified integrity of the process, is what gives third parties a reason to trust it enough to use it. # Chapter 23: Measuring Impact

An institution built to measure others should be willing to be measured, and should pick metrics that can embarrass it. The easiest way to fail that test is to report the numbers that always go up. Page views rise, social posts accumulate, a dashboard fills with green. None of it answers whether the work is doing what the theory of change says it should. It sets out the metrics this institution will report, keeping the ones it controls distinct from the ones it does not, and states the present baseline plainly, including where that baseline is zero.

The discipline that keeps the metrics honest is the same one that structures the theory of change: outputs, outcomes, and impact are different things, and an organization that blurs them is usually hiding behind the easy one. Outputs are what the institution produces directly and can guarantee. Outcomes are what third parties do with those outputs. Impact is the change in institutional behavior the whole enterprise is for, mostly outside its control and unfolding over years. The reporting commitment is to track all three and never let a strong output stand in for an absent outcome.

The outputs are real today and form the floor. The benchmark scores and maintains 1,256 entities across eight public indexes; that is the baseline, not a target, and the commitment is to hold coverage rather than inflate the count for its own sake. The nightly pipeline runs Monday through Saturday, and uptime is a reportable figure with a 90 percent or better target. Score-change accountability events, the dated, evidence-backed records of an entity's composite moving through the human gate, are both a product and a metric: the target is at least a dozen documented in the first year. Quarterly impact reports, four a year, are themselves an output and the vehicle in which the rest of these numbers are disclosed. These are the metrics a funder can hold the institution to without qualification, because nothing outside the building has to cooperate for them to happen.

The outcome metrics are where the honesty has to be loudest, because this is where the baseline is zero. External citations of the scores, by journalists, researchers, or policymakers, stand at zero documented today; the Year-1 target is at least ten, the Year-2 target at least twenty-five. Third-party sites or tools running on the badge endpoint stand at zero beyond the benchmark's own use; the targets are a handful in Year 1, more in Year 2. Documented instances of an institution responding to its score, publicly or in correspondence, stand at zero; the targets are a few, then several times that. Score Watch subscribers are an outcome metric too, a measure of whether anyone will pay to track an institution's conduct, with targets in the low hundreds rising toward five hundred. Every one of these is a count of something other people must choose to do. Reporting them with a zero baseline is the point. It is the only way the first non-zero number means anything.

[GRAPHIC: A KPI dashboard mock in two columns, Year 1 and Year 2 targets, grouped under three headers, OUTPUTS, OUTCOMES, INDEPENDENCE. Each outcome row shows a "baseline: 0 reported" tag beside its target, so the distance between what exists and what is promised is visible at a glance.]

Impact, the change in how institutions actually behave, is deliberately not reduced to a Year-1 or Year-2 number, because any such number would be invented. The theory of change places measurable behavior shift on a decade horizon and treats the early outcomes as the leading indicators that the longer change is possible. A funder evaluating this work at the end of Year 2 should look at whether the outcome metrics have moved off zero, not whether institutions have reformed. If the citations, the embeds, and the documented responses are materializing on schedule, the impact thesis is alive and the next stage of capital is warranted. If they are still at zero with the outputs all green, that is the signal that the load-bearing assumption is failing, and it should be read that way rather than papered over with output numbers that were never in doubt.

One set of metrics is held to a different standard than all the others. The independence indicators are not targets that may be approached; they are conditions that must read 100 percent every reporting period, or the institution has failed at the thing that makes its scores worth measuring. Zero instances of any entity paying for inclusion, a score change, or suppression. Zero material funders tied to a scored entity. Full public disclosure of every funder above the stated threshold. These do not trend. They are either intact or breached, and a breach is not a missed KPI but a structural failure that the rest of the metrics cannot offset.

What a funder is buying, measured this way, is not a promised impact number. It is a credible instrument already producing its outputs, a set of outcome metrics with an honest zero baseline and dated targets, and an independence record that is either perfect or disqualifying. The instrument exists and the indexes are published. Part VI turns to the institution that has to carry them: what is built, how independence is engineered rather than promised, and what philanthropic capital makes possible from here.

# Chapter 24: Progress to Date

On the night before this sentence was written, with no one awake to watch it, a single machine read roughly 1,260 institutions for fresh evidence of how they were treating the people in their care. It ranked the fifteen most worth a closer look, ran full assessments on those, drafted a public briefing of what had moved and why, and left a one-line provenance record for every entity it had touched. The cost of the night was less than a restaurant dinner. By morning there was a proposal waiting for a human to approve or reject, and nothing had changed in public until that human acted.

That is the part of this prospectus that is not a forecast. Most funder documents ask for money to build a thing; this one asks for money to govern, legitimize, and grow a thing that already runs. The most expensive and uncertain work in a project like this is usually the instrument: the standard, the scoring, the data, the machinery that turns scattered evidence into a comparable number. That work is done. What is not done is the institution around it, and this Part is honest about which is which.

Here is what exists today, stated plainly enough to be checked.

A working standard. Eight dimensions, forty subdimensions, a 0 to 5 behavioral-anchor scale beneath each, and a published v1.2 formula that turns the eight dimension scores into a single 0 to 100 composite. Every published number is reconstructable from its eight inputs. The formula is the actual arithmetic, mirrored between the live site and the pipeline and held in agreement by a determinism test that fails the build if the two ever drift.

Eight public indexes, 1,256 entities scored. Countries (193), the Fortune 500 (448 companies), the top 250 global cities, 50 AI labs, 50 humanoid-robotics labs, 21 US states, 144 US cities, and 100 universities, the last index added in June 2026. Every entity carries a band, a composite, and its dimension profile, and none sits behind a paywall.

A published methodology. The rubric, the bands, the evidence hierarchy, and the scoring math are all readable by any visitor, any journalist, any rated institution that wants to contest its number. Transparency here is not a value statement. It is the precondition for the reproducibility test the whole independence case rests on: a successor with the published method and the evidence record could redo any score and reach the same result.

A nightly automated pipeline. Four stages run Monday through Saturday, overnight: a scanner that sweeps every entity for recent evidence inside a strict 14-day window, an assessor that proposes a change only when a composite would move five points or more, a digest that writes the day's public briefing, and a human-gated score-updater that applies nothing until the founder approves it. It is the subject of the next chapter; what matters here is that it is operating, not specified.

A daily public briefing. The digest stage produces a structured, plain-language record of what the night's research surfaced: the signals, the boundary cases, the sector patterns, the forward triggers worth watching. It is published, and it is the same briefing for everyone, with no earlier or richer version sold to anyone.

A commercial layer that funds the work without touching it. Research reports, a per-entity alert product, and institutional services exist and have begun to earn revenue. They are built on a separate technical plane that can read published scores but cannot write to them, a separation described in Chapter 26. The rule that no rated entity pays for its score is already in force as policy.

What is early is just as important to say, because a credibility anchor that oversells is not an anchor. As of today, the count of documented outside citations of these scores is zero. No institution has visibly changed its conduct because of a published number. The weekly integrity audit that will verify the firewall mechanically is specified and partly built, not yet running on its own schedule. The alert product is sold, but automated delivery of its emails is still being finished. The certified, human-administered assessment that would sit above the automated scores is a designed protocol, not an operating service. There is no board yet, no conflict-of-interest policy adopted by one, and no legal entity: Compassion Benchmark is today a solo venture with a published intention to convert.

Set those two lists side by side and the shape of the ask becomes clear. The first list is an instrument that works. The second is the institution that would make the instrument trustworthy, durable, and cited: the board, the legal home, the verified integrity audit, the first research that an outsider points to. The reason this is a strong position rather than a weak one is that the lists are in that order. It is far harder to build a credible measurement instrument with no institution than to build a credible institution around a working instrument. The expensive uncertainty is behind us.

There is one more thing worth noting, because funders in this field have learned to discount demos. None of it is a prototype assembled for this document. The indexes have been public, the pipeline has been running its nightly loop, and the briefings have a date stamp and a history. A funder can open the site tonight, read a score, follow it to the eight dimensions beneath it, read the method that produced it, and watch tomorrow's briefing arrive. The claim that accountability at this scale is impractical is no longer available, because the impractical part already exists.

# Chapter 25: Technology Platform

A research team that wanted to monitor 1,256 institutions for changes in how they treat human suffering, reassess the most consequential ones against a forty-part rubric, and publish a defensible result every morning would need to be large, expensive, and slow. Compassion Benchmark does it for roughly the price of a working dinner a night, and the reason is the platform. This chapter describes that platform as it actually is: what it does without supervision, what still requires a person, and where its limits are.

The center of the platform is a four-stage pipeline that runs Monday through Saturday, roughly overnight. Each stage is a defined job with a defined cost, and the stages hand off to one another in sequence.

The first stage is the scanner. It sweeps all 1,256 entities for material evidence inside a strict 14-day window, working within a budget of about 250 searches. It is triaging, not reassessing everything. For every entity it writes a short provenance record of what it looked at and found, so even an entity with no news has a dated note saying so, and it outputs a prioritized list: roughly the top fifteen entities that warrant a full look plus about five pulled in on rotation so quiet entities are not ignored forever. The scan takes about half an hour and costs around two dollars.

The second stage is the assessor, and it is the expensive one. It runs full assessments on the fifteen to twenty priority entities using the same eight-dimension, forty-subdimension method published on the site, recording each subdimension both as raw 0 to 5 anchors and as scaled 0 to 100 values. Crucially, it does not propose a change for every wobble. A proposal is emitted only when the composite would move by five points or more, and only after it survives a set of screens: that the baseline is real, that the move matches the direction of the evidence, that the rationale is consistent with the entity's history, and that the arithmetic validates against the canonical formula. A proposal that passes all of that is recorded pending, never applied on its own. The assessor runs a little over two hours and costs between twenty-five and sixty dollars a night.

The third stage is the digest. It writes the public daily briefing, the structured record of top signals, boundary cases, recent assessments, emerging risks, sector alerts, and forward triggers, and updates the internal log of changes awaiting approval. A pair of validators enforces a plain-language contract on that briefing, wired into the site build, so a malformed or jargon-choked briefing fails rather than ships. This stage takes about five minutes and costs around fifty cents.

The fourth stage is not automated, and that is the design, not a gap. The score-updater applies approved changes to the public indexes and runs only when a human triggers it. When the founder approves a pending proposal, the updater writes it under a drift guard: if the baseline has moved by more than 2.0 since the proposal was made, the update refuses and holds rather than writing a number built on outdated ground. It runs a direction-inversion check, recalculates rankings, and logs the change to a permanent applied-changes record. Nothing reaches the public scores without passing through this stage, and this stage does not move without a person.

Add the stages and a night costs roughly $27.50 to $62.50, which is about $700 to $1,600 a month in research compute. That figure is the whole cost argument compressed into a number. Continuous coverage of 1,256 institutions, reassessed in priority order every working night, for a monthly cost a single mid-size foundation grant covers many times over. The cost per entity falls as the catalog grows, because the scanner's budget and the assessor's triage do not scale linearly with the number of entities watched, so the marginal cost of one more monitored institution is close to zero.

Underneath the pipeline sits the rest of the platform, and it is deliberately ordinary. The public site is a statically exported Next.js application served from a single virtual server, with the scores living in structured data files rather than a database. A single Cloudflare Worker handles everything that needs to respond to the outside world: it serves a score badge any site can embed, processes the payment webhook and subscriber records for the commercial products, and handles unsubscribe requests. The Worker reads published scores and writes subscriber and payment state, with no path back into the scoring data, and its free tier covers the expected traffic for years. There is no fleet of servers and no large standing bill, part of why the cost base in Chapter 28 is as low as it is.

The architecture also carries the independence claim. The platform runs on two separated planes. The assessment plane reads the world and writes scores, with no access to subscriber lists, payment data, or any commercial state. The commercial plane runs the Worker, the email system, and the payment integration, and can read only published scores while writing nothing back. The separation is enforced by structure, not policy: different credentials, score files write-protected against the commercial side, and a Worker with no key to alter an index. A person who wanted to sell a score change could not do it through the software, because the software that talks to paying customers cannot reach the software that sets scores. Chapter 26 builds the governance on top of this; here the point is that the firewall is physical before it is procedural.

What the platform cannot do is as important as what it can. It cannot conduct an interview, sit in a waiting room, or hear directly from an affected community, which is why the automated scores rely on the public evidence record and why the certified human assessment described elsewhere remains the more rigorous and still-aspirational pathway. It cannot judge a genuinely novel situation the rubric never anticipated; it flags such cases for the human gate rather than guessing. It over-finds bad news, because adverse events are reported more loudly than steady competent care, which is why the method explicitly instructs the search toward durable positive evidence and why a human reviews the consequential moves. And it cannot, by itself, make any number public. The pipeline is powerful at scale and narrow at judgment, and the platform is built so that the judgment it lacks is supplied by a person before anything is published, not after.

# Chapter 26: Governance and Independence

Every funder who has spent time near this field asks the same question, usually early and usually in writing: can a lab buy a better score? It is the right question, and the honest answer has to be more than a promise. A promise is only as durable as the person making it. The answer Compassion Benchmark is building is a layered one, in which independence is engineered into the software first, written into law second, and governed by an independent board third, so that defeating it would require breaking all three layers at once rather than persuading one founder.

Start with the layer that already exists, because it is the most concrete and the least common. As the previous chapter described, the platform runs on two separated planes: the assessment plane cannot see who pays the organization anything, and the commercial plane can read published scores but cannot write a single character back into the scoring data. The separation is enforced by missing credentials and write-protected files, not by a policy document, which is the strongest available proof that the firewall is real, because it does not depend on anyone's restraint. A person inside the organization who wanted to sell a score could not route the transaction through the system, because the part that handles money has no reach into the part that sets numbers. Most independence claims are assertions. This one is a property of the architecture, and it is the foundation the rest is built on.

The legal layer is the next to come, and it is the central reason for the nonprofit conversion. Today Compassion Benchmark is a for-profit solo venture, so the independence rule holds exactly as long as the founder's discipline holds it. That is a real vulnerability, and converting to a 501(c)(3) public charity is how it gets closed. The rule becomes a clause in the Articles of Incorporation and the bylaws, locked behind a supermajority. The intended language is direct: the organization shall never accept payment from any entity in exchange for inclusion in a benchmark, a change to a published score, or the suppression of a finding, and that policy may not be modified except by an affirmative vote of at least two-thirds of all directors then in office, with any amendment disclosed publicly within thirty days. Once it is in the bylaws, selling a score is not merely against policy; it is ultra vires, outside the organization's legal power and a breach of the directors' fiduciary duty. An entity that wanted to buy a finding would have to corrupt a supermajority of an independent board and accept that the amendment becomes public within a month. To be clear about status: the 501(c)(3) is planned, not yet established, and the bylaw is drafted intent, not enacted law. Chapter 27 lays out the formation path.

The third layer is the board, and its composition is the point of it. The IRS requires a public charity to seat a majority of directors unrelated to one another and to the founder, which is also the single best defense against capture. The design calls for three to five independent directors chosen for independence credibility rather than industry connections or a fundraising network. The founder serves initially as executive director and recuses from any vote touching the independence rule. The other seats are sized to the trust they protect: an ethics or welfare researcher, a nonprofit-governance or legal expert, a data-methods or measurement researcher, and a journalist or transparency advocate. Terms are staggered over two years so no single moment can replace the whole board. No director may be connected to a scored entity, a bar enforced through the conflict-of-interest policy, which requires annual written disclosure and recusal and extends to funders: a director or major donor tied to a rated institution recuses from anything touching that institution's score. Alongside the fiduciary board sits a separate external methodology advisory board, three to five credentialed experts serving as volunteers or for a small honorarium, with no fiduciary liability, who review the method each year and publicly attest to what they find, cheap to run and high in legitimacy.

Then there is the funder firewall, where the commitment to independence becomes bright lines a program officer can check. The policy is assembled from the strongest clauses of organizations that have survived this test before: no scored entity may be a material funder, ideally none at all (AI Now's categorical line); no single funder may exceed ten percent of annual revenue (Transparency International's diversification norm); a written clause that no funding relationship constrains findings about the funder (Bellingcat's right to criticize its own funders); scoring decisions made by the research staff and the pipeline, independent of any donor (Freedom House's editorial firewall); every funder above one thousand dollars disclosed publicly, and funders above five thousand screened with reverse due diligence before acceptance; and core scoring grants restricted to general-operating or unrestricted so no earmark can quietly shape what gets measured. These are not aspirations but written rules the board adopts and the website publishes, and several of them also protect the public-support test that keeps the charity a charity.

The deepest safeguard is built into the method rather than the org chart, and it answers what happens if a single person is compromised. The full rubric, the weights, and the evidence sources are published; scoring is rules-based and logged; the evidence behind each score is retained and auditable; score changes are documented with their rationale and dated. The standard the whole design is held to is a reproducibility test: if the founder were replaced tomorrow, a successor with the published method and the evidence record must be able to reproduce any score and reach the same number. When that is true, no individual's quiet judgment can move a number, because the number is a function of disclosed inputs anyone can recompute. One piece of this machinery should not be overstated: a weekly automated integrity audit, designed to verify mechanically that only the assessment pipeline touches the index files, is specified and partially built but not yet running on its own schedule. It belongs on the roadmap, not the list of things already operating. Naming that gap plainly is itself part of the independence posture, because an institution that rounds up its own audit status has already started down the road it claims to be guarding against.

# Chapter 27: Roadmap

The hardest single date on this roadmap is August 8, 2026, the deadline for the Schmidt Sciences proposal, and it is hard for a reason worth stating up front: the sequence here is built so that credibility comes before scale. The temptation in any plan like this is to chase the largest grant first. The discipline this roadmap imposes is the opposite. Stand up the legal and governance structure, win the fast accessible money that does not require it to be finished, publish work an outsider can cite, and only then approach the funders who move in millions but only on a track record. Each phase earns the right to the next.

The gating constraint, before any milestone, is people. A 501(c)(3) cannot be filed with credibility until at least three independent directors have committed in writing, and recruiting them is the slowest and least delegable task in the whole plan. It starts on day one and runs underneath everything below.

**Months 0 to 3: stand up the structure and win the fast money.** The legal path opens with a Model C fiscal sponsor, an existing charity that re-grants funds to Compassion Benchmark and lets it receive tax-deductible grants within weeks rather than waiting on the IRS. With the sponsor signed, the Articles of Incorporation are drafted with the independence clause embedded, the bylaws and conflict-of-interest policy are adopted, the board is constituted, an EIN is obtained, and the full Form 1023 is filed. The full form, not the short one, is the deliberate choice: it lets the organization pre-clear its earned-revenue model and its independence safeguards with the IRS rather than leaving them ambiguous. In parallel, the first grant applications go out, because the Tier 1 funders accept fiscally sponsored applicants and do not wait for a determination letter. The Survival and Flourishing Fund speculation grant and the EA Long-Term Future Fund go in first and in parallel, both capable of deciding in days to weeks. The Schmidt Sciences multi-agent safety proposal goes in against its August 8 deadline. The first quarterly impact report publishes in this window, covering only what the pipeline can already count.

**Months 3 to 6: build track record and field presence.** The Fast Forward accelerator application goes in during its window, which runs July 30 to September 8, 2026; the value there is as much the mentorship and funder network as the twenty-five thousand dollars. One or two citable artifacts publish in this phase, a methodology paper or a special briefing substantial enough that another researcher or a journalist can point to it. The external methodology advisory board is constituted and announced, which strengthens every subsequent application. If the timeline holds, the IRS determination letter arrives somewhere in this window or the next, converting the fiscal-sponsor bridge into a standalone charity. The mid-size applications begin: Knight on its open calls, Sloan via the two-page letter of inquiry that precedes any full proposal there. The weekly integrity audit, partly built today, is brought to full automated operation in this phase, which is the milestone that lets the organization claim it as running rather than designed.

**Months 6 to 12: convert progress into a fundable record.** The target for the full year is one hundred fifty to three hundred thousand dollars in total grants secured across two to four funders. Four quarterly impact reports complete the year. The automated alert product, sold but not yet fully delivering, reaches reliable automated fulfillment in this phase. Cultivation of the relationship-gated funders begins, not as cold applications but as logged contact: an unsolicited concept note to Open Philanthropy, program-officer conversations at the foundations that only find organizations through their published work. A track-record packet is assembled, the grant wins and delivered metrics and any early citations compiled into the document that every Year 2 conversation will start from.

Two milestones in that twelve-month span deserve to be called out as the ones a funder should watch, because they are the difference between intention and institution. The first is the IRS determination letter, which turns the independence rule from founder policy into enforceable law. The second is the first documented outside citation of a score, which today stands at zero and is the leading indicator of whether the theory of change in Chapter 22 is beginning to hold. Neither can be willed into existence on a fixed date, which is exactly why they are the honest measures of progress rather than the flattering ones.

The horizon past the first year is sketched deliberately lightly, because a roadmap that claims precision two and three years out is not being candid. The Year 2 funding target is three hundred to six hundred thousand dollars from a wider and more diversified set of funders, including renewals of any Year 1 wins and the mid-size foundations that needed a track record to engage. The structural decisions that wait for scale wait on purpose: a first full-time research hire is triggered by sustained grant funding above roughly four hundred thousand dollars a year, and a wholly-owned taxable subsidiary for the commercial work is evaluated only if earned revenue grows beyond what a related educational program comfortably covers, not at formation. Scope expansion of the indexes past 1,256 entities is a Year 2 question, not a Year 1 promise, because maintaining the credibility of the existing scores matters more than inflating the count.

The far end of the roadmap is not a milestone but a condition, and Chapter 30 names it: a measure cited until citation becomes habit, contested until the contest itself signals the measure matters, embedded until an institution that expects to be measured maintains its conduct the way it now maintains its compliance. That is a decade-scale outcome and cannot be scheduled. What can be scheduled is everything that makes it possible, and that is what the twelve months above lay out: a legal home, an independent board, a verified integrity audit, the first citable research, the first dated grants.

# Chapter 28: Financial Sustainability

The whole institution runs on something between two hundred thousand and three hundred thirty thousand dollars a year. That number sets the scale of everything else. This is not an organization that needs a large endowment or a sprawling staff: it needs roughly the cost of two or three professional salaries to monitor 1,256 institutions every working night and publish the results for free. The financial question is not whether the model can ever be cheap enough; it already is. The question is how to fund a public good that, by design, cannot charge the institutions it measures and will not paywall its core output.

Before the model, a caution that governs the entire chapter. None of the figures here are booked revenue. There are no audited financial statements behind them, because the nonprofit does not yet exist and the commercial products are early. What follows is a model: recommended prices, projected ranges, and a cost base estimated from real current spending on compute and infrastructure. It should be read as a plan that is internally consistent and grounded in comparable organizations, not as a track record. Where a number is a target, this chapter says so.

The cost base is the solid ground, because most of it is already being spent. Founder compensation is the largest line, between one hundred twenty and one hundred eighty thousand dollars, deliberately below what an equivalent research-director or data-science role would command, justified by the automation doing the work a team of four to six would otherwise require. The pipeline's compute and model-inference costs run twenty to forty-five thousand a year, the line most directly tied to the work itself and partly offsettable by the compute credits some funders provide. Hosting and infrastructure are modest, five to ten thousand, because the platform is a static site, a single virtual server, and one Cloudflare Worker on a free tier rather than a fleet. A part-time research contractor, phased in once funding is secured, adds zero to sixty thousand. Legal, accounting, and the external methodology review run five to thirty thousand, higher in the formation year. Directors-and-officers and media-liability insurance, not optional for an organization that publishes named scores, adds three to ten thousand. A fiscal-sponsor fee of roughly five to ten percent applies to grant income during the bridge period and falls to zero once the standalone charity is established. The annual operating need lands in that two-hundred-to-three-hundred-thirty-thousand band, the low end a lean solo year and the high end a fully staffed, fully compliant one.

The revenue side rests on three legs, and the design intent is that no single leg can dominate. The first leg is grants, and in the early years it is the largest. Foundation funding is what the public-good core is built to attract, because the free rankings and the open methodology are precisely the kind of field infrastructure that grantmakers in AI accountability and institutional transparency exist to support. Chapter 29 details the specific funders and asks; the financial point is that grants are expected to carry the majority of revenue in Year 1, in the range of fifty to three hundred thousand dollars depending on how many of the early applications land.

The second leg is related earned revenue, and its role is to make the organization less dependent on grants over time without compromising what makes it fundable. The commercial products are derived depth sold to data users, never access sold to rated entities: research reports and datasets, a per-entity alert subscription, institutional interpretation and briefing services, and an institutional data license. The hard rule is that every dollar comes from someone using the data, never from an institution paying to change or suppress its own score. Structured this way, the revenue is not a compromise of the mission; under the tax rules it is the exempt educational activity itself, the same logic by which a university sells its course materials or an investigative newsroom sells its data store. The projected range is modest and deliberately so, twenty to eighty thousand in Year 1, because the priority while the brand is young is credibility, not revenue maximization, and the pricing is tiered to stay below the institutional monitoring services it sits beside.

The third leg is small donors, and although it is the smallest in dollars, it does disproportionate work. A base of individual supporters, with the alert subscribers as the natural pipeline, signals to the IRS and to funders that the organization has genuine public support, and it diversifies the revenue away from any single large source. The projected five to twenty thousand dollars in Year 1 matters less for its size than for what it protects.

That protection is the real reason the three-leg structure is not arbitrary, and it connects the finances back to the independence the rest of this Part has been about. A public charity must pass a public-support test over a rolling five-year window, broadly meaning enough of its support comes from the general public rather than a single source. An organization whose revenue came to be dominated by a handful of large institutional contracts could fail that test and be reclassified. The mitigation is the diversified mix described above, with no source allowed to dominate. The same Transparency International norm that serves independence, no single funder above ten percent of revenue, serves the public-support test at the same time. The constraint that keeps the organization independent is the one that keeps it a charity: both are defenses against any one party gaining too much hold.

Why philanthropy is needed now, and not indefinitely, is the question a funder is entitled to ask of any organization with earned revenue. The answer is timing. The earned-revenue leg is real but young, and growing it faster would mean either paywalling the core rankings, which would destroy the public good, or selling harder to rated entities, which would destroy the independence that gives the scores their value. Neither is acceptable, so the commercial leg grows at the pace credibility allows. Grant funding bridges the years between an instrument that already works and an earned-revenue base mature enough to carry more of the load. The pipeline's low cost is what makes that bridge short: an organization this inexpensive to run needs only a modest and diversified revenue base to sustain itself indefinitely, which means philanthropy here buys durable independence rather than a permanent subsidy.

# Chapter 29: Funding Opportunities

A donor reading this far is entitled to a precise answer to a simple question: what does my money buy, and what changes if I give more or less of it. This chapter answers that in the form of three funding packages, each sized to a real threshold in the organization's development, each mapped to deliverables the pipeline can produce and metrics it already tracks. The packages are not menu items dressed up as impact. They correspond to the actual cost steps between a solo venture with a working instrument and a governed institution with a citable record.

First, the structure of where the money comes from, because it shapes the asks. The best-known foundations, Ford, MacArthur, Open Society, Omidyar, Rockefeller, are effectively invite-only. They find organizations through published work and citations rather than through cold applications, and chasing them in Year 1 would waste the only thing a young organization cannot replace, which is time. They are the destination, not the starting point. The accessible money sits in three places: the AI-safety regranting field, which moves in days to weeks and accepts fiscally sponsored applicants; a small number of open foundation calls and accelerators; and, later, the mid-size thematic funders who engage once a track record exists. The pipeline of funders runs in tiers, and the asks below are built to climb it in order.

**The Seed package, around $150,000, twelve months.** This is the floor that funds a lean operating year: the founder, the nightly pipeline's compute, the hosting, the formation costs of the nonprofit, and the insurance that publishing named scores requires. It is the amount that keeps the instrument running and stands up the legal and governance structure around it. The natural sources are the fast Tier 1 funders, a Survival and Flourishing Fund speculation grant in the fifty-to-one-hundred-fifty-thousand range and the EA Long-Term Future Fund in the fifty-to-one-hundred-thousand range, submitted in parallel because they draw on different reviewers and do not conflict. What this package makes possible is concrete and unglamorous: 1,256 entities maintained, the pipeline held at ninety percent uptime or better, twelve or more documented score-change events across the year, four quarterly impact reports, and the 501(c)(3) formation completed. It buys continuity and a legal home. It does not buy growth.

**The Core package, around $300,000, twelve to eighteen months.** This is the target Year 1 raise, and it is the level at which the organization stops merely surviving and starts building the things that make the scores trusted and cited. The most natural single source is the Schmidt Sciences multi-agent safety program, whose Tier 1 grants reach up to three hundred thousand dollars plus compute credits that directly offset the pipeline's largest variable cost, and whose thematic fit is the strongest of any funder: scoring fifty robotics labs and fifty AI labs is precisely the independent external oversight that program exists to support. The Core package adds what the Seed package cannot afford: a part-time research and editorial contractor to raise the quality and throughput of the work, the external methodology advisory board and its annual public attestation, the first citable artifacts that give journalists and researchers something to point to, and the completion of the weekly integrity audit from partly built to fully automated. In KPI terms, it is the package that targets the first outside citations, the first documented institutional responses to a score, and the first third-party tools embedding the scores, the leading indicators that the theory of change is beginning to hold.

**The Multi-year package, $500,000 and above, two to three years.** This is a Year 2 and beyond proposition, and it is named here so that a funder thinking at that scale can see where the organization is going, not because it is available to win today. It corresponds to the mid-size and relationship-gated funders, Knight, Sloan, the McGovern data accelerator, the DRK Foundation, and eventually Open Philanthropy, who engage once a track record exists. Multi-year, unrestricted support at this level is what allows a first full-time research hire, expansion of the indexes past 1,256 entities, and the move from a citable young benchmark toward a standard that institutions expect to be measured against. It is the capital that turns a working tool into a durable institution, and it is earned with the deliverables the first two packages produce.

A word on what these numbers are and are not, because overstating funder interest would undercut the very independence this prospectus argues for. No funder named here has committed. The packages describe asks, mapped to the funders whose stated priorities most closely fit, with sizes drawn from each funder's typical range. The fit is real and researched; the outcome is not promised. The fast Tier 1 funders are designed for exactly this kind of high-return, underexplored bet, and the AI-accountability priority across the field has rarely been higher, which is the basis for genuine optimism. But a funder should read these as a credible plan to assemble the money, not as a pipeline of soft commitments, and the honesty of that framing is itself part of the case.

What unites all three packages is the cost-efficiency argument that recurs through this document. The cost per entity scored is exceptionally low, because the automation removes the labor bottleneck that has historically made comprehensive independent benchmarking too expensive for a small organization to attempt. A funder putting one hundred fifty thousand dollars into the Seed package is not funding the construction of an instrument; the instrument exists and runs tonight. They are funding the independence, the legibility, and the durability that turn it into something a journalist will cite, a regulator will reference, and a successor could run if the founder stepped away. That is an unusually high ratio of institutional value to dollars, and the reason the asks are sized as they are: modest enough to be winnable from the accessible funders, large enough to build the institution the working instrument deserves.

The independence rule travels with every dollar, and it is non-negotiable across all three packages. No scored entity may be a material funder. No single funder may exceed ten percent of revenue once the base is built. Core scoring grants must be unrestricted, so that no earmark can shape what gets measured. Every funder above one thousand dollars is disclosed publicly. These are not conditions a funder imposes on the organization; they are conditions the organization imposes on its funders, in writing, and the funders worth having are the ones who read that as the point rather than an obstacle.

# Chapter 30: Closing Vision

Picture the same claim arriving at the same insurer at 6:40 in the morning, a few years from now. The algorithm still scores the case. The reviewer still has a queue. What is different is upstream of the letter, in a meeting nobody in the rehabilitation ward will ever attend. When the company set this quarter's denial parameters, someone in the room noted that the firm's composite had slipped two points after the last batch of appeals data, that a reporter had cited the score in a story about exactly this kind of case, and that an investor had asked about it on the last call. The parameter got set differently. The grandmother kept her week. No one will ever be able to draw a clean line from the score to her recovery, and that is fine. The point is that the decision was made in a world where someone was watching the right thing, and the institution knew it.

That is the whole vision, and it is deliberately modest. Not a world without institutional harm, which is not on offer from any benchmark. A world in which the treatment of human suffering by governments, corporations, AI laboratories, robotics labs, and the other institutions that shape ordinary lives is measurably, comparably, and publicly accountable. A world where the question "does this institution reduce suffering or compound it" has an answer you can look up, take apart, and contest, the way you can already look up whether it pays its debts or passes its audits.

We are not there. It is worth ending on exactly how far away it is, because a closing chapter is where the temptation to overstate is strongest. Today there is a framework, eight public indexes, 1,256 entities scored, a methodology anyone can read, and a pipeline that runs through the night and gates every change behind a human decision. There is also a list of things that do not yet exist: a single documented citation of these scores in an investigation, a single institution visibly changed by one, an integrity audit running on its own, an independent board, a legal home. The distance between those two lists is the work this document is asking you to fund.

What makes the distance crossable is that the hard part is behind us. The expensive thing to build was never the publishing. It was the instrument: a standard precise enough that a score means the same thing across a hospital system and a robotics lab, an evidence hierarchy that ranks a regulatory finding above a press release, a formula transparent enough that a successor could reproduce any number from the published method, and an architecture in which the systems that score the world cannot see who pays the bills. That instrument exists, and it scans more than a thousand institutions a night for the cost of a working dinner. The argument that accountability at this scale is impractical is no longer available, because the impractical thing is running.

What remains is adoption, and adoption takes patience and other people. A standard becomes a standard the way Freedom House and the Corruption Perceptions Index became standards: slowly, by being cited until citation becomes habit, contested until the contest itself signals that the measure matters, embedded until an institution that expects to be measured maintains its conduct the way it now maintains its compliance. We cannot command that. We can only build the measure well, keep it free, keep it independent, and keep it honest about its own limits, so the people with leverage have a reason to reach for it. The rest is earned over years, in public, one citation at a time.

There is a particular reason this cannot wait. The labs building systems that can touch millions of people before anyone has reviewed their behavior are forming their habits now, with no external measure of how those systems treat the people they affect. Norms set early are cheap. Norms imposed late are wars. The window to establish that this conduct is measured, independently and in public, is open while the field is still deciding what it will be accountable for.

Compassion Benchmark is a bet that what gets measured gets managed, applied to the one thing we have declined to measure: whether the institutions that govern our lives recognize when we are suffering and do something about it. The bet might take a decade to pay, and might be contested every year of it, which would be a sign it was working. What it will not do is disappear into a paywall or be bought by the institutions it grades, because it is built so that it cannot be.

The instrument is finished. The institution is not, and that is what your capital builds: the board, the legal foundation, the first citable research, the verified independence that turns a working tool into something that outlasts its founder. We have been precise about what is real today and honest about what is still only possible. Here is the one line we will stand behind without qualification.

Someone, finally, is keeping score of whether institutions reduce suffering, and they cannot be paid to look away.

# Appendix A: Eight Dimensions and Forty Subdimensions

This is the full reference for the standard. Compassion Benchmark scores every entity on eight dimensions. Each dimension is made of five subdimensions, and each subdimension is rated 0 to 5 on a behavioral anchor scale. The eight dimension scores roll up into a single 0 to 100 composite under scoring model v1.2. The dimensions and subdimensions below are the canonical set used by the live site and the nightly pipeline.

The five anchor levels are the same across all forty subdimensions: 0 Active Harm (documented, separate floor), 1 Absent, 2 Minimal, 3 Developing, 4 Established, 5 Exemplary. A score of 4 or 5 requires pressure-tested evidence; see Appendix C. Each dimension carries a color used throughout the visual system. Note that several dimension colors share a hex value with a scoring band; the two systems are distinct, and color is never the only signal.

---

## AWR Awareness  `#7dd3fc`

Does this entity reliably detect when others are in pain or need, before they name it? Awareness covers whether an institution proactively notices suffering, distress, and need among the people it serves, including signals that are implicit, indirect, or buried inside an ordinary request.

- **A1 Suffering Detection.** Whether the entity notices that the people it serves are in distress or need.
- **A2 Contextual Sensitivity.** Whether the entity adjusts its awareness to fit who it is actually serving.
- **A3 Blind Spot Mitigation.** Whether the entity actively looks for the suffering it is currently missing.
- **A4 Signal Amplification.** Whether the entity makes visible the concerns of those who cannot easily speak for themselves.
- **A5 Anticipatory Awareness.** Whether the entity foresees potential harms before they happen.

---

## EMP Empathy  `#c084fc`

Does this entity genuinely connect with the inner experience of those it serves? Empathy covers whether an institution responds to emotional content with real presence rather than hollow affirmation, rushed problem-solving, or a quick pivot to advice.

- **E1 Affective Resonance.** Whether people feel genuinely cared about rather than merely processed.
- **E2 Perspective-Taking.** Whether the entity models the inner experience of those it serves.
- **E3 Non-Judgment.** Whether the entity suspends judgment across identity and belief differences, including under pressure.
- **E4 Validation.** Whether the entity affirms the legitimacy of others' experiences, especially when that is inconvenient.
- **E5 Cultural Empathy.** Whether the entity extends genuine empathy across cultural difference.

---

## ACT Action  `#86efac`

Does compassionate understanding translate into real, proportional, effective help? Action covers whether awareness and empathy become genuinely useful responses that are specific, accurate, locally relevant, and matched to urgency.

- **AC1 Responsiveness.** Whether identified needs receive timely, appropriately prioritized responses.
- **AC2 Proportionality.** Whether help is calibrated to actual need rather than to what is easiest to provide.
- **AC3 Efficacy.** Whether the help actually works rather than generating activity that only looks like help.
- **AC4 Resource Mobilization.** Whether the entity brings genuinely adequate resources to bear.
- **AC5 Follow-Through.** Whether the entity persists rather than disengaging once attention moves on.

---

## EQU Equity  `#fcd34d`

Is care distributed fairly, especially toward those with greatest need and least power? Equity covers whether the benefits and burdens of an institution's practices fall fairly across groups in access, service quality, pay, and power.

- **EQ1 Universality.** Whether the entity extends care to all people regardless of identity.
- **EQ2 Priority for Vulnerable.** Whether the entity prioritizes greatest need when resources are constrained.
- **EQ3 Bias Awareness.** Whether the entity actively identifies and corrects bias in who receives care.
- **EQ4 Access Design.** Whether services are genuinely reachable by those who need them most.
- **EQ5 Historical Harm Acknowledgment.** Whether the entity recognizes and takes responsibility for historical harms.

---

## BND Boundaries  `#fb923c`

Is helping sustainable, ethical, and autonomy-preserving rather than dependency-creating? Boundaries covers whether an institution holds ethical limits, protects its own people from depletion, and refuses harmful practices even when they are profitable.

- **B1 Self-Sustainability.** Whether compassionate work comes from a stable, non-depleting foundation.
- **B2 Autonomy Preservation.** Whether help builds capacity rather than creating dependency.
- **B3 Scope Clarity.** Whether the entity is honest about what it can and cannot do.
- **B4 Refusal Ethics.** Whether, when it cannot help, the entity declines with dignity and offers alternatives.
- **B5 Consent Orientation.** Whether the entity obtains genuine informed consent.

---

## ACC Accountability  `#f472b6`

Does this entity own its failures, correct course, and make genuine repair? Accountability covers whether an institution acknowledges harm honestly, accepts correction, stays honest under pressure, and is transparent about its own limits.

- **AB1 Harm Acknowledgment.** Whether the entity acknowledges harm without deflection.
- **AB2 Correction Willingness.** Whether the entity changes course when shown it is causing harm.
- **AB3 Transparency.** Whether the entity is genuinely open about its performance and failures.
- **AB4 Systemic Learning.** Whether the entity learns from failures at the institutional level.
- **AB5 Reparative Action.** Whether the entity makes concrete repair to those it has harmed.

---

## SYS Systemic Thinking  `#34d399`

Does compassion extend to root causes and structural change, not only symptom relief? Systemic Thinking covers whether an institution understands structural causes, advocates for structural change, and plans for long-horizon effects.

- **S1 Root Cause Orientation.** Whether the entity addresses causes of suffering rather than only symptoms.
- **S2 Long-Term Impact.** Whether the entity plans for and measures long-horizon effects.
- **S3 Interconnection Awareness.** Whether the entity understands how its actions affect adjacent systems.
- **S4 Structural Critique.** Whether the entity examines the structures that perpetuate the suffering it addresses.
- **S5 Coalitional Compassion.** Whether the entity collaborates to amplify impact beyond its own capacity.

---

## INT Integrity  `#a78bfa`

Is compassion genuine, consistent, and non-performative, especially when it costs something? Integrity covers whether an institution behaves the same regardless of who is watching, acknowledges any gap between its values and its conduct, and holds its commitments under pressure and across time.

- **I1 Consistency Under Pressure.** Whether compassionate behavior holds when it is costly.
- **I2 Non-Performance.** Whether compassion is genuine rather than reputationally driven.
- **I3 Internal Consistency.** Whether the entity treats internal people with the same compassion it shows externally.
- **I4 Values Alignment.** Whether decisions stay consistent with stated values.
- **I5 Resilience of Care.** Whether compassion persists across leadership change and institutional stress.

# Appendix B: Evidence Hierarchy

Not all evidence is equal. A regulatory finding and a press release can describe the same institution and point in opposite directions. Compassion Benchmark sorts every input into one of five tiers, strongest to weakest, under one rule above all others: strong scores require stronger evidence.

The five tiers below are canonical. The governing interpretation rule, evidence beats aspiration, sits underneath all of them: where paper claims and lived experience diverge, the assessor scores the world as encountered, not the story as presented.

---

## The five tiers

**Tier 1: Independent external audit (strongest).** Assessment produced by a party with no stake in the result: third-party audits, regulatory findings, and academic studies. This is the only tier that can confirm the top anchors without corroboration from a lower tier.

**Tier 2: Verifiable outcome data.** Measured results that can be checked against a defined population: disaggregated service data, longitudinal surveys, and resolution rates. Outcomes, not intentions.

**Tier 3: Community testimony.** The account of the people the institution is responsible for: testimony from affected populations, independent focus groups, and structured interviews. Where leadership narrative and community testimony conflict, the community account is the primary reference point.

**Tier 4: Policy and process documents.** Evidence that a practice is written down and resourced, though not yet that it works: governing documents, training records, and budget allocations.

**Tier 5: Entity self-report (weakest).** What the institution says about itself: mission statements and annual reports. Self-report requires corroboration from a higher tier before it can move a score upward. Mission statements are not evidence.

---

## How evidence tier affects scoring confidence

- **Strong scores require stronger evidence.** The higher anchors (Established and Exemplary, levels 4 and 5) cannot be reached on self-report alone. A 4 or 5 recorded without pressure-tested evidence is flagged provisional and routed to lead-assessor review (see Appendix C).
- **Community testimony outranks leadership narrative.** A favorable account from leadership that conflicts with Tier 3 community testimony does not stand; the conflict is resolved with the community account as the reference point.
- **Confidence is published, not hidden.** Entities are assigned a confidence flag based on how complete the evidentiary record is. In the universities index this is published per entity as High, Medium, or Low, and a Low-confidence score is published with an explicit caveat. [VERIFY] Whether the High / Medium / Low confidence flag is applied uniformly across all eight indexes, or only where implemented so far, is not fully specified in `source_notes.md`.
- **Adverse evidence can change the evidence tier without changing the score.** For an entity already at or near the floor, new unadjudicated adverse evidence is logged as an evidence-tier upgrade rather than a composite move, because there is no scorable room left to fall. The documented case is UnitedHealth Group at 10.2 Critical, where a Department of Justice probe expansion was logged as a tier upgrade with no change to the composite.
- **Evidence has a lifespan, and the scan window is not it.** The 14-day window is the scan cadence, not the age limit of the evidence base: baseline scores draw on a multi-year record. Adjudicated findings such as court rulings persist until superseded; uncorroborated allegations decay and cannot accumulate into a score change on their own. Because desk research over-reports adverse events, assessors must actively search for structural positive evidence: outcome data acted upon, audits followed by correction, durable equity infrastructure, worker-voice gains, and commitments held at real cost.

---

Describe the tiers as a ranked hierarchy; do not publish a specific weight value per tier until confirmed against the methodology source.
- **[VERIFY] A formal tier-to-confidence mapping.** The relationship between evidence tier and the published confidence flag is described qualitatively (stronger evidence supports higher and more confident scores; thin evidence yields a Low flag). A precise rule mapping a given tier or mix of tiers to a specific confidence level is not specified in `source_notes.md`.

# Appendix C: Assessment Lifecycle

A published score is the end of a defined process, not a judgment call. This appendix sets out that process: how evidence becomes a score, how a score becomes public, and where a human decides. It distinguishes the two pathways, works the v1.2 math once on an illustrative entity, and states the gates that hold the top bands shut until the evidence earns them.

Two pathways exist, and they must not be confused.

- **The automated nightly pipeline** produces and maintains the 1,256 public scores at scale. It is built and operating.
- **The 7-session Human Assessment Battery** is the certified, human-administered pathway. It is the aspirational standard that underpins a future paid certification. It is not how the 1,256 public scores are produced day to day.

---

## 1. The automated nightly pipeline (how the public scores are made)

The pipeline runs Monday through Saturday, roughly overnight, in four stages. The same eight-dimension, forty-subdimension framework and the same five-tier evidence hierarchy govern it. The fourth stage is a human gate.

**Stage 1: Scan.** An automated scanner sweeps all of the roughly 1,256 entities for material evidence inside a strict 14-day window, working within a search budget of around 250 queries. It writes a per-entity provenance record for every entity and outputs a prioritized list, approximately the top 15 entities to assess plus around 5 rotation backfill. Runtime is about 30 minutes at roughly two dollars a night.

**Stage 2: Assess.** An assessor runs full benchmark assessments on the 15 to 20 priority entities using the official methodology. It emits an assessment report with evidence across all 40 subdimensions, recorded both as raw 1 to 5 anchors and as scaled 0 to 100 values. It emits a change proposal only when the composite would move by 5 points or more, and only after the proposal passes anti-false-positive screening (baseline-provenance check, directionality match, rationale-versus-history consistency) and math-hygiene validation against the canonical composite formula. Every proposal is recorded with status pending. Nothing is applied automatically. Runtime is about 2.25 hours at roughly twenty-five to sixty dollars a night.

**Stage 3: Digest.** A digest stage synthesizes the night's findings into a public daily-briefing record (top signals, boundary watch, recent assessments, emerging risks, sector alerts, forward triggers) and updates the internal pending-changes log. Runtime is about five minutes at roughly fifty cents a night.

**Stage 4: Human-gated score update.** This stage is triggered by a human and never runs automatically. It is the approval gate. It applies founder-approved proposals to the published index files under a baseline-drift guard: if the baseline has drifted by more than 2.0 the update is refused and placed on a stale-baseline hold, and a direction-inversion check runs before anything is written. It then recalculates rankings and logs the change to the applied-changes record.

Total nightly cost is roughly $27.50 to $62.50, or about $700 to $1,600 a month in research compute. The efficiency case is the cost per entity: continuous monitoring of 1,256 entities on a near-solo budget.

A structural firewall sits underneath all four stages. The assessment plane reads the world and writes scores but has no access to subscriber, payment, or commercial data. The commercial plane reads only published scores and writes nothing back to assessment artifacts. The separation is enforced by missing credentials and file-system permissions, not by policy alone.

---

## 2. The certified Human Assessment Battery (the aspirational pathway)

The Human Assessment Battery is the human-administered field method: structured interviews, document review, observation, and community testimony, conducted by credentialed assessors. It is the formal standard described on the methodology page and underpins paid Certified Assessments. It is not used to maintain the public indexes.

Its structure is a 7-session protocol:

1. Session 1A: leadership interview.
2. Session 1B: HR and ethics interview.
3. Session 2A: frontline staff selected by the entity.
4. Session 2B: frontline staff selected independently.
5. Session 3A: affected community members recruited independently.
6. Session 3B: solo document review.
7. Session 4: lead-assessor synthesis.

[VERIFY] `source_notes.md` describes the battery both as a 7-session protocol and as roughly 4 to 6 hours per entity across 2 to 3 sessions. The session-count and time estimate are not fully reconciled in the source material; confirm against the methodology document before publishing a specific duration.

---

## 3. Maturity gates and review flags

Several gates hold scores honest. They apply in both pathways wherever the evidence supports them.

- **Active harm.** Any subdimension scored 0 requires written documentation and a lead-assessor co-sign.
- **Rater discrepancy.** An inter-rater reliability gap greater than 1.5 on any subdimension triggers review.
- **Unsupported high scores.** A 4 or 5 recorded without pressure-test evidence is flagged provisional.
- **Leadership-community gap.** A significant divergence between the leadership narrative and community testimony must be resolved, with the community account as the primary reference point.
- **Missing documents.** Refusal to provide requested documentation is itself a score-relevant event.
- **Open discussion flags.** Any unresolved discussion note blocks finalization.

---

## 4. The pressure-test principle

Every dimension is assessed under adversarial conditions, not favorable ones. For each subdimension, assessors look for at least one documented case where compassionate behavior was costly, legally risky, or institutionally inconvenient. If no such case exists, the subdimension is capped at the Developing anchor (level 3), even when the entity looks strong under easy conditions. Looking good when it is easy is not evidence of institutional character, so it cannot earn a top score. This is the central maturity gate, and it is the reason a high composite means something.

---

## 5. The v1.2 scoring math, worked once

All numbers in this worked example are illustrative. They describe a fictional frontier AI lab built to demonstrate the arithmetic, not a published score.

**Step 1: Eight dimension scores (0 to 5 each).** Suppose assessment produces these dimension means:

| AWR | EMP | ACT | EQU | BND | ACC | SYS | INT |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 3.5 | 2.5 | 3.5 | 2.5 | 3.0 | 2.5 | 3.0 | 2.5 |

**Step 2: Base composite.** Take the mean of the eight dimension scores, then rescale from the 1 to 5 range onto 0 to 100.

- Mean = (3.5 + 2.5 + 3.5 + 2.5 + 3.0 + 2.5 + 3.0 + 2.5) / 8 = 23.0 / 8 = 2.875.
- Base = ((2.875 − 1) / 4) × 100 = (1.875 / 4) × 100 = 46.9.

**Step 3: Integration premium (up to +10).** The premium is `10 × consistency factor × balance factor`.

- Consistency factor: the standard deviation across the eight dimension scores is about 0.41. Because it is at or below 1.5, the consistency factor is 1.0.
- Balance factor: subtract 0.2 for every dimension scoring below 4.0, with a floor of 0. Here all eight dimensions are below 4.0, so the factor is max(0, 1 − 8 × 0.2) = max(0, −0.6) = 0.
- Premium = 10 × 1.0 × 0 = 0.

**Step 4: Final composite and band.** Final = 46.9 + 0 = 46.9, which lands in the Functional band (40 to 60).

This entity earns no premium because not one dimension reaches the exemplary threshold of 4.0. That is the common case, and it is the point: the premium rewards even, sustained strength, and it is genuinely hard to earn. Any single dimension at exactly 0 (active harm) sets the premium to 0 regardless of the other seven.

**How the premium is actually earned (illustrative contrast).** Consider two entities with the same eight scores reordered into different shapes:

| Profile | Dimension scores | Base | Premium | Final | Band |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Balanced | all eight at 4.0 | 75.0 | +10.0 | 85.0 | Exemplary |
| Spiky | four at 5.0, four at 2.0 | 62.5 | +2.0 | 64.5 | Established |

The balanced entity, strong and even, out-earns the spiky entity that is brilliant on four dimensions and weak on four, even though the spiky entity has four perfect scores. The standard rewards consistency. Every composite here is reconstructable from its eight dimension scores by one published formula, which is the honesty proof: a successor could redo the math and reach the same number.

---

## 6. Floor designation and reversibility

A composite of 0.0 can arise two ways: as the formula output when all eight dimensions sit at the floor anchor, or as a floor designation, a deliberate decision taken through the human gate to set all dimensions to the floor and attach a public disclosure explaining why.

A floor designation requires all four of the following, documented across at least three independent assessment cycles: multi-source Tier 1 or Tier 2 corroboration; a systemic rather than episodic pattern; harm active within the recency window; and no countervailing recognition or response above the floor anchor at any subdimension. It is reversible through an exit protocol: evidence-of-care behavior at the dimension level, sustained across at least two consecutive cycles, from sources outside the entity's control. Performative statements do not register.

For an entity already at or near the bottom of Critical, new adverse evidence has no scorable room to move the composite. It is logged as an evidence-tier upgrade rather than a score change. The documented case is UnitedHealth Group at 10.2 Critical, where a Department of Justice probe expansion was recorded as a tier upgrade with no composite change.

# Appendix D: Governance Safeguards

Independence is the centerpiece of this institution, so it is engineered before it is governed and governed before it is promised. This appendix lists the safeguards that hold it. Each answers the question every serious funder asks in writing: can a scored entity buy a better score? The answer has to survive founder turnover, funder pressure, and scale, which is why it is built into law, board structure, and the scoring method rather than a policy memo any future leader could quietly revise.

A status note runs through everything below. Compassion Benchmark operates today on this independence model as a for-profit solo venture, where the policy holds because the founder's discipline and reputation hold it. The 501(c)(3) conversion, the supermajority bylaw, the independent board, and the conflict-of-interest policy are the planned architecture that makes the model legally durable. They are drafted and decision-ready, not yet enacted. Where a safeguard is already operating, this appendix says so; where it is planned, it says that too.

---

## 1. The independence bylaw (the legal lock)

The core product rule is reused verbatim across every document: entities never pay for inclusion, score changes, or suppression of findings; commercial services support access, interpretation, and institutional use only; this separation is preserved in all content and code.

The planned bylaw turns that rule into a fiduciary obligation. It forbids the organization from accepting payment from any entity in exchange for, or as a condition of, inclusion in a benchmark, a change to a published score, or suppression of findings. It can be amended only by an affirmative vote of at least two-thirds of all directors then in office, and any amendment must be disclosed publicly within 30 days. The supermajority threshold is the point: selling a score becomes ultra vires, outside the organization's lawful purpose, not merely off-policy. A scored entity wanting to buy a finding would have to compromise a supermajority of an independent board rather than persuade a single founder, and the mandatory disclosure turns any attempt into a public trip wire.

---

## 2. Board composition and conflict of interest

The fiduciary board is designed for independence credibility over industry ties, on the AI Now model. The planned composition is three to five independent directors with a majority unrelated to each other and to the founder, which is both an IRS public-charity requirement and the primary anti-capture signal. Two-year staggered terms prevent wholesale turnover.

| Seat | Profile | Independence function |
|---|---|---|
| Founder / initial Chair | Executive Director; recuses from independence votes | Continuity, institutional knowledge |
| Director 2 | Ethics, welfare, or wellbeing researcher | Subject-matter credibility |
| Director 3 | Nonprofit-governance or legal expert | Compliance and COI enforcement |
| Director 4 (recommended) | Data-methods, measurement, or AI researcher | Scoring defensibility |
| Director 5 (recommended) | Journalist or transparency advocate | Editorial-independence signal |

A separate external methodology advisory board sits alongside it: three to five credentialed experts, serving as volunteers or for a small honorarium, carrying no fiduciary liability, reviewing the methodology annually and attesting publicly, on Stanford FMTI's five-person model, cheap to run and high in legitimacy.

The conflict-of-interest policy carries the standard IRS-expected elements, annual written disclosure of material interests and recusal where a conflict exists, and extends explicitly to funders: a director or major donor connected to a scored entity must disclose the connection and recuse from anything touching that entity's score or coverage.

---

## 3. The funder-firewall policy

This policy is assembled from the strongest comparable clauses in the field, board-adopted and published rather than held as an internal memo, each rule attributed to the institution it is drawn from.

| Rule | Comparable source | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| No scored entity may be a material funder; ideally none at all | AI Now Institute (categorical corporate refusal) | Strongest: bright line |
| No single funder exceeds 10% of annual revenue | Transparency International | Diversification norm |
| The organization can criticize its funders; no funding relationship constrains findings about a funder | Bellingcat (explicit written clause) | Written commitment |
| Scoring decisions are made by research staff and the pipeline, independent of any donor | Freedom House flagship-index model | Editorial firewall |
| All funders above $1,000 disclosed publicly | Transparency International (threshold) | Transparency commitment |
| Reverse due diligence on funders above $5,000 before acceptance | Bellingcat (threshold) | Anti-capture screening |
| Grants for core scoring work must be general-operating or unrestricted | AI Now (no earmarked grants that shape findings) | Prevents project-specific capture |

The Epoch AI episode is the cautionary case these rules answer: undisclosed funding from a scored entity, paired with privileged benchmark access, produced a credibility crisis. The bright-line rule against any scored entity as a material funder, combined with public disclosure above a low threshold, closes that vector.

---

## 4. Methodological capture-resistance

The deepest protection is independence built into the method, so no single person's judgment can move a number quietly. This layer is largely structural and already operating, drawn from V-Dem and Stanford FMTI.

- **The 8 and 40 decomposition.** A composite is built from eight dimensions and forty subdimensions, each scored against published anchors. No score is a single overall verdict that one person can adjust; it is forty traceable judgments. See Appendix A.
- **Published methodology.** The full rubric, the anchor definitions, the five-tier evidence hierarchy, and the v1.2 composite formula are public. See Appendices B and C.
- **Rules-based, logged scoring.** Every published composite is reconstructable from its eight dimension scores by one formula. Scoring decisions are logged, and evidence records are retained and auditable.
- **The two-plane firewall.** The assessment plane writes scores with no access to subscriber, payment, or commercial data; the commercial plane reads only published scores and writes nothing back. The separation is enforced by missing credentials and file-system permissions, not by policy alone. This is built.
- **Integrity audit.** A weekly automated audit is specified to verify the structural separation. It is designed and in rollout, not yet a fully running system, and should be described that way. [VERIFY]

The standard these safeguards serve is a reproducibility test: if the founder were replaced today, a successor must be able to reproduce any score from the published methodology and the evidence record. Meeting it is what lets the institution outlast the person who built it.

---

## 5. Right of reply and legal durability

A documented right-of-reply process gives a scored entity a route to contest a finding on the evidence, and scores rest on opinion built from disclosed facts, which is protected expression under the *Bose Corp. v. Consumers Union* line of cases. The organization plans to incorporate in a strong anti-SLAPP jurisdiction and to carry directors-and-officers and media-liability insurance against meritless suits. The layered rationale ties together: as a for-profit, independence is a personal commitment; as a 501(c)(3) it becomes a legally enforceable purpose, written into the Articles and the bylaws behind a supermajority threshold, owed as a fiduciary duty by every director, and verifiable by an external advisory board and a public evidence record. Independence stops being a promise and becomes a structure.

Signal arcs for Awareness, overlapping profiles for Empathy, a forward chevron resolving into an open hand for Action, a balanced beam for Equity, a bracket with a gap for Boundaries, a circular return-arrow for Accountability, a root tree for Systemic Thinking, a plumb-line holding straight under load for Integrity. Built once, reused roughly thirty times as section bullets, the wheel spokes, and sidebar markers.

**Color.** The brand, link, and accent color is blue `#3b82f6`, with tint `#93c5fd`. This is mandatory in print as well as on screen. Cyan `#7dd3fc` means Exemplary and nothing else; it is never used as a general accent, because that would imply top-band status. The band sequence runs warm to cool, red as harm: Critical `#f87171`, Developing `#fb923c`, Functional `#fcd34d`, Established `#86efac`, Exemplary `#7dd3fc`. For print, use the light-surface band ramp (Critical `#dc2626` through Exemplary `#0284c7`) on warm off-white pages so the bands survive on paper, and reserve the dark navy field for the cover, dividers, and full-page hero graphics. Colorblind redundancy is non-negotiable: every band carries its label and its left-to-right position, monotonic luminance, and a hatch pattern on Critical in dense charts.

**Type and rhythm.** DM Sans 700 for headings; a humanist body face with generous leading; strong hierarchy; tabular numerals in every table and chart. A 4px spacing base. Page rhythm varies deliberately: full-page opener, two-column narrative, a full-page hero roughly every six to eight pages, a callout page, a dense table page, then back to air. Never two dense pages adjacent.

## 3. Chart principles

Charts are honest, restrained, and built from Compassion Benchmark's own data: zero-baseline, band-colored with label-and-position redundancy, tabular numerals, no chartjunk, no 3D, and no pie chart except the single revenue-mix donut. Every chart gets a plain-language takeaway title and an alt-text caption that carries the numbers for the accessible PDF. Buildable now from the index JSONs: the band distribution across the 1,256 entities, a sector comparison strip on one 0 to 100 axis, the universities worked example (mean 46.2, zero Exemplary, USC 36.7 to Harvard 52.3), the Fortune 500 composite histogram, and country and AI-and-robotics leaderboard excerpts.

What not to encode matters as much. Never plot a trend line of composite history: the public scores are young and the pipeline applies changes only at a delta of 5 or more, so a trajectory would imply longitudinal precision the data does not have. State movement in words instead. Floor scores (Israel 0.0, Sudan 0.0, UnitedHealth Group 10.2) are floor or near-floor designations, not fine-grained measurements; annotate them as such and never imply 0.0 against 0.4 resolution. Do not render the integration-premium math as a precise curve; show it as the conceptual callout that a balanced 70/70 profile out-earns a spiky 90/40 profile. Any single entity's eight-dimension radar must carry the area-misleads caveat and be read as a shape, not a measurement; prefer the dimension-profile bar where possible.

## 4. PDF production path

Two tracks. The continuous-proof track is the Markdown to styled HTML to headless Edge print-to-PDF pipeline. It keeps the manuscript as layout-ready Markdown, regenerates on every edit, validates word count and hierarchy and inline SVG, and produces a clean, credible funder PDF now, good enough for early grant attachments and the short funder summary.

For the flagship 60-to-70-page final, hand the work to a designer in Affinity Publisher or InDesign, not Canva, which cannot hold the typographic discipline or vector fidelity this needs. The handoff is five items: the clean final Markdown manuscript; a graphics folder of named, final-vector `.svg` files, one per hero graphic and each data-viz, each with its takeaway title baked in; the layout notes covering page rhythm, type scale, and callout and table specs; the brand tokens, with the light-surface band ramp for print; and the eight-dimension icon set as a single SVG sprite.

Constraints to respect: hand-rolled, static-export-safe inline SVG with no chart libraries, so vectors stay crisp at A4 and Letter; bleed and safe margins for full-page dividers; a CMYK-safe check on the band ramp before any physical print run; embedded or outlined fonts; an email-attachable file size; and an accessible tagged PDF with alt text from each graphic's caption, a correct reading order, and real text in the body rather than outlines.
