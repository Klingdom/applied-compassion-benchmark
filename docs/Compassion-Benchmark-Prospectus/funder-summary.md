# Compassion Benchmark: Funder Summary

**compassionbenchmark.com** | Phil Kling, Founder | phil@mediafier.ai

*Condensed document for grant attachments, introductory funder conversations, and board review. Full prospectus available on request.*

---

## The Problem

We have built public accountability infrastructure for almost everything that determines an institution's standing. Financial performance has audited statements and credit ratings. Product safety has recalls and inspections. Artificial intelligence now has a fast-growing set of capability and safety benchmarks. Environmental and labor conduct sit inside a thicket of indices and certifications. Across all of it, one question has no comparable public measure: when an institution's decisions reach a person who is suffering, does it recognize that, respond proportionally, and reduce the harm, or not.

That absence is not an accident. It reflects an assumption that compassion at institutional scale is too soft to score. The assumption is wrong. Compassion at institutional scale is not a sentiment; it is a pattern of decisions: whether a complaint triggered a real review, whether a coverage denial got a second look, whether a system's harm was logged before or after the news cycle. Decisions like these leave evidence. Evidence can be scored against a published standard. A score can be made public, and a public score can be cited, contested, and acted on. What has not existed, until now, is the institution to do that scoring independently, continuously, and in public.

---

## The Solution

Compassion Benchmark is an independent benchmark institution that measures how institutions recognize, respond to, and reduce suffering, and publishes the scores. It is not a consultancy, not a certification body, and not a ratings service that institutions subscribe to in order to manage their appearance. The scores come from evidence, not from self-reporting, and no rated institution can pay for inclusion, a better number, or silence.

The measurement standard, scoring model v1.2, applies eight dimensions of institutional compassion to every rated entity: Awareness, Empathy, Action, Equity, Boundaries, Accountability, Systemic Thinking, and Integrity. Beneath those eight dimensions sit forty subdimensions, each assessed against documented behavioral evidence on a five-tier evidence hierarchy in which an institution's own statements are the weakest input and independent audit is the strongest. The eight dimension scores produce a single 0 to 100 composite by a published formula, with an integration premium of up to ten points that rewards institutions performing evenly across all eight dimensions rather than excelling in one while neglecting the rest. Every published composite is reconstructable from its eight inputs by anyone who reads the method. The arithmetic is not an approximation of how scores are reached; it is the actual calculation, and reproducibility is the honesty proof the whole system depends on.

The measure applies across sectors: governments, corporations, AI and robotics laboratories, cities, universities, US states. One standard, one scale, one formula, regardless of who is being scored.

## What Exists Today

Compassion Benchmark is not a proposal. The framework, the public indexes, and the machinery that maintains them are built and operating.

Eight public indexes cover 1,256 scored entities: 193 countries, 448 Fortune 500 companies, 250 global cities, 50 AI labs, 50 robotics labs, 21 US states, 144 US cities, and 100 universities, the last index added in June 2026. Every score is free to read. The full methodology and rubric are public.

An automated nightly pipeline maintains those scores. A scanner sweeps all 1,256 entities for material evidence inside a strict fourteen-day window, a prioritization step identifies roughly the fifteen most consequential entities for full reassessment that night, an assessor applies the forty-subdimension rubric and proposes a score change only when the composite would move by five points or more, and a digest produces the day's public briefing. Nothing reaches the public indexes automatically. Every proposed change waits, flagged pending, until a person approves it. The pipeline runs six nights a week at a cost of roughly twenty-five to sixty dollars a night, because the automation removes the labor bottleneck that has historically made comprehensive independent benchmarking too expensive for a small organization to sustain.

The findings are already specific enough to matter. The hundred universities scored average 46.2 out of 100, landing in the Functional band, and not one reached Exemplary. Harvard, at 52.3, is the highest in the university set. UnitedHealth Group scores 10.2 and sits in Critical. These are not impressions. Each number is the sum of eight dimension scores anchored in sourced evidence, and each is reproducible from published inputs.

A commercial layer, research reports, a per-entity alert product, and institutional interpretation services, provides earned revenue. Those products run on a technical plane that can read published scores but cannot write to them. The rule that no rated entity pays for its score is in force as policy today.

## What We Do Not Yet Claim

A funder summary that buries its limits in footnotes is not one to trust. The honest baseline:

The count of documented external citations of these scores is zero. No institution has visibly altered its conduct in response to a published score. The weekly integrity audit is designed and partly built but is not yet running on its own schedule. Automated delivery of the paid alert product is partial. The seven-session Human Assessment Battery, the deep certified pathway intended to sit above the automated scores, is an aspirational standard that underpins a future paid service; it is not the method behind the 1,256 public scores. The organization has no independent board and no legal entity yet. Compassion Benchmark is today a solo venture with a published intention to convert to 501(c)(3) status. The revenue figures in this document are projections, not booked revenue. The impact case throughout is forward-looking, and stated that way.

## Public Benefit

The free rankings and the open methodology are the public good. A shared, comparable measure of institutional conduct is the kind of infrastructure that civil society uses the way it already uses Freedom House scores or Transparency International's Corruption Perceptions Index: a common reference point that journalists can cite, investors can fold into due diligence, policymakers can question institutions against, and affected communities can point to as a shared vocabulary. No subscription is needed to read the scores, and no institution can opt out of being rated.

The value compounds as third parties adopt the standard. Citation by one journalist makes it easier for the next to reach for the same measure. An investor who folds the scores into a due-diligence checklist makes it harder for a competitor to ignore them. A standard becomes a standard the way Freedom House and the Corruption Perceptions Index became standards: by being used until use becomes expectation. The work is built toward that trajectory. It cannot yet report it.

## Governance and Independence

The question funders in this field ask early and in writing: can a lab buy a better score? The answer Compassion Benchmark is building is layered, because a promise from a single founder is not a durable answer.

The first layer already exists. The platform runs on two separated technical planes. The assessment plane reads the world and writes scores, and it has no access to subscriber lists, payment records, or any commercial state. The commercial plane handles payments and subscriber management and can read only published scores while writing nothing back into the assessment record. The separation is enforced by missing credentials and write-protected files, not by a policy document. A person who wanted to sell a score change could not route the transaction through the software, because the part of the system that handles money has no reach into the part that sets numbers. Independence is a property of the architecture before it is a promise in a document.

The second layer is the nonprofit conversion, planned and not yet enacted. Compassion Benchmark is pursuing 501(c)(3) status via a fiscal sponsor bridge, with the Articles of Incorporation and bylaws drafted to embed the independence rule at a supermajority-amendment threshold. The intended bylaw clause states that the organization may never accept payment in exchange for inclusion in a benchmark, a change to a published score, or suppression of a finding, and that clause may not be modified except by an affirmative vote of at least two-thirds of all directors, with any such amendment disclosed publicly within thirty days. The thirty-day disclosure is a structural trip wire: any attempt to weaken the rule becomes public before it can take effect quietly.

The third layer is the independent board, planned and not yet constituted. The design calls for three to five directors chosen for independence credibility, with no director connected to any scored entity, staggered two-year terms, and annual written conflict-of-interest disclosure. A separate external methodology advisory board, carrying no fiduciary liability, will review the scoring method annually and attest publicly to what it finds.

The funder firewall is specific and written: no scored entity may be a material funder; no single funder may exceed ten percent of annual revenue once the base is built; every funder above one thousand dollars is disclosed publicly; core scoring grants must be unrestricted so no earmark can quietly shape what gets measured. These are conditions the organization imposes on its funders, in writing. The funders worth having are the ones who read that as the point rather than an obstacle.

## Financial Sustainability

The full institution runs on roughly $200,000 to $330,000 a year. The low end describes a lean solo operating year. The high end includes a part-time research contractor, full legal and accounting compliance, directors-and-officers and media-liability insurance, and a fiscal-sponsor fee during the bridge period. The pipeline's compute and inference costs, twenty to forty-five thousand dollars annually, are the line most directly tied to the work itself, and partly offsettable by compute credits some funders provide.

Revenue rests on three legs by design. Grants carry the majority in the early years and are the appropriate vehicle for public-good infrastructure that cannot charge the institutions it measures. Related earned revenue from research assets and interpretation services, projected at twenty to eighty thousand in Year 1, grows at the pace credibility allows rather than the pace revenue maximization would prefer. A small-donor base, with alert subscribers as the natural pipeline, provides the diversification that both the public-support test and the independence rule require. No single leg is allowed to dominate, and that constraint serves both goals at once: an organization whose revenue is diversified is also an organization no single funder can quietly hold.

Philanthropy is needed now because the instrument precedes the revenue base. Growing the earned-revenue leg faster would require either paywalling the core rankings, which eliminates the public good the work is built to produce, or selling access to rated entities, which eliminates the independence that makes the scores worth anything. Neither is acceptable. Grant capital bridges the years between a working instrument and a revenue base mature enough to carry more of the load. Given how low the cost base already is, that bridge is short.

## Funding Priorities

The Year 1 ask is $150,000 to $300,000 in general-operating support over twelve months.

At $150,000, the Seed level, the grant covers the founder, the pipeline's compute, the hosting, the nonprofit formation, and the insurance that publishing named scores of real institutions requires. It buys continuity, a legal home, and the 501(c)(3) path completed. It does not buy growth: 1,256 entities maintained, the pipeline at ninety percent uptime or better, twelve or more documented score-change events over the year, four quarterly impact reports.

At $300,000, the Core level, the grant adds a part-time research and editorial contractor, the external methodology advisory board and its annual public attestation, the first citable research artifacts that give journalists and researchers something to point to, and completion of the weekly integrity audit from partly built to fully automated. The Core package is the level at which the organization stops merely running and starts building the things that make its scores trusted and cited.

The most accessible near-term funders are the AI-safety regranting field and a small number of open foundation programs. The Survival and Flourishing Fund speculation grant, the EA Long-Term Future Fund, and the Schmidt Sciences multi-agent safety program, which offers up to $300,000 plus compute credits against a hard August 8, 2026 deadline, are the Tier 1 targets: open-application, accepting fiscally sponsored applicants, and designed to move in days to weeks. No funder named here has committed. The fit between Compassion Benchmark's work and the stated priorities of these programs is real and researched. The outcome is not promised.

A multi-year grant of $500,000 and above is a Year 2 and beyond proposition, contingent on the track record the first year builds. Mid-size thematic funders, Knight, Sloan, the McGovern data accelerator, and eventually Open Philanthropy, engage once citable work and functioning governance exist. The roadmap is built to climb the funder tiers in order, earning each stage with the deliverables the previous one produced.

A funder at any level should read the ask in one particular light. The money does not build the instrument; the instrument exists and scans more than a thousand institutions tonight. The money builds the institution around the instrument: the board, the legal foundation, the verified integrity audit, the first research an outsider can cite. That is an unusually high ratio of institutional value to dollars, because the most expensive and uncertain work in a project like this is ordinarily the standard and the machinery. That work is done.

## Impact Expectations

The causal chain runs from published scores to third-party adoption to institutional accountability, and the honest version of that chain names where each link depends on actors outside the organization. Outputs are under our control: 1,256 entities scored and maintained, quarterly impact reports published, score-change events logged and dated, a daily public briefing. Outcomes depend on adoption by journalists, researchers, policymakers, and affected communities, none of whom we control. Long-horizon impact, the kind where institutions maintain their conduct because they expect to be measured, sits on a decade-scale horizon, not a grant deliverable.

The leading indicators that matter at the end of Year 1 are whether the outcome metrics have moved off their current baseline of zero. A reasonable target for a Core-funded year: ten or more documented external citations, three or more instances of an institution responding to its score in a documented way, five or more third-party sites or tools embedding the data. If those move, the theory of change is alive. If outputs are green and outcomes are still at zero, that is the signal that the load-bearing assumption is failing, and it should be named that way rather than obscured by output numbers that were never in doubt.

One set of metrics does not trend. Independence indicators must read intact at every reporting interval: zero instances of any entity paying for inclusion, a score change, or suppression; zero material funders tied to a scored entity; full public disclosure of every funder above the threshold. A breach there is not a missed target. It is a structural failure the rest of the numbers cannot offset, and the whole value of the work depends on it never occurring.

The scoring instrument is built and running. The institution around it is what this first year of philanthropic capital funds. The expensive uncertainty in this project is behind us. What remains is the slower work: governance, citable research, verified independence, and the patience for a standard to become a standard, one citation at a time.
