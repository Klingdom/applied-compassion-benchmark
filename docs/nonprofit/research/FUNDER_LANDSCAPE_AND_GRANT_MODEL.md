# Funder Landscape & Grant-Submission Model — Compassion Benchmark

**Prepared:** 2026-06-28
**For:** Compassion Benchmark (compassionbenchmark.com) — an independent, AI-native benchmark institution measuring how ~1,155 institutions (governments, corporations, AI labs, robotics labs, cities, universities) recognize, respond to, and reduce suffering, across 8 dimensions / 40 subdimensions, with a public transparent methodology and a nightly automated evidence-scanning + assessment pipeline.
**Status:** Planning to become a nonprofit (501(c)(3) pending). Strict independence — entities never pay for inclusion, score changes, or suppression.

> **How to read this document.** Section 1 maps the funder landscape into tiers. Section 2 explains how grant submissions actually work. Section 3 is the proposed, repeatable grant-submission model (pipeline, master template, theory of change, impact metrics, 12-month calendar, ask sizes). Section 4 lists sources with URLs. Where a fact should be confirmed directly with the funder before relying on it, it is flagged **[VERIFY]**.

## 0. Executive summary

- **Where Compassion Benchmark sits:** at the intersection of (a) **AI governance & safety** (it scores AI and robotics labs), (b) **institutional accountability / transparency / public-interest technology**, (c) **data & measurement public goods**, and (d) **human-rights / wellbeing / reduction-of-suffering** measurement. Most large foundations cluster their AI giving into four buckets — *safety & alignment* (Open Philanthropy, Schmidt, Mozilla), *fundamental research* (Sloan, Simons, Moore), *public-purpose deployment* (McGovern, Gates, Rockefeller), and *governance & rights* (Ford, Hewlett, Omidyar, Knight). Compassion Benchmark fits the **governance & rights** and **accountability** buckets most cleanly, with a strong secondary claim on **safety** (AI/robotics lab scoring) and **data public goods**.
- **The structural reality:** the largest, best-known foundations (Ford, MacArthur, Open Society, Omidyar, Rockefeller, Hewlett, Sloan, Siegel, Patrick J. McGovern) are **invite-only / no unsolicited proposals**. They are relationship-and-referral driven. They are *aspirational* targets, not first applications.
- **The accessible money for a solo-founder pre-501(c)(3) org** is in **open-RFP and rolling-application programs** that explicitly accept fiscally-sponsored or non-501(c)(3) applicants: the **AI-safety/longtermist regranting ecosystem** (Survival and Flourishing Fund, EA Long-Term Future Fund, Manifund regrantors, Foresight Institute), **Schmidt Sciences' open Trustworthy-AI RFPs**, **Mozilla's cohort programs**, **Fast Forward's tech-nonprofit accelerator**, and **competition-style funds** (Patrick J. McGovern's AI for Humanity Prize, Google.org Impact Challenges).
- **Recommended first three to approach:** **(1) Survival and Flourishing Fund / Speculation Grants**, **(2) EA Long-Term Future Fund**, **(3) Schmidt Sciences Trustworthy-AI / Multi-Agent-Safety RFP** — all accept non-501(c)(3) / fiscally-sponsored or global applicants, all are open-application, all map to the AI/robotics-lab-scoring side of the work, and the first two can move in **days to weeks**, not months.
- **Recommended ask sizes:** a first **seed / general-operating** ask of **$150k–$300k** (12 months) against a solo-founder + automated-pipeline cost base; scaling toward **$300k–$750k/yr** once a track record and fiscal sponsor are in place; aspirational tier-3 grants ($500k–$2M) are multi-year and relationship-gated.

## 2. Grant mechanics — how submissions actually work

### 2.1 The two-stage norm: LOI/concept note → full proposal
Most foundations gate on a short artifact first:
- **Letter of Inquiry (LOI) / concept note:** 1–3 pages. Problem, your solution, why you, rough budget/amount, fit to the funder's strategy. Many funders (Sloan, Open Society program areas) invite a full proposal only after a promising LOI.
- **Full proposal:** invited; 5–20+ pages plus attachments (budget, org docs, 501(c)(3) determination letter or fiscal-sponsor agreement, board list, financials).
- **Open RFP / competition:** a structured online form with set questions and a hard deadline (Schmidt, Mozilla, Google.org, McGovern Prize, Fast Forward). The "form" *is* the proposal.
- **Regranting / fast tracks:** SFF Speculation Grants, LTFF, Manifund — a single lightweight application, decisions in days–weeks.

### 2.2 Standard full-proposal components
1. **Problem / need statement** — the gap, with evidence and stakes.
2. **Theory of change** — the causal chain from your activities to outcomes to impact (narrative).
3. **Logic model** — the table version: Inputs → Activities → Outputs → Outcomes → Impact.
4. **Activities / workplan** — what you'll do, by when (often a Gantt or milestone list).
5. **Monitoring, evaluation & impact metrics (M&E)** — what you'll measure, targets, how you'll collect it.
6. **Organizational capacity** — team, track record, governance, why you can deliver.
7. **Budget & budget narrative** — line items + a plain-language justification of each.
8. **Sustainability / future funding** — how the work continues after the grant.
9. **Independence / conflict-of-interest statement** — *critical for this org;* state that entities never pay for inclusion/score changes.

### 2.3 Fiscal sponsorship — applying before the 501(c)(3) is final
- A **fiscal sponsor** is an existing 501(c)(3) that receives and administers grant funds for your project, lending you its tax-exempt status, compliance, and financial reporting. You apply *under the sponsor's EIN*; the award goes to the sponsor, which disburses to your project per the grant terms.
- This **unblocks most eligibility requirements** (Mozilla's "must be able to receive funds from a US 501(c)(3)," and any funder requiring tax-exempt status) **before** your own determination letter arrives, and it **reduces perceived risk** for reviewers.
- **Models:** *Comprehensive* (project becomes a program of the sponsor; sponsor holds all legal/fiduciary responsibility — good when you have employees) vs. *Pre-approved grant relationship* (Model C; you stay separate, sponsor regrants to you).
- **Cost:** fees typically **5%–15%** of funds received (range 1%–15%).
- **Caveat:** some funders fund only orgs with their *own* 501(c)(3); confirm per funder.
- **Relevant sponsors for this space:** Players Philanthropy Fund (turnkey), Social Good Fund (explicitly hosts AI-civic-infrastructure projects), Rockefeller Philanthropy Advisors, Anti-Entropy (hosts an AI-safety fund). The AI-safety regranting funders (SFF, LTFF, Manifund) effectively *act as* the charitable conduit, so you may not even need a separate sponsor for those.

### 2.4 Typical timelines (LOI → award)
- **Fast tracks (SFF Speculation, LTFF, Manifund):** ~1–6 weeks.
- **Open RFP/competition (Schmidt, Mozilla, Google.org):** deadline → notification 2–4 months later.
- **Invite-only relationship funders (Ford, Siegel, Rockefeller, MacArthur):** often **~12 months** from first contact to award (Siegel explicitly ~1 year).
- **Accelerators (Fast Forward):** application window → cohort start within a few months.

### 2.5 Reporting obligations (post-award)
- Expect **interim + final narrative reports** against the metrics you proposed, plus a **financial report** reconciling spend to budget. Larger/multi-year grants may require annual reports and a final evaluation. Fiscal sponsors usually handle the financial filing; you supply the program narrative. Build reporting into the workplan from day one — fund your own M&E.

## 3. Proposed grant-submission model for Compassion Benchmark

### 3.1 Prioritized funder pipeline (sequence and rationale)

**Phase A — Fast, accessible capital (Months 0–3).** Targets that accept non-501(c)(3)/global/fiscally-sponsored applicants and decide quickly; they monetize the AI/robotics-lab-scoring angle (the most fundable framing).
1. **Survival and Flourishing Fund — Speculation Grant** (1-week-class decisions; institutional-resilience + AI-safety framing).
2. **EA Long-Term Future Fund** (rolling; small-team-friendly; AI governance framing).
3. **Manifund regrantors + Foresight Institute** (rolling; small but fast; builds AI-safety credibility and warm intros).
4. **Schmidt Sciences — Scaling AI Safety for a Multi-Agent World** (full app target Aug 8 2026; robotics-lab scoring = multi-agent oversight; brings compute/API credits that subsidize the pipeline).

**Phase B — Capacity, network, and brand (Months 2–6).**
5. **Fast Forward Accelerator** (apply Jul 30–Sep 8 2026; $25k + the *exact* capacity stack a solo founder needs: impact measurement, board, scaling, funder intros).
6. **Patrick J. McGovern AI-for-Humanity Prize** (track 2027 cycle) and **Mozilla** cohort (track 2027 cycle) — competition/cohort routes that don't need relationships.

**Phase C — Mid-size thematic grants (Months 4–12, once fiscally sponsored + a 6-month track record exists).**
7. **Knight Foundation open calls** (tech × democracy / AI accountability research).
8. **Sloan** (2-page LOI; "measurement as a public good / public understanding" framing).
9. **Patrick J. McGovern Data Practice Accelerator** (if data-asset framing fits; ≤$125k).
10. **DRK Foundation** (early-stage; test the "post-pilot, pre-scale" + spin-out-from-fiscal-sponsor framing).

**Phase D — Aspirational relationship cultivation (Months 6–18, parallel, low-intensity).**
11. **Open Philanthropy / Coefficient Giving** (watch for the next AI-governance RFP; submit unsolicited concept note to be on radar).
12. **Current AI / Humanity AI / MacArthur Technology in the Public Interest** (watch for 2026–27 open mechanisms; their stated "accountability / auditing / public oversight" language is a near-verbatim fit).
13. **Ford, Omidyar, Open Society, Siegel, Luminate, Rockefeller** — cultivate via published work, citations, and program-officer contact; do **not** spend cold-application effort here yet.

**Why this order:** start where money is *applicable-for and fast*, use early wins + a fiscal sponsor + an accelerator to manufacture the track record and relationships that the Tier-2/3 funders require before they'll engage.

### 3.2 Reusable master proposal template (tailored to Compassion Benchmark)

> Maintain one master document; clone and trim per funder. Keep a "module library" so each section can be swapped to a funder's framing (safety / accountability / data-public-good / wellbeing).

1. **One-line identity** — "Compassion Benchmark is an independent, AI-native benchmark institution that measures how 1,155 institutions recognize, respond to, and reduce suffering — and makes the scores public."
2. **Problem / need** — Institutions face little standardized, independent, comparable measurement of how they treat human suffering; AI and robotics labs especially lack external accountability scoring. Existing ESG/rights indices are paid-for, narrow, or capturable.
3. **Our solution** — A transparent 8-dimension / 40-subdimension methodology applied uniformly across governments, corporations, AI labs, robotics labs, cities, universities; a nightly automated evidence-scanning + assessment pipeline; public rankings; documented score-change events.
4. **Why now** — AI/robotics deployment is accelerating; public-interest AI accountability is a top philanthropic priority (Current AI, Humanity AI, Schmidt, McGovern). An independent, automatable scorer is timely and scalable.
5. **Independence statement (load-bearing)** — Entities never pay for inclusion, score changes, or suppression. Commercial revenue supports access/interpretation only. Include the conflict-of-interest policy.
6. **Theory of change** (see 3.3) + **logic model** (see 3.4).
7. **Activities / workplan** — methodology maintenance, pipeline operation/expansion, publication cadence, integrity audits, stakeholder/citation outreach.
8. **Impact metrics / M&E** (see 3.4).
9. **Organizational capacity** — solo founder + automated pipeline (a *feature*: low cost-per-entity, scalable); advisory board (to recruit); fiscal sponsor; technical architecture.
10. **Budget & narrative** (see 3.5).
11. **Sustainability** — diversified: grants + independent commercial services (access/interpretation, never pay-to-rank) + potential earned income; roadmap to own 501(c)(3).
12. **Appendices** — methodology link, sample entity assessment, sample daily digest, integrity-audit report, letters of support.

### 3.3 Draft theory of change

**If** an independent institution publishes transparent, comparable, regularly-updated scores of how governments, corporations, AI labs, robotics labs, cities, and universities recognize and reduce suffering — and does so cheaply and at scale via an automated evidence pipeline while remaining strictly independent of the entities it scores —

**then** journalists, researchers, policymakers, advocates, and the institutions themselves gain a shared, citable measurement standard;

**which leads to** (short term) increased visibility and public discourse around institutional treatment of suffering; (medium term) institutions responding to score movements and accountability events, and third parties citing/using the scores in oversight, reporting, and policy; (long term) a measurable shift in institutional behavior toward recognizing, responding to, and reducing suffering — i.e., AI/robotics labs, governments, and corporations adopting more compassionate, accountable practices.

**Key assumptions (state them; funders reward honesty):** transparency drives accountability; independence is preserved and trusted; the automated pipeline produces credible, defensible scores; scores get cited/used; institutions are responsive to reputational measurement.
