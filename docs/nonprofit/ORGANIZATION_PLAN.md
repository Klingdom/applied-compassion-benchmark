## 1. Executive Summary

Compassion Benchmark already runs on the model that defines the strongest organizations in its category. It publishes free comparative rankings of named entities, and it never lets a scored entity pay for inclusion, a score change, or the suppression of a finding. The research behind this plan looked at sixteen comparable organizations and found that this independence model is the category standard rather than a constraint unique to one organization. None of those sixteen charge the entities they rate.

Converting to a nonprofit is therefore not about changing the model. It is about making the model structurally durable along three dimensions: legal, financial, and institutional.

The first is independence. As a for-profit solo venture, the independence policy holds only as long as the founder's discipline and reputation hold it. As a 501(c)(3), it can be written into the Articles of Incorporation and the bylaws behind a supermajority-amendment threshold. That makes it harder to undo than any personal commitment and turns it into a fiduciary obligation on every director. A scored entity that wanted to influence a finding would not merely be off-policy; it would be working against a legally enforceable organizational purpose.

The second is access to grant capital. Most foundations that fund AI accountability, transparency, and institutional-measurement work cannot legally grant to a for-profit without extraordinary due diligence (the "expenditure responsibility" rules). A 501(c)(3), or even a fiscal-sponsorship bridge to one, opens the Tier 1 fast-track grants in the AI-safety community (SFF, LTFF, Schmidt) within weeks, and positions the organization for mid-size grants in the $100k to $500k range and, later, multi-year grants of $500k to $2M as a track record accumulates.

The third is durability beyond the founder. The automated evidence-scanning and assessment pipeline is already an institutional asset that reduces key-person risk. A real board, a published methodology, and a fiscal structure that survives Phil are what let Compassion Benchmark outlast its founder, and outlasting its founder is the precondition for long-term citation, policy relevance, and field trust.

None of this requires abandoning the existing revenue model. Selling research assets and institutional interpretation services is defensible as substantially related educational program activity under the 501(c)(3) rules on unrelated business income, in the same way a university sells course materials or a think tank sells datasets. The ProPublica Data Store and Bellingcat's training revenue are direct precedents. The transition keeps Gumroad and the commercial services in place; it reframes them as educational program outputs and keeps the core rankings free and public.

## 2. Mission, Vision, and Charitable Purpose

**Mission.** Compassion Benchmark measures how institutions recognize, respond to, and reduce suffering, and makes the scores public.

**Vision.** A world in which the treatment of human suffering by governments, corporations, AI laboratories, robotics labs, and other institutions is measurably, comparably, and publicly accountable.

### Charitable Purpose Statement (exemption-ready language)

The organization is organized and operated exclusively for educational and scientific purposes within the meaning of Section 501(c)(3) of the Internal Revenue Code, specifically to:

- educate the public by researching, developing, and freely publishing transparent, independent, comparative benchmarks that measure how institutions across sectors recognize, respond to, and reduce human suffering;
- advance the scientific understanding of institutional behavior as it relates to human welfare, including through the development and public disclosure of reproducible measurement methodologies;
- support journalists, researchers, policymakers, and civil society in interpreting and applying comparative institutional-suffering measurements for the public benefit; and
- provide related educational materials, training, and tools that help institutions understand and improve their recognition and reduction of suffering.

This language is built to satisfy the IRS. It fits the educational and scientific purposes set out in IRC 501(c)(3) and Treas. Reg. 1.501(c)(3)-1(d)(3). Publishing comparative rankings of named entities educates the public, in the same way that Consumer Reports, ProPublica, and Freedom House do, each of which is a 501(c)(3). Developing and disclosing a reproducible methodology advances scientific understanding. The statement excludes pay-for-score by tying every activity to the public benefit of the research rather than to the interests of the entities being scored.

## 3. Theory of Change

### The problem

There is no independent, comparable, standardized way to measure how major institutions treat human suffering. The accountability indices that do exist (ESG ratings, human-rights rankings, AI ethics scorecards) are narrow, siloed, capturable by the entities they rate, or locked behind paywalls. AI and robotics laboratories, among the most consequential actors of this decade, face almost no standardized external accountability scoring on these dimensions at all. The result is that institutions manage reputational optics without measurable accountability, and no shared vocabulary exists for comparing institutional behavior across sectors.

### Activities

- Operate a nightly automated evidence-scanning and assessment pipeline across all 1,256 scored entities.
- Maintain and publish the full 8-dimension, 40-subdimension methodology and rubric.
- Publish free comparative rankings and periodic score updates.
- Conduct and publish integrity audits, automated weekly and external on a periodic basis.
- Publish daily and periodic research digests and special briefings.
- Provide educational interpretation services: research assets, institutional briefings, and self-assessment tools.
- Operate the Score Watch subscriber alert system.

### Outputs

- 1,256 entities scored across 8 dimensions and 40 subdimensions.
- Publicly accessible rankings and methodology documentation.
- A continuous evidence record with documented score-change accountability events.
- Research assets and interpretation tools available to subscribers and institutional buyers.
- Quarterly public impact reports.

### Outcomes (short to medium term)

- Journalists, researchers, policymakers, and advocates gain a shared, citable measurement standard for how institutions treat suffering.
- Institutions respond to score movements by publicly referencing or defending their scores.
- Third parties cite or embed scores in oversight reporting, policy analysis, and institutional communications.
- A subscriber and user base creates sustained demand for the measurement standard.

### Impact (long term)

The long-term aim is a measurable shift in institutional behavior: AI and robotics labs, governments, and corporations adopt more accountable and more compassionate practices because doing so is measurable and publicly visible. The further ambition is that Compassion Benchmark scores enter the standard due-diligence vocabulary for institutional assessment, in the way Freedom House democracy scores or the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index have. This is the destination, not a present claim.

### Key assumptions, stated plainly

1. Transparency drives accountability: institutions respond to public comparative measurement.
2. Independence is preserved and trusted: the structural firewall holds and is credibly communicated.
3. The automated pipeline produces credible, defensible scores at scale.
4. Third parties cite and embed the scores in consequential contexts.

## 4. Programs and Activity Lines

### Program 1: The Public Benchmark

This is the core educational output: free, publicly accessible comparative rankings of 1,256 entities across 8 dimensions and 40 subdimensions. The methodology is fully published, and every scoring decision is traceable to documented evidence.

It is a pure public good, with no paywall, no pay-to-play, and openly reproducible scoring. That is what makes it fundable. Foundations support it as infrastructure for the broader field. The boundary condition is simple and not negotiable: the core rankings are free and always will be, and no scored entity pays for any aspect of its score. This is encoded in the bylaws (see Section 6).

### Program 2: The Evidence Pipeline

This is the operational engine: nightly automated evidence scanning across all scored entities, automated preliminary assessments, periodic comprehensive reassessments, weekly integrity audits, and documented score-change events.

The pipeline is the central institutional asset. Its automation drives the per-entity cost to near zero at scale, which makes coverage of 1,256 entities possible at a cost base no manual research team could match. For funders, this is the durability argument: the work can outlast any individual person.

### Program 3: Education and Interpretation

Publication on its own is not enough; people need help using the benchmark. This program supports journalists, researchers, policymakers, and institutions in understanding and applying the scores. It includes published methodology papers and special briefings, the Score Watch alert system, public-facing data tools and API access, institutional briefings, training on applying the methodology, and self-assessment tools.

### Program 4: Related Earned-Revenue Program

This is the commercial arm, formalized and structured as educational program activity so that it stays substantially related to the exempt purpose under the unrelated-business-income (UBIT) rules. It includes tiered research datasets and reports (the Gumroad and Data Store model), institutional interpretation and benchmarking services, and custom analysis and briefings for institutional buyers.

The boundary conditions matter, and the full analysis is in STRUCTURE_AND_COMPARABLES.md Section 1.4:

- Core rankings and methodology are always free and public.
- Commercial products are derived from the benchmark (added depth, customization, access tooling), not the benchmark itself.
- Pricing is mission-aware, tiered for nonprofits, researchers, and press, consistent with the ProPublica Data Store model rather than revenue-maximizing.
- Earned revenue stays incidental and subordinate to the charitable purpose.
- File Form 990-T on any genuinely unrelated lines, and do not let this program become the dominant activity.
- If commercial revenue scales significantly, evaluate a wholly-owned taxable subsidiary (the Mozilla structure) at that point, not at formation.

## 5. Recommended Legal Structure and Formation Path

### Primary recommendation: US 501(c)(3) public charity

The full analysis is in STRUCTURE_AND_COMPARABLES.md Sections 1.1 through 1.6. In brief, three choices drive the recommendation.

Public charity, not private foundation. The intended revenue mix of grants, earned revenue, and small donations maps to the public support test, and the required independent-board majority is itself an anti-capture mechanism. Public charity is the form chosen by every close US analogue: ProPublica, Access Now, AI Now Institute, Freedom House, Epoch AI.

US, not a European charitable form. Funding, audience, and funder infrastructure are primarily US-based. A US 501(c)(3) opens the broadest donor-deduction profile, and the "independent American institute" identity is the clearest one to project.

A related earned-revenue program now, not a subsidiary. At current revenue scale, the existing Gumroad and services revenue is defensible as substantially related educational activity. A taxable-subsidiary structure (the Mozilla model) adds complexity without benefit at formation. Evaluate it only if commercial revenue grows beyond what a related program can comfortably cover.

### The bridge: Model C fiscal sponsorship

During the roughly three-to-six-month IRS Form 1023 review period, a Model C fiscal sponsor provides the immediate ability to receive tax-deductible grants and donations, back-office compliance support, and the organizational credibility that early funder conversations require. This is a bridge, not a permanent home. The full model comparison is in STRUCTURE_AND_COMPARABLES.md Section 1.2.

Model C, the pre-approved grant relationship, is preferred over Model A. Under Model C you keep your own entity and your own control, and the sponsor re-grants funds to you. Fees typically run 4 to 10 percent. The Ranking Digital Rights history, three sponsors over the life of the project, is the cautionary tale: it shows the risk of becoming dependent on a host. Treat sponsorship as a time-limited bridge to standalone 501(c)(3) status.

The shortlist of sponsors to evaluate should be matched to the first-funder pipeline (see GRANT_MODEL.md Section 3):

| Sponsor | Thematic fit | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Code for Science & Society** | Data, research, open science; public-interest data | Strong fit; has sponsored PREreview and the Dat Project |
| **Players Philanthropy Fund** | General-purpose; turnkey and efficient | Fast to set up; low friction |
| **Social Good Fund** | AI and civic-infrastructure projects | Good fit for the AI-safety funder pipeline |
| **Anti-Entropy** | Hosts AI-safety projects | Most compatible with the SFF and LTFF pipeline specifically |

### Formation steps and timeline

| Step | Action | Timeline | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Choose incorporation state; verify name availability | Week 1 | $0 |
| 2 | Draft Articles of Incorporation (501(c)(3) purpose, dissolution language, independence clause) | Week 1–2 | $0 DIY / $500–$2k with attorney |
| 3 | Recruit initial board (3+ independent directors, the gating step) | Weeks 2–8 | $0 |

| 5 | Sign Model C fiscal-sponsorship agreement | Weeks 3–6 | 5–10% fee on grants received |
| 6 | File Form 1023 (full, not EZ; see below) | Month 1–3 | $600 IRS fee |

A note on the form: use the full Form 1023, not the 1023-EZ. Compassion Benchmark will project more than $50k in combined grant and earned revenue, which disqualifies the EZ option. More importantly, the full Form 1023 lets the organization describe and pre-clear its earned-revenue model and its independence safeguards with the IRS, which reduces later ambiguity around UBIT and the commerciality doctrine. The extra work is worth doing.

Total formation cost runs about $700 to $1,000 done DIY, or about $2,500 to $6,000 with nonprofit-attorney review. Given the earned-revenue complexity and the defamation risk profile, attorney review is recommended. The realistic timeline from planning to operational is two to eight months, with fiscal sponsorship bridging the gap.

## 6. Governance and Independence Architecture

Independence is the centerpiece of this organization's value. The job of this section is to make independence structurally durable: not a founder's policy statement but a legal guarantee that survives Phil, survives funder pressure, and survives scale.

### Board composition

| Seat | Profile | Selection rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Founder / initial Chair (Phil) | Executive Director initially; may Chair or recuse | Continuity and institutional knowledge; recuses from independence-related votes |
| Director 2 | Ethicist, welfare researcher, or suffering/wellbeing expert | Subject-matter credibility; independence signal to the field |
| Director 3 | Nonprofit-governance or legal expert | Compliance, COI enforcement, fiduciary rigor |
| Director 4 (recommended) | Data-methods, AI/ML researcher, or measurement scientist | Methodology credibility; scoring defensibility |
| Director 5 (recommended) | Journalist, transparency advocate, or accountability practitioner | Editorial-independence signal; field legitimacy |

The design principles are drawn from the comparables in STRUCTURE_AND_COMPARABLES.md Section 2.2. A majority of directors must be unrelated to each other and to Phil, which is both an IRS public-charity requirement and the primary anti-capture signal. No director may be connected to a scored entity, enforced through the conflict-of-interest policy below. Two-year staggered terms prevent wholesale board turnover. AI Now's non-industry board is the model to follow: choose directors for independence credibility, not for industry ties or a fundraising Rolodex.

A separate external methodology advisory board sits alongside the fiduciary board. It comprises three to five credentialed experts (welfare economists, human-rights measurement scholars, AI governance researchers), serving as volunteers or for a small honorarium and carrying no fiduciary liability. It reviews the methodology annually and publicly attests to its findings. The model is Stanford FMTI's five-person advisory board: cheap to run and high in legitimacy. It strengthens grant applications and helps neutralize allegations of bias.

### The independence bylaw (the legal lock)

The following clause, or substantively equivalent language drafted by counsel, must appear in the bylaws and require a two-thirds supermajority of the full board to amend:

> *The organization shall never accept payment from any entity in exchange for, or as a condition of, inclusion in a benchmark, a change to a published score, or suppression of findings. This policy may not be modified, suspended, or waived except by an affirmative vote of at least two-thirds of all directors then in office, and any such amendment must be disclosed publicly within 30 days of adoption.*

The supermajority lock matters because of what it would take to defeat it. A scored entity that wanted to buy a score change would have to compromise a supermajority of an independent board, not simply influence one founder. The mandatory 30-day public disclosure turns any amendment attempt into a reputational trip wire.

### Conflict-of-interest policy

Form 1023 asks about the conflict-of-interest policy, so the standard IRS-expected elements should be in place: annual written disclosure by all directors and officers of material financial interests and relationships, and recusal from any vote, deliberation, or conversation where a conflict exists. The policy extends explicitly to funder conflicts. A director or major donor connected to a scored entity must disclose the connection and recuse from anything that touches that entity's score or coverage.

### Funding-acceptance and firewall policy

This policy is assembled from the strongest comparable clauses (detailed in STRUCTURE_AND_COMPARABLES.md Sections 1.5 and 3.3). It is written, board-adopted, and published on the website. It is not an internal memo.

| Rule | Comparable source | Strength level |
|---|---|---|
| No scored entity may be a material funder; ideally no scored entity at all | AI Now Institute (categorical corporate refusal) | Strongest: bright-line |
| No single funder exceeds 10% of annual revenue | Transparency International | Diversification norm |
| "We can criticize our funders": no funding relationship constrains findings about the funder | Bellingcat (explicit written clause) | Written commitment |
| Scoring decisions are made by research staff independent of any donor or external party | Freedom House flagship-index model | Editorial firewall |
| All individual and institutional funders above $1,000 disclosed publicly | TI (~€1k threshold) | Transparency commitment |
| Reverse due diligence on funders above $5,000 before accepting | Bellingcat (~€5k threshold) | Anti-capture screening |
| Grants for core scoring work must be general-operating or unrestricted | AI Now (no earmarked grants that shape findings) | Prevents project-specific capture |

### Methodological capture-resistance (the strongest safeguard)

The deepest protection, drawn from V-Dem and Stanford FMTI, is independence engineered into the method itself rather than only into the bylaws. The aim is that no single person's judgment can move a number quietly. The full rubric, weights, and evidence sources are published. Scoring decisions are rules-based and logged against the rubric. Evidence records are retained and auditable. Score-change events are documented with their rationale and published. Integrity audits run weekly, already operational via `research/scripts/integrity-check.mjs`. The external advisory board reviews the methodology annually and publicly attests to its findings.

The standard to hold to is a reproducibility test: if Phil were replaced today, could a successor reproduce any score from the published methodology and evidence record? The answer must be yes. That is the durability standard.

### Managing the public support test

The 501(c)(3) public-charity classification requires passing the public support test over a rolling five-year window, generally at least one-third of support from the broad public. There is a real risk to watch. If earned revenue from large institutional contracts or Gumroad volume comes to dominate, parts of it may not count as public support, and a single large grant may be capped at 2 percent for test purposes. The 509(a)(1) versus 509(a)(2) distinction behind this is set out in STRUCTURE_AND_COMPARABLES.md Section 1.1.

The mitigation is to maintain a healthy mix of broad-base grants, small donations, and earned revenue, and to actively build a small-donor base, with Score Watch subscribers as the pipeline. No single source should dominate revenue. Transparency International's "no donor above 10 percent" norm happens to serve both the public support test and the independence mission at once. If earned revenue becomes structurally dominant over time, the decision point is to apply for 509(a)(2) classification, which is supported by program-service revenue, rather than 509(a)(1). That is a question to revisit with a CPA once the Year 1 revenue mix is known.

## 12. Open Decisions for the Founder

These are the choices Phil must make to move from planning to execution. None can be delegated to an agent, and all are time-sensitive.

**Decision 1: Jurisdiction of incorporation.** Delaware is the standard for nonprofits that need legal credibility and well-developed case law. Incorporating in the home state avoids foreign-registration overhead if that is where the organization operates. California offers strong anti-SLAPP protection, which carries real weight given the defamation risk of scoring named entities. Recommendation: put this question to the nonprofit attorney first, because it shapes everything downstream.

**Decision 2: Fiscal-sponsor selection.** Which of the shortlisted sponsors (Code for Science & Society, Players Philanthropy Fund, Social Good Fund, Anti-Entropy) best fits the first-funder pipeline? Anti-Entropy and Social Good Fund sit closest to the AI-safety and civic-infrastructure funder community (SFF, LTFF, Schmidt). This decision is needed within four weeks, because the fiscal-sponsor agreement must be signed before the first grant applications go out.

**Decision 3: Board recruits (3+ independent directors).** This is the gating step for formation. Phil needs three specific people committed in writing before Articles can be filed with any credibility. The recommended profiles are a welfare or ethics researcher, a nonprofit-governance or legal expert, and a data-methods or AI researcher. Who, specifically, are these people? This is the hardest decision and the one that should start immediately.

**Decision 4: First funder targets and submission sequence.** Which of the SFF Speculation Grant, LTFF, Schmidt Multi-Agent Safety, and Fast Forward goes first, and in what order? The Schmidt deadline of August 8, 2026 is the hardest constraint. SFF and LTFF should go in parallel as the fastest cash. Fast Forward opens July 30. This needs a personal submission-calendar decision within two weeks; the full calendar is in GRANT_MODEL.md Section 6.

**Decision 5: Legal entity name and branding.** The legal entity name does not have to match the brand name. The options are "Compassion Benchmark, Inc." (clean and direct), a more descriptive name such as "Institute for Institutional Compassion Measurement, Inc." (more legible as a 501(c)(3) to the IRS), or a neutral holding name with "Compassion Benchmark" as a DBA. The name appears on all grant applications, the IRS filing, and every state registration. Decide before Articles are drafted.

**Decision 6: Nonprofit-attorney engagement.** Given the earned-revenue complexity (UBIT, the commerciality doctrine) and the defamation risk profile, professional review of the Articles, bylaws, independence clause, and Form 1023 narrative is strongly recommended. Budget $1,500 to $5,000. This is a binary decision: engage, or proceed DIY.
