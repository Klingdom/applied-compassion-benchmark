## 1. Grant Strategy (One Page)

### The Core Constraint
The majority of the best-known foundations — Ford, MacArthur, Open Society, Omidyar, Siegel, Rockefeller — are strictly invite-only or relationship-gated. Cold applications to them will not work and are not worth effort in Year 1. These foundations find you through your published work, citations, and field presence. They are the destination, not the starting point.

### The Opportunity
The AI-safety/longtermist regranting ecosystem (SFF, LTFF, Manifund), Schmidt Sciences' open RFPs, and tech-nonprofit accelerators (Fast Forward) are open-application, accept fiscal-sponsored or non-501(c)(3) applicants, and can move in days to weeks. This is where Year 1 grant capital comes from.

### Three-Phase Strategy

**Phase A — Start fast and applicable (Months 0–3).** Target only funders that accept applications now, accept fiscally-sponsored or non-501(c)(3) status, and decide quickly. Primary applications: SFF Speculation Grant, EA LTFF, Schmidt Multi-Agent Safety (Aug 8 deadline). These three alone, if successful, can cover the full Year 1 operating budget.

**Phase B — Build track record and field presence (Months 2–6).** Apply to Fast Forward (capacity accelerator; Jul 30–Sep 8 window). Publish 1–2 citable artifacts (methodology paper, special briefing). Recruit an advisory board. These actions simultaneously build the grant pipeline for Phase C and generate the field presence that invite-only funders notice.

**Phase C — Mid-size thematic grants (Months 4–12).** With a fiscal sponsor in place and a 6-month track record, approach Knight Foundation (open calls), Sloan (2-page LOI), McGovern Data Practice Accelerator, and DRK Foundation (early-stage framing). These represent the $75k–$400k/grant range.

**Phase D — Aspirational funder cultivation (Months 6–18, parallel, low-intensity).** Submit an unsolicited concept note to Open Philanthropy. Contact Ford/Omidyar/Siegel program officers with published work. Watch for Current AI / Humanity AI / MacArthur open mechanisms. Do not invest cold-application effort here — invest in being citable.

### The Flywheel
Early grant wins → proof of field engagement → advisory board → track record → mid-size grants → published impact metrics → invite-only funder relationships. Each phase depends on the previous. Start Phase A immediately.

### Operational Discipline
Maintain a funder CRM (status, deadline, contact, framing used last). Build a reusable module library (alternate versions of key proposal sections in each funder framing: safety / accountability / data-public-good / wellbeing). Keep an always-current metrics dashboard so any application can be assembled in days. Treat grant pursuit like the nightly pipeline — a repeatable loop, not a sprint.

## 3. First Three Applications: Mini Action Plans

### Application 1: SFF Speculation Grant

**Why first:** Speculation Grants are decided in approximately one week, require no 501(c)(3), accept global pre-exempt applicants, and are designed for high-upside, underexplored bets — exactly the framing a new independent benchmark institution plays into. They are the fastest possible path to grant capital and initial field credibility.

**Funder framing:** Lead with AI/robotics-lab scoring and institutional resilience. SFF's stated focus on "long-term survival and flourishing of sentient life" maps directly to an organization that scores how labs building potentially transformative AI treat human suffering. Position Compassion Benchmark as measurement infrastructure for the field — a public good that reduces uncertainty about how AI labs are behaving on dimensions that matter for long-run outcomes.

**Key argument:** An independent, automated, reproducible score for 50 AI labs and 50 robotics labs — updated nightly, publicly accessible, with a documented independence policy and weekly integrity audits — is the kind of field infrastructure that SFF funds. The cost-per-entity-scored is exceptionally low due to the automated pipeline: this is high leverage per dollar.

**Action steps:**
1. Verify the current Speculation Grant window is open at survivalandflourishing.fund (windows move; confirm before drafting).
2. Sign the Model C fiscal sponsor agreement first — SFF does not require a 501(c)(3), but a sponsor removes uncertainty about charitable routing of funds.
3. Write a 500–1,000 word application using the master template in §4 below, leading with the AI/robotics-lab scoring and institutional resilience framing. Use Section 5 (Independence Statement) verbatim.
4. Attach: methodology link, one sample entity assessment, one integrity-audit report.
5. Submit. Expect a decision within approximately one week.

**Ask:** $50k–$150k for 12 months of general operating support.

**Critical dependency:** Fiscal sponsor agreement must be signed before applying.

---

### Application 2: EA Long-Term Future Fund (LTFF)

**Why second (in parallel with SFF):** LTFF is rolling, fast (2–4 week turnaround), small-team-friendly, and explicitly funds AI governance and safety — including infrastructure and field-building work that makes the broader effort more tractable. It draws from a different reviewer pool than SFF and can be submitted simultaneously without conflict.

**Funder framing:** Lead with "improving global governance of transformative technologies, particularly AI." Emphasize: (a) the automated pipeline's scalability and reproducibility — a single founder can continuously monitor 50 AI labs because the pipeline does the evidence scanning; (b) this is the only comprehensive, independent benchmark scoring AI labs on compassion/suffering-reduction dimensions; (c) the independence model is structurally enforced, not founder-dependent.

**Key argument:** LTFF frequently funds work where reviewers think "this should exist and if it doesn't, the field is missing infrastructure." A nightly-updated, 50-lab AI accountability benchmark that no one else operates, run by a solo founder with an automated pipeline, at near-zero marginal cost per lab, is that kind of work.

**Action steps:**
1. Write a 1–2 hour application via funds.effectivealtruism.org/apply-for-funding (the LTFF process is designed to be fast and lightweight).
2. Use the master template in §4 below, trimmed to fit LTFF's format — their applications are shorter and less formal than Schmidt or Knight.
3. Emphasize: automated pipeline as scalable infrastructure; AI/robotics-lab scoring; field-building value; structural independence policy.
4. Attach: methodology link, sample score update, one integrity-audit report.
5. Submit in parallel with SFF — they do not conflict.

**Ask:** $50k–$100k for 12 months.

**Critical dependency:** None. LTFF does not require a fiscal sponsor or 501(c)(3).

### Application 3: Schmidt Sciences — Scaling AI Safety for a Multi-Agent World

**Why third (hardest deadline):** The Schmidt Multi-Agent Safety RFP has a fixed deadline of August 8, 2026 — the hardest constraint in the near-term calendar. It offers Tier 1 grants up to $300k plus compute/API credits that directly subsidize the evidence-scanning pipeline. The thematic fit is the strongest of any funder: robotics-lab scoring maps precisely to "multi-agent world" oversight.

**Funder framing:** Lead with the multi-agent safety angle — robotics labs are the quintessential multi-agent deployment context. Compassion Benchmark's 50-robotics-lab scoring provides an independent, external, standardized accountability layer for a sector with no comparable measurement. Schmidt explicitly values evaluation science and independent oversight mechanisms. The compute/API credits requested would directly lower the per-cycle evidence-scanning cost.

**Key argument:** Compassion Benchmark is already running the only external, independent, automated benchmark scoring robotics labs on compassion/suffering-reduction dimensions. The pipeline exists; the grant scales and sustains it. This is evaluation infrastructure, not a research prototype.

**Action steps:**
1. Immediately verify that a fiscally-sponsored nonprofit project qualifies as a "non-profit research org" for this RFP — FUNDER_LANDSCAPE_AND_GRANT_MODEL.md flags this as a [VERIFY] item; confirm before investing proposal time.
2. Write the full Schmidt proposal using the master template extended to Schmidt's required format (see their full application instructions at aisecurityandsafety.org/en/grants/schmidt-sciences-ai-safety/).
3. Emphasize: existing pipeline operation (not a prototype); 50-robotics-lab and 50-AI-lab coverage; independence policy and weekly audit trail; evaluation-science framing; compute credits request.
4. Target Tier 1 ($150k–$300k, 12 months). Optionally frame a Tier 2 ($300k–$750k, 2 years) version if eligibility is confirmed.
5. Submit before August 8, 2026. No flexibility on this deadline.

**Ask:** $150k–$300k for 12 months (Tier 1); optionally $300k–$750k over 2 years (Tier 2).

**Critical dependencies:** Fiscal sponsor agreement for eligibility; eligibility verification with Schmidt staff.

> Compassion Benchmark is an independent, AI-native benchmark institution that measures how 1,155 institutions recognize, respond to, and reduce suffering — and makes the scores public.

### Section 2: Problem / Need Statement

**Core text (use this as the base):**
Institutions face no independent, comparable, standardized measurement of how they recognize, respond to, and reduce human suffering. Existing accountability indices — ESG ratings, human-rights scorecards, AI ethics evaluations — are narrow, siloed, capturable by the entities they rate, or behind paywalls that limit their public-interest function. AI and robotics laboratories, among the most consequential actors of this decade, face virtually no external accountability scoring on these dimensions. The result: institutions manage reputational optics without measurable accountability, and no shared vocabulary exists for comparing institutional behavior across sectors.

**Swap module — Safety framing (SFF / LTFF / Schmidt):**
AI and robotics labs operate without a standardized, independent external measure of how they treat human welfare — a gap in the oversight infrastructure for transformative AI at the moment when that infrastructure is most needed.

**Swap module — Accountability / democracy framing (Knight / Mozilla):**
Institutional accountability for human-welfare outcomes is measured inconsistently and unsystematically, weakening civil-society oversight of governments, corporations, and AI labs that shape conditions for billions of people.

**Swap module — Data-public-good framing (Sloan / McGovern):**
There is no public-domain, reproducible, cross-sector measurement standard for institutional treatment of suffering — a gap in the scientific and journalistic infrastructure for welfare research and accountability reporting.

---

### Section 3: Our Solution

**Core text:**
Compassion Benchmark operates a transparent 8-dimension / 40-subdimension methodology applied uniformly across 1,155 entities — governments, corporations, AI labs, robotics labs, cities. A nightly automated evidence-scanning and assessment pipeline continuously updates evidence records. Rankings are published free and publicly accessible. The full methodology and scoring rubric are published. Score-change accountability events are documented. An integrity-audit pipeline runs weekly and results are committed publicly.

The core operational innovation: this is automated, reproducible, and founder-independent. It runs at scale on a near-solo budget and generates the evidence trail that makes scores defensible to any scrutiny.

**Guidance:** Include a direct link to the live site. Attach one sample entity assessment as an appendix. Funders can see the pipeline in production — use this advantage.

---

### Section 4: Why Now

**Core text:**
AI and robotics deployment is accelerating; public-interest AI accountability has become a top philanthropic priority (Current AI, Humanity AI, Schmidt Sciences, McGovern Foundation have each made major 2026 commitments). An independent, automatable, cross-sector scorer of this kind is both timely and uniquely feasible — the automated pipeline eliminates the labor bottleneck that has historically made comprehensive independent benchmarking prohibitively expensive for a small organization.

### Section 5: Independence Statement

**Do not abbreviate or move to an appendix.** Include verbatim in the body of every application. Funders in this space will ask — proactively and in writing — "Can a lab buy a better score?"

> Compassion Benchmark is strictly independent. Entities never pay for inclusion in the benchmark, changes to their scores, or suppression of findings. Commercial revenue (research assets, institutional interpretation services) supports access and understanding — not scoring outcomes. This policy is embedded in the organization's bylaws at a supermajority-amendment threshold and enforced by an independent board. A written conflict-of-interest and funding-acceptance policy prohibits any scored entity from being a material funder. All funders above $1,000 are disclosed publicly. Scoring decisions are made by the research staff and pipeline, independent of any donor or external party.

## 5. Theory of Change and Impact Metrics

### Theory of Change (Shareable / Submittable Version)

**Problem:** Major institutions — governments, corporations, AI labs, robotics labs — face no standardized, independent, comparable measurement of how they recognize, respond to, and reduce human suffering. Without measurement, accountability is impossible.

**Intervention:** An independent, automated, publicly accessible benchmark institution scoring 1,155 entities across 8 dimensions and 40 subdimensions, using a transparent published methodology, with nightly evidence updates and weekly integrity audits. Strictly independent: entities never pay for inclusion, score changes, or suppression.

**Causal pathway:**

```
INPUTS                     ACTIVITIES                       OUTPUTS
Founder + pipeline    →    Nightly evidence scanning     →  1,155 entities scored
Methodology                Reassessments                    Public rankings
Compute / API              Integrity audits                 Score-change events logged
Fiscal sponsor             Publishing + digests             Quarterly impact reports
                           Education + interpretation       Cited, public methodology
                                      |
                                      v
SHORT-TERM OUTCOMES:
Journalists, researchers, policymakers have a shared, citable measurement
standard. Institutional coverage is visible and comparable across sectors.
                                      |
                                      v
MEDIUM-TERM OUTCOMES:
Institutions respond to score movements. Third parties cite scores in
oversight, reporting, and policy. Score Watch community grows. Score-change
accountability events become a documented record of institutional response.
                                      |
                                      v
LONG-TERM IMPACT:
Measurable shifts in institutional behavior toward recognizing, responding
to, and reducing suffering. Compassion Benchmark scores enter standard
due-diligence vocabulary for institutional assessment.
```

**Key assumptions (stated, not hidden — funders reward honesty):**
1. Transparency drives accountability — institutions respond to visible comparative measurement.
2. Independence is preserved and trusted — the structural firewall holds; the brand remains credible.
3. The automated pipeline produces credible, defensible scores at scale.
4. Third parties cite and embed scores in consequential contexts (journalism, policy, oversight).

**Year 1 target:** $150k–$300k from 2–4 funders. The realistic combination is SFF/LTFF for fast cash ($100k–$200k combined) plus Schmidt for size ($150k–$300k). Fast Forward provides capacity value beyond the cash.

**Year 2 target:** $300k–$600k from 3–6 funders. Add Knight, Sloan, McGovern, DRK renewals and new applications. Renew SFF/LTFF if Year 1 metrics are strong.

### Budget Narrative (Line-Item Justifications for Applications)

**Founder compensation ($120k–$180k/yr):** Full-time Executive Director operating the benchmark institution, managing the automated pipeline, maintaining the methodology, drafting reports, and pursuing additional funding. Below market rate for an equivalent research-director or data-science role at a nonprofit of comparable scope. Justification for the pipeline-efficiency argument: without the automation, this work would require a research team of 4–6 people; the founder's investment in building the automation is what makes the per-entity cost tractable.

**Automated pipeline compute + LLM/API ($20k–$45k/yr):** Nightly evidence-scanning and preliminary scoring across 1,155 entities relies on LLM inference for evidence extraction. This cost scales sub-linearly with entity count — it is core infrastructure, not overhead. Schmidt Sciences' API/compute credits, if awarded, would offset $10k–$20k of this line annually.

**Hosting / infrastructure / domains / tooling ($5k–$10k/yr):** VPS hosting (Hostinger), Cloudflare Workers for badge and subscriber API, domain registration, development tooling. Already operational; grant covers continuation of existing infrastructure.

**Part-time research/editorial contractor ($0–$40k/yr):** Phased in at Month 3–6, once initial funding is secured. Approximately 10 hours/week for research verification, editorial review of digests, and pipeline quality assurance. At $40–$60/hr for a research-qualified contractor.

**Legal / accounting / external integrity audits ($5k–$15k/yr):** CPA for annual Form 990, UBIT review, and public-support-test tracking; nonprofit attorney for governance document review; external methodology peer review (annually). These are compliance and quality-assurance costs — not general overhead — and are directly required to maintain the independence and tax-exempt status that makes this organization grantable.

**Fiscal sponsor fee ($5–10% of grants received):** The fiscal sponsor charges a standard fee for providing 501(c)(3) status, back-office compliance, and financial reporting during the IRS determination period. This is normal practice and is disclosed to all funders in the budget. Once the standalone 501(c)(3) determination is received, this line drops to zero.

**Insurance ($3k–$8k/yr):** Directors and Officers liability insurance and media liability insurance covering defamation defense costs. Non-optional for an organization that publishes comparative scores of named governments, corporations, and AI laboratories. Cheap relative to the cost of a single meritless but expensive SLAPP suit.

**Total annual operating need: approximately $155k–$300k.** At the low end (solo, no contractor, lower founder compensation): $155k. At the full target (founder at $180k + part-time contractor + full compliance stack): $300k. The Year 1 grant asks from SFF + LTFF + Schmidt, if all successful, cover the full range.
