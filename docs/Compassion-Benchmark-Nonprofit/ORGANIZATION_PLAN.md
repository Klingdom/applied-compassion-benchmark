## 1. Executive Summary

### The Case for Converting

Compassion Benchmark already operates with the independence model that defines the strongest organizations in this category: it publishes free comparative rankings of named entities and never lets a scored entity pay for inclusion, score changes, or suppression of findings. The research found this is universal across 16 comparable organizations studied — it is the category standard, not a constraint unique to this organization.

The case for becoming a nonprofit is not about changing the model. It is about making the model structurally durable across three dimensions.

**Independence becomes legally locked, not policy-dependent.** As a for-profit solo venture, the independence policy is enforced only by founder discipline and reputation. As a 501(c)(3), it can be embedded in the Articles of Incorporation and bylaws at a supermajority-amendment threshold — making it harder to undo than any personal commitment and creating fiduciary obligations on every director to uphold it. A scored entity that wanted to influence a finding would be not just off-policy but acting against a legally enforceable organizational purpose.

**Grant capital becomes accessible immediately.** The majority of foundations that fund AI accountability, transparency, and institutional-measurement work cannot legally grant to a for-profit entity without extraordinary due diligence ("expenditure responsibility"). A 501(c)(3) — or even a fiscal-sponsorship bridge to one — unlocks Tier 1 fast-track grants in the AI-safety ecosystem (SFF, LTFF, Schmidt) within weeks, and positions the organization for mid-size grants ($100k–$500k) and aspirational multi-year grants ($500k–$2M) as a track record accumulates.

**The organization becomes founder-independent and institutionally durable.** The automated evidence-scanning and assessment pipeline is already an institutional asset that reduces key-person risk. A nonprofit board, a published methodology, and a fiscal structure that survives Phil make Compassion Benchmark something that can outlast its founder — which is the condition for long-term citation, policy relevance, and field trust.

**The existing revenue model is fully preservable.** Selling research assets and institutional interpretation services is defensible as "substantially related" educational program activity under 501(c)(3) UBIT rules — analogous to a university selling course materials or a think tank selling datasets. The ProPublica Data Store and Bellingcat training revenue are direct precedents. The transition does not require abandoning Gumroad or commercial services; it requires framing them as educational program outputs and keeping core rankings free and public.

## 2. Mission, Vision, and Charitable Purpose

### Mission
Compassion Benchmark measures how institutions recognize, respond to, and reduce suffering — and makes the scores public.

### Vision
A world in which the treatment of human suffering by governments, corporations, AI laboratories, robotics labs, and other institutions is measurably, comparably, and publicly accountable.

### Charitable Purpose Statement (501(c)(3)-Exemption-Ready Language)

The organization is organized and operated exclusively for educational and scientific purposes within the meaning of Section 501(c)(3) of the Internal Revenue Code, specifically to:

- educate the public by researching, developing, and freely publishing transparent, independent, comparative benchmarks measuring how institutions across sectors recognize, respond to, and reduce human suffering;
- advance the scientific understanding of institutional behavior as it relates to human welfare, including through the development and public disclosure of reproducible measurement methodologies;
- support journalists, researchers, policymakers, and civil society in interpreting and applying comparative institutional-suffering measurements for the public benefit; and
- provide related educational materials, training, and tools that help institutions understand and improve their recognition and reduction of suffering.

**Why this language satisfies the IRS:** It fits the "educational" and "scientific" purposes under IRC 501(c)(3) and Treas. Reg. 1.501(c)(3)-1(d)(3). Publishing comparative rankings of named entities educates the public — analogous to *Consumer Reports*, ProPublica, and Freedom House, each a 501(c)(3). Developing and disclosing a reproducible methodology advances scientific understanding. The purpose expressly excludes pay-for-score by tying every activity to the *public benefit* of the research, not the interests of scored entities.

## 3. Theory of Change

### Problem
There is no independent, comparable, standardized measurement system for how major institutions treat human suffering. Existing accountability indices (ESG ratings, human-rights rankings, AI ethics scorecards) are narrow, siloed, capturable by the entities being rated, or behind paywalls. AI and robotics laboratories — among the most consequential actors of this decade — face virtually no standardized external accountability scoring on these dimensions. The result: institutions manage reputational optics without measurable accountability, and no shared vocabulary exists for comparing institutional behavior across sectors.

### Activities
- Operate a nightly automated evidence-scanning and assessment pipeline across all 1,155 scored entities
- Maintain and publish the full 8-dimension / 40-subdimension methodology and rubric
- Publish free comparative rankings and periodic score updates
- Conduct and publish integrity audits (weekly automated; periodic external)
- Publish daily/periodic research digests and special briefings
- Provide educational interpretation services (research assets, institutional briefings, self-assessment tools)
- Operate the Score Watch subscriber alert system

### Outputs
- 1,155 entities scored across 8 dimensions / 40 subdimensions
- Publicly accessible rankings and methodology documentation
- A continuous evidence record with documented score-change accountability events
- Research assets and interpretation tools available to subscribers and institutional buyers
- Quarterly public impact reports

### Outcomes (Short to Medium Term)
- Journalists, researchers, policymakers, and advocates gain a shared, citable measurement standard for institutional treatment of suffering
- Institutions respond to score movements — publicly referencing or defending their scores
- Third parties cite or embed scores in oversight reporting, policy analysis, and institutional communications
- A subscriber and user base generates sustained demand for the measurement standard

### Impact (Long Term)
- A measurable shift in institutional behavior: AI/robotics labs, governments, and corporations adopt more accountable, compassionate practices because doing so is measurable and publicly visible
- Compassion Benchmark scores enter the standard due-diligence vocabulary for institutional assessment, analogous to Freedom House democracy scores or the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index

### Key Assumptions (Stated Explicitly)
1. Transparency drives accountability — institutions respond to public comparative measurement
2. Independence is preserved and trusted — the structural firewall holds and is credibly communicated
3. The automated pipeline produces credible, defensible scores at scale
4. Third parties cite and embed the scores in consequential contexts

## 4. Programs and Activity Lines

### Program 1: The Public Benchmark
The core educational output. Free, publicly accessible comparative rankings of 1,155 entities across 8 dimensions and 40 subdimensions. The methodology is fully published. Scoring decisions are traceable to documented evidence.

**Fundability argument:** Pure public good — no paywall, no pay-to-play, openly reproducible. Foundations fund this as infrastructure for the broader field.

**Boundary condition:** Core rankings are free and always will be. No scored entity pays for any aspect of its score. This is not negotiable and is encoded in the bylaws (see §6).

### Program 2: The Evidence Pipeline
The operational engine. Nightly automated evidence scanning across all scored entities, automated preliminary assessments, periodic comprehensive reassessments, weekly integrity audits, and documented score-change events.

**Why this is an institutional asset:** The pipeline's automation reduces per-entity cost to near-zero at scale, enabling coverage (1,155 entities) that no manual research team could replicate at this cost base. This is the durability argument for funders: it can outlast any individual person.

### Program 3: Education and Interpretation
Helping journalists, researchers, policymakers, and institutions understand and use the benchmark. Includes: published methodology papers and special briefings; the Score Watch alert system; public-facing data tools and API access; institutional briefings; training on applying the methodology; self-assessment tools.

**Charitable framing:** Publication alone is insufficient — the organization must also educate on how to interpret and act on the scores.

### Program 4: Related Earned-Revenue Program
The formalized commercial arm, structured as educational program activity to remain "substantially related" to the exempt purpose under UBIT rules. Includes: tiered research datasets and reports (Gumroad / Data Store model); institutional interpretation and benchmarking services; custom analysis and briefings for institutional buyers.

**UBIT boundary conditions (full analysis in STRUCTURE_AND_COMPARABLES.md §1.4):**
- Core rankings and methodology are always free and public
- Commercial products are *derived from* the benchmark (depth, customization, access tooling), not the benchmark itself
- Pricing is mission-aware: tiered for nonprofits/researchers/press, consistent with the ProPublica Data Store model — not revenue-maximizing
- Earned revenue remains incidental and subordinate to the charitable purpose
- File Form 990-T on any genuinely unrelated lines; do not allow this program to become the dominant activity
- If commercial revenue scales significantly: evaluate a wholly-owned taxable subsidiary (the Mozilla structure) at that point, not at formation

## 5. Recommended Legal Structure and Formation Path

### Primary Recommendation: US 501(c)(3) Public Charity

Full analysis in STRUCTURE_AND_COMPARABLES.md §1.1–1.6. The rationale in brief:

**Why public charity (not private foundation):** The intended revenue mix — grants, earned revenue, small donations — maps to the public support test, and the required independent-board-majority is itself an anti-capture mechanism. Public charity is the form chosen by every close US analogue: ProPublica, Access Now, AI Now Institute, Freedom House, Epoch AI.

**Why US (not European charitable form):** Funding, audience, and funder infrastructure are primarily US-based. US 501(c)(3) unlocks the broadest donor-deduction profile. The "independent American institute" identity is clearest.

**Why a related earned-revenue program now (not a subsidiary):** At current revenue scale, the existing Gumroad/services revenue is defensible as substantially related educational activity. The taxable-subsidiary structure (Mozilla model) adds complexity without benefit at formation — evaluate it only if commercial revenue grows beyond what "related" program activity can comfortably cover.

### Bridge: Model C Fiscal Sponsorship

During the ~3–6 month IRS Form 1023 review period, a Model C fiscal sponsor provides immediate ability to receive tax-deductible grants and donations, back-office compliance support, and organizational credibility for early funder conversations. This is a bridge, not a permanent home. See STRUCTURE_AND_COMPARABLES.md §1.2 for full model comparison.

**Model C (pre-approved grant relationship) preferred over Model A:** You keep your own entity and control; the sponsor re-grants funds to you. Fee: typically 4–10%. The Ranking Digital Rights cautionary tale (three sponsors over its life) shows the risk of over-dependence on a sponsor — treat this as a time-limited bridge to standalone 501(c)(3) status.

**Sponsor shortlist to evaluate (match to first-funder pipeline; see GRANT_MODEL.md §3):**

| Sponsor | Thematic Fit | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Code for Science & Society** | Data/research/open-science; public-interest data | Strong fit; has sponsored PREreview, Dat Project |
| **Players Philanthropy Fund** | General-purpose; turnkey and efficient | Fast to set up; low friction |
| **Social Good Fund** | AI/civic-infrastructure projects | Good fit for AI-safety funder pipeline |
| **Anti-Entropy** | Hosts AI-safety projects | Most compatible with SFF/LTFF pipeline specifically |

| Step | Action | Timeline | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Choose incorporation state; verify name availability | Week 1 | $0 |
| 2 | Draft Articles of Incorporation (501(c)(3) purpose + dissolution language + independence clause) | Week 1–2 | $0 DIY / $500–$2k with attorney |

**Why full Form 1023, not 1023-EZ:** Compassion Benchmark will project >$50k in combined grant + earned revenue, which disqualifies the EZ option. More importantly, the full Form 1023 allows description and pre-clearance of the earned-revenue model and independence safeguards with the IRS — reducing later UBIT/commerciality ambiguity. This extra work is worth doing.

**Total formation cost:** ~$700–$1,000 DIY; ~$2,500–$6,000 with nonprofit attorney review (recommended given earned-revenue complexity and defamation risk profile).

**Realistic planning-to-operational timeline:** 2–8 months, with fiscal sponsorship bridging the gap.

## 6. Governance and Independence Architecture

Independence is the centerpiece of this organization's entire value proposition. This section makes independence structurally durable — not a founder's policy statement but a legal guarantee that survives Phil, survives funder pressure, and survives scale.

### Board Composition

| Seat | Profile | Selection Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Founder / initial Chair (Phil) | Executive Director initially; may Chair or recuse | Continuity; institutional knowledge; recuses from independence-related votes |
| Director 2 | Ethicist, welfare researcher, or suffering/wellbeing expert | Subject-matter credibility; independence signal to field |
| Director 3 | Nonprofit-governance or legal expert | Compliance; COI enforcement; fiduciary rigor |
| Director 4 (recommended) | Data-methods, AI/ML researcher, or measurement scientist | Methodology credibility; scoring defensibility |
| Director 5 (recommended) | Journalist, transparency advocate, or accountability practitioner | Editorial-independence signal; field legitimacy |

**Design principles (sourced from comparables in STRUCTURE_AND_COMPARABLES.md §2.2):**
- Majority must be unrelated to each other and to Phil (IRS public-charity requirement and the primary anti-capture signal)
- No director may be connected to a scored entity (conflict-of-interest policy below)
- 2-year staggered terms prevent wholesale board turnover
- AI Now's non-industry board is the model: choose for independence credibility, not industry ties or fundraising Rolodex

**External Methodology Advisory Board (separate from the fiduciary board):**
- 3–5 credentialed experts (welfare economists, human-rights measurement scholars, AI governance researchers)
- Volunteer or small honorarium; no fiduciary liability
- Annual methodology review; public attestation of findings
- Modeled on Stanford FMTI's 5-person advisory board: cheap to run, high legitimacy output
- Strengthens grant applications and neutralizes bias allegations

### The Independence Bylaw (The Legal Lock)

The following clause — or substantively equivalent language drafted by counsel — must appear in the bylaws and require a **two-thirds supermajority of the full board** to amend:

> *The organization shall never accept payment from any entity in exchange for, or as a condition of, inclusion in a benchmark, a change to a published score, or suppression of findings. This policy may not be modified, suspended, or waived except by an affirmative vote of at least two-thirds of all directors then in office, and any such amendment must be disclosed publicly within 30 days of adoption.*

**Why a supermajority lock matters:** A scored entity that wanted to buy a score change would need to compromise a supermajority of an independent board — not just influence the founder. The mandatory 30-day public disclosure creates a reputational trip wire on any amendment attempt.

### Conflict-of-Interest Policy

Standard IRS-expected elements (Form 1023 asks about COI policy):
- Annual written disclosure by all directors and officers of material financial interests and relationships
- Recusal from any vote, deliberation, or conversation where a conflict exists
- Extended explicitly to **funder conflicts**: a director or major donor connected to a scored entity must disclose and recuse from anything touching that entity's score or coverage

### Funding-Acceptance and Firewall Policy

Assembled from the best comparable clauses (detailed in STRUCTURE_AND_COMPARABLES.md §1.5 and §3.3). This policy is written, board-adopted, and published on the website — not an internal memo.

| Rule | Comparable Source | Strength Level |
|---|---|---|
| No scored entity may be a material funder; ideally no scored entity at all | AI Now Institute (categorical corporate refusal) | Strongest: bright-line |
| No single funder exceeds 10% of annual revenue | Transparency International | Diversification norm |
| "We can criticize our funders" — no funding relationship constrains findings about the funder | Bellingcat (explicit written clause) | Written commitment |
| Scoring decisions are made by research staff independent of any donor or external party | Freedom House flagship-index model | Editorial firewall |
| All individual and institutional funders above $1,000 disclosed publicly | TI (~€1k threshold) | Transparency commitment |
| Reverse due diligence on funders above $5,000 before accepting | Bellingcat (~€5k threshold) | Anti-capture screening |
| Grants for core scoring work must be general-operating / unrestricted in nature | AI Now (no earmarked grants that shape findings) | Prevents project-specific capture |

### Methodological Capture-Resistance (The Strongest Safeguard)

Drawn from V-Dem and Stanford FMTI: independence engineered into the method, not just the bylaws. No single person's judgment moves a number quietly.

- Full rubric, weights, and evidence sources are published
- Scoring decisions are rules-based and logged against the rubric
- Evidence records are retained and auditable
- Score-change events are documented with rationale and published
- Integrity audits run weekly (already operational via `research/scripts/integrity-check.mjs`)
- External advisory board reviews methodology annually and publicly attests to findings

**The reproducibility test:** If Phil were replaced today, could a successor reproduce any score from the published methodology and evidence record? The answer must be yes. This is the durability standard.

### Public Support Test Management

The 501(c)(3) public-charity classification requires passing the public support test over a rolling 5-year window (generally ≥1/3 of support from the broad public). Key points and risks:

- **Risk:** If earned revenue from large institutional contracts or Gumroad volume dominates, portions may not count as "public support," and a single large grant may be capped at 2% for test purposes. See STRUCTURE_AND_COMPARABLES.md §1.1 for the 509(a)(1) vs. 509(a)(2) distinction.
- **Mitigation:** Maintain a healthy mix: broad-base grants + small donations + earned revenue. Actively build a small-donor base (Score Watch subscribers as the pipeline). No single source should dominate revenue.
- **Rule of thumb:** TI's "no donor > 10%" norm serves both the public support test and the independence mission simultaneously.
- **CPA decision point:** If earned revenue is structurally dominant over time, apply for 509(a)(2) classification (supported by program-service revenue) instead of 509(a)(1). This is a decision to revisit with a CPA once the Year 1 revenue mix is known.

## 12. Open Decisions for the Founder

These are the choices Phil must make to move from planning to execution. None can be delegated to an agent. All are time-sensitive.

**Decision 1: Jurisdiction of incorporation**
Delaware is the standard for nonprofits needing legal credibility and well-developed case law. Your home state avoids foreign-registration overhead if you operate there. California offers strong anti-SLAPP protection, which is meaningful given the defamation risk profile of scoring named entities. Recommend: ask the nonprofit attorney this question first — it shapes everything downstream.

**Decision 2: Fiscal sponsor selection**
Which of the shortlisted sponsors (Code for Science & Society, Players Philanthropy Fund, Social Good Fund, Anti-Entropy) best fits the first-funder pipeline? Anti-Entropy and Social Good Fund are the closest to the AI-safety/civic-infrastructure funder community (SFF, LTFF, Schmidt). This decision is needed within 4 weeks because the fiscal sponsor agreement must be signed before submitting the first grant applications.

**Decision 3: Board recruits (3+ independent directors)**
This is the gating step for formation. Phil needs 3 specific people committed in writing before Articles can be filed with credibility. The recommended profiles are: welfare/ethics researcher, nonprofit-governance or legal expert, data-methods or AI researcher. Who are these specific individuals? This is the hardest decision and the one that should start immediately.

**Decision 4: First funder targets and submission sequence**
Which of SFF Speculation Grant, LTFF, Schmidt Multi-Agent Safety, and Fast Forward gets submitted first and in what order? The Schmidt deadline of August 8, 2026 is the hardest constraint. SFF + LTFF should be submitted in parallel as the fastest cash. Fast Forward opens July 30. This requires a personal submission calendar decision within 2 weeks — see GRANT_MODEL.md §6 for the full calendar.

**Decision 5: Legal entity name and branding**
The legal entity name does not need to match the brand name. Options: "Compassion Benchmark, Inc." (clean, direct); a more descriptive name like "Institute for Institutional Compassion Measurement, Inc." (more 501(c)(3)-legible to the IRS); or a neutral holding name with "Compassion Benchmark" as a DBA. The name appears on all grant applications, the IRS filing, and every state registration. Decide before Articles are drafted.

**Decision 6: Nonprofit attorney engagement**
Given the earned-revenue complexity (UBIT, commerciality doctrine) and the defamation risk profile, professional review of the Articles, bylaws, independence clause, and Form 1023 narrative is strongly recommended. Budget $1,500–$5,000. This is a binary decision: engage or proceed DIY.
