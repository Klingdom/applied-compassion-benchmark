## 1. Positioning / The Hook

### The one-line frame

**"This is not US News."**

US News ranks universities on selectivity, reputation, and alumni wealth. This index scores them on how they treat the humans inside them — and the most selective institutions are not the most compassionate ones.

The sharper version for use in copy and social:

> "The most admissions-competitive universities in the world score mediocre on compassion. Harvard, Stanford, and MIT land mid-pack. A Scottish university that launched a slavery-reparations programme tops the table."

This frame is immediately citeable, immediately controversial, and immediately grounded in methodology the benchmark can defend: scored on conduct toward students and staff, not prestige.

---

### Top headline findings (5–8 ready to use)

Each finding traces directly to the distribution provided. Use these verbatim or minimally adapted.

**F1 — The prestige gap (the most shareable)**
"Harvard, Stanford, and MIT are elite by every admissions measure. They score mid-Functional on compassion — the same band as the median university in this index. Selectivity and compassion are not the same thing."

Evidence base: the Functional band (76 of 100) contains the institutions US News ranks at the top; the mean composite is 46.2 and these institutions cluster near it, not above it.

**F2 — Glasgow leads the table (the most citable)**
"The University of Glasgow scores 62.5 — the highest in the index — driven by its formal slavery-reparations programme, the first of its kind at a major research university. No US Ivy League school comes close."

Evidence base: Glasgow 62.5, top score; slavery-reparations programme is the documented driver of elevated Recognition + Response dimensions.

**F3 — The entire sector is mediocre**
"The world's top 100 universities collectively score 46.2 on compassion. No institution reaches Exemplary. Only 3 reach Established. 76 are merely Functional. The institutions responsible for training the world's professionals in ethics, medicine, and governance are themselves mediocre at recognizing, responding to, and reducing suffering."

Evidence base: mean 46.2, median 46.9; 0 Exemplary, 3 Established, 76 Functional, 21 Developing, 0 Critical.

**F4 — The Brown and UC Irvine contrast (equity vs. access)**
"Brown University (60.2) and UC Irvine (60.2) tie for second. Brown earned its score through the first Ivy League graduate worker contract and need-blind admissions. UC Irvine earned its score as a documented social-mobility leader — the highest-scoring public university in the index. Two very different paths to the same compassion outcome."

Evidence base: Brown 60.2, first Ivy grad-worker contract + need-blind; UC Irvine 60.2, access/social-mobility leader.

**F5 — Academic freedom as a floor trigger (the most geopolitically significant)**
"The bottom of the index is occupied by Hong Kong and Mainland Chinese institutions. Shanghai Jiao Tong scores 22.7 — the lowest in the index. Academic freedom suppression and conservative low-confidence scoring are the documented drivers. Institutions that restrict what can be thought cannot score well on an index measuring recognition of suffering."

Evidence base: Hong Kong + Mainland-China institutions at the bottom; Shanghai Jiao Tong 22.7, lowest composite; academic-freedom suppression as documented driver.

**F6 — Utrecht and Princeton: the European and American exemplars (shareable cross-cultural)**
"Utrecht (59.4) and Princeton (57.8) are the strongest continental European and American performers, respectively. Both earn their positions through documented welfare commitments and governance structures — not through reputational prestige rankings. Princeton is the top Ivy, but it scores below Brown by a statistically meaningful margin."

Evidence base: Utrecht 59.4, Princeton 57.8; Brown above Princeton.

**F7 — The developing band is not a scandal, it is a warning**
"21 institutions — one in five — score in the Developing band. These are not obscure universities. They are internationally recognized research institutions. A Developing score means the institution demonstrates only partial recognition of the suffering it generates and partial capacity to respond to it."

Evidence base: 21 Developing; 0 Critical (the floor is not abysmal, but the developing cohort is meaningful).

**F8 — Wealth does not predict care**
"The University Index finds no reliable correlation between institutional wealth (measured by endowment size) and compassion score. The endowment-rich US institutions cluster in the Functional band alongside institutions with a fraction of their resources. Wealth funds capacity. It does not produce compassion."

Evidence base: Harvard/Stanford/MIT (largest US endowments) land mid-Functional; Glasgow, Brown, UCI, Utrecht have far smaller endowments but score materially higher.

## 2. Audience + Channels

### Priority 1 — Higher Education Press (earned media; highest leverage)

**Who:** The Chronicle of Higher Education, Inside Higher Ed, Times Higher Education (THE), Campus Reform, Wonkhe (UK), University Affairs (Canada).

**Why they run this:** The prestige-compassion gap is exactly the kind of finding higher-ed trade press covers as a story, not an advertisement. "Glasgow tops Harvard in a new compassion benchmark" is a headline that writes itself. THE in particular covers international university comparisons as a core beat. Inside Higher Ed covers student welfare, labor relations, and access equity — all directly scored here.

**Acquisition angle:** Direct pitch to reporters on these beats with finding F1 (the prestige gap) and F2 (Glasgow leads). The hook is not "a new index launched" — it is a specific finding they can run as a news item. Offer: a data briefing from Phil, the full index URL, and the independence line ("No university paid for their score. Glasgow did not commission this finding.").

The independence statement is the pitch, not a caveat. Higher-ed press is professionally skeptical of rankings that universities fund or influence. The structural independence of this index is its primary differentiator from THE's own rankings and US News.

**Effort:** S-M. 5-10 personalized pitches. Founder-owned.

### Priority 2 — Student Affairs, Mental Health, and Student Advocacy Organizations

**Who:** NASPA (Student Affairs Administrators), ACPA (College Student Educators), Active Minds (campus mental health), NAMI on Campus chapters, National Association of Graduate-Professional Students (NAGPS), graduate worker union affiliates (UAW 2865, SEIU Local 500, Chicago GSU).

**Why they share this:** These organizations exist because universities systematically fail students and graduate workers. Finding F3 ("76 of 100 universities are merely Functional") validates their organizational purpose. Finding F4 (Brown's grad-worker contract as a scored driver) connects a specific labor victory to a measurable outcome.

**Acquisition angle:** Social sharing and newsletter mention. These organizations do not do press releases — they post findings to their social channels and newsletters when the findings support their advocacy framing. The asset they need is a shareable finding (F3 or F4) plus a URL to the index page. The independence framing matters here too: student advocacy organizations can cite an independent benchmark more credibly than they can cite their own research.

**Channel:** LinkedIn (NASPA, ACPA are active), X/Bluesky (Active Minds, NAGPS), and direct outreach to 3-5 national organizations that have newsletters reaching their membership. These are the NGO/advocacy segment (Segment E in GROWTH_AUDIENCE_2026-06-15.md) applied to the higher-ed vertical.

**Effort:** S. A single launch post with F3 + F4 + index URL is sufficient to seed sharing. No personal relationship required.

### Priority 3 — Faculty, Graduate Union, and Academic Labor Press

**Who:** Faculty Forward, AAUP (American Association of University Professors), Jacobin (academic labor coverage), Labor Notes, Academe Blog, graduate union chapter newsletters.

**Why they engage:** The scoring includes staff and graduate worker treatment as a dimension. **Acquisition angle:** Finding F4 is the academic labor hook. **Channel:** Direct email to AAUP communications. LinkedIn post tagging academic labor accounts. The Jacobin higher-ed beat takes pitches.

**Effort:** S. One targeted pitch to AAUP; one LinkedIn post framed for the academic labor audience.

### Priority 4 — Prospective Students, Parents, and General Interest

**Who:** The engaged public segment (Segment F in GROWTH_AUDIENCE_2026-06-15.md) as applied to university choice. Students researching university options, parents evaluating schools, international students choosing institutions.

**Why they engage:** "Is the university I'm applying to actually good to its students?" is a question every prospective student has. The superlative frame — "most compassionate universities" vs. "least compassionate" — generates organic search traffic and social sharing without requiring any relationship.

**Acquisition angle:** Organic search (students searching "[University Name] compassion score" or "most ethical universities") and social sharing of F1 ("Harvard and Stanford are mid-pack"). This segment generates long-tail entity-page traffic: a student researching Brown or Glasgow will find the entity page and read the score card.

**Channel:** Search (no action required beyond existing entity pages), social (F1 is the most shareable finding for this segment — "Harvard is mediocre at compassion" is a sentence a prospective student will screenshot and share).

**Effort:** Near-zero beyond the index launch itself. The long-tail traffic compounds passively.

## 3. Launch Special Briefing Recommendation

### Recommended title

**"The Prestige–Compassion Gap: What a University Really Owes the People Inside It"**

### Dek (2–3 sentences)

The most admissions-competitive universities in the world score mediocre on compassion. The new Compassion Benchmark University Index scores 100 institutions on how they recognize, respond to, and reduce suffering among students and staff — not on selectivity, research output, or reputation. The result: a Scottish university with a slavery-reparations programme tops the table; Harvard, Stanford, and MIT land mid-pack; and the sector's mean composite of 46.2 suggests that training the world's professionals in medicine, ethics, and governance has not required institutions to model the behavior they teach.

### Rationale for this angle over alternatives

This briefing has four properties that make it the strongest launch-companion for the University Index:

1. **It teaches the core framework distinction.** "Not US News" is the frame, and the briefing is the place to explain why conduct toward internal populations is the right measurement frame — more precisely than the index landing page can do.

2. **It is independently citable.** A journalist who reads this briefing can write: "According to a special briefing from Compassion Benchmark..." and cite a specific finding with a methodology grounding. The prestige-compassion gap finding (F1, F8) is exactly what higher-ed press will excerpt.

3. **It extends the Special Briefing rotation into a new sector.** The existing briefings cover governments, Fortune 500 companies, AI/robotics labs. A university-sector briefing signals that the institution's scope is genuinely cross-sectoral — this is the first briefing that will reach the higher-ed press audience that none of the prior briefings reached.

4. **The finding has narrative pull.** "Glasgow tops Harvard" is a story that moves. The briefing explains why in methodology terms — a more defensible and interesting story than the headline alone.

### Alternative angles considered and rejected

"What Compassion Looks Like on Campus" — too generic; does not foreground the prestige-contrast finding that will earn press pickup.

"Universities and the Suffering They Produce" — accurate but leads with blame framing that undermines the methodology-credibility tone of the briefing series.

"The Access Paradox" — relevant but narrows to a sub-finding (access/social-mobility) when the lead finding is the full prestige gap.

## 4. Independence Framing

### The posture

The independence claim is the distribution asset. When universities react — and elite institutions with communications offices will react — the correct response is to make the independence mechanism visible, not defensive.

Specific language to use in every channel:

> "No university paid for inclusion in this index. No university was informed of their score before publication. No score changed because a university complained. The methodology is public. The evidence for each dimension score is cited."

This sentence belongs in:
- The index page itself (a visible statement, not buried in the methodology)
- Every press pitch
- Every LinkedIn or social post mentioning the index
- The launch Special Briefing's opening paragraph
- Any response to institutional pushback

### Anticipating the reactions

**Reaction type 1 — "Your methodology is flawed" (elite universities)**
Harvard, Stanford, and MIT have communications offices that respond to unflattering rankings. They have argued with US News, QS, and THE. They will potentially argue here.

The correct response: refer to the published methodology. The score is based on documented conduct — not on reputation, not on prestige, not on research output. The scoring is consistent across all 100 institutions. The methodology is public and citable. Any methodology dispute is welcome as a public methodological dialogue — it drives awareness, not credibility erosion.

Do not engage with score-change requests. Do not modify scores in response to institutional communications. If a university provides documented evidence that a scoring decision was based on incorrect information, route it through the normal SBQ process — the same process used for every other entity in every other index.

**Reaction type 2 — "Glasgow paid you" (cynical press)**
The most damaging potential inaccuracy. The counter is structural: explain the independence model on the record. Glasgow did not commission this index, did not pay for inclusion, and was not informed of their score before publication. The slavery-reparations programme is a publicly documented institutional action with a verifiable public record.

**Reaction type 3 — "Chinese institutions are being unfairly targeted" (geopolitical)**
The lowest composite scores are explained by documented academic-freedom suppression, not by national origin. The scoring methodology applies consistently across all institutions. The methodology page and the entity pages are the evidence record. Any institution that disputes a specific evidence item can submit it through the public methodology inquiry channel.

**Earned coverage from pushback**
Institutional pushback generates press coverage. A statement from Harvard's communications office questioning the index is a press event that drives awareness. The correct response is always: "Our methodology is public. Our scores are based on documented conduct. Entities never pay to change their score. We welcome methodological dialogue." This is not defensive; it is the institutional posture of every credible benchmark.

## 5. Measurement

### The 4 metrics to watch for the University Index launch

**M1 — Named press/media citations (primary success metric)**
Target: 3+ named press mentions in higher-ed or general press within 30 days of launch (Chronicle, Inside Higher Ed, THE, or equivalent).
Measurement: Manual tracking (search "[Compassion Benchmark] university" on news aggregators weekly). This is the same metric defined in the annual report launch experiment (GROWTH_ACQUISITION_2026-06-15.md Experiment 6) and the primary signal that the launch is reaching the right audience.
Baseline: 0 prior named mentions in higher-ed press.

**M2 — University Index page sessions and entity-page clickthrough**
Target: University Index page drives entity-page visits at a clickthrough rate of ≥15% (visitors who land on the index page and click through to at least one university entity page).
Measurement: Umami. Specifically: sessions on `/university` index page, sessions on entity pages with referrer `/university`. The entity clickthrough rate tells us whether the index page is doing its job as a discovery surface, not just a landing page.
Baseline: 0 (new index; day-1 baseline established at launch).

**M3 — Newsletter subscriber growth attributable to the University Index launch**
Target: ≥25 new newsletter subscribers in the 14-day window post-launch, attributable to University Index or Special Briefing entry points.
Measurement: Listmonk subscriber source attribution. Entry points to instrument: `source=university-index` (from a subscribe CTA on the index page), `source=university-special-briefing` (from the Special Briefing completion block). Both sources require the `CompletionBlock` subscribe capture (GROWTH_ACQUISITION_2026-06-15.md Experiment 2) to be live at launch — if it is not live, this metric will undercount.
Baseline: trailing 14-day new subscriber count (establish at launch date).

**M4 — University entity page Score-Watch CTA clicks**
Target: ≥10 `score_watch_click` Umami events on university entity pages in the 30-day window post-launch.
Measurement: Umami `score_watch_click` event filtered by `entity_kind=university`. This is the leading indicator for revenue conversion — it tells us whether the audience the University Index attracts is the kind of audience that converts to paid monitoring (student affairs professionals, academic administrators, ESG analysts monitoring university holdings). Even if Score-Watch is still in the contact-sales routing (not the Gumroad live flow), clicks signal intent.
Baseline: 0 (new entity kind).

### Why these 4 and not others

Press citations (M1) are the primary signal because the University Index is a prestige-contrast story — the audience who will spread it is press and institutional press-watchers, not general search traffic. Without press pickup, the index reaches only existing site visitors.

Entity-page clickthrough (M2) tests whether the index page teaches the framework well enough to motivate exploration — the aha moment requires a visitor to land on a specific university's score page, not just the ranking table.

Newsletter attribution (M3) measures whether the new sector expands the subscriber base or just activates existing subscribers. If growth comes entirely from existing subscribers who were already going to subscribe, the University Index is not a new acquisition channel — it is a retention event. The source attribution distinguishes these.

Score-Watch clicks (M4) validate the commercial hypothesis: is the university sector a viable Score-Watch market? The buyer here might be a faculty union researcher tracking their institution's score over time, an ESG analyst at a fund with university bond holdings, or a student affairs administrator benchmarking peer institutions. If clicks materialize, that informs prioritization of university-sector Score-Watch product investment.
