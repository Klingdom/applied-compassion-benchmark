Key findings

Headline findings from the 2026 US Cities Index premium benchmark edition.

Buy Full Report
1No US city reaches Exemplary
Not one of 144 US cities scores above 80. Burlington, Vermont leads at 76.4. Boulder and Fort Collins follow at 63.9. The report argues this is the central finding: American cities have the resources to build higher-compassion governance, but the national ceiling remains self-imposed.
"No American city has built what Helsinki built. Every element is replicable. None of it requires Nordic culture. All of it requires political will."
217 cities score Critical
Seventeen cities fall in the Critical band, reflecting documented governance harm through water crisis, police violence, infrastructure collapse, or systematic service failure. Flint, Jackson, Memphis, Montgomery, and Camden anchor the bottom of the ranking.
"When Flint's children drink lead and nothing changes for a decade, that is not a tragedy. It is a policy choice."
3Wealth predicts little; governance predicts almost everything
San Francisco, Los Angeles, and New York all underperform relative to their resources, while smaller progressive cities outperform major metros. The benchmark argues that Housing First policy, police accountability, and equity-weighted budgeting matter more than size or wealth.
4Equity is the weakest dimension nationally
EQU scores just 2.12 out of 5.0 nationally — the lowest of all 8 dimensions. Cities repeatedly fail on displacement, over-policing, service inequality by race and income, and the inability or unwillingness to place enforceable limits on harm to vulnerable residents.
5Police accountability is the defining fault line
Cities under DOJ consent decrees, pattern-or-practice findings, or repeated police misconduct documentation overwhelmingly score in the lower half. Accountability weakness shows up across Baltimore, Louisville, Cleveland, Chicago, and other cities where institutional oversight failed when it mattered most.
6The South's governance crisis is structural
The Southeast averages 30.0 — the lowest of any US region. Nine of the bottom 20 cities are in the South. The report frames this not as cultural failure, but as the long-run governance consequence of underfunding, preemption, racially structured disinvestment, and extractive policy history.
7Progressive cities show what can scale
Minneapolis, Denver, St. Paul, Eugene, and Houston are highlighted as cities at a turning point. Programs such as STAR, CAHOOTS, Housing First investments, tenant protections, and civilian oversight demonstrate that higher-compassion urban governance is not theoretical — it is already operational in parts of the US.

Methodology summary

Scores use publicly available evidence across housing, public health, police accountability, environmental justice, poverty, equity, infrastructure failure, civic participation, transparency, and long-term systems reform.

Evidence sources include HUD homeless counts and housing data, DOJ civil rights investigations and consent decrees, CDC PLACES, EPA EJScreen, Vera Institute research, Urban Institute poverty analysis, PolicyLink equity indicators, FOIA data, accountability reports, and investigative journalism.
