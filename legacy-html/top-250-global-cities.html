Key findings

Seven headline findings from the 2026 Global City Compassion Index.

Buy Full Report
1Northern Europe Dominates — and Reveals What Is Possible
All 12 Exemplary cities are in Northern or Central Europe. Copenhagen and Helsinki both score 100.0. The shared policy elements are deliberately replicable: Housing First programs that eliminate chronic homelessness (Helsinki is the only EU city to have achieved this), universal healthcare and childcare from age one, tenant protections that prevent displacement-by-development, cycling infrastructure that democratizes mobility across income levels, and institutional anti-corruption with near-zero impunity. These are not accidents of geography or culture. They are the result of sustained policy choices made over decades.
"The cities at the top of this index did not arrive there by accident or luck. Every element of their high score maps onto specific policy decisions that other cities can examine."
2108 Cities Score Critical — the Largest Undercovered Human Rights Story
108 of 250 cities score in the Critical band (0–20), representing hundreds of millions of people living under governance that actively inflicts documented harm through extrajudicial violence, forced displacement, or denial of basic services. Port-au-Prince, Pyongyang, Asmara, and Bangui all score 0.0. This is not primarily a development finding. It is a governance finding — and it represents the largest undercovered human rights story in urban reporting.
"43% is not a developing-world statistic. It is a governance statistic. The question is not whether resources exist. It is whether governance chooses to use them for residents."
3Governance Architecture Matters More Than Wealth
There is no meaningful correlation between GDP per capita and compassion score in this index. Kigali (Rwanda, GDP per capita ~$900) scores 46.9 Functional — above Chicago (33.9) and Los Angeles (33.9) in cities with GDP per capita above $65,000. Gulf cities including Dubai (16.9) and Abu Dhabi (16.9) score Critical-adjacent despite extraordinary oil wealth — because the kafala labor system denies rights to 90% of residents. The variable that most predicts score is governance type: authoritarian systems, military juntas, and kleptocracies concentrate at the bottom regardless of resource level.
"A city can be poor and compassionate. A city can be wealthy and harmful. The difference is not resources. It is choices — specifically, choices about who governance is for."
4The USA Has the Widest Internal Variation of Any Country
American cities range from Boston (57.8, Functional) to Los Angeles (33.9, Developing). No US city reaches Exemplary. Cities with Housing First policies and mental health crisis response score higher. Chronic homelessness, police violence, and racial service gaps drive US scores below their wealth level. The gap between Boston and Los Angeles within a single country reveals more about governance choices than national wealth.
5Boundaries (BND: 2.11/5.0) Is the Weakest Dimension Globally
BND — what cities refuse to do to their most vulnerable residents — scores lowest of all 8 dimensions globally at 2.11/5.0. Cities routinely over-police, displace, surveil, and criminalize poverty. The cities scoring highest on BND share one characteristic: explicit policy limits on what governance is permitted to do. BND is the dimension that separates cities that manage the poor from cities that protect the poor.
"The question that most reveals a city's compassion is not what it does for its poorest residents. It is what it refuses to do to them."
6African Cities Are Under-Resourced, Not Uncompassionate
Africa has the lowest regional average (13.9) but this reflects governance capacity constraints and conflict contexts, not cultural indifference to suffering. Kigali scores 46.9 Functional with near-zero homelessness and universal community health workers at sub-$1,000 GDP per capita. Cape Town (53.0) maintains progressive municipal governance despite apartheid's inherited spatial inequality. The Critical-band failures in Africa are concentrated in conflict zones, military juntas, and kleptocracies — not across African governance broadly.
"Africa is not failing at compassion. Specific governance systems in Africa are failing. The distinction matters profoundly for policy, funding, and attribution."
7ACT Outscores BND and INT — Cities Act Without Restraint
ACT (2.72/5.0) and SYS (2.64/5.0) score higher than BND (2.11/5.0) and INT (2.14/5.0). Cities are far more willing to fund visible programs than to set limits on their own harmful behavior or to maintain stated values under pressure. This creates the archetypal high-ACT / low-BND city: one that builds social programs while simultaneously displacing the communities those programs serve.
"A city that funds services while evicting communities is not compassionate. It is managing the consequences of its own harm."

Methodology summary

Scores use publicly available evidence across urban governance, accountability, equity, housing, healthcare access, police conduct, displacement, civil society, press freedom, and state treatment of vulnerable urban populations.

Cities selected by metropolitan population (250 largest globally as of 2026). Evidence sources: UN-Habitat, World Bank, EIU Livability Index, Mercer Quality of Living, Transparency International, HRW, Amnesty International, OECD Urban Data, WHO City Health Profiles, and investigative journalism.
